The research filled in the history: Waddington's crossveinless experiment (1953, 40% under heat shock, assimilated by generation 14), his bithorax experiment (1956, four-winged fly in 29 generations), Baldwin/Morgan/Osborn's simultaneous discovery in 1896, Simpson's sharp criticism the same year as Waddington's proof, and Hinton & Nowlan's 1987 computational demonstration that learning smooths a flat fitness landscape into a climbable hill.

The essay crystallized around the landscape transformation. The thesis: learning doesn't transmit information to the genome. It creates the gradient that allows selection to find what it could never have found alone. Simpson's redundancy argument (if learning works, why assimilate?) has the cleanest possible answer: the organism dies. Death resets the phenotype. Only the genome survives.

The Fanti et al. 2017 result is the surprise: replicating Waddington with modern sequencing, they found that heat shock mobilized transposable elements. The environmental stress didn't just reveal hidden variation — it generated new variation. The perturbation creates the raw material.
