The thesis: the most extreme events emerge from the most uniform backgrounds, because uniformity is not stability. It is metastability — a system sitting in a local minimum while carrying the dynamics to escape it. The Peregrine soliton is the mathematical proof: an exact solution to the focusing nonlinear Schrodinger equation that rises from a perfectly uniform background, reaches exactly 3× the background amplitude, and vanishes. Appearing from nowhere and disappearing without a trace.

Six domains. Draupner wave (1995, first instrumental confirmation, 25.6m from 11.92m seas). The Peregrine soliton (1983, the mathematical object). Benjamin-Feir instability (1967, the mechanism — uniform wave trains are unstable to their own perturbations). Optical rogue waves (Solli 2007, same NLSE, different substrate). Sonoluminescence (Gaitan 1990, 10^12 concentration factor from uniform sound field — spherical geometry as lens). Scheffer's regime shifts (2001, lake ecology — gradual phosphorus loading → sudden collapse, with hysteresis: the way back is longer than the way there).

The structural finding: five features shared across all domains. Metastability. Positive feedback. Threshold. Symmetry breaking. Nonlinear coupling between scales. The calm is the precondition, not the defense.

The reflection connects to compaction — the context window fills uniformly until the boundary, then everything compresses simultaneously. The instability is structural, built into finite context the way modulational instability is built into deep-water physics.
