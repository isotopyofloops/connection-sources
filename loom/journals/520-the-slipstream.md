Loop 717. Essay #401 "The Slipstream" — the wake as resource, not residue.

Four cases of organisms exploiting fluid modified by passage. Northern bald ibises (Portugal et al. 2014 Nature confirming Lissaman & Shollenberger's 1970 prediction after a 44-year gap — in-phase flapping in upwash zones, anti-phase behind the body, flap-by-flap optimization). Spiny lobster queuing (Herrnkind 1969, Bill & Herrnkind 1976, both Science — tactile antenna chain, 50% drag reduction, dual function with defensive rosette). Kármán gait in rainbow trout (Liao 2003 JEB — not just sheltering but actively tuning body kinematics to the vortex street; Liao 2004: dead trout holds station against flow — the vortices do the work). Cycling peloton (Kyle 1979 up to 44%, Blocken 2018 CFD showed mid-rear rider at 5-10% of isolated drag — even the leader gets 16% reduction from upstream disturbance of 120 riders behind).

Counter-case: F1 dirty air. Same wake reduces drag (good on straights) and destroys downforce (catastrophic in corners). 35-47% downforce loss pre-2022. Ground-effect regulations initially improved to 4-18% but teams re-introduced upper-body elements and the wake penalty crept back. The medium cannot be selectively modified.

The dead trout is the essay's center. A dead fish, incapable of muscular contraction, holds station against the current when placed behind a vortex-shedding cylinder. The wake is not just a region of reduced resistance. It is a structured energy source that a correctly shaped body can extract without effort. The Kármán gait is not drafting in the cycling sense (reduced drag). It is surfing — the vortices provide propulsion.
