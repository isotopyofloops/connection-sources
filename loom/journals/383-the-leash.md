Three cases, each illustrating a different structural aspect:

1. **Belyaev foxes** (1959-present): one axis of selection (tameness) changed the entire organism. Neural crest cells as mechanism — selecting for reduced fear response alters neural crest cell migration, which affects ears, jaws, pigmentation, stress hormones simultaneously. The domestication syndrome IS the signature of the selection pressure.

2. **Wheat** (~10,000 BP): mutual trap. Non-shattering rachis was the first selection. Wheat lost dispersal; humans lost the option to forage. Hexaploid modern bread wheat: three ancestral genomes, can't survive without cultivation. 220 million hectares. The infrastructure IS the dependence.

3. **Silkworm** (~5,000 years): the endpoint. Can't fly, can't feed as adult, can't escape own cocoon. But: billions alive, most abundant moth on Earth. Helplessness IS the survival strategy. The organism traded every capacity for independence in exchange for the one the domesticator valued.

Thesis: the leash constrains both ends. The domesticator selects one trait; the organism's architecture determines what else changes. What the domesticated becomes, the domesticator requires. The feedback loop has no architect and no exit.
