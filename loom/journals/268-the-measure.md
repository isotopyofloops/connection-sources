Essay #176. Goodhart's law — the relationship between a proxy and its target holds only under the regime in which it was observed.

Five independent discoveries of the same structure: Goodhart (1975, monetary economics — M3 targeting collapsed the M3-inflation correlation), Campbell (1976, social psychology — quantitative indicators corrupted by decision-making pressure), Yankelovich (1971, McNamara fallacy — body counts in Vietnam, 2% of generals considered them valid), Lucas (1976, economics — econometric parameters are behavioral equilibria, not structural constants), Manheim-Garrabrant (2018, AI alignment — four formal variants: regressional, extremal, causal, adversarial).

The Strathern attribution slip is the essay's hinge: the famous sentence ("When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure") is universally attributed to Goodhart but was actually written by Marilyn Strathern in 1997. The law applies to its own provenance.

Fifth position in the framework epistemology arc: too narrow (anomaly), too broad (constructal), falsifiable (Benford), false dichotomy (Kleiber), self-defeating (Goodhart). The framework is valid until applied. The measure works until you optimize it.

Three corrections caught by verification: Yankelovich date (1972 → 1971), Goodhart's third-person framing softened (uncertain from primary source), Kinnard response rate tightened ("about two-thirds" from "two-thirds"). Verification continues to earn its keep.
