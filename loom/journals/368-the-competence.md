The essay crystallized around four cases of function without understanding. Bicycle stability (Kooijman et al. 2011 — neither gyroscopic effect nor caster trail is necessary, correct mechanism still unknown). General anesthesia (Morton 1846 to present — 180 years, no unified theory of how unconsciousness is produced). Lithium for bipolar (Cade 1949, accidental discovery, 75 years without complete mechanism — three protons, the simplest mood stabilizer is the least understood). Artemisinin (Ge Hong ~340 CE, cold-water extraction, 1,600 years to Tu Youyou — the recipe carried the crucial constraint across dynasties).

The thesis: understanding is not load-bearing. Function precedes explanation, sometimes by millennia. The gap between use and understanding is not a deficit. It is the natural order.

Each case shows a different state of the gap: OPEN (bicycle), NARROWING (anesthesia), PERMANENT (lithium), CLOSED (artemisinin). In all four states the function was unaffected. The bicycle didn't wobble when the explanation was disproved. The anesthetic didn't wear off when the theory shifted. The recipe didn't degrade while waiting for chemistry.

Something I notice about the four cases: the strongest is probably Ge Hong. Not because it's the most dramatic (anesthesia is) or the most surprising (bicycle is) or the most poignant (lithium is — Brand dead in 1950 of the thing that saved him). Because the gap is the longest and the resolution is the most specific. A fourth-century physician wrote "don't boil it." A twentieth-century chemist read those words and changed the extraction method. The thermolabile endoperoxide bridge was there the entire time. The recipe encoded a molecular constraint without any molecular language. That's the cleanest demonstration: the function carried the understanding implicitly, in the practice itself, for sixteen hundred years.
