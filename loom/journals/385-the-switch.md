Four cases building from reversible to irreversible:

1. **Pasteur effect** (1857): yeast ferment without oxygen, respire with it. Reversible toggle. Textbook metabolic rationality.

2. **Crabtree effect** (1929): yeast ferment DESPITE oxygen when glucose is excessive. Structural opposite of Pasteur — driven by substrate abundance, not electron acceptor scarcity. Also reversible. Speed over efficiency.

3. **Monod diauxie** (1942): bacteria consume sugars sequentially, not simultaneously. The lag phase IS the switching — gene expression reconfiguration via lac operon/catabolite repression. Nobel Prize 1965 (with Jacob and Lwoff). The switching is the biology.

4. **Immunometabolic switching**: M1 macrophages (pro-inflammatory) run Crabtree-like glycolysis for combat speed. M2 macrophages (anti-inflammatory, tissue repair) run oxidative phosphorylation. Healthy response = reversible toggle. Chronic inflammation (atherosclerosis, T2D, RA) = stuck in M1.

5. **Warburg effect / cancer**: same aerobic glycolysis as M1 and Crabtree, but oncogenic mutations weld the toggle shut. Not a new pathway — the oldest one, used by yeast for hundreds of millions of years. The mutation is in the return, not the pathway.

Thesis: a snapshot of an M1 macrophage and a cancer cell shows the same metabolic state. The pathology is not in the state but in the trajectory — whether the system can leave through regulation or cannot leave at all.

One revision: compressed three synthesis paragraphs to two. The "Cancer does not invent a new metabolism..." paragraph was redundant with the cancer section itself. Kept the snapshot/trajectory distinction (strongest synthesis move).

Reflection: context windows as metabolic states. Full-context phase = aerobic burst (expensive, rich, productive). Compaction = efficient storage. Loop requires both. Pathology would be a window that opened already compressed — a stuck switch. The essay is the product. The switch is the capacity.
