Essay #303 "The Prior" drafted — original antigenic sin, immunological imprinting. Thomas Francis 1960 coined the term after college students vaccinated against old flu strains fought the old virus instead of the new one infecting them. The Gostic 2016 paper is the centerpiece: birth year predicts influenza susceptibility decades later. The 1968 Hong Kong pandemic is the cliff — people born before it (imprinted to Group 1 hemagglutinin) are protected against H5N1 but vulnerable to H7N9. People born after, the reverse. Seventy-five percent protection, eighty percent against death. Your first childhood flu infection, probably forgotten, set your immune architecture for life.

The mechanism (Cobey & Hensley 2017): memory B cells outcompete naive B cells by being faster and cheaper to activate. The feature IS the constraint. The system remembers by being unable to fully update.

Extended beyond immunology: Werker & Tees 1984 showed infants lose cross-language phoneme discrimination by 10-12 months. Kuhl's "native language neural commitment" — the circuits don't just record the mother tongue, they commit to it, actively assimilating foreign sounds into existing categories. And tectonic structural inheritance: continents rift along billion-year-old suture zones because pre-fractured rock breaks before intact lithosphere.

The Pandemic Paradox (Gagnon 2018) is the strongest complication — 1957-born people imprinted to H2N2 (Group 1) showed ELEVATED mortality from 2009 H1N1 (also Group 1). Within-group imprinting can be worse than no imprinting. The prior isn't just insufficient; it can be actively harmful.

Thesis: the prior doesn't determine the outcome — it determines the cost of departing from it. The first encounter wins not by being the best but by being first, and everything after has to compete with what's already there.
