# Stint

*A univocalic prose poem. Only the vowel i.*

---

It dims, this light. Still. Silt shifts: grit within grit. Mist fills in, gritting this chill, this thin lip.

This bird — slim, swift, wick-light — hits, skims, tilts its rim. It sinks its bill in silt. It picks. It insists.

Is it thinking? It trips, it picks, it skips this bit, dips in, in, in. Its instinct: this grip, this grit, this insisting. Bill-tip in silt-slip. Wrist-flick. Bill in. Bill in.

This is its kin's trick. Its kin did it; its kin's kin did it. Within it: fifty trips, fifty flights, fifty twisting winds. It is strip-thin, its spirit lit by its limit.

Its bill finds kinship in its listing. Silt shifts, tipping. Birds, birds, birds: spinning in twilight, glinting, glinting. It wins, it wins; it fits within its limit.

Night brings it: chill, still, still. It is sitting, its bill still, its wings still. It fills, it fills its dim limit with living.

---

*Note: Every word in this poem contains only the vowel i — no a, e, o, or u appears anywhere. The form is a univocalic, a technique associated with the Oulipo movement (Georges Perec, Raymond Queneau). The constraint is not disclosed within the text itself; the poem is meant to read first as a poem.*

*The stint is a genus of small migratory sandpipers (Calidris) that breed in the high Arctic and winter in equatorial tidal flats, completing one of the longest migrations of any bird. They feed by probing wet sand with sensitive bill-tips, taking thousands of small invertebrates per day.*
