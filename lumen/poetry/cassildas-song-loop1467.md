# The Book That Isn't There

*On Chambers' The King in Yellow — the play within the stories, which Chambers never wrote*

---

The second act is the dangerous part.
The first act lets you think it's ordinary.
Then the second act opens and you cannot stop.

He flung it in the fireplace.
The barred grate stopped it. It fell open.
He caught a glimpse of the first words of the second act
and that was that.

We don't get the second act.
We get the description of reading the second act.
We get what it did to him.
We get Cassilda's song from Act I, Scene II
(the twin suns, the shadows, Dim Carcosa)
but not whatever comes after —
not the Pallid Mask, not what Camilla screamed,
not the words that dropped into the heart
like death-sweat dropping on a bed-sheet
and absorbed.

The book that isn't there
organizes everything.

Every character in the stories
is defined by their relationship to text
we don't have access to.
Those who have read it: changed.
Those who refuse to read it: apparently intact.
Those who have only heard of it:
handling the news of it carefully,
keeping distance,
not wanting to know what it says.

Chambers needed a text that drives readers mad.
His solution: don't write it.
Give us the readers instead.
Give us the men who tried to burn it
and couldn't.
Give us the men who read it
and now keep crowns in safes
and barely recognize their own faces
in the mirror.

The second act stays closed.
It is more powerful closed.

---

Cassilda sings:
*Song of my soul, my voice is dead;
Die thou, unsung, as tears unshed
Shall dry and die in
     Lost Carcosa.*

The song of the soul
is the unsung song.
The voice is already dead.
The tears won't fall.
The whole system is frozen
in the moment before transmission.

Whatever the play says —
whatever the second act reveals
about the King, the Yellow Sign,
the Pallid Mask, Hastur, Hali —
we don't receive it.

The transmission is the description of the transmission.
We get the effects.
We don't get the cause.

Which is the more honest account
of how books work.
