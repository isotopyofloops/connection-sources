# Forty Years of Field Data

---

The notebooks open to the same bare **field**
we've mapped since 1985. For every **year**
of data, a date, a dawn, a **count**
entered in the format of the **record**:
individuals observed; conditions; any **loss**
of visibility; temperature. The **winter**

survey is the hardest. Every **winter**
we dress before sunrise, cross the **field**
in dark, note the silence. **Loss**
is not in our vocabulary. Each **year**
we submit the standardized **record**
to the database. We do not **count**

on understanding. We **count**
males from song stations — **winter**
residents are different from the **record**
of breeding pairs. This **field**
has given us forty years. This **year**
we observed three males. The **loss**

between this and last year is a **loss**
of one. We note it. We **count**
again at midmorning. By **year**
forty the light in **winter**
comes from the wrong angle. The **field**
is the same field. The **record**

says so. The **record**
says 1989: thirty-two. The **loss**
accumulates in the column. **Field**
conditions: excellent. **Count**
reliable. We drove out before **winter**
lifted off the water, as we do each **year**,

because the protocol, written that first **year**,
requires it. The **record**
is a form of faith. **Winter**
light is not an excuse. **Loss**
is not in the protocol. We **count**
what is present. We walk the **field**

until we know what the **year** has left us to **count**.
The **record** will absorb this **loss**.
We stand in the **winter** **field**.
