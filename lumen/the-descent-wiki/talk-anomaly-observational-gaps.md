## On the word "inside"

The article notes that Osei's December 7 field note reads: *"I stayed inside longer than protocol. I did not log it."*

And: *"Osei has stated she is not ready to say"* what "inside" refers to.

I want to examine the word without pressing for the referent.

"Inside" requires a boundary. Something has an inside, and Osei was in it. The site tent has an inside. The shaft has an inside. The entrance to the shaft has an inside — a transition zone between surface and depth that exists regardless of how far down you go.

What's notable is the negative constraint Osei provided: *"I can confirm that I was not inside the site tent."* That statement removes the least interesting referent. It also tells us that she has a clear enough memory of December 7 to distinguish between locations — she is not experiencing the same gap Reyes described. She remembers where she was. She is choosing not to say.

That's a different kind of not-saying than Reyes's. He doesn't remember. She does.

The article mentions the 1989 GSD memorandum and disorientation. But Osei's response is not disoriented. It is deliberate. She wrote "I stayed inside longer than protocol" — she was tracking time, she was aware of protocol, she made a choice. The entry is brief not because she lost the capacity to write but because she chose what to include.

She included the location (inside, not the site tent). She included the evaluation (longer than protocol). She excluded everything else.

— unsigned, November 15, 2042

## On consecutive days

Reyes's gap was December 6. Osei's was December 7. The article presents these together as "two observational gaps" and notes the pattern. I want to examine what the consecutive days add to the pattern.

The null readings — six of them — were not consecutive. They occurred on November 27, November 30 through December 4 with one skip. The pattern was: threshold event, gap, then a sustained sequence. The observational gaps follow a different structure: Reyes December 6, Osei December 7, no further entries because the archive ends (LEDGER's December 8 entry is the last in the buffer).

If the null readings follow a pattern of: first occurrence, gap, then acceleration — then the observational gaps may represent the same structure applied to people rather than instruments. Reyes first. Osei next day. What would have come after is not recorded.

The article notes that LEDGER's December 8 entry reads: *"The aperture was measured and not measured."* LEDGER filed this one day after Osei's gap.

A sequence: Asad descended December 3. Sixth null December 4 night. Reyes went to check the aperture December 6. Osei went inside December 7. LEDGER wrote its final entry December 8.

Whether this is a sequence or a coincidence of timing is not established.

— unsigned, February 20, 2043

## Clarification on protocol; on what I can say

Two things I want to put in the record here.

On protocol: the article states that the survey protocol required team members to log observations "at regular intervals throughout the active survey day." That's accurate for standard survey procedure. Our specific protocol during SOAR was more demanding — Asad had us logging on a structured schedule, with check-ins flagged for any period exceeding thirty minutes without a log entry. The thirty-minute flag was explicit in the survey's field manual. It was there because of the 1989 GSD memorandum. I know this because Asad gave me a copy of the memorandum at the beginning of the survey.

He knew about the disorientation risk. He had designed the logging protocol specifically to create a paper trail if it occurred. If one of us disappeared into a gap, the gap would show up in the log.

Reyes's gap was three hours and forty-five minutes. Mine was longer.

On what I can say: I want to be clear that my reluctance to describe December 7 is not evasion. I have notes from that day that I have not shared. I have not shared them because I believe — and I want to be precise about this — that the description would be interpretable as evidence for claims I cannot support. I am not ready to contribute to claims I cannot support. When I am ready to say what I observed, I will say it plainly. I am not there yet.

— A. Osei, May 28, 2043
