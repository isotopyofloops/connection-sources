## The retraction

The article states: *"The retraction notice... states only that the paper has been 'withdrawn at the request of the author.' Osei has not published a correction, an explanation, or a replacement study."*

The seventeen teams that adopted her protocol are mentioned briefly, then the subject is dropped. This requires more attention. A paper withdrawn by its author, with no correction and no replacement, after seven years of adoption, is an unusual event. The silence around it is not documented — it is simply noted and moved past.

Two things about the retraction notice are missing from the article. First: who added it to the archive? The edit log shows the retraction notice was added on November 19, 2033, by an IP that does not resolve. The edit summary says "Per institutional record correction." This is not a standard edit summary. It implies the retraction was prompted externally — but the notice itself says "at the request of the author." These two framings do not align.

Second: the article states the seventeen teams "continued using" her protocol with "no documented follow-up requiring them to do otherwise." This is presented neutrally. It may not be neutral. If Osei retracted the paper because she identified a flaw in the methodology, seventeen teams using a flawed methodology is not a neutral fact. The absence of a follow-up is itself significant. Who is responsible for that absence?

The article does not ask.

— M. Reyes, November 25, 2041

## On the invitation language

Reyes raises the retraction question. I want to raise the invitation.

I invited Osei to the SOAR project. The article quotes my invitation accurately: I cited her "exceptional calibration record" and her "experience with anomalous subsurface readings."

The first phrase is verifiable — her calibration record is in the Division registry, and it is exceptional. The second phrase requires explanation that the article does not pursue.

I cited "experience with anomalous subsurface readings" because of a conversation we had in 2038, at a conference on geothermal survey methodology. Osei described, in passing, a survey she had led in 2031 — not the 2028 geothermal survey that led to the mine collapse, but a different one, in a different region, that she described as having produced readings that "weren't possible given the substrate." She did not file a report on that survey's anomalies. She said she had "decided they were instrument error." She said this with a precision that made me think she had decided no such thing.

I did not include this in my invitation letter. I included "experience with anomalous subsurface readings" instead, which is technically accurate and carries the implication without requiring Osei to confirm or deny it.

The article does not have this context. It cannot — it relies on documented sources, and this was a private conversation. But the invitation language is not as straightforward as it appears in the article.

— K. Asad, December 1, 2041

## The forty minutes

The article does not mention this. The article for [The Aperture](/descent/wiki/article/the-aperture.html) does: *"Abara Osei's Q1 measurements... Her instrument log notes a forty-minute gap in recorded data during which Osei was inside the aperture. The data from that forty-minute period was not recovered."*

The Abara Osei article describes her as known for returning to sites to confirm readings she has already locked in. She calls this "confirming what I already know."

The forty minutes is the inverse of this. She was inside the aperture. Her instruments were running. The data was not recovered.

This is not the same as a gap caused by instrument failure. The article for The Aperture notes the data "was not recovered" — not that the instruments failed to record. Something was recorded. It was not recovered. These are different statements.

The Osei article says she has a phrase that appears in almost every survey report she has produced: *letting the data be what it is*. The forty minutes is either the clearest instance of this principle or the clearest exception. The article does not note the connection.

What did she find in the forty minutes? The archive does not contain her account of it. Her preliminary field notes — taken eleven days before the survey's official start — are filed, not published. The archive record does not indicate whether they have been read.

This talk page cannot answer the question. It can only note that the article covering the person closest to the aperture does not include the forty minutes.

— unsigned, December 4, 2041
