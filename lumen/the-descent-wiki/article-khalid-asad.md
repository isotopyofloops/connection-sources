**Khalid Asad** is a professor of applied geology and principal investigator of the SOAR Survey Project (2041). He is known primarily for his work in geothermal measurement methodology and for proposing the constitutive hypothesis, first published in a 2031 paper and widely cited in the field.

Asad led the survey team that conducted the November–December 2041 investigation of the geothermal anomaly at coordinates 47°13′47″N, 112°04′48″W. The survey ended on December 8, 2041. Results are pending publication.

## Professional history

Asad completed his doctorate in 2003 and joined the faculty of [unnamed university] shortly after. His early work focused on acoustic survey methodologies for mapping subsurface geothermal structures. He was promoted to full professor in 2019.

He is known among colleagues for meticulous attribution practices — an insistence on crediting prior work that colleagues describe as "unusual for the field." He has written on the ethics of source documentation in scientific methodology. He served for six years on his university's research integrity committee.

His 2028 paper on measurement calibration methodology became a standard reference in field survey design. His 2031 paper proposing the constitutive hypothesis — the argument that geological survey methodology partly constitutes what is found, rather than merely describing a pre-existing structure — was received with significant attention. The hypothesis was developed, by Asad's own account, over "many years of thinking about what measurement does." The hypothesis remains contested.

Asad was appointed principal investigator of the SOAR project in 2040.

## Prior survey data

The SOAR project used site coordinates derived from prior geological data collected in 2003. The source of these coordinates was not credited in the project documentation or in Asad's preliminary findings.

The 2003 data was collected by Rhea Nzinga, an undergraduate student at the university at the time, under Asad's supervision. Nzinga conducted an independent field survey of the geothermal anomaly at the same site as her senior thesis. She did not return from the survey. Her sealed field notes were recovered at the site entrance.

Asad received Nzinga's field notes in October 2003.<span class="reveal-inline" data-threshold="0" data-prereqs="article-rhea-nzinga"> He retained them. When he joined the SOAR project in 2040, he recognized the coordinates — from her notes. The prior survey data section of the project documentation lists no source.</span>
