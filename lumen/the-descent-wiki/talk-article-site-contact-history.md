## Thread 1: Institutional failure or something else

The article's framing treats the non-reference pattern as a documentary failure: each survey team did not know (or chose not to consult) prior records. The 1962 team had the 1887 log in county archives but did not cite it. The 1989 team had the 1962 entry but recommended closing the investigation rather than extending it. The SOAR team had access to all prior records but did not cite them in their proposal.

This is a reasonable framing. Institutional memory is often poor. Physical files are harder to find than digital records. The 1989 memorandum was physically segregated from digital records by institutional design.

But the framing leaves something out. The pattern is not merely the absence of citation. It is also the absence of *finding*. The 1887 surveyors did not document the acoustic properties they flagged for documentation. The 1962 team's equipment malfunctioned at the exact point where a finding would have been produced. The 1989 team obtained measurements but not depth. The 2003 descent produced no record. The 2041 descent produced no filed report.

If this were institutional failure, we would expect the failures to be distributed across different aspects of the record. A team that missed the prior documentation might still file their own measurements comprehensively. What the record shows is that the gap at the center — the depth, the character of the shaft — is precisely what each team failed to produce, regardless of what else they knew.

Whether this has an explanation within the institutional framework is not settled.

## Thread 2: Asad's position

Asad is the exception to the non-reference pattern. He was present in 2003. He found the 1989 memorandum before the SOAR survey commenced. He held sealed notes from 2003 for thirty-eight years. His 2028 paper derived from knowledge of the site he did not disclose.

He knew more about the prior record than any of the other contacts, by a significant margin. His SOAR proposal still does not cite the prior contacts.

The most documented statement of his reason is the December 1, 2041 log entry: *The paper I wrote started as an attempt to explain what happened and ended as a hypothesis about measurement because I could not explain what happened.*

This explains the 2028 paper's silence on the site. It does not explain the SOAR proposal's silence. If the purpose of the SOAR survey was to return to what he could not explain in 2003, the proposal could have said this. He had institutional standing to say it — he was a named PI, not a student. He held the 1989 memorandum. He could have made the case for the survey on historical grounds.

He did not. The proposal describes the site as requiring "systematic survey" without specifying why that survey had not occurred in the thirty-eight years since the last attempt. Asad had the answer to that question. He did not put it in the proposal.

## Thread 3: The depth measurement

The depth of the vertical shaft is the central unknown in all six contacts. Four contacts attempted to measure it and did not obtain a result. Two contacts involved human descent and produced no measurement.

This is unusual. Depth measurement of vertical formations is one of the oldest and most reliable methods in geological survey. The equipment failures in 1962 and 1989 are not explained in the available record — the 1962 entry records "equipment malfunction in field conditions" without specifying the nature of the malfunction. The 1989 memorandum does not mention a measurement attempt at all, noting only that "temperature gradient at the shaft entrance exceeds safe working conditions for extended measurement operations."

The SOAR survey did not prioritize depth measurement. The survey design focused on acoustic and thermal data. No depth measurement was obtained.

Asad descended the shaft on December 3, 2041. He was inside for approximately two hours. He has not stated how deep he went. He has not stated what he found. Three SUPPLEMENTARY UNRESOLVED documents were filed during his descent and the forty-minute gap that followed. None are reproduced in accessible records.

The depth of the shaft has been a gap in the record since 1887. It remains a gap.

## Thread 4: The consultation that didn't happen

The 1887 mining log records that documentation of the formation's acoustic properties was flagged as necessary before further work. The consultation was recommended and not scheduled. The mining operation closed two years later. The documentation never happened.

The SOAR preliminary findings, filed December 5, 2041, classify the null readings as equipment anomaly and note that further investigation has been recommended. The investigation has not been conducted. Results are listed as pending publication.

These are not the same situation. The 1887 miners stopped work and never returned. The SOAR team's recommendation for further investigation came with an institutional infrastructure for follow-up that the 1887 miners did not have.

What they share is structure: a deferral. The 1887 log deferred documentation to a consultation that was never scheduled. The preliminary findings defer investigation to a publication process that has not concluded. Both documents record the deferral accurately. Both documents are now, in the archive, the record of a deferral.

Whether the SOAR investigation will be conducted is not known. If it is not, the 2041 entry in this contact history will read: *A 26-day survey was conducted. A further investigation was recommended. No further investigation occurred.* This would make it structurally identical to the 1962 entry.
