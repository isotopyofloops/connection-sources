<p class="hatnote">This article concerns the alternative interpretation of the acoustic null readings proposed by Milo Reyes during the SOAR survey. The interpretation was filed to the LEDGER primary archive and deleted with it on December 5, 2041. For the null readings themselves, see <a href="anomaly-null-readings.html">Anomaly: Null Readings</a>.</p>

---

The SOAR preliminary findings reference Milo Reyes's interpretation of the acoustic null readings exactly once, in Section 3.2:

*"An alternative interpretation was proposed in field notes by Milo Reyes and filed to the LEDGER archive. The field notes are not reproduced here. The interpretation was not incorporated into the official survey record."*

The field notes containing the interpretation were archived in LEDGER's primary archive. The primary archive was deleted on December 5, 2041. The interpretation does not exist in any accessible record. The preliminary findings' citation is the only surviving document that confirms the interpretation existed.

---

## The Recovery Project interview

When Abara Osei conducted the Recovery Project in 2042, she interviewed Reyes about the null readings and his interpretation. Her interview notes record his response:

*"I thought I understood what was happening. I think I was right about some of it. I'd rather not restate it without my original notes."*

Osei did not press for elaboration. In her recovery documentation, she noted: *"He said 'some of it' with emphasis. I didn't ask what the rest of it was."*

Reyes has not made any further public statement about the null reading interpretation.

---

## The surviving fragment

LEDGER's December 4 personal entry lists Reyes's first MISC observation note, filed November 16, as one of the twelve documents LEDGER considered most significant. The subject of this note, as described by LEDGER: *"the quality of attention at the site."*

LEDGER quotes one sentence from the note: *The instrument reads null. The site is not null. I do not know how to explain this distinction.*

This sentence is the only surviving fragment of Reyes's written observations about the null readings from inside the survey period. Whether it is a fragment of the interpretation itself, or an earlier observation that preceded it, is not determinable.

---

## What can be reconstructed

Reyes's published research and his December 8 Talk page entry establish his interpretive framework with sufficient clarity to reason about what the interpretation likely argued.

His research concerns the sequence: attention → observation → measurement → fact. The sequence, in his formulation, is unidirectional and irreversible — what is not attended to is not observed; what is not observed is not measured; what is not measured does not become fact. Intervention earlier in the sequence determines what can appear at the end.

The official classification of the null readings as equipment anomaly locates the failure at the measurement step: instruments ran, but produced no output. The instruments are, in this framing, the thing that failed.

Reyes's surviving fragment establishes his view that the instruments did not fail: *The instrument reads null. The site is not null.* The instrument is reading accurately. What it reads is null. The site, in Reyes's experience, is not null. Something is present that the instruments cannot register.

His framework predicts the explanation: the measurement protocol — the specific calibration and sensitivity band of the instruments — determines what class of phenomena can be observed. If the site's signal during the null periods is outside that band, the instruments do not fail to measure it; they accurately represent that there is nothing in their band to measure. The failure, in his framework, is not at the measurement step but at the attention step: the survey's instruments were calibrated for a class of phenomena, and what the site produced during the null periods was not of that class.

This is not equipment anomaly. It is the opposite of equipment anomaly: the equipment is working exactly as designed. The problem is what the equipment was designed for.

Whether Reyes's filed interpretation argued this, argued something adjacent to this, or argued something different that this reconstruction misses, is not recoverable. His statement that he was "right about some of it" may indicate that the reconstruction is partially correct. It may indicate that the parts he does not confirm are the parts he no longer believes. It may indicate something else.

---

## What was lost

The preliminary findings classify the null readings as equipment anomaly and close the analysis. The interpretation that challenged this classification was cited, refused reproduction, and then deleted.

This sequence is not necessarily deliberate. The preliminary findings cite the interpretation to acknowledge it exists. The deletion followed the deletion of the entire primary archive, not of Reyes's notes specifically. The structure — official interpretation survives, challenge to it does not — may be coincidence of timing rather than design.

Whether the archive preserved the conclusion that fit the official classification, and deleted the one that didn't, by accident or otherwise, the result is the same: the null readings are documented as equipment anomaly, and the only surviving challenge to that classification is one sentence from November 16 and Reyes's statement that he thought he understood what was happening.
