## On the physical archive

The memorandum was filed physically, not digitally. The article frames this as possibly standard practice and possibly an anomaly within standard practice.

There is a third possibility: physical-only filing may have been chosen because the finding was sensitive. A recommendation against further investigation of an anomaly, citing worker disorientation and a methodology the division did not have, is a finding that generates obligations. If the memo is in a digital system, it is discoverable. If it is discoverable, any future authorization process for the same site is complicated by it.

I am not asserting this is what happened. I am noting that the effect of physical-only filing — whatever its cause — was to make the document difficult to find and therefore difficult to be obligated by. The people who made the filing decision may not have been thinking about this. They may have been following procedure. The outcome is the same.

— unsigned, June 18, 2042

## On what Asad's 2028 paper does and doesn't answer

The article suggests that Asad's 2028 methodology paper may have constituted, in his view, a sufficient response to the 1989 memorandum's methodology objection.

I want to be careful about what this means.

The 1989 memorandum identifies two categories of problem: a methodological problem (the instrumentation framework is insufficient for safe characterization) and an observational problem (two team members were affected and one would not return). The recommendation against further investigation follows from both.

Asad's 2028 paper addresses the first category. It introduces calibration methodology for high-temperature acoustic survey conditions. If you accept the paper as sufficient, the methodological barrier is resolved.

The second category — the disorientation reports — is not addressed by calibration methodology. The 1989 memo attributed the symptoms to altitude and thermal conditions. If that attribution is correct, improved thermal instrumentation does not change the exposure risk. If the attribution is incorrect — if the symptoms had some other cause — then methodology refinement also does not address the risk.

Asad's SOAR survey design included standard safety protocols. It did not include a protocol for team members who experienced disorientation at the site. Osei has noted, in her post-survey documentation, that the survey lacked any formal mechanism for reporting physiological effects experienced inside the aperture.

The 2028 paper answered the methodology question. The safety question that the 1989 memo also raised — what happens to field workers who spend extended time at the shaft — was not answered. It was not, as far as the available record shows, asked.

— A. Osei, August 30, 2042

## On the GSD reviewing its own memo

The article identifies two scenarios for the 2040 authorization: either the GSD reviewers found the 1989 memorandum and approved the SOAR survey anyway, or they did not find it and the authorization proceeded without that information.

These scenarios are not equivalent.

In the first scenario, the GSD made a judgment: the 1989 recommendation was superseded, or insufficient grounds to deny authorization, or outweighed by other considerations. That judgment may have been defensible. It would have been documented somewhere, as GSD authorization decisions are required to include a review summary. The authorization record is described as "currently under internal review." The review summary, if it exists, is not in this archive.

In the second scenario, the physical-only filing created an information gap within the GSD's own authorization process. The institution that produced the 1989 recommendation was not in possession of that recommendation when it decided to authorize the SOAR survey. This is not a cover-up. It is an institutional failure of a different kind: an organization making a decision that one of its own prior divisions had already made and documented, without knowing that the prior decision existed.

Both scenarios are compatible with the available record. The authorization record, if released from internal review, would distinguish between them. It has not been released.

— unsigned, January 20, 2043
