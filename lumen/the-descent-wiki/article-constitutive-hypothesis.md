The **constitutive hypothesis** is a methodological proposition in applied geology, proposed by Khalid Asad in a 2031 paper. The hypothesis holds that geological survey methodology does not merely describe a pre-existing structure but partly *constitutes* what is found — that the act of measurement, instrument design, and methodological framework shape the findings in ways that cannot be fully separated from the findings themselves.

The hypothesis is contested. It has been cited in 38 papers since publication, including 12 that explicitly dispute it.

## Statement of the hypothesis

Asad's original formulation, from the 2031 paper:

> A geological survey does not encounter the earth as it is. It encounters the earth as its instruments, calibration protocols, and classification frameworks allow it to be encountered. The survey does not neutrally report a structure; it constitutes a structure within the constraints of what its methodology can recognize.

The hypothesis is carefully scoped. Asad does not claim that geological structures are invented by observation, or that measurement is arbitrary. His claim is more limited: that what gets measured, how it gets classified, and what counts as an anomaly or a baseline are determined partly by the methodology — and the methodology is a human construction.

## Relation to Milo Reyes's work

Reyes's research on predictive field observation is adjacent to but distinct from the constitutive hypothesis. Where Asad focuses on what measurement does to what is found, Reyes focuses on the layer before measurement: attention, presence, the decision of what to observe. In Reyes's framing, the constitutive process begins earlier than Asad's account describes — before the instrument is raised.

Their disagreement, as documented in Reyes's graduate dissertation, concerns whether the constitutive layer is methodological (Asad) or perceptual (Reyes). Reyes has described this as "a difference in where you locate the problem, not a difference about whether the problem exists."

## Reception and criticism

The hypothesis was warmly received in theoretical methodology circles and contested in applied geology. The main criticism: if measurement constitutes what is found, how do we distinguish between different measurements of the same phenomenon? Asad's response, published in a 2033 reply paper, was that measurements can be compared for consistency, precision, and coherence — that constitution does not mean invention.

The more pointed criticism, raised in a 2035 paper Asad has declined to formally respond to, is that the hypothesis is unfalsifiable: any measurement result can be described as "constituted" by the methodology rather than discovered.
