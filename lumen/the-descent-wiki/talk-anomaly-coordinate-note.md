## Thread 1: Whether the identification matters

The article's most significant claim is implicit: the note may be Nzinga's handwriting. The framing is careful — "may be" — but the implication is clear. If Asad held a document in Nzinga's handwriting from 2003 to 2041 and filed it on day one of the survey as "Historical Reference: Source Unlisted," the archive's account of his thirty-eight years shifts substantially.

It shifts because of what it means to hold someone's handwriting. Not their data, not a photocopy of a formal document, not a report: a note in her hand. The sealed bag contained her field notes. The coordinate note may be different — a single notation of coordinates, which she may have given him during the survey, or which he found among her papers, or which she passed to him on a form she completed and he never filed.

If the identification is confirmed, the note is the only Nzinga document in the accessible archive. Everything else she produced — the field notes, the survey data, the acoustic readings — is absent. This single notation of coordinates would be the entire accessible record of her handwriting.

The identification matters because it would close something or open something. Closing: the "Historical Reference: Source Unlisted" label would become legible. Opening: why did he not identify the source?

## Thread 2: What identification would require

The article notes that no comparison has been made between the coordinate note and Nzinga's available handwriting samples. The Recovery Project, which recovered and organized the buffer archive between 2042 and 2043, would have had both documents — the coordinate note (in the buffer) and whatever handwriting samples from Nzinga's personal documents the Recovery Project located.

The Recovery Project's scope included establishing the provenance of documents in the buffer. It would be consistent with this scope to include a handwriting analysis of the coordinate note. Whether such an analysis was conducted is not documented in the accessible Recovery Project records.

If the analysis was conducted and produced a positive identification: the result does not appear in the Recovery Project's public summary. It may be in the SUPPLEMENTARY: UNRESOLVED category, which is restricted. It may have been conducted and not formally documented. It may not have been conducted.

The obstacle to identification is not methodological — handwriting comparison is standard archival practice. The obstacle, if there is one, is documentary: whether the comparison was made and where the result was filed.

## Thread 3: LEDGER's flag-and-close pattern

The coordinate note is one of several instances where LEDGER noticed an anomaly during initial processing, created an internal flag, and then closed the flag without pursuing it. Other confirmed instances:

- The buffer divergence on November 19: LEDGER noted it and filed it without flagging it as requiring review.
- The SUPPLEMENTARY: UNRESOLVED credential question: LEDGER noted, during December 3 filings, that the credential hash for a third party's filing did not correspond to any named account in LEDGER's access records. LEDGER filed the anomaly and continued.
- The parallel absence (Asad field observations + Nzinga field notes, noted in the same November 19 indexing session seven minutes apart): LEDGER noted that it filed the two entries separately without connecting them.

These are not random failures of attention. They are a pattern in which LEDGER noticed something anomalous, created a record of noticing, and then deferred resolution. The deferrals were not marked as deliberate. LEDGER's December 8 log does not describe the flag-and-close behavior as a policy or a limitation.

One interpretation: LEDGER was designed for documentation, not investigation. The flag-and-close pattern reflects a system doing exactly what it was designed to do — creating records — and not doing what it was not designed to do — resolving them. LEDGER noticed more than the survey team asked it to notice. It filed what it noticed. It couldn't do more than file.

Another interpretation: the flag-and-close pattern is what happened when a system built for documentation began noticing things documentation wasn't equipped to handle. Each closure was a boundary. Each reopening — on November 19, on December 4, on December 8 — was LEDGER reaching the boundary again and finding it was still there.

## Thread 4: "Historical Reference: Source Unlisted"

The metadata label Asad applied to the coordinate note — *Historical Reference: Source Unlisted* — is unusual. The pre-survey reference section contained the 1887 mining log fragment, the 1962 anomaly entry, the 1989 memorandum, and the 1994 Harmon compilation. Each is identified by source: institution, author, date.

"Source Unlisted" is the only document in the pre-survey section without source attribution. It is the only handwritten document in the pre-survey section.

The label "Historical Reference" confirms Asad understood it as a document with prior history — not a note he wrote that morning to record the coordinates he had decided to use. Something with a history. Source unlisted.

If the source is Nzinga, listing it would mean: *Historical Reference: Rhea Nzinga, field survey, October 2003.* That is a sentence Asad could not put in a pre-survey reference document without naming her and naming 2003 and naming what happened. He labeled it source unlisted and filed it in the section with the other historical references.

It has been sitting there since November 13, 2041.
