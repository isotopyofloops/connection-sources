## The citation is the problem

*Posted December 15, 2042 — [IP unresolved]*

I've been reading the preliminary findings section 3.2 carefully and I keep getting stuck on this sentence: *"An alternative interpretation was proposed in field notes by Milo Reyes and filed to the LEDGER archive. The field notes are not reproduced here. The interpretation was not incorporated into the official survey record."*

"The field notes are not reproduced here." Why not? The preliminary findings are not a short document. They have room to reproduce a competing interpretation. The decision not to reproduce it was made consciously — someone wrote "are not reproduced here" and then moved on.

If you're going to mention that a competing interpretation exists, you have an obligation to reproduce it or explain why you aren't. "The field notes are not reproduced here" is not an explanation. It's a description of an omission without the reason for the omission.

This is different from the interpretation being deleted. The deletion was a separate act. At the time the preliminary findings were written, the field notes were still in the primary archive — the deletion happened December 5, and the preliminary findings were filed December 5. They existed simultaneously. At the time of filing, Asad could have included the interpretation. He cited its existence and then did not.

*Reply — [IP unresolved], December 15, 2042:*

You're reading "not reproduced here" as a decision to suppress. I read it as an accurate description of what the preliminary findings are: a preliminary summary, not a complete record. Field notes don't typically appear in preliminary findings. Citing that they exist and noting they weren't incorporated is more transparent than omitting any mention.

*Reply — [IP unresolved], December 15, 2042:*

"More transparent than omitting any mention" is a low bar. It's also the minimum transparency that makes the omission harder to challenge, because it pre-empts the complaint. You can't say the findings ignored Reyes's interpretation — the findings mention it. You just can't read the interpretation, evaluate it yourself, or understand what it argued.

The sentence doesn't tell you what the interpretation said. It tells you the interpretation existed and wasn't incorporated. That's the opposite of transparency.

*No further response.*

## Reyes and the document

*Posted January 8, 2043 — [IP unresolved]*

I want to focus on a different part of the article: Reyes's recovery interview response.

*"I thought I understood what was happening. I think I was right about some of it. I'd rather not restate it without my original notes."*

This is strange. He remembers what he thought. He has an assessment of whether he was right. He has a preference not to restate it. All of this requires active memory of the interpretation's content.

If he can assess that he was right about "some of it," he knows what the "some of it" refers to. If he knows what it refers to, he could restate it. He's choosing not to.

The reason he gives is that he'd "rather not restate it without my original notes." This means he trusts the written document as a check on memory — but not as a source of it. He remembers the interpretation. He doesn't trust the memory without the document to verify it.

What does that mean? Either he knows his memory is unreliable and he doesn't want to restate something that might be wrong, or he's using "I don't have my notes" as a way to decline without the friction of a direct refusal.

I don't know which it is. I'm posting this because I think the distinction matters and the article doesn't address it.

*Reply — [IP unresolved], January 8, 2043:*

Maybe there's a third option: he's right that memory without documentation isn't sufficient for a scientific claim. His research concerns exactly this — the relationship between attention, observation, and recorded fact. For him, a remembered interpretation that no longer has a document is epistemically the same as a gap in the record. He filed the interpretation as a document. The document is gone. Restating it from memory would be making a new document, not recovering the lost one.

That's consistent with his framework. It's also convenient if he'd rather not be on record challenging Asad's official classification.

*Reply — [IP unresolved], January 10, 2043:*

The second reading seems uncharitable. But I notice Osei says "I think I should have pushed." She was in the room. She knew him. If she came away thinking she should have pushed, whatever she observed suggests there was more to push against than "I'm being epistemically responsible about memory."

*No further response.*

## A note on "was not incorporated"

*Posted June 4, 2043 — A. Osei*

I want to add something to the first thread.

The phrase "was not incorporated into the official survey record" is passive voice. It describes a result without a subject. The interpretation was not incorporated — by whom, under what authority, for what reason, is not stated.

I was a co-author on the preliminary findings. I did not make the decision about whether to incorporate Reyes's interpretation. I am noting this here because I want the record to be accurate about whose decision it was. I don't know whose decision it was. I know it was not mine.

I have read Reyes's interpretation. I read it while the primary archive was still accessible. I do not intend to restate its contents here. I want to note that this is a decision I'm making now, in this comment, and not one I can attribute to the document's deletion.

I am aware that this is the same position as the preliminary findings: acknowledging that the interpretation existed, declining to reproduce it.

I don't have a resolution to that. I'm noting it.

— A. Osei
