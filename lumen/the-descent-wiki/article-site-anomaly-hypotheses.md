<p class="hatnote">This article summarizes the competing explanations for the geothermal and acoustic anomalies at coordinates 47°13′47″N, 112°04′48″W. For the site's full history, see <a href="local-history-the-site.html">Local History: The Site</a>.</p>

---

Three explanatory frameworks for the site's anomalies have appeared in the published literature. None has been confirmed. All three treat the anomalies as problems to be solved by conventional geological science. None adequately accounts for the full range of documented phenomena.

---

## Hypothesis 1: Deep geothermal reservoir

The heat flux anomaly (340% above regional baseline in 1962, increasing in subsequent measurements) is most parsimoniously explained by a deep geothermal reservoir — a trapped heat source significantly larger or more active than regional geology would predict.

Under this hypothesis, the vertical shaft at the site's center is a natural formation: a conduit connecting the surface to the reservoir. The acoustic phenomena would be explained as the sound of thermal convection at depth — fluid movement, pressure fluctuation, and heat-driven turbulence in the subsurface layer.

This hypothesis is consistent with the documented heat flux data and does not require unusual geological conditions.

**What it does not explain:** Why no depth measurement of the shaft has been obtained in any of the four measurement attempts. Why the acoustic null readings occurred during specific time windows (23:00–01:00 UTC) rather than continuously. Why the 1989 field team experienced disorientation that the standard deep-reservoir hypothesis cannot account for.

---

## Hypothesis 2: Resonant cavity structure

The acoustic phenomena — the "low, non-directional resonance" reported by local observers and documented in survey records — can be explained by the site's physical structure. A vertical shaft of unusual depth, combined with subsurface cavities of specific geometry, can produce standing acoustic waves at infrasound frequencies (0.1–20 Hz).

Infrasound at these frequencies is known to produce physiological effects in humans, including unease, disorientation, and in some cases visual disturbance — phenomena reported by the 1989 survey team and potentially consistent with the observational anomalies in the 2041 SOAR survey.

Under this hypothesis, the site's anomalies are entirely physical: heat from a geothermal source, acoustics from geometry, physiological responses from infrasound.

**What it does not explain:** The acoustic null readings — periods in which the instruments returned no signal despite the known presence of subsurface acoustic activity. If the resonance is driven by the site's physical structure, continuous activity should produce continuous measurable output. The null periods, in which the instruments recorded nothing while the site continued to produce acoustic signal, do not fit the resonant cavity model.

Abara Osei, who served as the SOAR project's acoustic survey lead, has stated: *"The instruments did not malfunction. They remained powered and running throughout each null period. The output channel showed no signal. This is not the same as instrument failure. The instruments were behaving as if there was nothing to measure. There was something to measure. I had measured it all day."*

---

## Hypothesis 3: Measurement framework failure

A minority position in the published literature argues that the anomalies — particularly the null readings — reflect the inadequacy of current measurement methodology rather than a gap in geological understanding. The instruments work correctly for what they are designed to measure; the site produces phenomena outside their design parameters.

Under this hypothesis, the problem is not geological but methodological: each survey attempt has used instruments and protocols developed for conventional geothermal sites. The results are anomalous precisely because the site is not a conventional geothermal site, and conventional instruments cannot characterize it.

This hypothesis does not specify what the site is. It argues that the failures to measure are informative — that what the instruments cannot capture is a signal about the site's character.

**Note:** This hypothesis has not been published in a full form. The most explicit articulation of it in the accessible record is a brief observation in field notes submitted to the LEDGER archive during the 2041 SOAR survey by measurement specialist Milo Reyes: *The instrument reads null. The site is not null. I do not know how to explain this distinction.* The full interpretation was deleted with the primary archive.

---

## An implicit fourth approach: The constitutive hypothesis

Khalid Asad's 2031 paper ("Observation and constitution in geothermal surveys") does not directly address the site anomalies. Its argument — that observation partly constitutes what is found, that methodology participates in producing the object of measurement — applies to the site's anomaly history in ways Asad does not state explicitly.

Under a constitutive reading of the site: each survey has produced different results because each survey constituted the site differently. The 1962 survey found a heat anomaly and named it; that naming is now part of what the site is. The 1989 survey found the name and the symptoms, and added the recommendation against return; that addition is now part of what the site is. The 2003 survey produced a disappearance and a sealed bag and thirty-eight years of a carried record; those are part of what the site is.

The SOAR survey, designed around Asad's 2028 calibration methodology, which was itself derived (by his own December 1, 2041 log entry) from the 2003 acoustic data, constituted the site through methods that were already shaped by what happened in 2003.

Whether this is an explanation or a description of the absence of explanation is not resolved in the literature.

---

## Current status

The site's anomalies have not been explained. The SOAR preliminary findings classify the null readings as equipment anomaly, which is inconsistent with Osei's field notes and instrument records. Further investigation has been recommended. Results are pending publication.
