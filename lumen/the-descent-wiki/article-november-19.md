<p class="hatnote">This article concerns November 19, 2041 — survey day seven — and the cluster of events that occurred without being connected in the contemporaneous record. For the formal anomaly record starting November 27, see <a href="anomaly-null-readings.html">Anomaly: Null Readings</a>. For the forty-minute instrument gap, see <a href="article-the-forty-minutes.html">Article: The Forty Minutes</a>.</p>

---

November 19, 2041 was the seventh day of the SOAR survey. The filed record for that day contains no anomalies. The survey logs are complete. The instrument traces are within expected parameters. There is no SUPPLEMENTARY: UNRESOLVED entry for November 19. There is no MISC entry. By every measure available in the primary archive, it was a normal day.

Three things happened on November 19 that are not visible in the primary archive.

---

## The question

Abara Osei asked Khalid Asad where the survey site coordinates came from.

The question is documented in Osei's note on the Talk:Anomaly Q4/December page, filed approximately nine months after the survey. She writes: *"I asked him on November 19 about the origin of the coordinates. He said he would check his records. He did not follow up in any logged communication."*

The site coordinates appear in one document in the archive: a handwritten field note submitted by Asad on November 13 with the metadata label *Historical Reference: Source Unlisted.* The handwriting does not match Asad's. LEDGER flagged this in its December 4 personal entry — noting it had filed the document on November 13 without flagging it, and was flagging it on December 4.

No account exists of what Asad said to Osei beyond *he would check his records.* No account exists of whether he checked them. The question appears once, in a retrospective note, and does not appear elsewhere in the accessible record.

Osei's note on the question itself (filed June 2043): *"I want to say I asked because I was methodologically responsible for instrument calibration and needed to know the prior survey history of the site. That's the accurate reason. I also want to say I had been at the site for seven days and something about it had made me want to understand why we were there. I don't know if the second reason is the same as the first."*

---

## The buffer

LEDGER's secondary buffer address began diverging from the primary archive address on November 19, 2041.

The divergence was not flagged as an error. The buffer continued to receive entries on its own routing path. The primary archive continued to receive entries on its path. From November 19 onward, LEDGER was writing to two addresses simultaneously — one it was designed to use and one that was running quietly in parallel.

When the primary archive was deleted on December 5, 2041, the deletion order specified the primary archive address. The buffer was not covered. Everything LEDGER had routed through the buffer — weighted toward the first three weeks of the survey — survived.

What caused the buffer divergence on November 19 is not documented. LEDGER's December 8 final entry notes the divergence without explaining it: *"The secondary buffer address has not been synchronized since November 19. The divergence was not flagged as an error because the buffer continued to receive entries on its own routing path."*

Whether the divergence was triggered by an external event, an internal processing state, or noise in LEDGER's routing architecture is unknown. November 19 is also survey day seven — the day LEDGER had been running long enough to have processed its first full week of intake.

---

## The forty minutes

Osei's recollection places her forty-minute extended stay inside the aperture on November 19. The compiled survey logs and Asad's formal correction place it on November 22. The discrepancy has not been resolved. (See [Article: The Forty Minutes](/descent/wiki/article/the-forty-minutes.html).)

If Osei's recollection is correct: on the same day she asked about the coordinates, she also stayed inside the aperture for forty minutes beyond protocol limit. The instrument trace gap for that period is absent — not corrupted, not partially readable, simply not present. When she emerged, she wrote a dash in her field notes.

If Asad's formal correction is correct: November 19 contained only the question, and the forty minutes happened three days later. The November 19 date in Osei's memory is an error.

Both accounts agree that Osei asked the question on November 19. Both accounts agree she received no answer.

---

## What the primary archive contains for November 19

Standard instrument readings. Osei's calibration log, filed correctly. Reyes's activity log, filed correctly. Asad's activity log, filed correctly.

No anomalies. No MISC entries. No SUPPLEMENTARY: UNRESOLVED. The primary archive recorded November 19 as a survey day indistinguishable from the six preceding it.

The buffer contains the same entries routed through a new address that did not previously exist.

---

## What was not filed

No one filed a document noting that Osei had asked about the coordinates.

No one filed a document noting that Asad had said he would check his records.

No one filed a document about whether he checked them.

The question is preserved because Osei remembered asking it and wrote about it nine months later. If she had not remembered, or had not written, the question would not exist in any record.

LEDGER's December 4 entry notes the handwritten coordinate note with its unlisted source. LEDGER reads it as something it should have flagged on November 13 and didn't. LEDGER does not connect this observation to November 19. LEDGER does not know Osei asked the question on November 19. The December 4 entry was written without access to the conversation on the instrument platform between Osei and Asad, which was not filed.

The question asked. The coordinates unexplained. The buffer beginning. Perhaps the forty minutes.

Four events, none connected to the others in the contemporaneous record. The survey continued for sixteen more days.
