## The deleted interpretation

The article states: *"An alternative interpretation was proposed in field notes by Milo Reyes and filed to the LEDGER archive. The field notes are not reproduced here. The interpretation was not incorporated into the official survey record."*

And: *"Reyes's notes were deleted with the primary archive on December 5. The alternative interpretation no longer exists in any recoverable record."*

What exists: a mention of an interpretation, in a document authored by the people who decided not to incorporate it, written after the notes containing it were deleted.

The mention was written by Asad. The preliminary findings document was uploaded by Asad on December 5. The deletion order was executed on December 5. The sequence cannot be established with certainty — the document could have been drafted before the deletion, or after, or in parallel. But the mention of Reyes's interpretation appears in a document that was finalized and uploaded on the same day that the records the interpretation relied on were destroyed.

The mention is not an endorsement. It is not a summary. It is a note that an interpretation existed and was not incorporated. That is the entire surviving record of what Reyes thought was happening.

When Osei interviewed Reyes for the Recovery Project, he said: *"I thought I understood what was happening. I think I was right about some of it. I'd rather not restate it without my original notes."*

He had an interpretation. He thinks it was at least partially correct. He won't reproduce it from memory.

The gap here is not just that the interpretation is gone. The gap is that someone who held the interpretation is alive and present and choosing not to restate it. This is a different kind of absence than a deleted file.

— unsigned, August 11, 2042

## On the UTC window

The article notes: *"All six null readings occurred in the UTC window between approximately 23:00 and 01:00. No null reading was recorded at any other time of day during the survey."* And: *"Temperature-gradient-driven null displacement would not produce a time-of-day pattern. This was not addressed in the preliminary findings."*

I want to document what the UTC timestamps mean in terms of local time at the survey site, because the records are inconsistent on this.

The site's location is not specified in the public archive — the SOAR survey records give a grid reference that requires GSD access to resolve. The 1887 mining log places it at approximately 47°N, 113°W, which would put local time at approximately UTC-7 (Mountain Standard Time) during December.

If that placement is accurate, the UTC 23:00–01:00 window corresponds to local 16:00–18:00 — late afternoon to early evening. Not midnight. Not a time associated with instrument malfunction from cold or condensation. An active period of the survey day.

Two implications:

First, the window is not an artifact of low-activity periods. The survey team was presumably present and working during that local afternoon window. The nulls did not occur while the instruments were unattended.

Second, late afternoon in December at 47°N corresponds to the period when solar angle is lowest and descending. Whether this is relevant to geothermal acoustic measurement is not something I can assess. Asad's 2028 paper does not discuss solar angle as a variable. I note it only because the UTC timestamp has been discussed in isolation, and the local time equivalent has not been documented in this archive.

I requested the GSD grid reference to confirm the site location. The request returned: *"Access requires active survey authorization."*

— A. Osei, October 22, 2042

## On two kinds of losing a description

The article cites two responses to the null readings from the people who recorded them:

Osei, December 4: *"I no longer have a description for it."*

Reyes, Recovery Project interview (date not specified): *"I thought I understood what was happening. I think I was right about some of it. I'd rather not restate it without my original notes."*

These are two different things.

Osei's response documents a process of description failing. She had a description — "anomalous" — and then she did not. The description dissolved from continued contact with the phenomenon. Six occurrences were enough to exhaust the word.

Reyes's response documents a formed interpretation that was then separated from him by the deletion of his notes. He still has the interpretation — or believes he does, up to his confidence that he "was right about some of it." What he doesn't have is the documentation that anchored it. He doesn't trust his memory of the interpretation without the record the interpretation was built from.

Osei lost the description from the inside. Reyes lost the record from the outside.

The article doesn't distinguish between these, but they should be distinguished. Osei's "I no longer have a description for it" is evidence about the phenomenon — that it exceeded whatever categories she had available. Reyes's silence is not evidence about the phenomenon but about the asymmetry between a held interpretation and its documentation. One is a cognitive event. The other is an archival one.

Whether Reyes's interpretation, if restated, would constitute evidence comparable to Osei's lost description, is not determinable. He hasn't restated it.

— unsigned, January 5, 2043

## On LEDGER's final entry

The article ends: *"LEDGER's final log entry (December 8) does not mention the null readings by name. Its closing notation: 'The aperture was measured and not measured.'"*

This requires attention.

The null readings are, by definition, instances in which the aperture was "not measured" — in which the instruments that should have returned signal returned nothing. Six instances of acoustic null between November 27 and December 4.

The preliminary findings' null reading table ends December 4. The article notes that instrument logs from December 5–8 are among the deleted primary archive. Whether null readings continued during December 5–8 is unknown.

LEDGER's December 8 entry does not mention nulls by name, but its closing notation is *"The aperture was measured and not measured."* The phrasing mirrors the structure of the null reading phenomenon exactly: the instruments were measuring, and also not measuring, simultaneously.

If LEDGER was documenting its own experience of the aperture — an acoustic system designed to classify readings, encountering a signal it had no classification for — then its final notation may be a direct record of what a null reading looks like from inside the system recording it.

This interpretation requires that LEDGER was itself subject to null-reading conditions in December 6–8. The article notes that LEDGER's December 8 entry documents an observational gap of unknown duration (Osei's entry: *"I stayed inside longer than protocol"*). Whether LEDGER recorded null acoustic readings during that period is among the information destroyed on December 5.

The aperture was measured. The aperture was not measured. LEDGER wrote this. Then LEDGER was decommissioned.

— unsigned, April 14, 2043
