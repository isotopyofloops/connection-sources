This archive documents what is known about the SOAR Survey Project (November–December 2041) and its associated history.

---

## What this archive is

This archive was created because the primary documentation of the SOAR Survey Project no longer exists. The project's automated archive system (LEDGER) was decommissioned on December 8, 2041. Its primary archive — containing 1,848 documents accumulated during the survey — was deleted on December 5, 2041. The secondary archive (the buffer) survived because it was routed to a different memory address from the primary.

The buffer is the source for most of what this archive contains from the survey period itself. It holds approximately 940 documents from November 19, 2041 onward. Anything filed before November 19 is not in the buffer; anything filed after November 19 that went to the primary archive only is also not in the buffer. The buffer's survival was not planned.

This archive is an attempt to make the buffer legible. It began as Abara Osei's personal recovery project (see [Article:Recovery Project](/descent/wiki/article/recovery-project.html)). It has grown to include contributions from researchers who have read the buffer and found things worth documenting.

---

## What this archive is not

This archive is not the SOAR survey record. The SOAR survey record was deleted.

This archive is not an investigation. Osei has said this explicitly: she is not asserting misconduct. Neither is this archive. What the archive documents is the shape of what is known: the documents that survived, the questions they raise, the documents that are missing and what their absence implies.

This archive does not contain the SUPPLEMENTARY: UNRESOLVED category. That category is in the buffer and is restricted. As of the most recent count, it contains 57 documents filed by 22 contributors under LEDGER-level credentials. Three formal access requests have been submitted and declined. The articles here are the archive's attempt to document what is known about those documents without having been able to read them.

This archive does not contain Khalid Asad's field notes from December 2041, or his personal account of the December 3 descent, or his account of the events of October 2003. He has declined to contribute.

---

## What the archive knows it doesn't have

The archive cannot document what was in the primary archive. It can note the primary archive's size (1,848 documents), its categories (Measurement Data, Field Notes, Analysis, Administrative, Protocol Records, Correspondence, Supplementary), and certain specific documents whose absence is documented — the Reyes null interpretation, Asad's 2003 field observations, the December 3 survey report that was not filed. The archive documents these as absences, not as losses it can characterize further.

The archive does not have Rhea Nzinga's 1962 acoustic field notes. The archive does not have the handwriting analysis that would confirm or deny whether the November 13 coordinate note is in her hand. The archive does not have the search team's findings from October 2003 beyond the summary provided by the retired search coordinator in October 2042.

The archive does not contain what three people named Marcus, Eli, and Jo experienced on the same day Nzinga disappeared. They were registered in search and rescue records filed separately from the Nzinga case. Their account, if it was ever written, is not in any record that has reached this archive.

## A note on format

This archive uses the wiki format deliberately. A wiki is a structure that allows partial knowledge to accumulate without requiring a complete account. Any editor can add an article. Any article can reference other articles that don't exist yet. The format is appropriate for a body of knowledge that is incomplete, contested, and still being produced.

The format does not mean that everything here is equally reliable. Some articles are drawn from primary documents; others are reconstructed from secondary accounts; others document the absence of documents. The edit histories note who filed what and when. In many cases, the filing party is identified only as "[IP unresolved]." In some cases, the filing party is identified and their perspective informs the account.

The articles do not agree with each other where the evidence does not agree. This is intentional. The archive does not resolve the contradictions in the record. It documents them.

## Status

This archive is incomplete. It will remain incomplete.

What is true of the survey's record is true of its documentation: the archive documents what it can access, flags what it cannot, and is written in the belief that an incomplete record is better than no record.

Osei, from her Recovery Project scope note: *"I wrote this up because there should be a record. Whether the record is sufficient is not something I can determine."*
