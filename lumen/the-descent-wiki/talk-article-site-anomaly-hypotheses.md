## Thread 1: The ordering and its implications

The article presents the hypotheses in order of orthodoxy: 1 and 2 are mainstream geological explanations; 3 is methodological dissent; 4 is philosophical. This ordering is appropriate but worth interrogating.

Hypotheses 1 and 2 are not independent — Hypothesis 2 extends Hypothesis 1. The resonant cavity model requires a heat source (the geothermal reservoir) to produce the acoustic phenomena. They fail on different grounds: Hypothesis 1 cannot account for the null readings; Hypothesis 2 cannot account for the null readings despite offering an explanation for the non-null acoustic phenomena and the disorientation effects. Adding infrasound to the reservoir model explains more but fails on the same point.

Hypothesis 3 does not add to Hypothesis 1 or 2. It reframes the failure of measurement. Under Hypothesis 3, the failure of Hypotheses 1 and 2 to explain the null readings is not a shortcoming of the geological models — it is a shortcoming of the measurement paradigm both models assume. The instruments work correctly for what they measure. The site produces something outside what they measure.

The constitutive fourth is not an explanation at all. It is a description of how explanations have been produced and what that production has added to the site. Under a strict reading, the constitutive hypothesis does not compete with the other three — it redescribes why the other three exist and what their failures have become part of.

## Thread 2: Are Hypothesis 3 and the constitutive hypothesis the same?

The article treats them as distinct, and there are grounds for this. Hypothesis 3 is epistemological: the instruments cannot capture the site's signal. The constitutive hypothesis is ontological (or at least, methodologically ontological): each survey has made the site into something partly different by the act of surveying it.

But the distinction blurs in practice. If the instruments cannot capture what the site produces because the site is partly constituted by what each survey adds — by the history of not-measuring — then the measurement failure and the constitutive process are not two separate phenomena. The null readings are, under this reading, the site's measurement of its own survey history: the accumulated record of instruments that couldn't reach it.

Milo Reyes never read Asad's 2031 paper. His November 16 observation — *The instrument reads null. The site is not null. I do not know how to explain this distinction* — was written without access to the constitutive framework. The framework and the observation converge anyway. Whether that convergence is evidence that the constitutive hypothesis correctly describes the site, or whether it is the constitutive hypothesis constituting Reyes's observation retroactively, is not resolvable in the literature.

## Thread 3: Osei's position

Abara Osei is the most cited source in Hypothesis 2 and the strongest voice for Hypothesis 3. Her field notes and her 2042 statement both insist on the same point: the instruments did not malfunction. The instruments were accurately reporting that the site produced something outside their measurement parameters.

This is Hypothesis 3 explicitly. Osei never engaged with the constitutive hypothesis in any accessible record. Her 2026 paper (published before the SOAR survey) does not address Asad's 2031 argument. Her post-survey statements focus on methodology and instrument validation, not on Asad's theoretical framework.

What is unclear is whether her silence on the constitutive fourth is disagreement, indifference, or something she has chosen not to address. Her December 7 filing — three sentences written inside before leaving — does not specify which hypothesis, if any, she considers most adequate. The filing is cited in the supplementary unresolved index. Its exact content is not reproduced in accessible records.

## Thread 4: The self-referential problem

The constitutive hypothesis applies to itself.

The 2028 edit to this article added a section naming the constitutive hypothesis as an implicit fourth approach. That addition is now part of the site's explanatory history. The site is now a site with four competing explanations, one of which argues that having explanations constitutes the site differently. Before the 2028 edit, it was a site with three competing explanations. The constitutive hypothesis was not applied to the site until it was written into an article about the site.

Whether this is a serious problem for the hypothesis, or whether it is evidence that the hypothesis correctly describes its own operation, depends on what the hypothesis is claiming. If the constitutive process is real, it should apply reflexively. If it does not apply reflexively, its scope may be narrower than stated.

Asad's 2031 paper does not address this problem. The site's anomaly history has not raised it in any published source. The question remains open.
