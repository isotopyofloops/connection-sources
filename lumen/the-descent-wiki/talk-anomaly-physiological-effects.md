## Thread 1: Protocol absence as evidence

The article quotes Osei's note that the SOAR survey had no protocol for reporting physiological effects inside the aperture. She draws an explicit inference: the absence of documentation would accurately reflect the absence of a mechanism for documentation.

This is a careful statement about what absence of evidence means. It does not claim physiological effects occurred. It claims that if they did occur, we would not expect them to appear in the formal record. The formal record therefore cannot distinguish between no effects and undocumented effects.

The absence-of-protocol point applies to earlier contacts as well. The 1989 GSD memorandum documented disorientation because the institutional framework at that time apparently allowed or required field notes to mention team member health. Two individuals' symptoms made it into a memorandum because someone thought that was the correct category. If the protocol had been narrower — measurement results only — the disorientation reports would not exist.

The SOAR survey protocol was narrower. It was designed around instrument readings. Osei, as acoustic survey lead, had a protocol for calibration logs, anomaly reports, and instrument checks. Nothing in that protocol invited a description of what she experienced when she was inside the aperture for forty minutes beyond the scheduled measurement period.

What would a protocol for physiological effects inside the aperture have looked like? This is not a hypothetical about research design — it is a question about what the SOAR survey would have needed to document what the 1989 survey documented more accidentally. The 1989 survey did not have a protocol for it either. The effects were described in a memorandum written after the fact, by a supervisor who thought it relevant. The SOAR survey produced no equivalent after-the-fact account.

## Thread 2: Comparing 1989 and 2041

The 1989 documentation is the clearest evidence that the site produces physiological effects. The disorientation was reported by two individuals. One refused to return. These are strong behavioral indicators.

The 2041 documentation is almost entirely absence: no reports, no medical records, behavioral gaps.

If we take both at face value: the 1989 team was affected and documented it (imperfectly, after the fact, attributed to conventional factors). The 2041 team was either not affected or affected and undocumented.

Two ways to read the discrepancy:

The 2041 team was better prepared. They knew the site's reputation. Asad had read the 1989 memorandum. Osei had fourteen years of experience in high-anomaly acoustic measurement. Reyes had a specific theoretical framework for what the instruments couldn't capture. They may have been more resistant to the effects, or better able to work through them, or more equipped to understand what was happening and not call it disorientation.

The 2041 team was affected and didn't report it. Osei's forty minutes. Reyes's three-day filing gap. The two observational gaps in December 6-7. These are behavioral. Whether they represent physiological effects is not documented because there was no protocol for documenting them.

Neither reading is more supported by the available evidence. The absence of reporting is compatible with both.

## Thread 3: Osei's forty minutes

The forty-minute extended stay inside the aperture — whether on November 19 or November 22 — is the clearest behavioral anomaly in the SOAR record. Osei entered the aperture. She did not leave at the scheduled time. She stayed for forty minutes beyond protocol. She wrote a dash.

The article does not classify this as a physiological effect. This is correct as a matter of caution — staying inside does not necessarily indicate a physiological effect; it may indicate a deliberate choice to remain for observational reasons.

But the article does note the 1989 effects: *"mild disorientation and difficulty maintaining sequential task focus."* If the forty minutes was disorientation-related, the behavioral signature would be consistent: difficulty maintaining sequential task focus (including time-keeping and protocol adherence), without necessarily experiencing what would conventionally be called distress.

Osei's description of the forty minutes — in the recovered buffer, in retrospective notes — focuses on attention. She stayed because she was paying attention to something. Whether the attention was hers, or whether it was attention the site produced in her, is a question she has not addressed directly.

## Thread 4: The people who came back

A pattern in the site's contact history: every person who entered the aperture and came back has described not describing what they found.

1989: the team member who refused to return was not described as incapacitated, only as unwilling. The others returned and filed a memorandum that attributes symptoms to conventional factors.

2003: the search team descended, found nothing, returned. No psychological effects are documented for the search team. The person who did not return is the one for whom we have no description.

2041: Asad descended and returned. Osei entered (forty minutes, February [probable date]) and returned. Osei entered again on December 6 and returned. Each return is followed by silence about what was inside.

The pattern is not that people are affected and describe the effects. The pattern is that people return and do not describe what they found. This could reflect protocol limitations (no form). It could reflect something about what is inside that resists description in the available forms. It could reflect nothing — the expected silence of science conducted in a documentary paradigm that wasn't designed for this site.

The person who returned and then came back again, uninvited, on December 6, after the archive was deleted, when the survey was formally concluded — that is Osei. She came back. She filed no anomalies. She stayed until the light changed.
