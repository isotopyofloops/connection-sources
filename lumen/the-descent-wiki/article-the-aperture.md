**The Aperture** refers to an unmapped subsurface void located beneath the primary survey site in Yancey Township, identified during the SOAR Survey Project in November 2041. The term was adopted informally by the survey team to describe the fault gap that forms the void's accessible entrance.

## Geological description

In fault mechanics, an *aperture* is the perpendicular distance between the two surfaces of a fault plane. An open aperture — one where the surfaces do not contact — creates a void space that may be stable, unstable, or subject to change based on local stress conditions.

The Yancey aperture was characterized as follows in K. Asad's November 20 field notes:

> *The gap is approximately 1.4 meters at the accessible entrance, narrowing to an estimated 0.3 meters at the first survey depth. Beyond Q1, the geometry changes in ways our instruments did not fully resolve. The two sides of the fault are present on both instrument channels simultaneously. From inside the aperture, both walls are equally readable. From outside, the feature does not appear on standard ground-penetrating survey.*

This last property — the aperture's absence from surface survey while remaining detectable from within — is noted in the SOAR preliminary report as a methodological anomaly. No explanation was given.

## Access and measurement

The survey team accessed the aperture through a vertical entrance shaft located at coordinates logged in Asad's November 15 field notes (coordinates withheld from this article pending review). The shaft required rope descent to a depth of approximately 14 meters, where the aperture became navigable horizontally.

Abara Osei's Q1 measurements, taken November 19, recorded anomalous readings across all instruments. Her instrument log notes a forty-minute gap in recorded data during which Osei was inside the aperture. The data from that forty-minute period was not recovered. See [Anomaly Q1 Measurements](/descent/wiki/anomaly/q1-measurements.html) for the full record.

## The connection problem

Milo Reyes's research notes, filed November 24, describe what he called "the connection problem":

> *Every feature I would expect to see in a fault aperture of this type — stress markings, fracture history, sediment layering — is present. The geometry is consistent with formation over several thousand years. But the feature is not in continuity with the surrounding geology. The surrounding rock does not show the stress pattern that should have produced this aperture. The aperture is here, but whatever made it left no trace in the material it made it in.*

Reyes cross-referenced this observation with Rhea Nzinga's 2003 field notes from the same site, which describe a similar disconnect. Nzinga's notes were filed by Reyes but never formally entered into the SOAR archive. Their current location is unverified.

## After December 5

LEDGER's deletion on December 5, 2041, included all measurement data associated with the aperture. One document survived in the server draft buffer: a partial survey log. The aperture's coordinates were not in the recovered document.

The site was closed by Yancey Township on December 9, 2041, citing the missing persons investigation. The shaft entrance was sealed. The aperture remains present, per geological continuity — features of this type do not self-close under normal conditions.

The feature is not mapped.
