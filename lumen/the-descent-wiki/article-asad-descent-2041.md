During the SOAR survey in December 2041, Khalid Asad descended the shaft at the center of the survey site. This is documented in the SOAR survey records as a single-person descent during week three of the survey. It is the first documented instance of Asad descending the shaft.

## Background

In October 2003, Asad was present at the site as the supervising faculty member for a student field survey. Four other individuals went missing at the site that day. Asad was the only member of the group to return.

When the search team arrived following the missing persons report, they descended the shaft in a two-person operation with a rope line and lights. Asad did not descend with them. He was at the surface when the team came back up. The shaft was searched and found empty — no trace of any person, no equipment. The field notes belonging to the missing student were found at the surface, sealed in a plastic bag at the shaft entrance. Asad received the field notes.

Asad's field notes from October 2003 are not part of any public record. The public record begins with the missing persons report he filed within 24 hours.

## The 2041 descent

The SOAR survey's primary instrumentation was deployed at the surface and shaft entrance for acoustic measurement. Survey protocol did not require personnel to descend the shaft.

Asad descended during the third week of the survey. The descent is logged in the SOAR survey records with a timestamp but no accompanying field notes in the accessible archive. The log entry records duration and return. It does not record what Asad observed during the descent.

Osei's correction note on the survey schedule (filed May 2043): *"Asad's descent was on December 3. He told me in advance he intended to go in. He came back out approximately two hours later. He appeared — I want to be precise — focused. Not distressed. He did not describe what he found. I did not ask. That is the most accurate record I have of that day from my perspective."*

December 3 is the fifth null reading night (23:09–01:01, 112 minutes — the longest null in the survey record). Asad's descent was during the day, not during the null period.

## The interval

The 2003 events and the 2041 descent are separated by thirty-eight years.

In that interval: Asad published a methodology for acoustic null point correction (2028). He identified and recruited Abara Osei for her acoustic measurement expertise (2038–2039). He submitted the SOAR survey proposal (2039) and received authorization (2040). He located the 1989 GSD memorandum documenting worker disorientation during extended shaft proximity (date of location: prior to the survey). He designed and led an acoustic survey of the site.

The proposal notes that the survey methodology was *"specifically developed, in part, to address the measurement challenges presented by sites of this type."* The 2028 null-correction methodology predates the survey by thirteen years.

What Asad was working toward during those thirty-eight years is not documented in this archive. The most specific description available is the SOAR survey itself.

## What the descent record contains

The accessible SOAR survey record documents:
- That the descent occurred
- Its date (December 3, 2041)
- Its approximate duration (approximately two hours, per Osei's account)
- That Asad returned

The accessible record does not document:
- Asad's field notes from the descent, if any were taken
- Asad's account of what he observed
- Whether the descent informed the survey's subsequent procedures

The preliminary findings document, filed December 5, does not reference the December 3 descent.

## After the survey

Following the survey, the primary archive was deleted on December 5. The preliminary findings were uploaded the same day. Asad is listed as the submitting author.

The preliminary findings classify all anomalous readings as equipment anomaly. They do not contain a section addressing the December 3 descent.

The SOAR survey ended officially on December 5. Activity at the site is documented through December 8 in the buffer archive.
