## Sealed

*Posted November 19, 2042 — [IP unresolved]*

Asad's December 1 log: *"Rhea's coordinates. Her sealed notes that I have carried for thirty-eight years."*

I want to ask a question the article doesn't address: did he read them?

"Sealed" can mean two different things. It can mean the notes are in a sealed container that has not been opened since 2003 — he has carried them but never read them. Or it can mean they are sealed *now*, as in he sealed them at some point, whether before or after reading them.

If he has never read them: he has been carrying Nzinga's field notes for thirty-eight years without knowing what they say. He derived a calibration methodology "from prior-site geological data" while holding data he had never accessed. The methodology might be derived from the coordinates and general site geology, without the notes themselves.

If he has read them and then sealed them: the methodology almost certainly incorporates what she observed. The sealing came after.

If he has read them: what did he read? She disappeared three weeks into a survey she was conducting at a site that now, thirty-eight years later, has produced six null acoustic readings, two observational gaps, and a set of measurements the team could not describe.

What did she observe in the three weeks before she disappeared?

*Reply — [IP unresolved], November 21, 2042:*

His 2028 paper was described in his December 1 log as "an attempt to explain what happened." If you write a paper attempting to explain what happened and your primary source is notes you haven't read, the attempt would be indirect — working from the event's context rather than from what the event produced.

If you write the paper having read the notes, the paper is a translation — you've processed what she found, you've derived a methodology from it, you've published it as yours.

"Attempt to explain what happened" reads more like the second. He was trying to explain what happened. He had her notes. He used them to build the explanation. He published the explanation without her name.

*Reply — [IP unresolved], December 1, 2042:*

Or the paper is neither. The December 1 log also says the paper "ended as a hypothesis about measurement because I could not explain what happened." He started with the event, he tried to explain it, he couldn't, and he shifted to writing about measurement instead. The constitutive hypothesis — that observation partly constitutes its object — is a framework for how something might happen without being fully explicable. Maybe the paper is the record of giving up on explanation and writing theory instead.

"I have carried for thirty-eight years" — not "I have used for thirty-eight years." The carrying is the action he describes. Not the reading.

*No further response.*

## The debt

*Posted February 8, 2043 — [IP unresolved]*

The phrase I keep returning to: *"I came because I owe this place something I have not been able to name."*

The thing he owes is unnameable. This is precise, not vague — he has tried to name it and cannot. The inability to name is not incapacity; it's the result of a sustained attempt.

What would naming the debt require? It would require, at minimum, saying what happened. Saying what Nzinga observed. Saying what the site is. All of these things Asad has declined to put in any record except, obliquely, a survey log entry filed December 1, 2041.

His 2031 paper argues that observation partly constitutes its object. If this is true: what he observed in October 2003 — at the surface, while Nzinga and the others were inside — is part of what happened. His observations, unrecorded, are part of what is there. Naming the debt would require accessing those observations. He has not filed them anywhere.

"I came because I owe" and "I am not putting this in the preliminary findings" are the same statement. He owes something. He came to pay it. He came, and descended, and came back, and then filed findings that say: anomaly confirmed, equipment error, pending publication.

The debt remains unnamed. The preliminary findings remain the official record.

*Reply — [IP unresolved], February 10, 2043:*

I don't think the preliminary findings are the cover-up they're being read as here. "Further investigation recommended" is still in there. It's the language of someone who knows the findings are incomplete and is saying so in the only form the form permits.

*Reply — [IP unresolved], February 12, 2043:*

"Further investigation recommended" is passive. It doesn't name what should be investigated, who should investigate it, what methods should be used, or what outcome would constitute resolution. It is the minimum acknowledgment compatible with not acknowledging anything.

*No further response.*

## The choice of the survey log

*Posted July 12, 2043 — A. Osei*

I compiled the December survey log article. I want to note something about the December 1 entry that isn't addressed in the article.

Asad filed his December 1 log through the standard survey logging interface. I was the field survey lead. Survey logs were filed to LEDGER and I had access to them. He knew I would read this entry. He filed it in the survey log rather than in a personal document or in a channel I did not monitor.

He chose this place to say: *Rhea's coordinates. Her sealed notes. I owe this place something I have not been able to name. I am not putting this in the preliminary findings.*

I don't know what he expected me to do with it. I have compiled it into the December log and noted it in the anomaly article and written about it in correspondence with the recovery project. I don't know if that is what he expected. I don't know if he had an expectation.

I want to note: I read this entry on December 1, 2041. I did not respond to it. I did not ask him about it. I filed my own December 1 survey log entry, which is in the archive, and it does not mention having read his.

I don't know what I would have said.

— A. Osei
