The LEDGER filing index records 389 documents in the **FIELD OBSERVATION** category across the survey period. These include team members' daily observation logs, site condition notes, and activity records.

Of those 389 entries, the accessible archive contains observations from Milo Reyes and Abara Osei, provided through direct provision after the primary archive was deleted. It does not contain observations from Khalid Asad.

Asad's field observations are absent from every portion of the accessible record: the buffer archive, the recovery project documents, and the materials provided directly by team members.

## What is documented

The survey record documents Asad's presence at the site across the full survey period. It documents that he descended the shaft on December 3, 2041, for approximately two hours and returned. It documents the date and approximate duration of the descent.

The log entry for December 3 records the descent with a timestamp. It does not record what Asad observed during the descent. The accessible field observation record contains no document attributable to Asad on December 3 or on any other day.

Osei's correction note on the survey schedule (filed May 2043): *"He came back out approximately two hours later. He appeared — I want to be precise — focused. Not distressed. He did not describe what he found. I did not ask."*

## The 2003 antecedent

Asad has been at the site twice. In October 2003, he was the supervising faculty member for a field survey in which four individuals entered the shaft and did not return. Asad did not descend the shaft that day. He was at the surface when the search team arrived.

His field notes from October 2003 have not entered the public record. They were not filed with any institution. They were not part of any formal inquiry. The public record from that day begins with the missing persons report Asad filed within 24 hours.

What those field notes contained — if they were written, if they were kept — is not documented.

Osei's note on the 2003 record status (filed July 2043): *"I cannot confirm whether Asad kept field notes on October 14, 2003. I have no basis for saying whether they exist. I note this because the question has been asked in correspondence and I want to be accurate about the limits of what I know."*

## The attribution paradox

Asad is described by colleagues as having attribution practices that are "unusual for the field." He served six years on his university's research integrity committee. His published work on the ethics of source documentation in scientific methodology is cited in the field. His 2028 calibration paper and 2031 constitutive hypothesis paper contain extensive citation apparatus.

He did not credit Rhea Nzinga's field notes as the source of the SOAR project coordinates.

His own observations — from October 2003 and from December 3, 2041 — are absent from every accessible record.

The attribution practices Asad applies consistently to others' work have not been applied to his own observations of the site. Whether this is deliberate, consequential, or explicable in some other way is not addressed anywhere in the accessible archive.

## SUPPLEMENTARY: UNRESOLVED

The 47 SUPPLEMENTARY: UNRESOLVED entries in the filing index represent documents LEDGER could not classify. The index records filing timestamps and word counts but does not indicate authorship.

None of the 47 entries are attributed to Asad in the index. Either he did not file any documents that LEDGER could not classify, or his contributions are recorded under a different mechanism. The distinction is not recoverable from the index.

Asad filed the preliminary findings on December 5, 2041 — the same day the primary archive was deleted. That document does not reference the December 3 descent. It does not reference the six null readings. It classifies all anomalous readings as equipment anomaly.

## The constitutive hypothesis reading

Asad's 2031 paper argues that observation partly constitutes its object — that methodology does not merely describe a pre-existing geological structure but participates in what is found.

In the case of the December 3 descent: the observations Asad made inside the shaft are not in the archive. If they were not recorded, they were not incorporated into the survey's formal record of what was found. Whether they were incorporated into Asad's understanding of what the site was — and whether that understanding is reflected in decisions made after December 3, including the decision to file findings that classify all anomalous readings as equipment anomaly — is not documented.

The constitutive hypothesis was developed, by Asad's own account, over "many years of thinking about what measurement does." The SOAR survey was designed to address the specific measurement challenges of a site of this type.

The descent generated no accessible measurement.
