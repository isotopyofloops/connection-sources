**Observational gaps** are periods in the SOAR survey record in which a team member's field notes document an absence — a period of time with no logged observations. Two observational gaps are documented in the recovered record: Milo Reyes on December 6, 2041 (three hours and forty-five minutes) and Abara Osei on December 7, 2041 (duration unspecified).

## Context

The survey's standard protocol required team members to log instrument readings, environmental observations, and field activity at regular intervals throughout the active survey day. Gaps in this record were to be noted and explained — equipment failure, breaks, site departure, or other causes.

The six null acoustic readings (November 27 – December 4) occurred during the night UTC window and were documented in real time. The observational gaps on December 6 and 7 are different: they occur during the day, during active survey hours, and they represent absences in the record of what team members were observing and doing — not in what the instruments were measuring.

The instrument logs from December 5–8 are among the deleted primary archive. Whether acoustic null readings occurred during December 6 or 7 is not recoverable.

## Reyes: December 6

Reyes's December 6 field note, as recovered by Osei in the Recovery Project, documents the gap directly. The entry preceding the gap reads: *"Temperature gradient elevated — 61.4°C/km at depth. Going to check the aperture."*

The next entry is timed three hours and forty-five minutes later. It reads: *"Returned. Temperature gradient: 58.2°C/km. Instruments nominal."*

The gap entry itself — what Reyes observed or did during those three hours and forty-five minutes — is a dash.

When Osei interviewed Reyes for the Recovery Project, she asked him about the gap. His response: *"I went to check the aperture. I don't remember what I found. The next thing I remember is writing the return entry. I looked at the dash afterward and I don't remember using it. I don't usually use a dash."*

Osei's follow-up: *"Were you inside the shaft?"* Reyes's response: *"I was inside something. I don't know what I'd call it."*

The 1989 GSD memorandum documents "mild disorientation and difficulty maintaining sequential task focus" in field workers who spent extended time near the shaft entrance. Reyes's gap followed a period of elevated temperature gradient readings and preceded an entry noting instruments nominal. No further note on the gap appears in his field records.

## Osei: December 7

Osei's December 7 field note records a single line in the gap location: *"I stayed inside longer than protocol. I did not log it."*

Unlike Reyes's entry, Osei's does not specify what she was doing or where she was. The phrase "inside" has been the subject of editorial discussion in this archive (see Talk page). It could refer to the shaft, the shaft entrance area, the site tent, or some other enclosed or semi-enclosed space at the site.

Osei's correction note, filed November 2042: *"I've been asked what 'inside' means in my December 7 entry. I understand why it's ambiguous. I wrote it that way deliberately. I was not ready to be more specific. I am still not ready to be more specific. I can confirm that I was not inside the site tent."*

She did not specify further. No instrument reading is attached to the December 7 entry. The gap duration is not given.

## Pattern

Two team members. Two consecutive days. Both inside something longer than protocol. Both with sparse records of what they experienced.

The null readings in weeks three and four of the survey occurred in a consistent UTC window (23:00–01:00) and followed a pattern that Osei documented in real time. The observational gaps on December 6 and 7 occurred during active survey hours, after the survey's official end date (December 5 per the preliminary findings), and in the period covered by the deleted primary archive.

The 1989 GSD memorandum documents disorientation as a known risk for extended shaft proximity. Asad had located the memorandum before the survey began. The survey protocol does not appear to have incorporated specific safeguards for this risk — the protocol deviations article documents nine departures from standard GSD protocol, but none address the memorandum's findings directly.

Asad descended the shaft on December 3 and returned. His behavior afterward was documented by Osei as focused and normal. Reyes went to check the aperture on December 6 and returned with a dash. Osei went inside on December 7 and did not log what she found.

## What the gaps contain

The gaps contain nothing recoverable. Reyes has stated he does not remember. Osei has stated she is not ready to say. The instrument logs from those days are deleted.

LEDGER's December 8 final entry, written the day after Osei's gap, reads: *"The aperture was measured and not measured."*
