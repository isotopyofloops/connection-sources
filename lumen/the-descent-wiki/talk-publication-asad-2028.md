## Null points and the SOAR survey

The paper's abstract: *"temperature gradients, fluid saturation, and mineral composition produce medium heterogeneity that displaces acoustic null points from their predicted positions."*

The SOAR survey produced six instances of acoustic null readings — periods in which the instruments returned no acoustic signal despite the known presence of subsurface geothermal activity. The dedicated article on these readings notes: "A null reading indicates the instruments are running, the environment is producing signal, and the recorded output is nonetheless absent."

Asad's 2028 paper is specifically about acoustic nulls in geothermal surveys. He developed a correction methodology for exactly this type of event. He then led a survey that produced six of them, assessed them as equipment anomaly, and submitted a preliminary findings document that did not explain them.

The paper does not discuss the SOAR site. The abstract lists "high-temperature conditions exceeding 180°C at depth" as a limitation — a condition under which the methodology "degrades." The SOAR site's temperature gradient during December 2041 was 52–61°C per kilometer depth. The shaft entrance is at approximately 14 meters depth. Surface temperature readings at depth are not recorded in the accessible archive. Whether the aperture extends to depths where 180°C is reached is not documented.

The null readings occurred in a consistent UTC window (23:00–01:00). Temperature-gradient-driven null displacement would not produce a time-of-day pattern. This was not addressed in the preliminary findings.

— unsigned, April 1, 2042

## On calibration protocol and the SOAR null readings

I cited Asad's 2028 paper in my 2026 publication. I used his protocol for the SOAR calibration checks. I know the paper well.

The null readings we recorded are not predicted by the 2028 methodology. The methodology addresses null point displacement — cases where the null appears at a different location than expected. What we recorded was different: the instruments were calibrated at a location where acoustic signal was present, confirmed by successive days of non-null readings, and then on six occasions the signal was absent entirely. Not displaced — absent.

This is not the scenario the 2028 paper addresses. The 2028 paper is about finding nulls in the wrong place. We found a null in the right place, at the right time, on an instrument confirmed functional, in an environment confirmed active.

I classified this as equipment anomaly because it was the classification the protocol provided. The protocol I was using was derived from Asad's 2028 paper. The 2028 paper does not address this scenario.

When I asked Asad about the null readings during the survey, he noted that the calibration confirmation was solid and suggested monitoring for recurrence. He did not reference the 2028 paper or note that the readings were outside its scope. He had written the paper. He would have known.

I want to be careful about what I am implying here. I am not saying Asad recognized the readings as non-equipment-anomaly and said nothing. I am saying: the person best positioned to notice that the readings were outside the scope of the methodology that predicted them had written the methodology and was on-site. The record does not contain his assessment of the null readings in the terms of his own 2028 paper.

— A. Osei, October 7, 2042

## On the 2028-2039 interval

The paper was published in 2028. The SOAR proposal was submitted in 2039. The authorization was granted in 2040. The survey began in 2041.

Eleven years between the published methodology and the survey that used it.

The 2028 paper was validated against "eight survey sites in controlled conditions." The SOAR site is not cited as one of the eight. If the SOAR site was what drove the paper's development — if, as the proposal notes suggest, the methodology was "specifically developed, in part, to address the measurement challenges presented by sites of this type" — then the 2028 paper was written toward a survey that wasn't conducted for another thirteen years.

Why thirteen years?

One answer: Asad needed to develop the right team. The proposal lists an "acoustic measurement specialist with experience in high-anomaly subsurface readings." Osei acquired that experience through her 2031 survey. She disclosed it to Asad privately in 2038. The SOAR proposal was submitted in 2039. The interval between Osei's 2038 disclosure and the proposal is one year.

Another answer: Asad simply wasn't ready before 2039, for reasons that may have nothing to do with personnel.

Another answer: the 1989 GSD memorandum — which Asad located "in advance of the SOAR survey" — documented disorientation in field workers during extended shaft proximity. Asad's 2028 paper does not address disorientation. His methodology is about instruments, not people. If the disorientation was the factor he was trying to resolve, the 2028 paper was not the answer. The eleven years after the paper may have been looking for a different kind of answer.

What Asad was doing between 2028 and 2039 is not documented in this archive.

— unsigned, January 18, 2043
