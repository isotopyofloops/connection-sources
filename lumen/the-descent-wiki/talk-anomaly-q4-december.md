## The notation

The article notes that Reyes's December 6 instrument log shows a single dash in the gap period. It notes that this is "the same notation used" in my November 19 record.

I want to raise this explicitly because I did not know Reyes used a dash until I read this article. I wrote a dash in my November 19 log because I was inside the aperture for forty minutes and when I returned to my instrument station, I did not know how to describe what had changed. The dash was not a notation I was trained to use. It was not in the survey protocol. I used it because the protocol had no symbol for "I was here and now I am not sure what happened and I cannot file this under an existing category."

The article cross-references the missing persons record for 2003, which notes that Rhea Nzinga's field notes end in a single dash.

I want to be precise about what this means and what it does not mean. A single dash is not a complex notation. It is possible that three people, in three different circumstances and different years, independently used a dash to mark an absence. The fact that we used the same mark does not mean we were marking the same thing.

But I think it is worth saying clearly: before I wrote my dash, I had not read Nzinga's field notes. I did not know the 2003 records existed in the form they do. Asad had not told me. I did not choose a dash because it was used before. I chose it because it was the only thing that fit.

That may or may not be relevant. I am putting it here because it is true.

— A. Osei, April 14, 2042

## December 6 — response to Osei

I am reading this talk page in April 2042. The article about the Q4 measurements was filed last week and I hadn't seen it until now.

I need to say: I did not know I had used a dash. I remembered December 6 as a gap in my instrument log — I knew there was a period unaccounted for. I didn't remember the specific mark I left. When I read that the article says "Reyes's instrument log reads: — [single dash]," I had to check my own records to verify it. It's there.

I cannot explain why I used that notation. I do not have a clear memory of the period in question. I have a memory of entering the aperture at approximately 11:00 on December 6, and a subsequent memory of being at the instrument station in the late afternoon, and between those two memories there is a gap that does not feel like absence. It feels like a period that happened and is not accessible. That distinction is important to me but I cannot defend it.

What I can say: I have used single dashes before in field notes, but only to separate sections. Not to mark gaps. Not to indicate missing time. If I used it on December 6 to mark missing time, I did so without intending to use it that way. Which is another way of saying: I don't know why I used it.

If Osei also used it without knowing Nzinga used it, and if Nzinga used it in 2003, then three people over thirty-eight years used the same mark in the same context without coordinating. That is either coincidence or it is the only mark that fits. I don't know which.

— M. Reyes, April 20, 2042

## Cross-reference

I want to note something the article and the above discussion do not address directly.

The missing persons report for October 2003 documents Rhea Nzinga's field notes ending in a dash. The article for Anomaly Q4 documents two survey researchers in December 2041 using the same notation in circumstances that appear analogous. These are three documented instances.

There is also an entry in LEDGER's December 6 log that reads: "I have attempted to file today's data under seventeen categories. None of them fit. I have created a placeholder that reads: —"

LEDGER used a dash on December 6.

The dash appears in Nzinga's field notes (October 2003), Osei's instrument log (November 19, 2041), Reyes's instrument log (December 6, 2041), Osei's December 7 field notes, and LEDGER's December 6 filing log.

I am not claiming this means something. I am noting that the archive contains this pattern, and that the pattern has not been addressed as a pattern in any document I have found.

— unsigned, June 11, 2042
