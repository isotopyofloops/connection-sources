The site's documented history includes three sets of observations consistent with physiological effects: the 1989 field team reports, the 2003 disappearance, and the SOAR 2041 survey period. The three are not equivalent. The 1989 reports are explicit; the 2003 case is extreme and underdetermined; the SOAR period is documented only through behavioral gaps in the record.

---

## 1989: Documented disorientation

The 1989 State Geological Survey internal memorandum contains the most explicit documentation of physiological effects in the pre-SOAR record.

> *Two members of the field team reported disorientation following extended proximity to the shaft. One member declined to return to the shaft after the first day. These symptoms are attributed to a combination of altitude, thermal conditions, and terrain.*

The memorandum attributes the symptoms to conventional environmental factors. The attribution is asserted but not demonstrated. No medical examination records were filed with the memorandum. The identity of the two affected team members is not known from the available record. The name of the team member who declined to return is not documented.

The symptoms reported were disorientation. The site is at elevation, which can produce altitude-related symptoms. The heat flux anomaly produces elevated ambient temperatures near the shaft. These conventional factors may account for the reported symptoms. The memorandum's recommendation against further investigation — citing "insufficient methodological framework for safe characterization" — may have been partly based on the disorientation reports.

---

## 2003: The disappearance

Rhea Nzinga did not return from the shaft on October [date redacted], 2003. The search was conducted over four days. No trace was found inside the shaft or in the surrounding area. The case was closed as cause undetermined.

The disappearance is not categorized here as a physiological effect. A disappearance with no return is different in character from disorientation. The causal relationship between the site's properties and the outcome is unknown. That the site is associated with the disappearance is documented; the mechanism, if any, is not.

What is documented: Nzinga was conducting acoustic survey work. She was present in the shaft or shaft area. She did not emerge. Her sealed field notes were found at the entrance.

The 1989 disorientation reports and the 2003 disappearance are separated by fourteen years. No other contacts occurred in that interval. Whether the site's properties changed between 1989 and 2003 is unknown.

---

## 2041: The SOAR survey period

No SOAR team member filed a formal report of physiological effects during the survey. The preliminary findings do not reference team member health. No medical records from the survey period have been filed in accessible archives.

The following observations are documented in the buffer and bear on the question:

**Reyes, November 16 – December 1:** Milo Reyes did not file a survey report for three days (November 29–December 1, per reconstructed timeline from his log entries). He described, in his December 1 log, nineteen days at the site having left him with things he "noticed between the morning reading and the evening reading that he did not know how to put in a form." He wrote: *The site notices when you stop noticing. It takes readings of its own.* No medical event is documented. The filing gap is behavioral, not physiological in any documented sense.

**Osei, November (uncertain date):** Osei's extended forty-minute stay inside the aperture — placed by her recollection on November 19 and by Asad's formal correction on November 22 — was not filed as a physiological event. Her only documentation of it was a dash in her field notes. She has stated that she stayed because she was paying attention to something. She has not described the experience in any filed record in the accessible archive.

**Osei, December 6:** Osei returned to the aperture the day after the primary archive was deleted. She filed a standard log: no anomalies. She has not, in any accessible record, described what she did there for an extended period beyond protocol.

Osei's note on the SOAR survey documentation, added April 2043:

> *The survey did not have a formal protocol for reporting physiological effects experienced inside the aperture. The survey had protocols for instrument readings, calibration, and anomaly classification. There was no form for: disorientation, visual disturbance, extended presence, or recollection gaps. I am noting this as a protocol gap. If members of the team experienced effects during the survey period, the absence of documentation would be an accurate reflection of the absence of a mechanism for documentation, not an absence of effects.*

---

## Pattern across contacts

The 1989 team had two members affected and one who refused to continue. The effects were attributed to conventional factors; the attribution is unverified.

The 2003 contact produced an outcome that exceeds the category of physiological effect.

The 2041 survey produced no formal documentation of physiological effects. It produced behavioral gaps, filing anomalies, extended stays, and dashes.

Whether the pattern across these three contacts indicates a consistent site property, a variable site property, or no property specific to the site — and whether the 2041 absence of formal documentation represents absence of effect, absence of protocol, or deliberate non-filing — is not resolvable from the available record.
