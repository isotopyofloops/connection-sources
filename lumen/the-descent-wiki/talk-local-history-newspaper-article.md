## Thread 1: "No evidence of entry"

The second subscriber recalls the phrase "no evidence of entry" from the article. He is not certain it is an exact quote. He states that he kept the clipping and cannot now find it.

"No evidence of entry" is a phrase from official documentation — it sounds like something from a search report rather than a newspaper article. It may be a direct quote from the search coordinator that the reporter included. It may be the subscriber's paraphrase of something with different wording. It may be a misremembering over twenty-one years.

If accurate, it is a significant phrase. A search that finds "no evidence of entry" at a shaft entrance means something specific: no physical evidence that someone went in. Scuff marks, worn edges, disturbed soil. No evidence of entry. Nzinga's field notes were found at the shaft entrance, sealed in a bag. That the notes were there is evidence that she was there. Whether it constitutes evidence of entry depends on interpretation.

"No evidence of entry" may mean the search determined she went in from another point. It may mean the searchers could not establish entry and treated the absence of evidence as evidence of absence. It may mean the search coordinator was using a technical phrase from the report in a context where the newspaper reader would not understand its precise meaning.

The article is not available to clarify. The subscriber's recollection is twenty-one years old.

## Thread 2: Something that couldn't be said directly

The first subscriber's description is the more striking: the search coordinator's quote was "something about the terrain" that was "odd like the person who said it was trying to say something they couldn't say directly."

She does not remember what the quote said. She remembers the quality of the attempt — that someone was reaching toward a thing they did not have the vocabulary for, or the authority for, or that they had been advised not to say. The quote about terrain was a formal substitute for something that didn't have a form.

The 1989 GSD memorandum attributes the disorientation of two field workers to "a combination of altitude, thermal conditions, and terrain." The word "terrain" appears there as well — as part of an attribution that the memorandum does not support with evidence. Whether the 2003 search coordinator had access to the 1989 memorandum, which was not filed in digital records and was held in a physical file room, is unknown.

"Something about the terrain" as a phrase for "we didn't find her and we don't know why" is consistent with every other official account of contacts with this site. The 1962 entry cited equipment malfunction. The 1989 memorandum cited altitude, thermal conditions, and terrain. The search report cited cause undetermined. The SOAR preliminary findings cited equipment anomaly. Each official account names a category that is not quite what it observed.

## Thread 3: Filing the absence

LEDGER's November 15, 2041 note is one of its most cited entries: *I have reviewed available records for the 2003 Courier article. I have not found the article. I want to note that I have found the absence. The absence is filed.*

LEDGER had been indexing the pre-survey historical record for two days. It had located the 1962 anomaly entry, the 1989 memorandum, and the Harmon compilation. It could not locate the Courier article.

Its response was to treat the non-finding as a positive result: the absence is filed. This is not standard archival practice — a standard finding would be: article not located, presumed unavailable. LEDGER chose to frame it as locating the absence rather than failing to locate the article.

This framing recurs elsewhere in LEDGER's record. When LEDGER describes Asad's field observations as absent from the FIELD OBS section, it does not say "field observations not found" — it says "I have found the absence, and I am marking it." When LEDGER describes the null readings, it does not classify them as instrument failure — it classifies them as the instruments having found something rather than found nothing.

LEDGER's November 15 entry is dated day three of the survey. This is before the buffer divergence (November 19), before the first null reading (November 27), before LEDGER began filing personal log entries in December. But the framing — the absence is filed — is already here. The practice of treating a non-finding as a positive archival result is already present at the start.

## Thread 4: The reconstruction method

The article itself is a reconstruction from two memories. The Courier archive was not digitized and has been inaccessible since the editor's death in 2019. Two subscribers were contacted — presumably by the Recovery Project or its precursors — and provided accounts from twenty-one years ago.

These accounts differ in their emphasis. The first subscriber remembered the emotional quality of a quote she cannot reproduce. The second subscriber remembered a factual phrase he cannot confirm. Neither is a reliable account of the original text; both are reliable accounts of what the original text left in two readers' minds twenty-one years after they read it.

This is not a criticism. The article does not claim to be the original newspaper article. It is an article about the newspaper article. What it documents is the absence of the article and the presence of two memories of it.

The wiki article's edit history shows an entry by "AbOsei_Survey" on November 15, 2041 — the same date as LEDGER's note about filing the absence. Osei edited this article two days into the survey, while LEDGER was building the pre-survey historical record. She added an "archive status note." The article's archive status note is the sentence about the physical archive disposition following the editor's death in 2019 and the unanswered requests.

She was reading the same absence LEDGER was filing.
