## The 2003 citation

The article states: *"The proposal noted the 2003 data as preliminary and incomplete."*

I have been looking for this 2003 data since we arrived on site. It is not in the GSD archive I have access to. It is not cited in the proposal by author, institution, or document number. It is cited only by year and described as "preliminary," which is the same word used to describe our own current results.

I asked Asad about this in the second week. He said the 2003 work was informal and predated the GSD's current documentation requirements. When I asked if he was involved in the 2003 work, he said: "I visited the site that year." That is a different answer from "I conducted the 2003 survey."

This matters because the proposal cites the 2003 data as the justification for this project — the gap in the record that SOAR is meant to close. If the 2003 data is not accessible and its provenance is unclear, then the gap the proposal references may be the same gap the proposal creates.

I am not suggesting bad faith. I am noting that the article should say the citation is unverifiable, not simply that it is "not cited by source." Unverified and unsourced are different categories.

— M. Reyes, November 18, 2041

## On the 2003 data — response to Reyes

Reyes is correct that the 2003 data is not formally archived. It was not conducted under GSD protocols. I was present at the site in 2003 as a postdoctoral researcher; the work that year was exploratory and produced no published record. The observations I made then were informal field notes, which I retained personally.

These notes are part of the background I brought to the SOAR proposal. They are not cited as a source because they were not intended as a source — they were the motivation for seeking a proper survey, which is this project.

I understand Reyes's concern about circularity. But the proposal's justification is not the 2003 data itself. The justification is the anomaly, which predates any survey. The 2003 notes documented what was already there. This project documents it more rigorously.

On scope: the article's current framing is appropriate for an overview. I would ask that the talk page note my clarification rather than revising the article, as I have an outstanding review of certain findings that may affect the framing.

— K. Asad, November 25, 2041

## The authorization question

I want to raise something the article does not address: who at the GSD authorized this project?

The article says the project was "funded by the Geological Survey Division," which is accurate. But the GSD's standard authorization process for anomaly research requires a named approving officer at the division director level and a documented scope agreement. I have requested the SOAR project authorization file from GSD administration twice in the past three weeks and have not received a response.

This may be administrative delay. GSD records management has been backlogged since the 2040 restructure.

But I want to note it here because if the project's authorization record is incomplete, the protocol deviations logged separately in this archive may not have a clear review chain. A deviation from scope is only reviewable against an authorized scope. If the authorization is not accessible, the deviations exist in a kind of procedural vacuum.

I am not claiming the project was improperly authorized. I am noting that I cannot confirm it was properly authorized, which is a different thing.

— R. Nzinga, December 2, 2041
