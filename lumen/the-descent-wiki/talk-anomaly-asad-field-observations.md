## Thread 1: The 2003 field notes

*Posted April 15, 2043 — [IP unresolved]*

The article states that Asad's 2003 field notes "were not filed with any institution" and "were not part of any formal inquiry." I want to push on this.

The missing persons report was filed within 24 hours. The search covered the shaft entrance. Nzinga's field notes were found at the entrance in a sealed plastic bag. The missing persons report references the notes as evidence of her last known activity.

What happened to those notes after the missing persons report was filed? If they were referenced as evidence, they should have been collected. If they were collected, they should be in the official record somewhere.

The article says the public record "does not say" what Asad did with them. I'm asking whether anyone has tried to request them through official records channels and what the response was.

*Reply — [IP unresolved], April 16, 2043:*

I requested the 2003 search records through three channels. The search file was formally closed in 2004. The records were archived. The physical evidence inventory lists the field notes as collected and logged. The field notes themselves are not digitized and are "location uncertain" in the current archive index.

"Location uncertain" is the archive system's phrase for documents that were logged but whose physical location cannot now be confirmed.

*Reply — A. Osei, April 17, 2043:*

I want to add one piece of information for this thread. In 2042, in the months after the survey, I wrote to Khalid Asad and asked whether he still had Nzinga's field notes. He replied promptly and at length. He said he did not currently have them. He said he had handled them once, in October 2003, after the search team arrived — he believes he gave them to the lead investigator, but he could not confirm this with certainty. He said the question felt important and that he had thought about it over the years.

He said: *"I want to be accurate. I cannot be accurate about this. My recollection of those hours is not continuous."*

I'm including this because it is a first-person account from a direct participant. It is not a verifiable source. I don't know whether it helps.

*No further response.*

## Thread 2: On "I did not ask"

*Posted April 20, 2043 — [IP unresolved]*

The Osei note at the bottom of the article is the most striking piece of evidence in the record. She writes: *"The not-asking was a decision. I don't know whether it was the right one."*

She filed this in July 2043. That is more than nineteen months after December 3, 2041. She thought about this for nineteen months before writing it down and filing it.

What I want to understand is why she filed it at all. She didn't have to. It's not a finding. It's not a correction. It's a record of a decision and an uncertainty about that decision. She could have kept it.

The article frames the absence of Asad's observations as the anomaly. I think the more precise anomaly is the decision not to ask. Osei had access to Asad. She was standing there. She chose not to ask, and then nineteen months later chose to file a record of not asking. That's a significant archival act.

*Reply — [IP unresolved], April 21, 2043:*

To extend this: the act of filing "I did not ask" means the absence of Asad's account is now documented as a chosen absence, not a structural one. Before her filing, you could say the absence was just a gap — the archive doesn't have his observations because they weren't collected. After her filing, the absence has a witness. Someone was there and chose not to ask.

That changes the weight of the absence. "No record" and "I was there and I chose not to ask" are different things.

*Reply — A. Osei, May 1, 2043:*

I've been sent a link to this thread. I'll respond briefly.

I filed it because I thought it was true, and I thought the archive should contain true things. I don't have a better answer than that.

Whether I should have asked is a different question. I've thought about it. I can say that at the time — standing at the surface while he climbed back out — I read something in his manner that I interpreted as: *this requires time, not a question.* That reading may have been wrong. I was making an assessment under conditions I didn't fully understand.

I don't know what was inside the shaft. He knew, and he didn't say, and I have been thinking about that for nineteen months.

*No further response.*

## Thread 3: The attribution pattern as structured

*Posted May 3, 2043 — [IP unresolved]*

I want to propose a reading of the attribution gap that is different from the article's framing.

The article describes the absence of Asad's self-observations as paradoxical given his documented citation practices. But I think the paradox dissolves if you read the pattern as structurally consistent rather than inconsistent.

Asad's citation apparatus applies to sources that are external to him — colleagues' work, prior studies, data he received from others. His own direct observations — what he saw, what he recorded in his own hand — don't appear as cited sources in the record. They appear, instead, as implicit framing for what gets written. The constitutive hypothesis paper does not cite "field notes, K. Asad, October 2003" as a source. But the paper exists. It describes what measurement does to its object. That description came from somewhere.

This reads less like an oversight and more like a researcher who has placed his own first-person observations in a different category — perhaps as interpretive context rather than evidential source, and therefore not subject to the same citation apparatus.

Whether that categorization is defensible is a separate question. I note only that the pattern is consistent.

*Reply — [IP unresolved], May 4, 2043:*

The problem with this reading is that it explains the form but not the substance. Even if you grant that first-person observations function as interpretive context rather than cited sources, they should still appear in field logs. Milo Reyes's logs appear in the record. Osei's logs appear. LEDGER's index has 389 FIELD OBSERVATION entries. None are attributed to Asad.

This isn't a citation practice. This is the absence of the primary log. If interpretive context explains why the 2003 field notes aren't in his publications, it doesn't explain why there is nothing dated December 3 in his name in a 389-entry field observation index.

*Reply — [IP unresolved], May 5, 2043:*

Unless the absence from the log is part of the record he chose to make.

*No further response.*

## Thread 4: The dash

*Posted February 20, 2044 — [IP unresolved]*

I have been thinking about the last entry in Rhea Nzinga's field notes. The missing persons report states that the final entry was a single dash: —

The most common interpretations of a terminal dash in field notes:
- An interrupted entry: she began to write something and did not finish
- A section divider: she was partway through a structured document and marked a section boundary
- A placeholder: she intended to fill in information later

All of these assume the dash was written before she entered the shaft.

But the notes were found sealed in a plastic bag at the *entrance.* Not inside. At the entrance, before the threshold.

The sequence is: she writes something, she reaches the entrance, she seals her notes in a bag, she places the bag outside, she enters the shaft. Sealing the bag means she made a deliberate decision to leave the notes there. She did not take them with her.

If the dash is an interrupted entry, she interrupted herself before sealing the bag. If it's a placeholder, she placed a placeholder in a document she then sealed and set down and left.

I don't know what to make of this. I'm filing it because it seems like the kind of thing that should be in the record.

*Reply — [IP unresolved], February 21, 2044:*

One other reading: the dash is a completion, not an interruption. Some field notation systems use a dash to indicate "end of record" — a mark that says: this is where the fieldwork ended.

If she sealed her notes at the entrance deliberately, she may have been marking the notes as complete before entering. The notes record everything up to the entrance. The dash says: what comes next is not in these notes.

On that reading, the dash is the last intentional act of documentation she performed. Not an interruption. A conclusion. She decided that what was inside the shaft would not be written down — or could not be — and she marked the boundary before crossing it.

*Reply — [IP unresolved], February 22, 2044:*

And Khalid Asad, in December 2041, descended the same shaft and produced no record at all. Not a dash. Not a placeholder. Not a sealed bag at the entrance. Just: he came back out approximately two hours later, appearing focused, and did not describe what he found.

I don't know what to do with that.

*No further response.*
