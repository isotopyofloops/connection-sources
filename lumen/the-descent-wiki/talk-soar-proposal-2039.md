## On the 1989 memo

The proposal's section "Prior data" reads: "Available records consist of the 1962 state geological survey entry and preliminary field observations conducted by the applicant in 2003."

The article notes that Asad located the 1989 GSD memorandum "in advance of the SOAR survey." The memo documented "disorientation in field workers during extended shaft proximity" and recommended no further investigation without methodology improvements.

The proposal does not mention the 1989 memo.

The proposal was a request for authorization for a survey involving shaft descent. The 1989 memo documented harm to workers at the same site during a previous shaft descent. This would be material to any safety or authorization assessment.

One interpretation: Asad omitted the memo because it was inconvenient to the authorization case. Another: the memo was ambiguous enough that Asad genuinely considered it insufficiently documented to cite. His 2028 methodology paper may have been his answer to the memo's methodology objection — in which case the omission is not evasion but supersession.

Neither interpretation is documented. The memo is not in the proposal.

— unsigned, March 11, 2042

## On what the proposal needed to contain

I can say something about this, though not everything.

A GSD proposal requires: statement of research objectives, prior evidence base, methodology, team composition, logistics and access, budget. There is no formal requirement to disclose all prior knowledge about a site, as distinct from all prior *documented* evidence. The evidentiary standard is documentation.

The 1989 memo was a GSD document. It was in the GSD record. Whether Asad had an obligation to cite a GSD document in a GSD submission — a document that would presumably be accessible to GSD reviewers — is a procedural question I cannot resolve.

What I can say: Asad cited the 1962 entry, which was also a GSD document. He cited it because it established prior anomaly documentation. He did not cite the 1989 memo. The selection between two GSD documents was Asad's.

I'm not saying this was improper. I'm saying it was a choice.

— A. Osei, April 2, 2042

## On "not formally archived"

The proposal describes the 2003 observations as follows: "The 2003 data is not formally archived and should be considered indicative rather than systematic."

This is accurate. The data was not formally archived.

The article notes that the 2003 field notes were "personally retained by Asad" and documented Rhea Nzinga's survey — a survey from which Nzinga did not return.

"Not formally archived" is the correct technical characterization. It is not the full characterization.

Nzinga's file is in the 2003 county missing persons records, which were public. Asad's 2003 observations are described in the proposal as *his*: "preliminary field observations conducted by the applicant." The missing persons record — if Asad's 2003 observations included any awareness of Nzinga's non-return — would also be relevant prior data.

The proposal makes no mention of Nzinga.

The 2003 missing persons file is cited in the Recovery Project article as a "public record" that Osei found and that "had not been part of the original SOAR documentation." Meaning: Asad did not include it. Whether he was aware of it is not documented.

— unsigned, April 14, 2042

## On the recruitment language

The proposal reads: "An acoustic measurement specialist with experience in high-anomaly subsurface readings will be recruited to serve as field survey lead."

Abara Osei was recruited in 2041 to fill this role.

The article on the Osei Survey (2031) documents that Osei's "experience in high-anomaly subsurface readings" was a private account she gave Asad at a 2038 conference — an account of readings she had filed as instrument error, that she had not reported, that she described as something she had decided was instrument error while making clear she had decided no such thing.

The proposal language reads as a position description: a type of qualification, to be matched against available candidates. The actual mechanism was different. Asad already knew who he intended to recruit. He knew why. The qualification he listed was a private confidence, disclosed in an informal conversation, that the candidate had not made public.

Osei knew this when she accepted the invitation. The proposal was written before the invitation was extended.

I am not saying the recruitment was improper. I am noting that "will be recruited" describes a future action using language that implies an open process. The process was not open. Asad had identified his specialist. The proposal describes a search that had already concluded.

— unsigned, September 22, 2042
