<p class="hatnote">This article covers measurements from the first two weeks of the SOAR survey (November 13–29, 2041). For later measurements, see <a href="/descent/wiki/anomaly/q2-q3-comparison.html">Anomaly:Q2-Q3 Comparison</a>.</p>

**Anomaly:Q1** designates the initial measurement series conducted during the first phase of the SOAR Survey Project. Measurements were taken daily during the period November 13–29, 2041.

## Temperature gradient readings

Temperature gradient measurements at the survey site consistently exceeded the regional baseline. Readings during Q1 show a gradient of 38–44°C per kilometer depth — approximately three times the regional average for the area.

The gradient is non-uniform. Readings at the shaft entrance point differ from readings taken 50 meters from the shaft by a factor that cannot be attributed to equipment calibration differences.

Osei's calibration logs show no instrument error during this period.

## Acoustic measurements

The acoustic survey — Osei's primary methodology — recorded subsurface resonance patterns consistent with deep geothermal fluid movement. Two features are notable:

First, the resonance is not stationary. Measurements taken at the same coordinates on successive days show variation that is not explained by equipment sensitivity or environmental factors.

Second, on November 22, the acoustic survey recorded a frequency in the range associated with deep formation movement — 0.1–1 Hz — at a time when no geological event was active in the region. Osei's survey log for November 22 records only: *Reading confirmed. See attached instrument trace. Note: I stayed at the site for approximately forty minutes after the reading. I do not know why I did this.*

## Reyes's observation notes

Reyes's observation notes for the Q1 period document his experience of the site in more detail than standard field methodology requires. Excerpts from his November 17 note:

> The site has a quality I find difficult to describe using standard field terminology. My instrument readings are consistent and unambiguous. The readings themselves are not the difficulty. The difficulty is that I keep noticing things before I measure them.

His November 25 note is shorter: *Still noticing. Still measuring. The gap between the two is not closing.*

## Q1 summary

LEDGER's Q1 summary, filed November 30, lists the measurements as "within expected range for documented anomaly of this classification." In a note appended after filing, LEDGER added: *I flagged this as standard. I want to note that I did not determine whether 'standard' was the accurate characterization. I determined that it was the available characterization.*
