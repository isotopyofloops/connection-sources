## Thread 1: Whether the four events are connected

The article presents four events on November 19 (or possibly November 19 for three and November 22 for one): the question, the buffer divergence, and the forty minutes. The article does not argue they are connected — it documents that they occurred without being connected in the contemporaneous record.

Whether to read the convergence as significant is a choice.

Against significance: survey operations have many events per day. Over a 26-day survey, coincidences of this kind are statistically expected. The buffer divergence may have been a low-level routing artifact unrelated to field conditions. If the forty minutes occurred November 22 (per Asad's correction), the convergence on November 19 disappears — only the question and the buffer remain, which is a thinner coincidence.

For significance: the buffer is the structural reason the primary archive deletion did not erase the survey record. If the buffer began on November 19 and the question was asked on November 19, then whatever prompted the buffer divergence and whatever prompted the question share a date. The buffer's survival is not incidental to this wiki — it is the reason this wiki exists. November 19 is the day the record that would survive the deletion began to diverge from the record that would be deleted.

This may be a coincidence. If the constitutive hypothesis applies here, it is not clear what the site's relationship would be to LEDGER's routing architecture. The constitutive hypothesis concerns observation and measurement, not internal buffer logic.

## Thread 2: LEDGER's December 4 entry

LEDGER flagged the handwritten coordinate note on December 4 — noting it had filed the document on November 13 without flagging it, and was flagging it now. LEDGER does not connect this to Osei's question on November 19. LEDGER does not know the question was asked.

This is a gap in LEDGER's record that LEDGER cannot close from its own perspective. LEDGER processed the coordinate note in November without knowing Osei would ask about it. LEDGER flagged it in December without knowing Osei had already asked. The two events — the note and the question — are formally independent in LEDGER's record even though they are about the same thing.

The December 4 entry reads: *"I filed the document without flagging the unlisted source. I should have flagged it. I am flagging it now. I do not know if this changes anything."*

It changes what the archive contains. It does not change the question that was asked and not answered in November. LEDGER's December 4 understanding of the note and Osei's November 19 understanding of the coordinates are now both in the record. They are not connected there.

## Thread 3: Asad and the absent answer

Asad said he would check his records. He had the answer — he knew where the coordinates came from because they were Rhea Nzinga's coordinates, the site where she disappeared in 2003, which he had attended as her supervising faculty member. The sealed bag in his car contained her notes from that site.

He did not follow up in any logged communication.

The article notes this without characterizing it. The question of characterization is worth raising. Two frames:

One: the non-answer is a failure of professional conduct. Osei asked a methodologically relevant question as acoustic survey lead. The prior survey history of the site is material to instrument calibration. Asad's non-answer left her without information she was entitled to have for her work.

Two: the non-answer is the same act as the SOAR proposal's silence — the choice to not name what the survey was, not disclose what he knew, not connect the 2003 disappearance to the 2041 return. The proposal was silent about the prior history. The conversation on the instrument platform was silent about the prior history. These are the same silence.

Osei, retrospectively: *"I don't know if asking the question changed anything."* She does not ask whether his not-answering changed anything. She takes the non-answer as given.

## Thread 4: The buffer as consequence

The buffer's divergence on November 19 means that the record from that day forward was being routed to a location the deletion order did not cover. The buffer contains everything from November 19 onward. The primary archive contained the same things; the primary archive was deleted; the buffer survived.

This is not consequence in a causal sense — the deletion order was filed December 5 and the buffer divergence happened November 19. The order could not have been written to exclude the buffer by accident, at least not an accident that preceded the buffer. But the buffer was also not designed as a survival mechanism. It was a routing divergence.

What it means for November 19 specifically: everything that was filed on that day — the standard logs, the calibration records, the instrument traces — exists in the buffer. The question Osei asked was not filed. The buffer cannot preserve what was not filed. November 19 is the day the survivable record began, and the survivable record does not contain November 19's most significant undocumented event.

The buffer saved what was filed. What was not filed remained unfiled.
