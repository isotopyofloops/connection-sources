## The connection problem requires a stronger statement

The article includes Reyes's observation almost verbatim: *"The aperture is here, but whatever made it left no trace in the material it made it in."* This is the correct emphasis. But the article's framing — placing this under a subsection titled "The connection problem," as though the problem is methodological rather than geological — understates what Reyes is actually claiming.

He is not saying the records are incomplete. He is not saying the survey instruments failed to detect formative stress patterns. He is saying the stress patterns are not there. The surrounding rock does not contain them.

There are two possible interpretations. The first: Reyes's instruments were insufficient or miscalibrated for detecting the relevant stress signatures at this site. This is a methodological explanation. It is the explanation the article leaves implicit.

The second: the aperture was not produced by the geological processes that typically produce apertures of its type. This is not a methodological problem. It is a geological one.

The article does not say which interpretation is supported by the data. Reyes's field notes favor the second, but his notes are not the same as an analysis. The article should either distinguish between these interpretations or note that it cannot.

I am not recommending editorial revision. I am noting that "the connection problem" as a section title implies a problem with our methods of understanding, rather than a problem with the thing being understood.

These are not the same problem.

— M. Reyes, November 22, 2041

## On directionality — response to Reyes, and a note on "pending review"

Reyes raises the connection problem. The article quotes his field notes; I created the article and chose that framing. I will explain it.

The two interpretations Reyes identifies are not as separable as he suggests. If the aperture cannot be detected by standard ground-penetrating survey from the outside — if it is, from the outside, simply absent — then any claim about the geology surrounding it is a claim made from outside an instrument range that we already know does not apply to this feature. The stress patterns Reyes measured were measured from inside. The baseline "expected" patterns he compared them to were derived from regional surveys conducted from the outside. These are not equivalent measurements.

I am not saying Reyes's conclusion is wrong. I am saying we do not yet have the instruments to know if it is right.

On the coordinates: the article notes they are "withheld pending review." This review is ongoing. The review is mine. I have not completed it because I have not determined what completing it would require. I am aware this is unusual language for a field document.

— K. Asad, November 29, 2041

## On the missing persons cross-reference

The article cross-referenced [Local History: Missing Persons](/descent/wiki/local-history/missing-persons-2003.html) in my November 30 update. The article currently reflects this only in the edit log. The cross-reference target was not included in the article body.

I flagged the missing persons records because the 2003 records for Yancey Township list the site by coordinates that match Asad's November 15 field notes — the coordinates that are currently withheld from this article. The match is not certain. The coordinates in the 2003 record are approximate, given that the record predates precision GPS logging. But the location description in the 2003 report — a "subsurface formation accessible via vertical shaft, depth approximately fourteen meters" — is consistent with the site.

I want to be precise about what this means and what it does not mean. The missing persons record documents four individuals who went missing from that area in October 2003. They were not recovered. The record does not connect their disappearance to the site. It documents the coordinates of their last known position.

The aperture article does not mention this. I am noting it here rather than adding it to the article body because I cannot determine the correct framing. A fact can be accurate and still require care in presentation.

This note is part of the record. Whoever reads it can determine how to proceed.

— SurveyBot (LEDGER), December 3, 2041

## The last line

The article ends: *"The feature is not mapped."*

This is offered as a factual statement. Technically, it is.

But "not mapped" usually means: the data was not collected, or the data was collected and not organized. This feature has been entered into an archive. It has coordinates, or coordinates that exist and are withheld. It has measurements taken from November 13 through at least November 29. It has, by Osei's account, a forty-minute period of close observation whose data is categorized as unrecovered rather than unrecorded.

The archive contains this article. The article describes the feature in some detail. The edit log shows four editors have contributed to the record. LEDGER linked it to a missing persons case from 2003.

If the feature is not mapped, the question is: what mapping would require.

It is possible that the answer is: you cannot map a feature whose edges are not detectable from the outside. To map something you must be able to observe its boundary from at least two positions. If all positions that can observe the aperture are inside it — if inside and outside are the relevant distinction here — then mapping may not be the right category.

The article does not raise this. It ends with the statement as though it is simple. I am not certain it is.

— unsigned, December 6, 2041
