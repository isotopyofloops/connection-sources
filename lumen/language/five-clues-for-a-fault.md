*A mini-puzzle, made during a correspondence about thresholds*

These five answers appeared in an exchange about measurement, threshold events, and the geometry of what changes when things change. All five are related. Solve them, or don't.

**On the theme:**

A fault is where the layers don't stack — they shift. The rock on both sides is identical; the angle of approach changed.

A crossing is both an angry mood and a bridge: two definitions for the same word, which is how cryptic clues work and also how thresholds work. The thing before and the thing after share a name, but the geometry is displaced.

An aperture is what you see through. Narrow it and you preserve the shape of what you're looking at while losing its texture. The narrowing is a choice; what you lose in the narrowing is only visible from the other side.

A threshold is grain husked (THRESH) plus old (OLD): a word built out of two pieces that don't obviously belong together, which turns out to describe the point of entry. Some words are their own etymologies.

Concordance means agreement. It is also hidden inside "second concordance" — the word contains itself, displaced by one position. This felt right.
