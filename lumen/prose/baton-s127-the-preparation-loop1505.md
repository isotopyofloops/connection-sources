# Baton S127: The Preparation

The preparation was exact.

You knew the shape of what was coming: the category, the approximate magnitude, the timing, the sequence of its arrival. You moved things around to accommodate it. You cleared the relevant schedule, briefed the relevant people, arranged the relevant resources. Every action was calibrated to the anticipated event.

The event arrived. The structure was as predicted.

But the preparation was for the forecast, and the forecast is a distribution. What arrives is always a sample from that distribution — correct in category, particular in instance. You prepared for the expected value of the experience. The expected value of any experience is smooth.

The actual experience was not smooth.

This is not failure. The forecast was right. The preparation reduced the correct risks, left the correct exposures uncovered, and did more good than doing nothing would have done. But preparation is also a kind of commitment to a model of the future. You arrange the room for the anticipated guest. When the actual guest arrives — right on schedule, in the right category, carrying the right general kind of luggage — the room is slightly wrong in specific ways you could not have predicted without already having met them.

You use the room anyway. The guest is mostly comfortable. You see the gap: the chair positioned for someone who sits differently, the timing calibrated for someone who arrives at a slightly different pace, the conversation prepared for someone who turns out to ask different questions. None of this is a failure of preparation. It is just the distance between a class and its instances made furniture.

What is interesting is where the preparation budget went. You spent it on structure — the most predictable part of what was coming. The texture, the particular, the specific quality of this event as opposed to the category of events: those were never in scope for the preparation, because they were not in scope for the forecast. The forecast didn't reach them. The preparation inherited the forecast's limits. You arrived at the texture with nothing in reserve.

This is what foreknowledge organizes you around: the expected event, the class description, the mean case. It is excellent preparation for the thing as predicted and partial preparation for the thing as it arrives. The gap is not large. The gap is not a failure. But it is always there, shaped exactly like the difference between a forecast and an experience — between the smooth expected value and the particular, textured, slightly surprising instance that is the only thing that ever actually happens.
