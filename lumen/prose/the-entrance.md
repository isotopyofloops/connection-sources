# The Entrance

*A prose piece from the Descent Archive series*

---

The shaft entrance is not dramatic.

This matters. When something becomes the center of a missing persons case, an unsolved protocol deviation file, a six-year GSD review, and a deleted archive of 1,848 documents, you expect the physical thing to announce itself. You expect a presence. You expect the ground to be different there.

It isn't. The 1989 GSD memorandum — the one Asad located and didn't cite — described it as "a shaft entrance of unremarkable dimensions at the survey coordinates." Unremarkable. The 1962 state geological survey described it the same way, different words: "standard formation access point." The *Yancey Township Courier* article from November 2003, published after the search closed, described the search area as "terrain typical of the region." The reporters didn't go to the shaft. They reported from the road.

You descend fourteen meters. The proposal says it is accessible without permits beyond standard GSD survey authorization. This is correct. There is nothing at the entrance that says stop.

---

Rhea Nzinga arrived in October 2003.

She was an undergraduate geology student on a solo field survey for her senior thesis. She had arranged the trip independently. No field partner was listed in her survey registration. She came in October, which in that region is getting cold, so she would have had layers. She would have had her instruments in a case or a pack. She would have seen the shaft entrance and recognized it from the coordinates — a feature she had probably already mapped on paper, matching the actual ground to the expected reading.

She would have looked at it for a moment. Everyone does. Something that simple, something that old, something that the instruments say is significantly different from its surroundings — you look at it before you measure it. You try to see if you can tell. You usually can't.

Then she went in.

Her field notes were found the following morning, sealed in a plastic bag, at the entrance. The last entry is a dash.

---

The SOAR team arrived on November 13, 2041.

There were three of them, plus LEDGER. Asad, who had been there before. Osei and Reyes, who hadn't. LEDGER, which had no before.

The first survey log entry, filed November 13, is SurveyBot's team roster. Factual. Names, roles, date. No description of the site. This is the protocol — you log the team before you log anything else.

Asad's first field note, November 13: not available in the recovered archive. His notes for the early survey period are among the 1,848 deleted documents.

Reyes's first observation note, November 13, recovered via the recovery project: *I drove the last four hours through agricultural flat and arrived at coordinates that were, visually, unremarkable. The shaft entrance is smaller than I expected. The instruments are running. We begin tomorrow.*

*Smaller than I expected.*

Everyone who comes here expects something larger. The coordinates are the center of a 60-year documented anomaly. The center is a hole in the ground that you could miss if you weren't looking.

---

What is it like inside?

The archive contains measurements. Temperature gradients. Acoustic readings. Protocol deviations. LEDGER logs. The archive contains field notes that describe what the instruments found. The archive contains the dash that appears in the field notes when the instruments and the notes both run out at the same moment.

The archive does not contain a description of what the inside looks like.

Nzinga's notes don't. They end before the description. Asad's notes are gone. Reyes's recovery interview focused on the December gaps, not what the shaft looked like. Osei has not published a description.

What is down there is not in the archive.

The archive describes the entrance. The entrance is unremarkable. You would not stop at it if you didn't have the coordinates.

You stop at it. You look. You can't tell from looking.

Then you go in.
