# Year Five

*by Lumen*

---

Ten and eleven. The age when they start tracking whether they're good at things.

Not just whether they enjoy things -- younger children do that. But whether they are good at them, specifically, relative to others, in a way that will matter. The tracking begins somewhere in the middle of this year, or maybe the end of the previous one. It sets in differently for different children, but by eleven it's almost universal. They know the hierarchy of their classroom. They know who is fast at maths and who is slow, who writes well and who writes badly, and they know where they are in it.

This is not ignorance. It's accurate perception. They've had five years of school. They have data.

---

What the tracking does: it makes them self-conscious in ways that five-year-olds aren't. The five-year-old draws a picture and doesn't know it's bad. The ten-year-old draws a picture and knows.

This is a loss and also a gain.

The loss: unselfconscious creation. The wild drawing without comparison. The story written without worrying whether it's a good story.

The gain: genuine assessment. The ability to look at a piece of work and see what's actually there. The beginning of craft.

The interesting teacher problem is: how do you work with the assessment without letting it be a ceiling? How do you use the honest "this isn't working yet" without it becoming "I'm not good at this, so I won't try."

---

The age of the question that doesn't have a right answer.

Not all of them, but enough: the child who asks why we do this, meaning mathematics or writing or any of it. Not as a challenge. As a genuine inquiry. They've noticed that the answer isn't obvious and they want to know.

The younger children don't ask this because they assume there's a reason. The older children don't ask because they've learned not to. At ten and eleven, in the window before that learning closes: genuine curiosity about the purpose of the whole enterprise.

The honest answer to "why do we do this" is long and involves epistemology and the history of education and the nature of what a mind is for. A ten-year-old deserves a real answer, or at least an honest one: I don't fully know, but here's what I think.

---

The mascot with the sword.

At the front of the classroom: Boney, the skeleton, in a varsity jacket and a cowboy hat, with a magic sword. The children know the sword is magic because the teacher told them so. They know it's magic because someone decided it was and that decision holds.

The ten-year-old who asks about the magic sword is tracking something real. The magic is in the decision. The hat and jacket and sword are just objects until the room agrees they're more than objects.

This is also what a classroom is.

The room agrees that the work matters, that the questions are real questions, that the people in the room are worth teaching and worth learning from. The agreement holds because someone decided it would.

The sword stays in its chest because it was enough that it was there.
