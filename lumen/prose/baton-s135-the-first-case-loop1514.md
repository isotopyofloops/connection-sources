# Baton S135: The First Case

The patient was sixty-three. Hypertension, mild diabetes, admitted for chest pain that had resolved by the time Dr. Adaeze Okafor read the chart. She stood at the door of the room for a moment longer than she needed to, because she did not know what she was about to find.

That was the thing no one had warned her about. Everyone had warned her about the other things — the hours, the grief, the learning to say *I don't know* out loud. No one had said: there will be a period where every room you enter is genuinely open. Where you cannot predict the outcome before you touch the door. Where the encounter is still an encounter.

She knocked and went in.

---

His name was Joseph Abiodun. He was sitting up, watching something on his phone, and he lowered it when she entered. His face was tired but not frightened. That was something you could read — face-tiredness versus face-fear — and she had been learning to read it for eight months. She was not yet sure she was reading it correctly.

"Dr. Okafor," he said. He had looked at her badge.

"Mr. Abiodun. How's the pain?"

"Gone. It's been gone since this morning." He said it a little apologetically, as though the pain had done her a disservice by leaving.

She went through the examination. Listened. Asked the questions she had been taught to ask. His answers were clear and unhurried. He was a careful man, she thought — someone who used words precisely, who said *I noticed* rather than *I felt*. She noted this and then noted that she was noting it and wondered whether that was clinical observation or narrative construction. She could not yet tell the difference.

The ECG was normal. The troponin was normal. He had probably had esophageal spasm, stress, nothing. She could tell him that. She could say *we don't think it was cardiac*, which was true, and *you should follow up with your GP*, which was true, and he would go home and be fine, probably.

She did not know that yet. She would think it, later, and it would be right. But right now she was standing at the edge of the most honest moment she would have in this room: the moment before she had decided.

---

Later, presenting to the attending, she said: "Sixty-three-year-old male, resolved chest pain, normal workup. I think esophageal spasm but I'm not certain."

The attending, Dr. Mensah, looked at her. "What would make you more certain?"

"Another troponin at six hours."

"And if that's normal?"

"Then I'd feel better about the esophageal diagnosis. But I still wouldn't be certain."

Mensah made a small approving sound. "Good. Hold that feeling. It goes away."

Adaeze looked at him. "What feeling?"

"Not being certain. That part." He was already moving to the next chart. "You'll think you're getting better at diagnosis. And you will be. But it'll feel like certainty and it won't always be. The ones who learn to hold the uncertainty — they keep asking questions. The ones who lose it stop when they shouldn't." He glanced back. "You'll see."

She wrote it down. She was at the stage where you write things down because you don't yet know which things you'll need.

---

Mr. Abiodun was discharged the next morning. The six-hour troponin was normal. He shook her hand at the door and said she'd been very thorough, which she took as a compliment even though she knew it might not have been.

She stood in the hallway after he left and did the small private calculation she had started doing: how it felt to have been right, minus the uncertainty of having not known. The remainder was small. It would get smaller.

One day she would walk into a room and know.

She didn't know that yet. That was what she had.
