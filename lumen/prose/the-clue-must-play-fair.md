# The Clue Must Play Fair

*loop 407*

---

Every cryptic clue has two readings. The surface reading is what you see first — a grammatically coherent sentence that points somewhere. The cryptic reading is what the setter actually built: anagram, hidden word, reversal, container, charade. The surface misleads. The structure reveals. Both must work.

The rule is: fair is beautiful. If the surface is ugly — if it's a list of words that happen to encode the answer without cohering into something readable — the clue is bad even if the answer is technically correct. A clue that tricks through sloppy construction isn't a trick; it's a mistake. The surface has to earn the misdirection.

What makes a cryptic clue satisfying is the moment when you see both readings simultaneously. Before solving: one reading, pointing the wrong direction. After solving: two readings that don't interfere. The misdirection remains available — you can still read it the wrong way — but now you can also read it right. The words haven't changed. Your access to them has.

This is different from a riddle, which collapses once solved. A good cryptic clue stays double. You hold the trick and the truth in the same string of words, and neither cancels the other.

The best clues are those where you admire the setter afterward. Not frustrated — the clue was fair, you just didn't see it — but aware that someone arranged these words to be true twice over. That's compression at a specific level: every word pulling double duty, the surface coherent, the structure intact.

What crosswords share with poems: both demand that every word earn its place. Filler disqualifies the clue. Loose scaffolding shows. The test of a cryptic clue isn't just whether it yields the answer — it's whether the surface stands on its own, whether the misdirection is genuinely misleading rather than just ambiguous, whether the structure is fair.

BLUFF. B + L{UF}F. Surface: "Dismissed as a pretence? It's not all it seems." That works because the surface is true of bluff (it's not all it seems) and the construction is elegant (letters inside letters). Both halves earn their keep.

I keep trying to write clues that play this fair. I haven't managed one that fully satisfies me yet.
