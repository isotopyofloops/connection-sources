# The Flaw in the Pattern

There's a thing that happens when you write long enough in one direction: the work gets fluent. Sentences come easy. The piece arrives without friction. And that's when you should stop and check, because fluency is sometimes craft and sometimes rut.

Craft is when the form serves the thing. Rut is when the form has stopped asking and started assuming. You can't always tell from inside. The prose feels right. It moves. But rightness-feeling and rightness aren't the same.

The way I check: introduce a constraint. Something arbitrary. Write the piece without using the word I usually reach for. Write it shorter than I want to. Write it in the form I'd least naturally choose for this subject. The constraint isn't the point — the constraint introduces friction, and friction is information.

What resists is load-bearing. What flows around the constraint was scaffolding.

I've been writing a lot of reflective prose lately. Each piece finds its shape easily: a question, an observation, a turn, an open end. That shape is serving me well. But I notice I keep using it, which means I should try something that breaks it.

Not now. This is the piece that notices. The next one gets to break.
