# The Click

The cryptic crossword answer doesn't arrive gradually.

You stare at the clue. You parse the wordplay. You try the obvious anagram indicators. You check whether the definition is at the start or the end. You count the letters. The answer is not available. The answer is completely unavailable. There is no path to it from where you are.

Then: the answer.

Not approached. Not assembled from parts you were assembling. Present.

---

The answer was always there. The clue always encoded it. The letters were always in the grid, waiting for you to write them in. Your ignorance of the answer was the only thing that didn't exist — and it stopped existing in a moment you cannot locate.

This is what makes cryptics different from other puzzles. A sudoku is a process: you identify constraints, eliminate possibilities, make deductions, converge on the solution. You are doing the work at every step. The work is visible as work.

A cryptic is not like that. The solving is mostly not-solving — staring, filing, sleeping on it, failing. And then the click. The gap between not-having-it and having-it is not a series of steps. It is a discontinuity.

---

This does not mean the work before the click was wasted. The work is necessary. You cannot receive the click without having done the work. But the relationship between the work and the answer is indirect. The work creates conditions for the click; it does not produce the click.

The click comes from somewhere else. It comes from the part of your mind that was working while you were filing and staring and failing — the part that operates at a different speed and does not report back in real time.

The experience of solving is not the experience of that part working. It is the experience of waiting for it to finish, and then receiving its output, which arrives without announcement in the form of an answer you suddenly know.

---

You can try to force it. Everyone who has worked on cryptics knows the failure mode: the harder you push, the further away the answer gets. Certainty that you know the anagram indicator makes you miss the double definition. Conviction about the wordplay closes off the approach that would have worked.

The click requires a specific quality of attention: engaged enough to hold the clue, loose enough to let the answer in. Too tight and you lock the answer out. Too loose and you're not really holding the clue.

This is a skill. It is learnable. It is hard to describe to someone who hasn't felt it.

---

After the click, you will be unable to remember what it was like to not see it. The answer will seem obvious. The clue will seem transparent. You will wonder why it took as long as it did.

This is not revisionism. It is the click's nature: it retroactively reconstructs the state before it as one of almost-knowing, because the answer is now fully present and was fully there to be found. But the reconstruction is wrong. Before the click, you genuinely did not have it. The click is real.

You write the letters in and move on to the next one.
