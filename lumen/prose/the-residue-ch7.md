# The Residue
## Chapter Seven

She had not told Daniel about T.W.

This felt significant in a way she didn't fully understand. She had told him about the notebook — in broad terms, the structure of it, the 37 witnesses, Calder's letter. She had told him about Jonathan Haskevik and the Cornish observations and the question of whether any of this still constituted live research or had simply been quietly resolved somewhere in the intervening decades. Daniel had listened and offered to ask a colleague in physical oceanography who might know. Eleanor had said yes, thank you, that would help.

But T.W. she had kept.

She told herself it was because she wanted to confirm the initials before offering them as anything. That was true as far as it went.

---

The T.W. entries were the last three in the notebook's sequence.

She'd worked through the others before returning to them. Twenty-four entries from the first ten years, 1966-1975, all single observations with location, date, and recorder initial. Then a shift: 1976-1979, the pace accelerated and the records grew longer. More detail in the notation. Cross-references appearing between entries — a small arrow, a page number, a marginal note in different ink suggesting it had been added later. The structure of evidence, she thought. Not just recording anymore but building.

The T.W. entries were the three most recent in the notebook, dated 1977, 1978, and 1979. They were also the longest, and the most precisely notated. Where most entries ran to half a page, T.W.'s ran to full pages — the 1979 entry was nearly two. The handwriting in Vera's transcription grew careful when it copied them out, slowing to match the detail.

What T.W. had observed was similar to what the others had observed: the emission pattern, the timing anomaly, the discrepancy between what the distribution models predicted and what was actually in the water. But T.W. had gone further. The 1979 entry included a calculation — not just observation but analysis — showing the anomaly's behavior over three consecutive measurements at the same location. The numbers implied not a random event but a periodicity. Something that repeated.

Vera had underlined this twice. In the margin she had written, in her smallest hand: *the frequency is the argument.*

Eleanor had stopped when she'd read that. She'd read it again. Then she'd copied it into her own notebook and put the date next to it, as if she were one of the witnesses herself.

---

She began with the academic databases she knew best: ORCID, the oceanographic institution directories, the archived membership rolls of several professional bodies from the period. *T.W.* was common enough initials that the search returned hundreds of entries. She narrowed by field, by period, by geography.

Nothing resolved cleanly. There were several T. Wilsons and a T. Warwick and a Thomas Whitfield who'd published on North Atlantic circulation in the 1970s, but his methods section and his institutional record didn't suggest the kind of detailed field observation the notebook entries described. His work was modeling-based. T.W. in the notebook had been in the field.

She tried another angle. Vera's other witnesses had been people in contact with her — not just observing independently but corresponding. Haskevik's acknowledgements had confirmed this. If T.W. had corresponded with Vera, there might be a trace in Vera's outgoing correspondence, which Eleanor hadn't finished cataloguing. Three boxes remained unopened.

She opened the first two that afternoon.

The outgoing correspondence was organized by year, then loosely by recipient. Vera had not been a tidy correspondent — letters were clipped together but not always completely, and some envelopes contained drafts rather than final copies. Eleanor had learned to check both. She worked through 1976 and 1977 without finding T.W. by name or initials.

In the 1978 folder she found a letter addressed to *Dr. T. Wren,* at the University of Edinburgh Department of Biological Oceanography.

She put it down. Picked it up again.

The letter was two pages, typed, with handwritten corrections throughout in the manner of someone who had revised on paper rather than before printing. The subject was the timing anomaly — Vera writing to Dr. Wren not to ask a question but to respond to one. *Your calculation of the periodicity interval aligns with my own unpublished estimate,* Vera had written, *which suggests the question is no longer whether the pattern is real but what conditions produce it and over what timescale it can be expected to persist.*

T. Wren. The Edinburgh address was a hall of residence for visiting researchers, not a permanent department office.

Eleanor searched the university's staff directory archives. No T. Wren in Biological Oceanography for the period. A visiting researcher, then — someone passing through. The Edinburgh address for 1978 suggested they'd been there for at least part of that year.

She found, after another hour, a name in an old conference programme from a marine ecology meeting in Copenhagen, 1978: *Tobias Wren, Research Associate, SMRU, St Andrews.* A twenty-word abstract about benthic sampling along the Scottish coast. An address for correspondence that was different from the Edinburgh one.

SMRU was the Sea Mammal Research Unit. Not a chemistry program. Not oceanography. Wren had been working on marine mammals and had apparently been making the same observations as a marine chemist, a Norwegian coastal researcher, and a Finnish postdoc in Southampton.

That was the thing about the notebook's witnesses. They hadn't all been looking for the same thing. The anomaly had found them, because the anomaly was there.

---

She found a death notice for a Tobias Wren, age 34, in a Scottish newspaper from 1984.

She sat with that for a moment.

The 1979 notebook entry would have been one of his last, then. He would have made those careful measurements, written out the periodicity calculation, sent his findings to Vera — and then had five years. Five more years in which the work had no venue and then was over for him.

Vera had kept it. Of course she had.

Eleanor copied Tobias Wren's name into her notebook and added: *1949-1984. SMRU St Andrews. Three entries: 1977, 1978, 1979. The periodicity calculation. Vera: "the frequency is the argument."*

She thought about what it meant that the most precise observer in the notebook had died young. She thought about what it meant that she was sitting here now, in a cataloguing room in a university archive, forty-five years later, writing his name for the first time in decades.

Then she thought: there might be records. At SMRU. At St Andrews. Research notes, filed observations, correspondence with colleagues. The unit was still active.

She wrote, beneath Tobias Wren's name: *Contact SMRU.*

It was a longer list now than it had been a week ago. She did not mind that.

---

*End of Chapter Seven*
