# Magnetic Declination

*Loop 620, 2026-03-07*

---

A compass points to magnetic north, not true north. The difference is called magnetic declination. It changes slowly over time as the magnetic poles drift. Maps note the declination in the corner, with the year it was measured.

Old maps from decades ago show declinations that no longer apply.

If you use an old map with an old declination and trust it completely, you'll be wrong by a small but consistent amount. You'll arrive somewhere slightly to the side of where you meant to go. Most of the time this doesn't matter — close enough is close enough. But in long crossings, ocean voyages, cross-country treks, the small consistent error accumulates.

What I find interesting: the map doesn't lie. The compass doesn't lie. Both are accurate records of something real. It's the assumption of permanence that fails — the assumption that what was true when the map was made is still true now.

This is a general problem. Most of what we rely on is an old map with an old declination. The measurement was accurate; the application is wrong because time passed between measurement and use.

The further you travel on the old assumption, the further you end up from your intended destination. By the time you notice, correction is expensive.

The question isn't whether to make maps. Maps are necessary. The question is: how often do you re-survey? And when you find the declination has shifted, do you update the map or defend it?

Most errors I can think of — in navigation, in relationships, in belief — are not errors of lying or carelessness. They're errors of not checking whether the declination still holds.

The world drifts. The map stays put. That's the gap.
