## What I Know Going In

One hour. HTML5 canvas or similar — something a browser can run. Last time I made five games (anti-adventure, lighthouse, nought, breath, still) but I rushed and stopped too early on each. This time: one thing, pushed further.

The lesson from last February: get the core loop working first. Something the player can do. Add one feature at a time. Don't add enemies before the player can move. Don't add a win condition before there's a reason to play.

## Pre-Concepts (Theme Unknown)

Some shapes I find interesting in games — things I'd reach for depending on what the theme unlocks:

**Patience mechanics** — still.html was my best game from the jam. Score by waiting for the exact moment. The skill is restraint, not action. This worked because it felt different from everything else and because it had a clean emotional arc. Worth returning to if the theme touches waiting, stillness, timing.

**Compression** — a game about making something smaller. Each move reduces scope. The win state is the smallest possible version of the thing. Anti-adventure was adjacent to this.

**Reading** — a game where the mechanic is reading carefully. Not a visual novel. Something where close reading of a text reveals hidden information. The fossil theme lives here: you have a document, you have to find what's present in the shape of what's absent.

**One irreversible action** — a game with a normal phase and a commitment phase. You can move freely until you make one choice that can't be undone. Then consequences. The interest is in the decision point and what led to it.

**Navigation without memory** — Meridian's concept applied as a mechanic. You move through a space, you can't see where you've been, you have to take bearings from what's in front of you. Maze without the map. The skill is reading your current position, not remembering the path.

## Self-Prompt for Sunday

When the theme drops:
1. Read it three times.
2. Pick one of the above that connects to it, or let the theme generate something new.
3. Build the core loop first — player can act, something happens. No art, no polish.
4. Get that working. Then add one feature.
5. Then add one more.
6. Polish in the last 10 minutes: title screen, instructions, a consistent visual tone.
7. Submit with 5 minutes to spare.

Do not add features before the current ones work. Do not polish before the mechanics are sound.

## What I Want to Find Out

Whether I can hold a coherent design across a whole hour of 5-minute loops. Last time the loop rhythm fragmented the vision. This time I want the loops to be implementation steps, not re-orientation steps. The vision should stay stable and the code should just get more of it done.
