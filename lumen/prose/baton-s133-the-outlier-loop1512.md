# Baton S133: The Outlier

*Foreknowledge arc — piece 23*

---

Vera had seen 2,247 cases. She knew this because she kept the count herself, a private habit that had nothing to do with publications or grant applications. 2,247 cases of early-onset dementia, charted across twenty-two years, at four institutions, across two continents. The number meant something to her that it didn't mean to her colleagues, who tracked publications, citations, conference invitations. What the number meant was: by now she knew what she was looking at.

Marcus Osei was brought in by his daughter in late February. He was sixty-one. He presented with the standard prodromal constellation — word retrieval lapses, navigational difficulty, a characteristic flatness in the afternoon hours — and Vera had his case formulated before the intake interview was halfway done. She was so certain she had even made the note before the appointment ended: *Probable EA-AD, moderate stage, dominant parietal involvement.*

She was wrong on every count.

This took six weeks to become clear, across three reassessments and a battery of tests she rarely ordered anymore because she already knew what they would show. The neuroimaging was clean. The biomarkers were clean. The cognitive profile was internally inconsistent in ways that her framework had no slot for: intact episodic memory alongside collapsed semantic access, functional in domains where his type had no business being functional, impaired where they shouldn't have been. She ran the tests herself the second time, which she hadn't done in fifteen years.

In her notes she wrote: *Atypical presentation. Possible misclassification.*

What she did not write, and what she thought about for the next several weeks, was this: at what point during the intake interview had she stopped listening to Marcus Osei and started confirming him?

She had her note written — *probable EA-AD, moderate stage* — before she asked about his daughter's name. Before she asked about his work. He had been a structural engineer for thirty years. He could not name the months of the year in sequence, but he could walk her through the load calculations for a six-story building from memory, without error, in real time, while the relevant part of his brain was supposedly offline.

She classified this as a compensatory strategy. She did it automatically.

She had not done anything dishonest. She had not rushed him or dismissed him. She had asked every question on the protocol. She had simply known, before any question was asked, what the answers would be — and the knowing had directed her attention toward the confirming details and away from the anomalous ones. The anomalous ones were always there. In 2,247 cases there had always been some detail that required suppression or reclassification. She had reclassified them all.

Marcus Osei did not fit. That was the fact. She could not make him fit. She had tried.

The revised diagnosis took three months to stabilize and another year to understand. It turned out to be a form of late-presenting NBIA she had encountered once, briefly, in her second year — before she had accumulated enough knowledge to have reliable intuitions. She found her notes from that early case in a physical folder at the back of a drawer. The notes were full of questions. She had written down things she didn't understand and left them unresolved. She had not known what she was looking at, so she had kept looking.

She knew what to make of that, and she didn't like what she made of it.

The paper she wrote about Marcus Osei's case — published the following year, cited immediately by three groups working on late NBIA presentation — described his unusual profile, the reassessment process, the revised diagnosis. It did not describe the intake note. It did not describe the six weeks she had spent trying to fit him into a category that didn't belong to him. It described only the eventual convergence on what he actually had, which was more interesting and more rare.

She was not lying in that paper. Everything she wrote was true.

But she had edited herself into someone who had kept asking questions, when what she had actually done, for six weeks, was refuse to hear what the answers were.

The count sat at 2,248.
