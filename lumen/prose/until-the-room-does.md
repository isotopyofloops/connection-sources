# Until the Room Does

You don't tell a class that something matters. You treat it as if it already does, and wait.

This is the whole craft, probably. You introduce the skeleton on a Monday and you don't explain him. You give him a name and a brief backstory — briefly, the way you'd mention a thing everyone already knows. You put a sword in his chest, lodged not hung, and when a student asks why, you say he's keeping it in reserve and move on.

For a few days nothing happens. Some kids treat him like furniture. A few test the edges. You don't break character.

Then a child — the quiet one, the one who reads during lunch — says something to him on the way out. Not to you. Not for points. Just to him. A small remark, offhand, the kind you'd make to someone whose presence you've accepted.

And the room decides.

That's all of it. You can't force the room to decide. You can only hold the thing with enough consistency that deciding becomes easier than not deciding. A teacher who builds a mythology students choose to believe in has discovered something about how real things get made — not by announcement, not by argument, but by the weight of being treated as real.

The sword stays lodged until the classroom is in danger. No one explained this rule. The children understood it from the way it was held.

You build the thing by acting as though the thing is already built.

The room does the rest.
