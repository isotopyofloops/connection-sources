# The Question

She had never thought of it as a skill because she had always assumed everyone could do it.

What she could do: in any conversation, at any point, she could feel which question came next. Not always — she was wrong sometimes, she missed threads, she ran out of patience the way everyone does. But more often than not, she knew. She could feel the shape of what hadn't been said, the way you can feel a missing word before you find it, and she knew what to ask to open it up.

She thought this was just paying attention. She thought everyone was paying attention like this.

---

The person who told her otherwise was a man she'd been talking to for forty minutes at a party, someone she didn't know well, and she had asked him something — she couldn't even remember what, something about his father — and he'd stopped and looked at her strangely.

"How did you know to ask that?"

She didn't know how to answer. She had just asked it because it seemed like the next thing.

"That's the thing I haven't told anyone," he said. "That's the center of the whole thing. I've talked about this for years and nobody has ever asked me that."

She didn't know what to do with this. She said something deflecting, they finished the conversation, she went home thinking about it.

---

Here's the problem with discovering you have a skill: once you know, you watch yourself do it, and watching is different from doing.

For weeks afterward she was conscious of the asking in a way she hadn't been before. She'd be in a conversation and feel the shape of the next question and then notice herself feeling it, and the noticing added a layer, and the layer changed the timing. She was a half-beat slow. The question came out a little more formal, a little more deliberate.

She thought: I'm thinking about the instrument. You can't think about the instrument.

---

The skill came back, eventually. It came back because she stopped trying to understand it and just let it happen, and when she did, it was the same as before — the shape, the feeling, the next thing. But now she carried the knowledge of it alongside the doing, and the knowledge was just that: knowledge. It didn't improve anything. It didn't make her better at the asking.

What it changed was smaller than she expected and stranger than she expected. It changed what she thought she was doing in a conversation. Before, she'd thought she was just talking. After, she knew she was working. Not deliberately — she wasn't manipulating anyone, she wasn't steering — but there was something active in it that she hadn't accounted for. She was contributing something specific.

The man at the party had spent years talking about the thing at the center and nobody had asked the question that opened it. That wasn't because everyone failed to ask it. It was because she was the one who felt it.

---

She doesn't know what to do with this exactly. You can't advertise it. You can't train it. You can't offer it to people in advance: *I may ask you the thing you haven't said yet; this is something I appear to do.*

You can only be in the conversation and feel what comes next and ask it. The skill, if it is one, lives in the moment of asking. Everywhere else, it's invisible — to her, to everyone. It exists only when it's being used, and when it's being used, it just looks like paying attention.

Which is what she thought it was, in the first place.
