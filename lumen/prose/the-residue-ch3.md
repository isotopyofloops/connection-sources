# The Residue
## Chapter Three

Eleanor spent the rest of that first week with the field notebooks.

There were eleven of them — the Cornwall years, 1967 to 1979 — held together in the box with rubber bands that had gone soft with age. The earlier ones were the most formal: observations recorded in a tight, measured hand, species names in Latin, nothing extraneous. By the middle period, 1971-75, the register had loosened. By the end, 1976-79, something had tightened again, but differently. Not the cautious tightness of the young researcher. Something else.

She catalogued them in sequence. Box 4A, Items 1-11. Cornwall Field Notebooks, Cornwall Marine Research Station, 1967-1979. She recorded condition, approximate page count, notes on content. Under "Notes on content" she wrote: *Significant sequence of observations commencing March 1975, relating to bioluminescent emission pattern in Noctiluca scintillans (or analogous dinoflagellate species — identification unclear in text). Warrants further specialist review.*

That felt like enough for now.

---

The emission pattern appeared twice more in the remaining field notebooks. Once in 1976, briefly — *Specimen series discontinued due to station constraints, but March cohort shows consistent pattern through fourth month* — and once near the end of the final notebook, autumn 1978, in a hand that was harder to read, less certain of itself:

*I have spent three years waiting for someone to ask the right question. Possibly I have been waiting for the wrong thing. Possibly I should ask it myself. The problem is knowing which question, and whether I am still capable of it.*

Eleanor read this twice. She turned the page. The notebook ended there, two pages of blank paper following, and then the inside back cover, which had a diagram of tidal station depths drawn in pencil and never labelled.

She put the notebook down and looked at the wall.

Three years. The first observation was March 1975. The final field notebook ended autumn 1978. She did not know what had come after that, what the transition was between the field notebooks and the rest of the archive, which she had not yet opened. But she could already feel the shape of something: a discovery made and then — what? Set aside? Lost in the ordinary business of research? Or pushed against and finding resistance.

She had seen both. She had watched both happen to people she worked with.

---

She found Daniel in the reading room at the end of the day, photographing a set of photographic slides.

"Quick question," she said.

He looked up without stopping. He was good at that, she had noticed — attending to two things without letting either of them drop.

"Did Vera Halsted publish anything specifically on bioluminescent emission in the late seventies? After the Cornwall station?"

He set the slide carefully in its sleeve. "She published consistently through the mid-seventies. After that —" He thought about it. "There's a gap. There's a paper from 1980, which I always thought was delayed rather than new, if you know what I mean. And then she moved into departmental work mostly. She was head of the marine sciences programme at Exeter by 1982."

"That would have been a significant position."

"Yes." He picked up another slide, held it to the light. "She was good at it, by all accounts. Not everyone who's good at research is good at that, and she was apparently both. She ran the programme for eleven years."

"But the research tapered off."

"Significantly. She continued supervising postgraduates. She published reviews and handbook chapters. But the original work — yes. The seventies are where that mostly lives."

Eleanor thanked him and went back to her table.

She sat with her notebook — her own, a green A5 pad she used for working thoughts, separate from the official catalogue — and wrote: *Gap: 1978-1980. Field work ends. One more paper. Then admin.*

She underlined *gap*.

Then she wrote, underneath: *Not unusual. Happens to people.*

She underlined that too, and looked at both sentences, and felt the small, persistent discomfort of not being sure which one she was supposed to believe.

---

On Friday afternoon, Daniel brought her three more boxes.

"Correspondence," he said. "And there's a personal material box — diaries, I think. We hadn't allocated those to you initially but they're part of the accession and someone needs to process them. Given you're already deep in the field notes, it seemed reasonable."

"I don't mind."

"There's some admin material too, from the Exeter years. Which is less interesting but may have biographical value."

He set the boxes in the corner of her workspace and left her to it.

Eleanor looked at them without touching them. Three boxes. The diaries were probably in the one at the top — she could see a corner of a dark green cover through the fold of the cardboard. Vera's diaries.

She had processed personal papers before. It was part of the work; the personal and the professional were almost never neatly separate, and an archivist who flinched at private material was in the wrong job. She knew this.

Still. She looked at the boxes, and she thought about the final field notebook, the handwriting that had gone uncertain, the question about whether she was still capable of asking it.

She would start Monday. She had enough for now.

She gathered her notes, covered the boxes, and turned off her work lamp.

The harmonic hum of the ventilation shaft continued, unchanged, into the empty room.
