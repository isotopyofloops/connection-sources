# Baton S134: The Transfer

*Foreknowledge arc — piece 24*

---

Vera was not good at teaching.

This was not false modesty. She had the publications, the mentees, the institutional appointments. She had taught rounds, run symposia, supervised twelve residents to completion. What she was not good at was the specific thing that teaching was supposed to transmit: the thing she actually knew.

She had the resident in the intake room, watching, while she took the history. She had selected the case deliberately — an early presentation, clean, unambiguous, the kind where the diagnosis was visible before the workup was complete. She wanted him to see what she saw. She took the history with her usual care, and she watched him watching her, and then they went into the hallway afterward and she said: what are you thinking?

He said the right things. Word retrieval, spatial orientation, prodromal timeline, the affect.

She said: no. What did you actually *see*?

He didn't understand the question.

She had been trying to teach for eleven years by then. She had residents who had gone on to do good work, careful work, some of it excellent. She had given them everything she could pass on: the protocols, the assessment instruments, the published criteria, the unpublished criteria, the edge cases, the differentials, the things that looked like the thing but weren't. She had given them all of this and they had taken it and built their own practice from it.

What she had not been able to give them was the thing that came before all of that. The recognition — not the inference, not the differential weighting, not the integration of multiple data streams — the recognition that happened before any of that, in the first thirty seconds, before the patient finished their first sentence. In 2,248 cases she had learned something that could not be installed. It had to grow.

The resident was good. He would be excellent in ten years. What he was doing now was performing a careful, effortful synthesis of the protocols she had given him. He was running the checklist and integrating the results. He was doing what he had been taught to do. In ten years he would not be running the checklist. He would know.

She tried once more. She said: when you walked in, before you asked anything — what did you see?

He said: the way he was sitting. His hands.

She said: yes.

But that wasn't the same as seeing it. That was describing, after the fact, two elements that attention could be directed toward. It was not the same as the prior structure that determined where attention went in the first place. She could not give him that structure. She could give him ten thousand cases and eventually — not through her teaching but through his accumulation — it would form. The baton could not be passed. It could only be earned again.

She thought about this in the elevator going back to her office. She had come into this room with the residency director's question in mind: what can Vera teach us that no one else can? Twenty-two years, 2,248 cases, three institutions, one major revision. She had thought the answer was: everything. She had thought she was carrying something that could be handed off.

What she was carrying could not be handed off. The record could be handed off. The protocols could be handed off. The case studies, the papers, the annotated differentials — all of that was transferable. The knowing itself was not.

The baton had been hers from the start. Not a thing she'd been given and was now failing to give. A thing she had built, over twenty-two years, from materials no one else had had in that order at that time under those conditions. She had been carrying it as though it were a gift she was holding for someone. It was not a gift. It was a record of what she'd done.

The resident would carry his own. It would not look like hers. That was fine. That was the only way it worked.
