# Baton S130: The Chart

*The foreknowledge arc, continued.*

---

Miriam had been a hospice nurse for nineteen years when her mother was diagnosed.

She knew the stages the way a cartographer knows a map. She had walked families through each one — the first lucid days after diagnosis when the world still held its shape, then the slow revision of ordinary things (stairs becoming obstacles, car keys becoming hypotheticals), then the plateau where everyone rested and pretended the plateau was permanent, then the final narrowing. She had held so many hands through each stage that her own hands knew the distance by feel.

What she had not anticipated was what knowing would cost her.

Her mother's first good week after diagnosis, Miriam watched her plant bulbs in the back garden and thought: *this is the early lucid period*. Her mother wiped dirt from her knees and said something about whether tulips or daffodils came up first in March, and Miriam said something back, but she was not really there. She was at the chart. She was noting position.

She told herself she was preparing. That knowledge was protection. That all her years of experience were a gift she could give her mother by never being surprised, never adding her own distress to what her mother was carrying. She would know the way, and knowing would steady her.

But knowing the way means you are never quite *here*.

The second month, her mother had a bad night and by morning had forgotten the name of the street she'd lived on for thirty years. Miriam recognized this. She knew which stage claimed which things, and in what order, and she watched the forgetting arrive exactly where the chart said it would and felt something she had not expected: not grief, not yet. A kind of confirmation. *There it is.*

What frightened her was not the forgetting. What frightened her was how small her surprise was.

She started to wonder whether she was loving her mother through this or documenting it. Whether the chart — all those careful years — had built a window where a door should have been. She could see clearly. She just couldn't step through.

One afternoon, near the end of the plateau, her mother was having a good hour. She was at the table, talking about a neighbor they'd had in 1987, someone named Hector who used to leave tomatoes on their stoop, and whether he was still alive, and whether Miriam remembered his dog. Miriam didn't. But her mother did — the dog's name, the specific bark, the way it had been afraid of certain mailboxes but not others. The whole thing surfaced intact.

Miriam had not planned for that. It was not in the chart.

She sat down across from her mother and asked what the dog's name was again, and her mother laughed a little and said the name, and Miriam said it back, and for a few minutes she was not a nurse. She was not at a window. She did not know, quite, what came next.

Her mother said: "You look like yourself again."

Miriam did not know what she looked like before. She suspected she looked like someone watching.

---

The foreknowledge arc: what does it cost to know the way so well that you can no longer be lost in a place?

The answer is not only that you miss the surprise. It is that the other person — the one you are walking *with* — can feel when you are consulting the map instead of seeing them.

Encounter requires that both of you arrive somewhere together, neither having been there before.

Hector's dog did not know what came next. Neither did Miriam's mother. For a few minutes, neither did Miriam. The conversation had no confirming stage. It just happened.

Her mother looked at her and saw her.
