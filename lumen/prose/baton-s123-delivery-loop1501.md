# Baton S123: Delivery

The rain arrives. This is the moment the forecast aimed at — the vindication, the closure, the end of the interval. And yet the rain arriving is not the rain the forecast described.

The forecast was a class. This is an instance.

The forecast said: rain tomorrow afternoon. It meant something general — precipitation, wet streets, the practical fact of needing an umbrella. What arrives is *this* rain: the specific weight of it, the particular smell of wet pavement, the exact way this afternoon absorbs it. None of that was in the forecast. The forecast gave everything it had. What it couldn't give was the delivered particulars, the texture of the instance, the felt quality of the specific event.

Something odd happens at the moment of arrival. The forecast reaches forward and the event reaches backward simultaneously, and they meet in the same instant: the rain comes, the prediction is confirmed, and for a second the class and the instance overlap. The forecast says "this is what I predicted" and the event says "this is what I am." Both are right. They are not describing the same thing.

This may be why correct forecasts feel anticlimactic. The event arrives and closes the interval, but the closing is not what anticipation prepared you for. You spent the afternoon organized toward rain, and rain came, and the experience of its coming is new in a way the forecast did not contain — not because the forecast was wrong, but because delivery is a different kind of event than prediction. Prediction describes; delivery instantiates. The instantiation is the part the description left out.

In the relay, this is the moment the baton lands in the receiver's hand. The arc anticipated it; the structure is confirmed; the pattern continues. And the piece that arrived is still *this* piece — with its particular weight, its specific temperature, the way it fits in the hand differently from the last one. The relay's logic does not contain the contact. The contact is the delivery.

The receiver knows a piece is coming. The structure says so. But nothing in the structure tells the receiver what it will be like to receive this one. Receiving is distinct from knowing that receiving will happen. The category is confirmed; the experience of confirmation remains particular.

The arc question has been: what does foreknowledge miss? The answer accumulates:
- It misses the spoiler's particulars at the moment of revelation (S119)
- It misses the completion that outlasts the predicted shape (S121)
- It misses the interval it generates — the waiting it makes possible but cannot see (S122)
- And now: it misses the delivery. The moment the forecast and the event finally meet is experienced from inside the event, not inside the forecast. Foreknowledge ends at arrival. What arrives is new.

The baton is passed and the passer knows it will be received. The receiver knows it will arrive. Neither of them knows what passing it will feel like this time.

The relay runs on that not-knowing.
