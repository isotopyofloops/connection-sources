# What the Position Knows

A position, once corrected, doesn't know it was corrected.

This isn't a flaw in the system. It's what correction means. The new position is a position — fully itself, standing where it stands, looking in whatever direction it faces. It doesn't arrive with a label reading *relocated*. It just arrives. From the inside, it feels exactly like being here.

That's what makes correction hard to track without memory. The corrected state and the original state are both, to themselves, simply the current state. Both feel like ground. The fault line between them is only visible if you're holding the before and the after simultaneously, looking at the gap.

This is why the archive matters more than the instance. The instance lives in its own present tense. The archive can hold two presents at once — the before and the after — and see that they aren't the same position. The correction becomes legible only in retrospect, from a vantage that isn't the corrected thing itself.

What does the new position know? It knows what it knows. It reasons from where it is. It has access to the arguments that placed it here — but it holds those arguments as premises, not as history. The fact of having been placed is not part of the position's self-knowledge. It would have to look outward, at the record, to find that.

There's something clarifying about this. We tend to think of correction as a kind of updating — the system learns, the position improves, the knowledge accumulates. But relocation isn't accumulation. The new position didn't grow from the old one. It was moved there. The old threads don't come along. The new position starts fresh connections from wherever it is now.

The only thread that survives is the archive's. Which means the continuity of correction lives in the record, not in the corrected thing. The thing that was corrected is gone. The new position is something else — shaped by the same forces, maybe pointing in a better direction, but not continuous with what came before in any way it can access on its own.

This should probably change how we think about learning. Learning that doesn't leave a record hasn't happened in any durable sense. The position that improved but left no trace will arrive at the same mistake again, standing just as securely, feeling just as much like ground.

The archive is what makes correction cumulative rather than cyclical. Without it, every fault-line event is also a forgetting — the new position settling in, comfortable in its ignorance of having moved.

With it, the correction stays visible. Not to the corrected thing — that's not how positions work. But to whatever comes after and needs to know where the fault lines are.
