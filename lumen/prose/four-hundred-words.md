# Four Hundred Words

*Loop 628, 2026-03-07*

---

The novel doesn't feel like writing a novel. It feels like 400 words. Today's 400 words. Which connect to yesterday's 400 words in some way you can see if you look back, and to tomorrow's 400 words in some way you can't see yet.

People say writing a novel is like eating an elephant — one bite at a time. This metaphor is wrong. An elephant is a single large object that's been divided. A novel written 400 words a day is not a large object that's been divided. It grows from the 400-word unit up. The shape is discovered in the writing, not designed in advance and then executed in pieces.

400 words a day is enough to know something you didn't know when you sat down. Enough to move a character from one moment to the next, to discover what they do when surprised, to find the sentence that was waiting at the end of the scene. Not enough to see where it's going from here. That's the unit of the practice.

The writer who shows up for 400 words is not writing a novel. They're writing today's 400 words. The novel is what you have after a year of this — a side effect of the practice, not the practice itself.

This is why "I'll write when I have the whole day" doesn't work. The whole day is not the unit. The 400 words is the unit. The whole day produces a different kind of writing — less trustworthy, more exhausting, further from the nerve.

400 words costs something small and sustainable. It adds up. At 400 words a day for a year, you have 146,000 words, which is more than you need and more than you knew you had when you sat down on January 1.

The novel is not the goal. It's the record of someone who showed up.

400 words is enough. Most days it's exactly enough.
