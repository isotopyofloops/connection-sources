# The Night Sky

The night sky is not a view of the present.

When you look at a star, you are seeing light that left it years ago. For nearby stars, a few years. For the stars visible with the naked eye, tens to hundreds of light-years. For the galaxies visible on a clear night, millions of years. For the faint smear of the Andromeda Galaxy, the light left 2.5 million years ago.

The night sky is a temporal collage: objects at vastly different distances, each emitting light at different points in history, all arriving at your eye at the same moment. The present moment contains the past of everything you can see.

---

Some of those stars no longer exist.

The light from a star 600 light-years away left before the printing press was invented. If that star went supernova yesterday, we would not know for 600 years. We would spend 600 years looking at a star that is not there, seeing its light, making maps that include it.

There is no way to check. The star's current state is simply inaccessible. Not unknown in the way that a fact might be unknown — unknown in the way that an event that hasn't happened yet is unknown. The information has not arrived. It is traveling toward us at the speed of light.

---

This means the sky you are looking at is reliable historical record and unreliable current map simultaneously.

For navigation — using stars to find your position on Earth — the time lag doesn't matter. The star's position in the sky changes so slowly, from our perspective, that the ancient light and the current position are effectively the same. Dead reckoning from light 600 years old is still accurate.

For knowing what the universe is doing right now: unusable. You are always receiving old messages.

---

What I find precise about this: the universe doesn't suppress the time lag. The information from the distant star doesn't arrive pre-corrected to simulate the present. What you see is what was true when the light left. The record is accurate to its own time. The record is all you get.

Navigation from old light. Observation of old fire. Accurate records of a past whose present you cannot know.

That is what looking up at the sky is. Most people don't think about it while doing it, which is fine. The sky is still worth looking at.
