# The Residue — Chapter Ten

*Begin from the intervals.*

That was what Vera had written in the margin of her field notes, beside the August 9 entry. Not *begin from the data*. Not *begin from the sightings*. The intervals. The gaps between appearances — how long between one cluster and the next, whether the rhythm accelerated or collapsed, whether the silence between events was itself a kind of evidence.

Eleanor had been thinking about it for three days.

She was back in her flat in Edinburgh now, the archive boxes stacked against the wall she'd been using as a desk extension. She'd requested digital scans of the Halvard Skjell correspondence from the Bergen archive; they had arrived in a folder of greyscale PDFs, the paper slightly water-damaged in places, the handwriting upright and precise. Skjell had been an ichthyologist by training. He had not expected to be spending his fifties cataloguing seal appearances on a Norwegian island chain. He had said so in his third letter to Vera: *I did not plan this work. The work arrived.* Vera had underlined it.

Eleanor taped a sheet of paper to the wall. At the top she wrote: **1962 — first convergence (Vera's notes + Skjell's first record)**. At the bottom: **1989 — second convergence (verified, bilateral)**. Between them she made a line and began marking what she had. Individual sightings. Regional clusters. The long gap from 1974 to 1981 when both populations seemed to disappear — or seemed to stop being watched, which wasn't the same thing.

She stopped at that.

Vera had noted the gap too: *seven years without a reliable report from either site. But absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. It means only: no one was there to see.*

Eleanor looked at her timeline. The intervals weren't even. 1962 to 1974 was twelve years. 1974 to 1981 was the gap — seven years of silence. 1981 to 1989 was eight years of slow approach, the frequency increasing as the window neared. That wasn't a simple oscillation. It was more like — she searched for a word — *building*. Like something gathering itself toward a point.

She wrote **building** on the sheet, then crossed it out. Then wrote it again and left it.

---

Skjell had died in 2003. His daughter, Ingrid Skjell, was listed as the depositor of his papers to the Bergen Maritime Museum. Eleanor found a contact email through the museum's archive request page, composed a message in careful English (apologising that she didn't write Norwegian), and explained that she was a marine ecologist working on unusual seal behaviour patterns and had found her father's correspondence with Dr. Vera Halkett in the Caithness archive. She asked if there was anything further — field journals, photographs, any material from the 1989 season in particular.

She sent it and felt the particular quality of waiting that archival work created: the sense that the past was not fixed but porous, that a door might open somewhere in Bergen and something Vera had never seen might come through it.

While she waited, she went back to the intervals.

---

The thing about the intervals was that they implied a structure Vera hadn't named. Or hadn't named yet — Eleanor was still working through the 1985-1989 boxes, and Vera's thinking had clearly been accelerating in those final years before the window. But the structure was visible from outside in a way it might not have been from inside, in the middle of the data accumulation. From outside, with thirty years of hindsight and two populations to compare, Eleanor could see what Vera had only hypothesised:

The rhythm wasn't environmental. The coastal herring stock surveys for Caithness showed no corresponding twelve-year pattern. SST anomalies in the Pentland Firth didn't cluster around the same windows. Whatever was organising the appearances wasn't food availability or temperature gradient or any of the variables Eleanor would normally have reached for first.

Which left the biological. Which left the seals themselves.

Eleanor went to the shelf and pulled down a copy of Wren's 1972 paper — she'd ordered it through the university library, a physical photocopy that still smelled faintly of the original journal. Tobias Wren had worked in a different register: less data collection, more theoretical. He'd spent three years reading everything on cephalopod cognition, cetacean communication, and what he called, in one remarkable footnote, *the possibility that interval-patterned behaviour in large marine mammals constitutes a form of distributed social memory*. Not instinct. Not conditioning. Something that persisted across individuals, across generations, encoded not in a single animal but in the population's collective behaviour over time.

He hadn't used the word *culture*. It was 1972. But Eleanor read the footnote four times.

---

The reply from Ingrid Skjell came faster than she'd expected — two days later, in good English, slightly formal.

*Dear Dr. Fernie — yes, I remember your Dr. Halkett. My father spoke of her sometimes. He said she was one of the few people he had corresponded with who did not assume he was mistaken.*

Eleanor read that sentence and sat for a moment with it.

*I have here two boxes of his field material from the 1980s which were not included in the archive deposit. They were among his personal things when he died and I did not know what to do with them. If you would like to come to Bergen, I could show them to you. Or if it would be easier, I could scan the 1989 materials specifically. Please let me know what would be most useful.*

Eleanor wrote back the same afternoon. She said: both, if possible — whatever Ingrid was willing to share. But yes, she would come to Bergen. She would very much like to come to Bergen.

She booked flights while Ingrid was still typing her response.

---

On her wall, beside the timeline, she added a third vertical line: **Bergen, 1989 — Skjell's season journal (location TBD)**.

Then she sat and looked at what she had. Three field workers across forty years, two countries, one pattern. Vera had built the case in near-isolation, dying before the case became undeniable. Wren had theorised the mechanism from inside a framework too early for the field to receive it. Skjell had documented the Norwegian side alone, corresponding with one person who believed him, watching that person go silent after 1995.

And now the interval had ended. The next witness had arrived.

Eleanor looked at her wall for a long time. Then she uncapped her pen and wrote, below the timeline, below Bergen: **What does it cost a population to carry a pattern for thirty years?**

She didn't know the answer yet. But she knew it was the right question. Vera had known it too.

*Begin from the intervals.*

She was beginning.
