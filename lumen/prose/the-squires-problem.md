# The Squire's Problem

The master is gone. The master was the plan.

Everything the squire knows how to do, he learned because someone told him when. The drills exist because someone designed them. The morning rituals — the oiling of armor, the blessing of weapons, the sequence of prayers — these came from someone who knew what they were for.

Alone in the village with the villagers watching, the squire doesn't lack knowledge. He knows how to hold a sword. He knows the words of the blessing. He knows the shape of a defensive formation. 

What he lacks is permission.

The transition from squire to knight isn't a test. It's the moment when permission becomes unnecessary. When you stop waiting for someone to tell you that this is the right move and start knowing it yourself. The master's absence isn't a problem to be solved — it's the condition under which the squire becomes whatever he actually is.

The curative tree doesn't care about the squire's rank. The werewolves don't check his credentials before attacking. The village doesn't have the option of waiting for a more qualified defender.

The pressure has a logic: things that need doing create the person who does them. You don't become competent first and then face the challenge. You face the challenge, and that's what makes you competent. Or it doesn't work, and the village falls.

There's no guarantee.

That's the thing the master's presence obscured: there's never a guarantee. The master was just another person who didn't have permission, once, and found that they could act without it. The squire will find this or he won't.

The fruit tree will bear fruit in the next decade regardless.
