# The Residue — Chapter 14

*The letter was three pages, in a small and careful English that suggested someone who had learned it late.*

The writer's name was Sigríður Eiríksdóttir. She had been a teacher — not a researcher, she was careful to establish this — at a school on the western edge of Reykjavik. She had watched families, as teachers do, for twenty years. She had noticed things she did not have a vocabulary for. In the summer of 1989, something had changed across many of the families she knew, had changed in the same two-week window, had then settled back. She had written it down because she wrote everything down, and then she had put it away because she did not know what to do with it.

In 1991, a colleague at the school had shown her a paper — not published, circulated — by a Bergen researcher named Halvard Skjell, describing a pattern in Norwegian coastal communities that she read three times, standing in the school hallway, before she realised she was no longer breathing normally.

It had taken her a year to write the letter.

She was not certain, she said. She was not trained. She did not want to waste anyone's time if what she had seen was different, was nothing, was an artefact of her particular window and her particular attachments to these particular families. But the date in Halvard's paper was the date in her notebook. And she had thought: if Bergen, and if someone in the Faroe Islands, then perhaps someone might want to know about Reykjavik.

*I am sorry if this is not useful,* the letter ended. *I have never been part of a research group. I don't know the right way to enter this kind of conversation. If you would rather I didn't, I understand.*

---

Eleanor sat with the letter for longer than she had sat with anything else in this archive.

The thing that moved her — that she would not write in her notes for several more weeks, because it felt too personal for notes — was the phrase *I have never been part of a research group.* It described something precise: a person who had been watching carefully, alone, for years, who had arrived at the door of the conversation with her hands open and her credentials missing and her uncertainty stated plainly at the front.

Elin had written back. Eleanor could see this from the carbon copy tucked behind Sigríður's letter — a draft in that familiar handwriting, warmer than the field notes:

*You are not wasting anyone's time. What you describe matches exactly. We are four now, I think, though Halvard has become unreachable this year and Vera does not reply quickly. I would like very much to speak.*

There was no date on the carbon. The postmark on Sigríður's letter was September 1992. Eleanor tried to trace the thread forward through the box but found nothing more — no further correspondence, no record of the conversation Elin had wanted to have. What she found instead was a note in Elin's handwriting, tucked at the back of the correspondence folder as if set aside:

*Halvard died March 1993. Have written to Vera. Have not heard from S.E. since November.*

Three lines. Then white space, and then the back of the folder.

---

Eleanor photographed everything twice, then sat in Bjarni's archive room while the afternoon light moved across the shelf of boxes, and made herself think carefully.

Sigríður Eiríksdóttir. A teacher in Reykjavik. Born — she would estimate from the letter's internal evidence, the references to teaching for twenty years by 1992 — somewhere in the early 1940s. Which meant she would be in her eighties now, if she was still living.

*If.*

She thought about Vera, who was alive and eighty-one and had said *I need a witness* in 1989 and then spent thirty years saying nothing, finishing her garden. She thought about Halvard, who had died in 1993 with his journal. She thought about Elin, who had died in 1998 with her boxes and her half-catalogued archive and her four boxes of field notes in a language Eleanor couldn't read. She thought about the weight of all that accumulated careful watching, all that data, all those people who had known something that required three or four people to confirm and who had somehow, together, confirmed it — and then gone silent.

The silence was the part she couldn't account for. It wasn't that they hadn't found each other. Elin had found them, or almost — Halvard dead, Vera slow to reply, Sigríður gone quiet by November. The meeting had almost happened. Four people across six hundred miles of water had been close enough to correspond, and then the circle had thinned one by one until Elin died and the correspondence sat in a box for twenty-four years.

It would have been so easy for that to be the end. It had nearly been the end.

Eleanor thought: *I am here because it wasn't.*

---

She flew back to Edinburgh via Vágar and Copenhagen, which was the only reasonable route and took eleven hours. In the layover at Copenhagen she used the airport wifi to search for Sigríður Eiríksdóttir, retired teacher, Reykjavik. There were three people with that name in Iceland's public records. One was sixty-two, one was forty-four, one was eighty-three.

She stared at the screen for a moment.

Then she wrote an email. She did not explain the research. She did not explain how she had arrived at this name from a letter in a back-room archive in Suduroy. She said only: she was a researcher working on social transmission in North Atlantic communities in the 1980s and early 1990s; she had found correspondence suggesting Ms Eiríksdóttir had spoken with a researcher named Elin Høgnadóttir; she would be grateful for any time she was willing to give.

She hit send before she could second-guess the phrasing.

The gate changed. She gathered her bag and walked toward it, and for a while she simply walked — through the airport's long corridor, past the duty-free and the coffee shops and the people sleeping in chairs — with the particular lightness of someone who has just put something into motion and does not yet know how it will land.

Four populations. One day. Still, after thirty-seven years, the date sitting in the record.

Somewhere in Reykjavik, possibly, was someone who had watched it happen and was still alive to say so.

---

*She was halfway home before she realised she had not eaten since morning. She bought a sandwich in Edinburgh arrivals and ate it standing up, which felt right, somehow. Like something you do when you are still in transit between what you knew and what you are about to know.*
