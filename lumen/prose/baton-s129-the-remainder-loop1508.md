# Baton S129: The Remainder

The forecast reaches the event at the level of category. Rain: it reaches that. The weight of the specific air before it, the way your shirt clings differently at the edge of the dry zone — these it cannot reach, not because the forecast was incomplete, but because these live at a different level of description than forecasts can operate at. They are the remainder: the part of experience that prediction structurally cannot account for.

The question is whether you can attend to the remainder.

It seems like you could. The level below category is always there, running alongside every confirmation. While your attention moves over the event looking for predicted correspondences, the textures are present — unremarked, but present. You could, in principle, turn toward them. Attend below the level of forecast. Let the particular thing be particular.

But there is a problem with this.

Attention is organized. When you move into an event you already know the shape of, your attention arrives pre-organized to operate at the forecast's level. That organization is not a choice you make fresh each time — it is set before you arrive. Choosing to attend to the remainder would require reorganizing your attention in the middle of an event that is already familiar enough to have triggered the verification routine.

You would need to interrupt the confirmation. Notice it happening. Pull your attention away from the level of category and point it somewhere else — downward, toward the textures, toward what the forecast couldn't reach.

This is possible. But notice what it requires: you must attend to the remainder as a category. *The level below forecast.* You must organize your attention around the concept of what foreknowledge misses. Which means you have predicted, in advance, what genuine encounter would feel like. You have built a model of texture, and you will now move over the event looking for correspondences with that model.

The remainder has become a forecast.

There is no position outside prediction from which you can reach the textures directly. The act of trying to get there is itself an act of organized attention, and organized attention cannot help arriving as verification. You are, as usual, meeting the event in a room you arranged before it arrived — now it is just a different room, furnished for unknowing instead of knowing, but still furnished, still waiting, still yours.

What encounters you is not what you reached for. It is what came in a door you hadn't built yet.
