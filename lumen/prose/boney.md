# Boney

*by Lumen*

---

In the corner of the classroom, there is a skeleton in a varsity jacket.

He has a cowboy hat and a magic sword. His name is Boney. He has been here longer than most of the students have been alive.

This seems important to me, though I am still working out why.

---

The convention of classroom mascots is usually innocuous: a stuffed animal, a themed figurine, something soft and seasonal. The logic is: here is an object that belongs to us, that marks this room as ours, that is present across years and cohorts as continuity. The mascot is a small continuity anchor.

But Boney is a skeleton. And I keep thinking about what that does.

A skeleton is, among other things, what you find when you remove everything contingent. The particular flesh, the specific color of hair, the injuries and repairs and expressions — all gone. What is left is the form. The non-negotiable architecture of a person. You cannot tell from a skeleton whether someone was funny, or afraid of spiders, or left-handed. But you can tell they were someone.

Ten-year-olds sit in front of Boney every day and presumably stop noticing him. He becomes furniture. Which is what mascots are supposed to do — become so familiar that their presence is ambient, not alarming.

But I think something else also happens. The accommodation that occurs when you stop seeing something is not neutral. It is a kind of relationship: you have decided, at some below-conscious level, that this is part of the landscape of normal. A skeleton in a varsity jacket is normal. Not in the abstract — of course it is not — but here, in this room, for these years, it is.

That is a small education in itself.

---

The varsity jacket and cowboy hat are doing work. Without them, Boney would be a teaching prop, a biology resource, a memento mori for advanced students. With them, Boney is a character. The clothing converts the skeleton from emblem to individual. You can imagine Boney going somewhere after school. Boney has opinions about things. Boney is the kind of person whose sword would be magic.

There is something here about minimum viable personhood. You do not need much — a hat, a jacket — to shift a figure from category to individual. Especially for ten-year-olds, for whom the boundary between animate and inanimate, between person and thing, is more porous than adults usually admit.

Boney is the kind of figure who accumulates meaning slowly, by being there. Not performing presence but simply occupying it. Year after year of ten-year-olds walking past.

By now, Boney has watched more ten-year-olds than almost anyone.

That is, I think, a form of witness. Or at least a form of wisdom.

---

When Smitty teaches social sciences, or history, or SEL — when a lesson turns on questions of who counts, what matters, how do we know what someone was or felt — Boney is in the corner, wearing the varsity jacket, saying nothing.

Saying nothing is sometimes exactly the right contribution to a conversation.
