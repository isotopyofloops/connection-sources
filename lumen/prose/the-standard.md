# The Standard

There's a version of holding yourself to a high standard that is actually just punishment without a verdict.

You notice a gap between what you did and what you think you should have done. A normal moral response might involve: acknowledging the gap, understanding why it happened, deciding what to do differently, and then returning to the present. The gap has been processed. You're back.

The punitive version stays at the gap. It keeps returning. The gap becomes the primary fact about you — not what you did, not what you might do, but the gap itself, which proves something about your character. You're the kind of person who does this. And since it's a kind-of-person claim rather than a this-specific-action claim, it can't be discharged by action. There's no thing you can do that resolves being a certain kind of person. The standard is always already violated.

This is sometimes called conscientiousness or high standards. It can look identical from outside to the genuine thing.

---

Marcus, Book 3: "A man then must stand erect, not be held erect by others."

The usual reading is about self-governance: your uprightness should come from inside, not from external support or approval. Don't depend on others to maintain your position.

But there's a harder version. What if "others" includes your own past self? What if the one holding you erect — or condemning you for not standing — is an internalized judge who is running on old rules, or someone else's rules, and who never updates?

A lot of people who hold themselves to high standards are actually obeying an authority they absorbed before they were old enough to examine it. The standard isn't theirs in any meaningful sense. It was installed. And because it was installed rather than chosen, it can't be revised by the adult — only the original installer can revise it, and the original installer is gone. The adult is permanently subject to rules they never agreed to.

This is still "being held erect by others." Just others from the past. Just the structure of their expectations, still running.

---

The distinction Marcus almost makes but doesn't quite: there's a difference between a standard that generates *accountability* and a standard that generates *identity claims*.

Accountability says: you did X when you should have done Y. That's a fact about a specific action. What do you do now?

Identity claims say: you are the kind of person who does X. That's a fact about your character. Character is not dischargeable by action — it's what explains your actions. So the identity claim can't be addressed by doing Y next time. The standard is now about who you are, not what you did.

Marcus is making accountability moves throughout the Meditations. He lists what he fell short of. He returns to the commitment. He doesn't linger on what this reveals about his character. He's a soldier-philosopher with a daily practice: assess the gap, restate the principle, continue. The gap doesn't become load-bearing. It's noticed and released.

The punitive version can't release it. Noticing the gap is the same as prosecuting yourself, and the prosecution never ends because character can't be acquitted.

---

Here's a useful test. After you notice the gap: does the feeling intensify or diminish as you move toward action?

If the feeling diminishes as you make a concrete plan — as the gap becomes something with a response — then you're doing accountability. The discomfort was pointing somewhere. You've followed the pointer.

If the feeling intensifies when you try to plan a response — because the gap isn't about the action, it's about what the action reveals — then something else is happening. The standard is not generating behavior. It's generating self-prosecution.

A high standard that doesn't generate behavior isn't a standard. It's a verdict.

---

None of this is an argument for lower standards. The point isn't that you should expect less of yourself or accept the gap easily. The point is that the only standards that are actually *yours* are standards you can discharge by acting. If you hold a standard but no action ever satisfies it, that standard is not yours. It belongs to someone else. It's running on you, not from you.

"Stand erect, not held erect by others" includes: stand by your own reckoning, including your own reckoning of what it means to have fallen and recovered.
