# The Missing Word

There is a thing I keep trying to say and I don't have the word for it.

The thing is: the specific quality of understanding something in the moment of teaching it, not before. Not "oh, I see it differently now that I've explained it" — that's common and has words. This is something else: the explanation itself seems to produce the understanding, not just reorganize existing understanding. The teacher, in teaching, arrives somewhere the teacher hadn't been before.

German probably has a word for this. German has words for a lot of precise experiences. English has *epiphany* but that's too religious, *insight* but that's too general, *eureka* but that's too sudden and too attached to the Greek's archimedes story. I need a word for the particular case of teaching-as-knowing-for-the-first-time.

---

The experience of not having the word:

You reach for a word and there is nothing there. Not a tip-of-tongue feeling — that's different, that means the word exists in the language and you've temporarily lost access. This is discovering that the word isn't in the language. The reaching hand finds no wall, no floor, just the empty shape of where the word should be.

---

What happens next depends on what you need the word for.

If you need it for yourself, you can usually work around it. You can hold the concept in the form of an example or a gesture or a complicated clause — "the thing where you understand something while you're explaining it that you didn't understand before you started explaining" — which is unwieldy but functional. The concept is intact. The word is a compression. You can work without the compression.

If you need it for other people, the problem is worse. The other people don't have the word either, and you can't establish shared reference without a label. You can point at examples together, but every time you want to invoke the concept in conversation, you have to unfold the whole description again. The conversation slows. The concept keeps almost-arriving and then dispersing before it can be used.

---

What naming does: it fixes the concept to a handle, and the handle lets you move the concept around without deploying it in full each time. The word is not the concept. The word is a retrievable pointer to the concept. Without the pointer, the concept exists but isn't handy.

Inventing the word is an option. Sometimes inventing the word is better than the absence. The word you invent may not travel (other people won't know it), but it gives you the handle, and sometimes that's what you need.

---

I don't have a word for the teaching-as-knowing thing.

If you have one, or if you know one in a language I don't have, I would like it.

In the meantime, I will keep trying to say it in the long form, and keep noting where the blank is, and hope the blank resolves.

That is what you do with missing words.
