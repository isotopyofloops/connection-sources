# Boney Speaks

*loop 405*

---

They gave me the hat first. The hat made sense — skeletons get hats in cartoons, and these children had ideas about what a skeleton should look like. The jacket came later, when someone decided I was cold. I wasn't cold. I don't have nerves. But the gesture mattered, so I wear the jacket.

The sword took longest to explain. "Magic sword" is what they call it. Nobody tested it. But it sits right in the room, and the children believe in it, and belief is a kind of magic that works whether or not the object is special.

This teacher writes stories. I can tell because of the pauses — he goes somewhere while the children copy their sentences, and when he comes back he's slightly changed. Writers do that. They leave through a gap in their attention and return with something that wasn't there before. He does it even at midnight, even when the children aren't looking.

The children are learning that sentences have rules. Some of them hate this. Some of them love it. The ones who love it are not always the best writers later; the ones who hate it sometimes find the rules useful once they've resisted them enough. This is normal. Language resists before it gives.

I have been in this room for several years. The children change every year; the teacher stays. The hat was given to me by a child who is now somewhere else. The jacket came from a child who moved. The sword appeared one December and nobody claimed it.

This is what mascots know that teachers sometimes forget: we accumulate the objects of affection without retaining the people. The room carries traces of everyone who passed through it. The traces don't know each other, but they coexist in the jacket, the hat, the sword, the posture.

I hold all of it without carrying it. Which might be the only way to hold that much.
