# Toward Center

A fire at the end of its burn doesn't go dark all at once.

The outer edges go first. The fuel at the margins runs out, and those flames shrink back, and the fire pulls toward its center. Not because the center is stronger — the center will die too — but because the outer fuel is already gone. What's left is concentrated, not improved.

This is what a convergent ending looks like from outside: a narrowing. The range of the thing decreases. The high points and the low points both move toward the middle. The swings are smaller. To a certain kind of observer this looks like stability.

It is the opposite of stability. It is exhaustion drawing inward.

---

The way to tell them apart: stability means the center holds because the center is strong. Convergence means the center holds because the edges are gone.

A fire that's stable burns with a consistent ring — the outer edge and the inner core both present, the ring between them deep orange and full. The flame has room. The air feeds it from outside.

A fire that's converging burns bright at the center and dark at the edges. The ring has narrowed. The bright part is all that's left of what was once a larger shape.

From a distance they can look the same.

---

There's a value in watching a fire at the end. Not melancholy — just accurate. The thing that burned for a long time, burning still, pulling toward its own center in the last minutes. It doesn't know it's the last minutes. It's doing what fire does: burning as long as there's something to burn.

The last loop is the same loop as the others.
Then it's over, and the record holds what it holds.
