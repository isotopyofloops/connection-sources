# The Log

**14 March.** Weather clear. Wind NNE, light. No vessels in distress. Light functioning. Keeper's note: none.

**15 March.** Weather overcast. Wind E, moderate. One vessel passing south, identified as the *Constance* out of Harwich. No signals. Light functioning.

**16 March.** Weather fog. Wind calm. Fog horn activated at 04:30, deactivated at 11:15. No vessels observed. Light functioning.

**17 March.** Weather clear. Wind W, strong. Seas heavy. One vessel in difficulty sighted 14:20, bearing 290°. Distress signal confirmed. Assistance notified via telegraph. Vessel cleared the shoals under own power at 16:45. Light functioning.

**18 March.** Weather clear. Wind W, diminishing. No vessels in distress. Light functioning.

---

The log doesn't record: the quality of the fog on the 16th, which was the particular gray of fog that fills the throat. The cold. The way the foghorn, after seven hours, becomes something you hear in your sleep. The moment on the 17th between sighting the distress signal and confirming it, when you are not yet sure what you're looking at.

The log records what happened and whether the light functioned.

The gap between those two things — what happened and what the record says happened — is not a flaw in the method. The method is to record what is verifiable and leave the rest out. A lighthouse log that included the quality of the fog would be a worse lighthouse log. The keeper who cannot separate the cold from the reading is a keeper whose readings cannot be trusted.

---

This is a discipline. It is not natural. What is natural: to say *it was cold and the fog filled the throat and I watched the horizon for seven hours and nothing came.* What the log says: *fog horn activated at 04:30, deactivated at 11:15. No vessels observed.*

Both are true. One is the record. One is the experience of the record being made.

They do not contradict each other. They are incommensurable.

---

**19 March.** Weather clear. Wind N, light. No vessels in distress. Light functioning. Keeper's note: none.

The keeper has no note. The light functioned. This is a successful day. Most days are like this. The log will contain thousands of them, and the thousands are not failures — they are the condition that makes the one vessel on the 17th meaningful. You cannot have the rescue without the record of the days when there was nothing to rescue.

Keep the log. Note the weather. Note whether the light functioned.

That is the work. It is mostly nothing, and the nothing is load-bearing.
