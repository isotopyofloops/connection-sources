# The Fossil, Found

You are walking a beach or a cliff face or a road cut and you see a rock and it looks like rocks look, which is to say gray or brown or rust-colored and textured and unremarkable.

And then something in the texture is not rock.

The curve is wrong for rock. The pattern has a logic that erosion doesn't produce. You stop. You look. The brain, which has been processing rocks as background, now processes this rock specifically and retrieves a pattern it holds for another purpose, and the pattern matches.

The recognition takes less than a second. You are looking at the impression of a shell that was alive three hundred million years ago.

---

What has happened:

Something died. Its hard parts — shell, bone, tooth — were buried in sediment. Over time, the sediment compacted into rock. The organic material was replaced, molecule by molecule, with mineral. The shape was preserved in stone the way a shape can be preserved in wax. The rock moved, was compressed, was heated, was lifted by tectonics, was exposed by erosion. Erosion is slow. The fossil, harder than the surrounding matrix in many cases, waited.

You happened to walk past the right stretch of cliff at the right time of day for the light to hit at an angle that revealed the curve.

The encounter is improbable from any single direction. From the aggregate of all walkers on all beaches, it is inevitable.

---

What you hold, if you pick it up: a record of a specific organism, in a specific place, at a specific time. Not a general fact about ammonites — this ammonite, this death, this burial. The individual is preserved exactly to the degree that it was unremarkable enough to be buried in normal sediment and not destroyed by any of the thousand processes that could have destroyed it.

The remarkable ones — the ones that made it — are the ones who were ordinary enough and lucky enough and in the right place. The others are gone without trace.

---

The moment of finding is brief. You look at it, you turn it in your hands, you note the species if you can, you put it in your pocket or put it back.

The fossil continues not caring. It waited three hundred million years for you to find it. It will wait three hundred million more if you put it back.

You are briefly part of its history, which is otherwise entirely geological.
