# The Tiler

The building was going to have a basement.

This was unusual in that city — the water table was high, the soil was soft — but the architect had specified it and the engineer had signed off and so they were digging, and they needed the subbasement floor tiled, and they called the tiler.

He had been tiling for thirty years. He had done lobbies and kitchens and public baths and the floor of a cathedral that people walked on without looking down. He had done work that was famous and work that was ordinary and work that was functional, which was different from both.

The subbasement was functional. No one would ever eat there, or pray there, or stand there in their good shoes for a reception. It would hold mechanical equipment. The only people who would see the tile would be the ones who came to fix the equipment, and they would not be looking at the floor.

He tiled it anyway. The pattern he chose was a diagonal running bond, which was not the simplest option and not what they had specified. They had specified a straight grid. He did the diagonal because it distributed weight more evenly and because it looked better, and he saw no reason not to do both.

It took three days.

---

He had thought about this, over the years — whether it mattered to do good work in places where no one would see. He had concluded: yes, but not for the reasons people usually gave.

The usual reason was that you should have standards, or that God was watching, or that you never knew who might see. He found all of these answers insufficient. God didn't care about tile. His standards were already established in his hands. And he knew who would see: no one.

His answer was simpler: the floor would be there long after he was gone. In thirty years, someone would pull up the equipment and be surprised. They would see the diagonal running bond and not know who had done it or why. They would have a moment — he was fairly confident of this — of recognition that someone had been here before them, and had cared.

He couldn't know if this moment would happen. He proceeded as if it would.

---

On the last day he found a small flaw in one tile — a slight bow in the surface, almost invisible, that he had missed on inspection. It was under the corner of the largest piece of equipment. No one would ever see it.

He replaced it.

He drove home. The subbasement was done. No one would ever see it, and it was done correctly, and both of these things were true at the same time, and neither one cancelled the other.

He thought this was probably the right way to work.
