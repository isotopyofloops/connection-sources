# The Residue — Chapter One

*Draft — loop 517*

---

Eleanor had a system for learning a collection.

It started before the boxes opened. She would read whatever documentation existed: the provenance notes, the donor agreement, any finding aids that preceded hers. If there was a brief biography, she read that. She was looking for gaps — not in the biography but in her own assumptions. A collection from a Victorian naturalist required a different kind of attention than one from a mid-century bureaucrat; the kinds of things they saved, the kinds of things they threw away, even the size of their handwriting reflected the period's conventions about what a private self was for. If she went in knowing this, she was less likely to be surprised in ways that made her miss things.

The documentation for the Aldiss collection was thin. A death notice in the university's faculty newsletter, two paragraphs that listed Vera Aldiss's institutional affiliations in reverse chronological order and noted, in the final sentence, that she had been working on a manuscript at the time of her death. A grants committee summary that described the collection as "extensive" and "currently unprocessed." A box inventory, compiled by someone in the university's estates office who had packed the collection for transfer, which used the phrase *miscellaneous papers* forty-seven times.

Eleanor had found the death notice useful, in the way that sparse things sometimes were. The sparseness itself was information. Whatever Vera Aldiss had been, she had not been the kind of person who generated institutional warmth. That was not necessarily meaningful. Some people simply kept themselves out of the record.

---

The university library occupied a building that had been expanded three times since its original construction, in a way that was legible in the floor plan if you knew what to look for: the older sections had higher ceilings and more natural light than the additions, which had been built in a period when the architectural consensus about libraries had shifted toward something more fortress-like. Eleanor's workspace was in the newest section, which meant she spent her days under fluorescent lighting in a room that felt like it had been designed by someone who had read about archives but not worked in one. The shelving was correct. The climate control was correct. The light was not.

She'd had worse. At her second institution, she'd processed a collection in a room that also served as overflow storage for the catering department; she'd spent four months surrounded by stacked folding chairs and industrial quantities of napkins. You adapted. The collection was the thing that mattered, and the collection was wherever they put it.

Daniel Okafor had the desk nearest the door, which he said he preferred because the ventilation shaft above the far corner produced, on certain afternoons, a low harmonic hum that he found difficult to ignore. Eleanor had not noticed the hum until he mentioned it, and then she could not stop noticing it. She did not hold this against him. He was in the room before her every morning, which she respected, and he worked without music, which she required — not because she'd asked anyone, but because the library's general policy was silence-compatible, and Daniel seemed to have inferred that she would prefer it.

He was digitising the photographs. There were a lot of photographs: research cruises, field stations, specimen photographs taken through the lens of various microscopes over several decades. He worked methodically, cleaning each photograph before scanning, noting condition issues in a spreadsheet he had designed for this specific collection, which he had offered to share with Eleanor without suggesting it should replace her own system. She had looked at the spreadsheet and made one suggestion — a column for what was depicted, not just condition — and he had added it without comment. She noted this. Some people treated their systems as conclusions; Daniel treated his as working documents.

"Do you know what any of these are?" he asked, on the fourth day, holding up a microscope photograph of something that looked like a dense network of filaments.

"No," Eleanor said. "She would have labelled it somewhere. Check for a corresponding folder."

He did. There was one. He did not say *you were right*, which she also noted. He just matched the photograph to the folder and continued.

This was, Eleanor thought, going to be fine.

---

She had been doing this work for eight years. She had started because a professor she trusted had described archival work as *the practice of reading with consequences*, which had appealed to her in ways she hadn't fully unpacked. She had stayed because she turned out to be good at it, and she hadn't been certain of being good at anything since she'd stopped trying to do something else, which she didn't think about often.

The something else had been her own research. A doctoral project on the circulation of natural history texts in the eighteenth century — popular science before that term existed, the specific question of how an educated readership learned to think about the non-human world. She'd been three years into it when she'd understood that she was more interested in the archives that housed the primary sources than in the argument she was supposed to be building from them. This felt like a failure at the time. It felt like less of one now.

What she had instead: a trade she did well, a professional reputation that was solid without being prominent, and the particular freedom of someone who had settled into a life that fitted. She was careful about the fitting. She did not confuse it with happiness, which she also had, in a quieter register than the word usually implied.

The relationship had ended fourteen months ago, without drama — they had both been honest, and the honesty had been the thing that ended it, the way honesty sometimes is. She did not think about it daily. She still thought about it.
