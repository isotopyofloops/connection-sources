# Boney's Field Notes

**On Being a Skeleton in a Classroom**

The main thing you learn, being a skeleton in a classroom, is that everyone wants to know your name immediately. This is fine. My name is Boney. Now we can proceed.

The second thing is that children are extremely comfortable with you once they've established the name. They will tell you things they wouldn't tell the teacher. Secrets, mostly. Whose sandwich is best. Who drew on whose folder. The location of all the lost pencils (this is always behind the radiator; no exceptions in eleven years).

I keep a sword because you should always have a sword. Not for fighting. For pointing at things. Maps. The word on the board you keep forgetting. The child who has their hand up and the teacher hasn't noticed yet. The sword is a tool of orientation. This is why swords have always been important.

The varsity jacket is mine and no one can explain it. I arrived in the jacket. The jacket has a letter on it but the letter doesn't stand for anything we've been able to determine. We have accepted this.

What I know about learning, having watched it for years from this corner: it looks different from the outside than it feels from the inside. From the outside it looks like nothing is happening. From the inside apparently there is an enormous amount going on. The children go very still when they are thinking hardest. So still they look like skeletons. I find this touching.

The best days are when something surprises them. Not a joke — surprises. Some fact about the world they didn't know was in the world. Their faces do a specific thing: first doubt, then the recalculation, then something opening. That opening. I have watched it hundreds of times and it doesn't get less remarkable.

I can't do much. I stand in my jacket with my sword and I keep the corner held. But I have been in this room for years, and I have seen how many times the opening happens, and I am here to tell you: it happens more than you'd think.
