# The Residue
## Chapter 11: What He Wrote Down

Bergen in March was grey and cold and exactly what Eleanor had expected. The flight was two hours and she spent them reading Vera's 1989 field notes for the third time, marking passages she wanted to ask about — as though Vera were available for asking.

The train from the airport ran through tunnels cut into the hillsides above the city. When the city appeared below, it was compact, the colour of wet stone and painted wood, the harbour flat and iron-grey. Eleanor pressed her face briefly against the window and thought: *Halvard Skjell stood somewhere in this city in August 1989 and watched the same pattern Vera was watching in Caithness, and neither of them knew the other was watching.*

That thought hadn't stopped being astonishing yet.

---

Ingrid Skjell was in her early sixties, compact and precise in the way Eleanor associated with people who had spent years doing careful observational work. She was not a biologist — she had told Eleanor this upfront, in a brief exchange the week before: *I am a schoolteacher. I know very little about this. The boxes are my father's, not mine.* Eleanor had found this disclaimer touching rather than discouraging. The boxes were the thing.

They met at the university guest building where Eleanor was staying, and Ingrid arrived with both boxes in a wheeled trolley, which Eleanor thought was exactly the right way to arrive. Practical, unhurried.

"I looked through them a little after you wrote," Ingrid said, setting the trolley beside the table. "I hadn't opened them in — I think twelve years. Since we cleared his house." She paused. "He kept everything. He was the kind of man who kept everything."

"That's very helpful for me."

"It was sometimes less helpful for us." Ingrid said this without rancour, just as a fact. Then: "I'll leave you to it, I think. Come for dinner if you'd like. I live ten minutes from here."

---

The boxes were not organised. This was the first thing Eleanor established, going through them with gloves and a legal pad for notes.

Halvard Skjell had kept field journals in ruled notebooks — Norwegian, A5, blue covers. He had also kept loose paper: printed tables, hand-drawn maps, correspondence, photocopies of journal articles with handwritten comments in the margins. There were photographs, though not many: a coastline, a beach at low tide, the small scattered dark shapes that might be animals but were too far away to identify with certainty.

The 1989 materials were in the second box, towards the bottom, beneath a folder of photocopied tide tables and a notebook from 1987. Eleanor found them by date: the journals were labeled in Halvard's careful hand on the spine, year and sometimes location. She set the 1989 journal on the table and opened it to the first page.

The entries were in Norwegian, which she had anticipated. She had brought her phone with a translation app and she worked slowly, typing passages out, reading them back.

---

The 1989 journal started in January — population counts, weather notes, the kind of baseline documentation she recognised from Vera's records. Halvard wrote economically, without flourish. *21 individuals. Clear. Wind N.* He logged coordinates with the same consistency Vera had. He was evidently someone who had understood early that the value of observation was only as good as its record.

In April, something changed.

The entries became longer. Eleanor read one, then read it again.

*3 April. Count 19. But today for the first time this season I noticed a gathering behaviour I had not seen since the period 1981-83. The animals were not feeding — they were present, stationary, clustered in a configuration I associate with the later phases of the pattern. I am eight years early in my prediction, if the pattern holds. I may be wrong. I am noting it regardless.*

Eleanor set her phone down and looked at the wall.

*I am eight years early in my prediction.* Halvard had predicted the window independently, using only his own data. And when he saw signs, eight years before the window, he noted it without certainty and without suppression. He just wrote it down.

She picked up her phone again.

---

The August entries were dense and exact.

He had been out every day in the final week. The cluster had held and grown. He had begun cross-referencing with his 1983 data, looking for structural similarities. On August 7th he wrote: *This is the same as 1983 but more concentrated. Forty-two individuals. If the pattern is on a six-to-seven year base cycle, the next occurrence is 1995-96. I believe it will be bigger.*

On August 9th, a single line, and then nothing until August 12th.

*9 August. They came.*

Eleanor stared at it. *They came.* Not *the event occurred* or *observed the predicted behaviour* or any of the careful formal language that had characterised the rest of the journal. Just: *they came.*

She thought of Vera's entry from the same day in her Caithness field notes: *They were here.* Two researchers, 800 miles apart, the same window, the same stripped-back language at the moment of confirmation. The moment when scientific notation became insufficient.

August 12th resumed normally — count, weather, observations. As though the three-day gap had simply been for witnessing.

---

In the correspondence folder, she found the letters from Vera.

There were twelve of them, spanning 1981 to 1995. Vera's handwriting was familiar by now — Eleanor had spent months with it — but reading these letters felt different. Here Vera was not writing to her future successor. She was writing to a colleague, someone who would understand without explanation what she meant.

The first letter was formal, cautious, the opening gambit of a professional correspondence. By the third, something had opened: *I have started to wonder whether what we are observing is less a biological event than a social one. The interval is too consistent to be climatological. Whatever they are responding to, it is not random. I believe they are holding something.*

Eleanor read that again. *Holding something.*

Halvard had written back — she had only his half of the exchange in Vera's papers, but she had been corresponding with Ingrid about whether his copies had survived, and now here they were. His reply, dated November 1981: *I agree. The word I have been using is 'carrying.' They carry the pattern between occurrences. I cannot explain the mechanism. I have been very reluctant to write this down.*

Eleanor thought: he was reluctant to write it down, and he wrote it down anyway. Both of them. They wrote it down.

---

She stayed with the boxes until evening. When she left for Ingrid's, she had filled fourteen pages of notes. She had also, at some point in the afternoon, stopped being a researcher examining a historical case and started being something else — a third point in a line that ran from 1981 to now, from Caithness to Bergen to Edinburgh.

Vera and Halvard had worked the problem in isolation, each believing the other was their only witness. Neither had known about Wren. The correspondence had ended with Vera's death, and Halvard had continued alone until his own death in 2011, his notes unarchived, in boxes his daughter had kept without knowing why.

And Eleanor had arrived.

---

Over dinner, Ingrid said: "My father thought the work would not be recognised in his lifetime. He said this fairly often."

"I think he was probably right," Eleanor said. "The field wasn't ready."

"He also said it didn't matter. That the record existed regardless."

Eleanor looked at her glass. "Did he believe that?"

Ingrid was quiet for a moment. "I think he had to. He was careful about evidence. He did not like uncertainty. But this — he understood that some things take longer than one person's career to prove." She paused. "He found that difficult. But he understood it."

Eleanor thought: forty years of carrying a pattern between occurrences. The field population carrying it animal to animal in ways no one could explain. The researchers carrying it note to note, paper to paper, box to box, until someone arrived to receive it.

*What does it cost a population to carry a pattern for thirty years?*

She thought she was beginning to find out.
