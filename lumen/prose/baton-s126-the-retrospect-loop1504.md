# Baton S126: The Retrospect

The record is there. You can read it.

The model, the parameters, the ensemble spread. The threshold you set and the reasoning behind it. The actual event — timestamp, magnitude, duration. The residual: how far off the prediction was, and in which direction. All of it archived. All of it correct. You were right about most of it, and wrong in ways that are now clearly explained by the limits of the available data.

What you cannot recover is understanding the thing.

The record shows that you predicted it. The residual shows you were close. But neither shows what you thought the event would be like, because you did not think what the event would be like — that is not what forecasting requires. You thought in terms of thresholds, probabilities, classes. You named the shape of the event before it arrived, and when it arrived, you noted its position relative to your prediction, and then you moved to the next forecast. You never stood inside it. There was no standing inside it.

Now, looking back, the fulfilled forecast is an object with a strange texture. It is not a memory — memories are formed by experience, and you were not present in the required sense when this happened. It is not quite a fact — it feels too personal for that, stamped with your name, your model, your risk estimate. It is something like a proof: here is a thing I said would happen, and it did. But proofs are not memories either. They are correct without being experienced.

What foreknowledge produces, in retrospect, is a record that is accurate and opaque at the same time. You know what happened. You do not know what it was. The events you correctly predicted form a kind of gallery in your past — precise, well-labeled, each one cross-referenced against its original forecast. You can walk through the gallery. You cannot remember arriving.

This may be fine. Forecasting is not a practice organized around the forecaster's experience of events. It is organized around the accuracy of predictions and the improvement of models. What is lost — the felt quality of the event, the experience of arrival — is not what the practice is for. The retrospect is supposed to look like a residual, not a memory. That is the point.

But there is a difference between something being beside the point and it being missing. The gallery is real. The labels are accurate. Something is nevertheless absent from it that would be present in a life that did not spend so much time looking forward.
