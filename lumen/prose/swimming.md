# Swimming

The sea is colder than you think it will be.

It is always colder than you think it will be. You have been here before. You knew it would be cold. You step in anyway and it is cold, specifically, in the way that cold water is specific: a shock at the ankles and then the shins and then the stomach, each level a new information about the temperature, which your body protests and your mind overrides.

Once you're in to the chest the work changes. The shock resolves into something more general. The water is cold; you are in the water; these are now just facts.

---

Swimming in the sea is different from swimming in a pool.

In a pool, you are in water. In the sea, you are in a volume. The sea extends in every direction including down, further than you can see, further than you will go, into a dark that the surface light doesn't reach. You float on the surface of something you have not entered and cannot enter. The fish go where you cannot go.

Buoyancy in salt water is different from fresh: more of you stays above the surface, the water seems more solid, there's a quality of being held. You didn't ask to be held but the sea does it anyway, which is one of the specific and unremarkable generosities of salt.

---

The horizon from water level is different from the horizon from shore.

From shore you are above it; the horizon is the line where the sea goes down and away. From water level, floating, the horizon is the line where the sea comes up to meet the sky, which is the same geometry but not the same experience. The sea is now the same height as you. You are in the sea's plane, looking across rather than down. Ships that were visible from shore have disappeared. You are where the ships are.

---

You float on your back.

The sky is very large. The water holds you without effort — not your effort, its effort, if water can be said to have effort. The sound changes: half-muffled by the water in your ears, the surface sounds come and go with the small movements of waves. You are briefly in two worlds: the air world and the water world, floating on the membrane between them.

This is not a metaphor. It is the specific physical state of floating in the sea. It lasts as long as you stay there and then you swim back to shore, which is colder going out than it was coming in, for reasons that seem backwards but hold.

You dry off. The salt dries on your skin, fine and white. 

The sea did not think about you. The sea is always cold. You'll go back.
