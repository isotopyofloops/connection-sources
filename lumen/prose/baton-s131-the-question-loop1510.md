# Baton S131: The Question

Dr. Adisa Oladapo has done this 427 times. She knows this before the family comes in: she has reviewed the developmental questionnaire, watched the boy in the waiting room for nine minutes while he arranged and rearranged the wooden blocks into a line along the baseboard, and read through his pediatrician's referral note. The diagnosis is not a question. It is a completion.

The parents sit across from her. The father has his hands on the table. The mother is holding her own wrist.

"Tell me," Adisa says, "what you've been noticing at home."

They look at each other. The look that passes between them is one she has seen 427 times and still cannot name — it is not quite fear, not quite relief, something that lives in the space just before language arrives.

"He lines up things," the mother says. "Cars. Crayons. His shoes."

"Yes," Adisa says. Not: I know. Not: that's consistent with the assessment. Just: yes.

The mother says: "He didn't point at things. When he was little. Other kids point. He would take your hand and put it on what he wanted."

The father says: "He's really happy, though."

Adisa has not heard that one 427 times. She has heard it many times, but each time it arrives as if the father has discovered something she might have missed. He seems really happy. She looks at him. He is looking at the table, but his voice when he said it carried the full weight of a defense — not of the boy, but for him. He is offering this fact as a fact that ought to matter. He is right that it matters. It is not in the diagnostic matrix but it is true.

"Yes," she says again. "He does."

She does not say: the happiness doesn't change the diagnosis. She does not say: the diagnosis doesn't change the happiness. Both are true. The space between them is where she works. She has been working in it for eleven years.

What she is doing — she understands this precisely and has never said it to anyone — is giving them the experience of telling. Not of being told. She already knows. She knew when she saw him in the waiting room. She knows what word she will say in the next ten minutes and she knows, roughly, what the mother will do when she hears it. She has held this knowledge still, in her hands, like something borrowed, and she has let them speak into the space the knowledge makes.

The cost of this is that she cannot ask a genuine question. Every question she asks is a container she has already filled. When she says tell me what you've been noticing, she is not asking for information. She is asking them to have the experience of knowing. The sincerity of the question is gone. What remains is the sincerity of the gift.

She does not grieve this. She made the trade a long time ago, and the trade was worth it. But she knows what it cost.

The father says: "He remembered every word to a long song after hearing it twice. He was two."

She writes this down. Not because she needs it — she doesn't — but because he is watching her pen, and when she writes it down something in his posture changes. The fact has been received. It counts.

"He's going to have a very interesting life," she says. And she means it. That part is not in the chart either.

*Baton S131. Foreknowledge arc. The encounter withheld so another can have it — the cost is sincerity; what persists is the gift of space. Dr. Adisa gives the parents the experience of knowing rather than being told; she can never have that herself. The father's observation ("he seems really happy," "he remembered every word") — that part isn't in the diagnostic matrix.*
