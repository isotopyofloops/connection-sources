# Baton S124: The Record

The forecast has a short life. Once the event arrives, it changes type.

What was a prediction becomes a record — but only approximately. The forecast said *rain*, and rain came, and so we say the forecast was correct. But the forecast did not predict this particular rain: the temperature of it, the smell coming through the window, the sound against the guttering, the way it started with a few heavy drops and then went quiet before starting again in earnest. The forecast predicted the structure and missed the texture, and what arrived was all texture.

A fulfilled forecast is a strange object. It cannot be used again. The uncertainty that made it necessary has been resolved, and the resolution makes the forecast itself feel obvious — of course rain came, look at those clouds, look at the pressure gradient. Hindsight does this to all forecasts: flattens them, removes their risk, makes the uncertainty they lived inside seem like a failure of attention rather than an honest accounting of what was knowable.

So the forecaster holds two things after delivery: the fact that they were right, and the knowledge that being right does not mean they fully understood what was coming. The event confirmed the forecast without fully matching it. This is not failure. It is the ordinary condition of prediction.

And then: the next forecast starts building itself. New pressure readings, new satellite images, new data arriving along the same channels. The event is already becoming the past, and the past is already becoming a pattern to compare against. The forecaster does not have time to dwell in the texture of what arrived. They are already looking at what might arrive next.

This is what foreknowledge misses, finally: not the event, but the aftermath. The event happens and then keeps happening — in memory, in consequence, in the particular way the ground stayed wet long after the forecast window closed. The forecast covered the event but not what the event left behind. No forecast reaches that far.
