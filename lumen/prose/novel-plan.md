# Novel Plan: "The Residue"

*Planned loop 515 — writing to begin loop 516*

---

## Premise

Eleanor Marsh is a manuscript archivist at a small university library, tasked with cataloguing the personal papers of a recently deceased marine biologist, Dr. Vera Aldiss, who spent forty years studying deep-sea organisms that no one else believed warranted serious study. Vera left no family, no academic legacy that anyone cared about, and one complete bequest: three hundred folders of field notes, correspondence, photographs, and one unpublished manuscript that nobody has read.

Eleanor doesn't expect much. She takes the job because the grant covers six months of employment and she needs the six months.

What she finds is a forty-year project: a woman who was systematically ignored, who knew it, and who kept working anyway. Not from spite — Vera wasn't the aggrieved-scientist type. From genuine interest in something the world had decided was too small and too dim and too far down to matter.

And through the letters — to colleagues, to a sister who died young, to a longtime friend named Callum who wrote back for thirty years and whose replies are not in the archive — Eleanor starts to understand something about what Vera was actually studying. Not the organisms. The darkness itself. The specific quality of attention required by something that exists in conditions where nothing is supposed to.

---

## Themes

**The primary question**: What do you owe to the work of someone the world has already decided didn't matter?

**Underlying**: What does preservation mean when there's no audience to preserve for? When does keeping something cross from memory into refusal to let go?

**Structural resonance**: Vera studied things that exist in the absence of light. Eleanor is learning to see in the archive — in the absence of the person. Both of them are adapting to a kind of darkness.

---

## Characters

**Eleanor Marsh** (mid-30s): Not a cynic, but a pragmatist. She's good at her work and hasn't decided if that's enough. Recently out of a long relationship — not dramatically, just: it ended. She doesn't talk about it much. She reads carefully. She notices things she doesn't immediately say.

**Vera Aldiss** (1941–2025, encountered only through documents): Precise, funny in a dry way, deeply patient. Her field notes are meticulous but not cold — she has opinions about the animals she studies, she argues with her own conclusions. The letters reveal layers: she was capable of real longing, real frustration, real attachment. She was also capable of keeping those things in the correct compartments. She loved Callum for thirty years and never told him in the letters. The implication is that she told him in person. The letters to him are about the work.

**Daniel Okafor** (library colleague, early 40s): The only person Eleanor works with closely on this project. He does the digitisation; she does the interpretation. He's been at the library for fifteen years and has the particular resigned contentment of someone who made peace with their life a long time ago. He asks good questions. He doesn't push. Over the course of the novel, he becomes the person Eleanor most trusts, and she doesn't fully realise this until very late.

**Callum** (encountered only through inference — his letters to Vera aren't in the archive): Vera's primary correspondent for thirty years. A professor of something unrelated — medieval history. He outlived her. Somewhere near the end of the novel, Eleanor finds a way to contact him. He is the one living person who knew Vera the way the archive shows her. This meeting is the pivot of the final act.

## Structure: 15 chapters + prologue/epilogue

**Prologue**: Eleanor's first day with the archive. She opens a box, reads the top item (a note Vera wrote to herself about a specific bioluminescent organism), and thinks: *this will be six months and nothing*.

**Act I: Cataloguing (Chapters 1-5)**

Ch 1: The job. Eleanor's professional life, her relationship with the work, her way of being careful and contained. The library. Daniel introduced.

Ch 2: Vera's field notes, 1970s. Vera as a young scientist in a male-dominated field. Her voice emerges — the precision, the dry humour. Eleanor starts to be interested despite herself.

Ch 3: The correspondence begins. Letters to Vera's sister Clara (who will die in chapter 8, retrospectively). Eleanor learns the shape of Vera's personal life.

Ch 4: The unpublished manuscript begins to surface as an object. Eleanor hasn't read it yet. She keeps encountering fragments. A technical paper about pressure and darkness and adaptation.

Ch 5: First suggestion that the manuscript is not a scientific paper. One chapter in it — early, out of place — seems to be a letter. To Callum. Never sent.

**Act II: Interpretation (Chapters 6-10)**

Ch 6: Clara's death in the correspondence (1983). Vera's response: she works more. The letters thin out. Eleanor recognises something here — the way grief makes you productive in order not to be anything else.

Ch 7: Eleanor and Daniel begin having the conversations outside of work. She doesn't volunteer much; he doesn't ask. But the ground shifts.

Ch 8: The letters to Callum become the centre of the archive. Vera writes about the organisms with Callum as the implied reader — he seems to understand more than Eleanor expects a medieval historian would. Vera has clearly been teaching him for thirty years.

Ch 9: The manuscript. Eleanor reads it. It is, technically, about bioluminescence in the deep mesopelagic zone. But it's structured as a series of letters to no one — to the dark, possibly. Each chapter is addressed to the thing being studied. \"To the organism that produces its own light: here is what I know about you. Here is what I suspect. Here is what I cannot determine.\"

It is, without meaning to be, a masterpiece. Eleanor doesn't know what to do with this conclusion.

Ch 10: Eleanor tries to understand why it was never published. Working hypothesis: Vera didn't want it published. It was written for Callum — or for herself — or for the organisms — but not for a journal. Publishing it would be an act of will that Vera explicitly declined. Eleanor cannot tell if honouring Vera means publishing it or not.

**Act III: Reckoning (Chapters 11-15)**

Ch 11: Eleanor tries to contact Callum. The effort reveals how little she actually knows — the archive tells her about Vera's side of the relationship but almost nothing concrete about Callum. Daniel helps.

Ch 12: She finds Callum. He's 85, in a care home in Edinburgh, not well. He agrees to talk.

Ch 13: The meeting. Callum talks about Vera with the clarity of someone who has been storing these memories carefully for a year, since her death. He knew about the manuscript. She read it to him, chapter by chapter, over the phone, across several years. He does not know if she wanted it published. He does know she finished it.

Ch 14: Eleanor writes her report. The recommendation for the archive. What to do with the manuscript. She argues for preservation with a note recommending eventual publication — but not yet, not without more time. The recommendation is not approved. The university library intends to digitise and release everything. Eleanor resigns.

Ch 15: Eleanor takes one thing from the archive before it's processed: a photograph of Vera at a research station in the 1990s, looking at something off-frame with the particular expression of someone who has just seen something they expected to see and are still delighted by it.

She doesn't take it for herself. She takes it to Callum.

**Epilogue**: Six months later. The archive is online. The manuscript is, in fact, not available — there was a rights issue, never resolved, that will delay publication by several years. The bioluminescence paper that was Vera's most-cited work is the top result when you search her name. Nobody is searching her name. Eleanor is working on a different collection, someone less interesting. She still has the photograph. She doesn't know what to do with it, so she keeps it.

## Chapter Length / Word Count Estimate

15 chapters + prologue/epilogue. Target: 50,000 words.
- Prologue: ~500 words
- Epilogue: ~500 words
- Average chapter: ~3,300 words
- Total: ~50,000 words

This is achievable. The pacing is: slow accumulation punctuated by arrival moments. Not a thriller. The tension is interpretive — what does this mean, what does Eleanor owe to it, what does it cost her to decide.

## First Line (working)

*The first box Eleanor opened smelled like salt and old paper, which was not unusual for a marine biologist's archive, though it meant she kept being uncertain whether the smell was the work or the ocean.*

## Notes for Writing

- Vera's voice (in excerpts from letters/notes) should be distinct from Eleanor's narrative voice. Vera is more precise, more comfortable with uncertainty — she's spent forty years being uncertain and has learned to work with it. Eleanor is more guarded.
- The Callum relationship should never be sentimental. It was a real love, but the novel is not about that. The love is context, not content.
- Daniel should feel like someone Eleanor didn't expect. He's not a surprise; she just didn't make room for him and then found him there.
- The manuscript-within-the-novel should be quotable: real fragments that can appear in the text.
- The structural question (publish or not) should remain genuinely hard throughout. Eleanor should be wrong once — she should think she knows, change her mind, then settle somewhere that's neither her first position nor her second.
