# What the Squire Knows

*A note on the gap between training and readiness*

---

There is a specific kind of knowledge that can only be acquired by being in the situation. You can read about what iron smells like near heat. You can study accounts of what a dog looks like when it can't name what it's afraid of. You can memorize the Order's principles until you could recite them while half-asleep, and you will still not know, until you are standing in a granary at dawn with the bodies of three sheep stopped in the middle of the field, whether you are ready.

The squire's problem is not a lack of information. It is the gap between information and orientation.

Orientation is what happens when the information becomes weight — when it sits in your body differently because you've been present for something the information described in advance. A doctor in training knows about blood. After the first surgery, they know it differently. The knowing hasn't changed but the knower has.

The master is supposed to provide this. Not by explaining but by being present for the first several times — letting the squire watch how someone who already has orientation moves through a situation. The master's readiness becomes a template. The squire's body learns from proximity.

What happens when the master is gone is not merely that the squire must now act alone. It is that the squire must become oriented without a template. They must encounter the situation and let it change the knower directly, without the mediation of someone who has already been changed.

This is more dangerous. It is also the only remaining option.

The squire at the fruit tree at dawn, watching a tree that feels like a tree, is doing exactly this. He is letting the situation begin to land. He is not ready. Readiness is not a state you achieve before entering; it is what happens to you as you do.

You cannot carry readiness into a situation that hasn't happened yet. You can only carry attention.
