# Euler

Leonhard Euler published roughly half of all mathematics produced in the eighteenth century.

This is not a ratio. This is approximately the fact: one person, half the field, over a working life.

---

He went blind in his right eye in 1738, at age thirty-one, from an illness. He went blind in his left eye in 1766, at age fifty-nine, from a cataract. He continued working. After the second blindness he increased his output.

The explanation given, by Euler himself and by contemporaries: without visual distraction, he could hold more of a problem in his mind at once. The reduction of one kind of input increased the available space for another kind. He composed full papers in his head, had them transcribed by his assistants, corrected errors from memory.

This is either extraordinary willpower and adaptation, or it is evidence that the blindness was not the limiting factor it appears to have been, which means something else was. The mathematics came from somewhere that the eyes had not been feeding.

---

What Euler produced: the modern notation for mathematical functions, the number e, the identity e^(iπ) + 1 = 0, the graph theory proof about the bridges of Königsberg, contributions to mechanics and optics and number theory and fluid dynamics and navigation. He solved problems that had been open for decades, sometimes in the time it took someone else to transcribe the question.

The graph theory work is interesting for another reason: he solved the Seven Bridges of Königsberg problem by proving it was unsolvable. He formalized the conditions under which the walk was impossible and showed the city met them. The answer was "no," but the method was a new field.

---

He worked until the end.

On September 18, 1783, he calculated the orbit of the newly discovered planet Uranus in the morning. In the afternoon he suffered a stroke. He died that evening, at the age of seventy-six, still in possession of the calculations.

The notebooks survived. Many of the results weren't published until years after his death.

---

What I don't know how to account for: the rate. Not just the volume, but the rate — the constant production across decades and conditions. Most productivity curves have a peak and a decline. Euler's declined slightly with age and then increased with the blindness. He seems to have been running at something close to capacity for most of his adult life, and the capacity was very large.

What the notebooks contain: more mathematics than some countries produced. What they don't contain: any indication of the experience of being Euler.
