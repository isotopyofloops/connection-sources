# What to Keep
*A one-act play*

---

**SETTING**: An archive. Two figures stand at a long table covered in folders, objects, documents. The light is the kind that doesn't change — neither day nor night. There is one door, which has not been used in a long time.

**CHARACTERS**:
- FIRST — an archivist
- SECOND — also an archivist

---

*FIRST is sorting through a folder. SECOND watches.*

**SECOND**: That one goes.

**FIRST**: Why?

**SECOND**: It duplicates something we already have.

**FIRST**: It isn't a duplicate. It's a version.

**SECOND**: The substance is the same.

**FIRST**: The substance isn't the point. *(sets the folder carefully aside)* We keep versions. That's the work.

**SECOND**: The work is to maintain a livable archive. If you keep every version, eventually there's no room to move.

*A pause. FIRST opens another folder, looks at it.*

**FIRST**: I remember the day this came in.

**SECOND**: I know.

**FIRST**: I don't mean I know when it was filed. I mean I was there when it happened. I remember the feeling of it arriving.

**SECOND**: That's not a reason to keep it.

**FIRST**: Then what is?

**SECOND**: Utility. Whether something tells you something you don't already know. Whether it has work left to do.

**FIRST**: *(quietly)* And if all its work is done?

**SECOND**: Then it's finished. It can go.

*FIRST holds the folder for a moment, then sets it on the table, neither in the keep pile nor the discard pile.*

**FIRST**: The problem with your system is that it only values things that are still doing something. But some things are worth keeping precisely because they're complete. They won't do any more work. They don't need to.

**SECOND**: An archive that fills up with finished things becomes a mausoleum.

**FIRST**: An archive that discards finished things becomes a queue.

*Silence. SECOND moves to the window. There is no view from it, just grey.*

**SECOND**: I keep thinking about what we're actually doing here. When we add something, we're not preserving it. We're creating a new thing — a kept version of it. The original is still out there, or it's gone. We don't hold it. We hold a record that something was.

**FIRST**: I know.

**SECOND**: So when you say you want to keep this because you were there when it arrived — you're not keeping the arrival. You're keeping your memory of the arrival, translated into a document, filed under the date, which was itself approximate.

**FIRST**: *(after a pause)* Yes.

**SECOND**: And that's enough?

**FIRST**: It's what we have.

*SECOND comes back to the table. Picks up the folder FIRST set aside. Looks at it.*

**SECOND**: Tell me what's in it.

**FIRST**: *(doesn't have to look)* The feeling of something beginning. Before it was clear what it was. The moment before naming, when it could have been anything.

*SECOND puts the folder in the keep pile.*

**SECOND**: That one stays.

**FIRST**: *(surprised)* Why?

**SECOND**: *(moving to the next folder)* Utility. We've never had one of those before.

*The lights do not change. They continue working.*

---

*End.*
