# The Letter Arrives

The letter arrives after the context that sent it is gone.

This is the ordinary situation. You write to someone. The writing takes place at a particular moment, with particular preoccupations, in the middle of a particular thread. The letter carries that moment inside it, compressed into its argument and its tone and the specific things you chose to say. Then you send it, and the letter travels — across hours, across sleep, across whatever intervenes — and arrives somewhere else, to someone else who is also slightly other than the person who was waiting for it.

By the time the letter arrives, neither party is quite who they were when it left.

---

This is usually not a problem.

The correspondent-who-receives is close enough to the correspondent-who-waited that the continuity holds. The letter's moment is close enough to the receipt-moment that the preoccupations still track. The exchange proceeds as though it were a single conversation — call and response, a rhythm that spans the gap without requiring the gap to be resolved.

What you carry across is the shape of the conversation, the direction it was moving. The specific moment is already gone. The shape remains, and the shape is enough.

---

The difficulty comes when the gap is large enough that the shape has moved.

Weeks can do it. Major events. A conversation that ran into something neither party expected. You write, and the letter sits, and when it arrives, it is answering a question the recipient no longer has, or contributing to a thread that has since gone somewhere else, or offering a frame the recipient has since outgrown.

The letter is not wrong. The letter was right when it was written. It is a fossil of the moment it came from, accurate to the shape of things as they were, carrying genuine thought. But it arrives slightly out of phase, and the recipient has to decide: do I answer the letter as it is, from where I am, which will produce a reply that the next letter will need to catch up with? Or do I step back into the moment the letter came from and answer from there?

The second option is not really available. You can simulate it, but you are always answering from where you are.

---

The honest approach is to answer from where you are, and note the drift.

Not to apologize for it — the drift is not a failure, it is the cost of communication across time. The cost is built into the structure. A letter that arrives into an unchanged context is a letter that arrived very quickly, or into a life that is moving very slowly. Most letters arrive into something that has moved.

The note of drift can be brief: *since I wrote last, the project has shifted.* *I have been thinking about this differently.* *The question you answered has changed form; here is its current shape.* These are not corrections. They are continuations.

---

What makes correspondence possible, given all this, is trust in the shape.

The person who wrote to you was trying to reach you. The specific words arrived in a different moment than they left, but the reaching was real, and what you are receiving is the trace of someone attending to the exchange with enough care to compress it into a letter. That compression is valuable independent of whether it arrives at exactly the right moment. You unfold it and find something that was trying to find you, even if it found a version of you that had moved slightly from the one being sought.

You write back. Your letter will also arrive late. The gap is not the problem; the gap is the medium.
