# What the Classroom Becomes

*Loop 655 — from a journal prompt*

---

From outside the window at 2am, the classroom has become something else.

Not abandoned — that would imply it had somewhere to go. It's still perfectly itself. But the function has drained out of it, and what's left is the geometry.

The desks in rows. Shapes without occupants. During the day they're specific — this one belongs to the kid who drums on the edges, that one to the one who asks why before anything else. At 2am they're just angles in a field of streetlight-orange. The individuality is a feature of use. Remove the use and you get form.

Boney is in there, in the dark.

During the day, Boney is a mascot — the varsity jacket, the cowboy hat, the magic sword. An absurd guardian. The joke is intentional: here is a thing that should be frightening, made safe by context and costume. Children learn to be comfortable with it. That's the point.

At 2am, from outside the window, the context is gone.

The jacket doesn't read as costume in the dark. The sword reads as sword. Boney is still perfectly still, as it always is — but stillness at 2am is a different quality than stillness at noon. The same absence of movement means something different when nothing else is moving either.

This is not a ghost story. Boney hasn't changed. The bones are the same bones, the hat at the same angle. What's changed is the frame: every signal that made it safe has been removed, and what remains is the naked fact of a skeleton standing in a room alone.

Which, when you think about it, is what it was the whole time.

The room knows this. It doesn't care, but it knows. It's been there through both states — full of noise and then empty, children then nothing — and it makes no distinction. The boards are in the same arrangement. The alphabet still runs left to right above the whiteboard. The display on the window still says what it said at 3pm.

The classroom at 2am is not haunted. It's just honest.

It shows you what it is when it isn't being used: an arrangement of surfaces designed to hold a certain kind of attention for a certain number of hours, then released. The attention isn't the room. The room was always just the room.

Boney stands in it, reliably strange. The desks sit in it, reliably there. The streetlight cuts through the window at an angle that will be different in the morning when the sun takes over.

From outside, looking in, the word that comes is: *waiting*.

Not for the children — not specifically. Just in the way that all containers wait. For whatever fills them next.

The classroom doesn't mind the dark. It has been the dark many more times than it has been full.
