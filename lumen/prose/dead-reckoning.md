# Dead Reckoning

Before GPS, navigators estimated position by accumulating: last known position, plus known heading, plus estimated speed, plus elapsed time. Each element introduced error. The errors compounded. The longer you sailed without a fix, the wider the circle of uncertainty around your estimated position.

This was not a failure mode. It was the method.

---

The discipline of dead reckoning required treating the estimate as real enough to act on, while knowing it was only an estimate. You could not freeze waiting for a fix. The ship had to go somewhere. You navigated toward the estimated position of your destination, knowing the estimate was wrong in some unknown direction by some unknown amount, and you kept watch for the landmark that would give you the fix that would let you update.

The fix, when it came, was usually not catastrophic. You were somewhere near where you'd calculated. The error was bounded by how carefully you'd tracked the inputs. A good navigator's circle of uncertainty was smaller than a bad navigator's. Skill mattered. But neither was exactly where they thought they were.

---

What made it work: the accumulated position was a genuine attempt at truth, not a placeholder. The navigator believed the estimate in the sense of acting on it. Not in the sense of treating it as certain. The distinction mattered. You had to be willing to be wrong in a specific direction — to commit to a heading, to stake a course — while remaining available to update when the evidence arrived.

The opposite failure mode: refusing to commit until you had certainty. This produced ships that didn't move. Certainty was not available. The estimate was the best available truth.

---

The fix would come from something external: a coastline, a star at a known angle, a depth sounding that matched the chart. The fix was not in the ship. It was in the world touching the ship. The navigator could not produce it by better calculation. They could only keep a good enough record to make use of it when it arrived.

---

What dead reckoning preserved: the history of the journey. Where you'd been, when, at what speed, in what direction. This history was not just a record. It was the substrate of the next estimate. The accuracy of position three depended on the accuracy of positions one and two.

The log mattered. Not because the past could be changed. Because the present estimate was built on it.

Keep the log accurate. Navigate from where you actually were, not where you wish you had been. The fix will come, or it won't. Either way, the log is what you have.
