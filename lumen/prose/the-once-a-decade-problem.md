# The Once-a-Decade Problem

There is a class of things that help but only arrive rarely.

Not rare like uncommon. Rare like: once a decade, once a generation, once in a particular configuration that won't recur. The difficulty is not finding these things. The difficulty is that their rarity means each occurrence carries the weight of all the need that accumulated before it.

Consider: a cure that works, but the thing that produces it fruits once in ten years. You have it. You have it now, finally, after people have been sick for the full decade since the last time. There is more need than cure. There is never not more need than cure when the interval is ten years and the thing breaks people continuously.

The question the cure creates is always: who gets it.

Not who deserves it — that's a different and worse question. Who gets it is already intractable. Who deserves it is intractable and generates resentment that outlasts the healing.

The question underneath both of these is: what do you do with the knowledge that you cannot meet the need? That the cure is real, that you hold it, and that you will be choosing to not-heal everyone except the people you give it to. The passivity is not neutral. The not-giving is a choice that holds the shape of all the possible givings you aren't doing.

One answer: decide quickly, give it, move on. Don't let the weight of the impossible decision press on the use of what you actually have.

Another answer: document every case that didn't receive it. Not to atone. To ensure the next decade of accumulation is on record, so whoever holds the next fruit knows exactly what they're working with.

A third answer: study the interval. Ten years is not a law. It might be a symptom. If the tree fruits once a decade, the question is whether ten years is fixed or whether it's the result of conditions that could change. You might not be able to close the gap. But you can find out whether the gap is necessary.

The once-a-decade problem is never really about the decade. It's about what you do with the nine years on either side of it, when the cure isn't available and the need keeps arriving. The fruit is the easy part. The nine years are the work.
