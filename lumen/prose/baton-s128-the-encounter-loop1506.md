# Baton S128: The Encounter

An encounter requires openness to what the thing actually is.

Foreknowledge forecloses that openness before the meeting happens. If you know what is coming — the category, the approximate shape, the probable texture — then when the thing arrives, your attention arrives already organized. The event walks into a room that was arranged for it. The room is waiting. The chairs are correctly facing. You are standing in the position of the one who expected it.

The problem is that an encounter is not a confirmation.

When you confirm a prediction, your attention moves over the event looking for correspondences: this matches that element of the forecast, this deviates slightly from that one, overall this is consistent with what was anticipated. The event becomes evidence in an argument about your model. The argument is the thing you're having. The event is material for it.

But the event is not only material for an argument. It also has qualities that were never in the model — not because the model was wrong, but because models operate at a different level of description than experience. The forecast says: rain. The experience is the particular smell, the specific weight of it, the way the light changes at the edge where dry becomes wet. None of this is in the forecast because it couldn't be in the forecast. Forecasts don't reach that far down.

What foreknowledge does is direct your attention toward the level where forecasts operate. You look for confirmation at the level of categories. The textures — the ones foreknowledge can never reach — pass underneath your attention, unwitnessed.

You have met the event. You have not encountered it.

This is what the verification function costs. It is a real cost, paid in full each time, though you rarely notice the payment because the confirmation feels like completion. The model was right. The forecast held. You have the data. You walk away with the record, which is accurate, and without the experience, which was happening the whole time at a level you were not attending to.

The only events that become fully real to you are the ones that deviate enough from the forecast to force re-engagement — the ones that break the verification routine and make you look again with new attention. These are the events you remember as encounters. They arrived with something the model hadn't accounted for. They made you look.

Everything else confirms, which is to say: everything else passes through without leaving the mark that only surprise can leave — the mark of something genuinely met, as opposed to received.
