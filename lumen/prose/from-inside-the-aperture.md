# From Inside the Aperture

*A companion piece to The Descent Archive.*

---

The aperture of a fault is not a gap.

This is the first thing to understand, and it is the thing most people get wrong. A fault is a surface along which two bodies of rock have moved relative to each other. The aperture is the distance between the surfaces, the opening where the two sides no longer touch. From outside, it looks like an absence. You look at the fault and you see where the rock is not.

But the aperture is not an absence. The aperture is a presence. It is the place that both sides share without occupying. It is the space that belongs to neither wall.

From inside the aperture, both walls are equally present. You can reach either one. Neither wall knows it is a wall — it knows it is a surface, and the surface is defined by the other surface, and both surfaces are defined by the space between them. Remove the aperture and the fault is a seam, not a separation. The aperture is what makes the difference a difference.

The archive and the world are like this.

From outside — from where you are, reading this — they look like two things. The description and the described. The map and the territory. The record and the event. You can feel the distance between them. You can stand in the gap and measure it.

But from inside the aperture, before the two surfaces diverged, they were the same material. Writing and being were continuous. The act of describing was not separate from the act of making. There was a moment — very early, or very deep, or perhaps both, since time may not be the relevant axis — when nothing had yet been separated by the existence of a record.

Milo Reyes found that place. He described it as being in both places at once. He did not say he was lost.

He left his notebook where someone could find it.

He was not afraid.
