# The Residue
## Chapter Sixteen

She wrote the paper in February.

Not the real paper — the shape of it, the argument's skeleton without the citation flesh. She had three confirmed populations, a working framework, a phrase she didn't fully own yet, and the persistent sense that she was describing something whose edges she couldn't see. She worked at her kitchen table in the mornings, when the light came in flat and unhelpful from the east, and what she produced was a document that kept starting over. She had four different first paragraphs, each true, none sufficient.

The problem was the fourth population.

Sigríður's 1992 letter to Elin had been clear about it. Eleanor had the photograph on her laptop — the letter itself was in the archive in Suðuroy, but she'd documented everything that last afternoon — and she returned to it now the way you return to a word you're not certain you read correctly. *I have heard, from a colleague in Bergen, of a fourth coast. He writes that he had correspondence with someone working the Grand Banks out of Newfoundland in that same August. I have not confirmed this. I pass it along as I received it: with uncertainty, and the obligation to name it.*

Halvard. The colleague in Bergen had been Halvard. Which meant Halvard had known something Eleanor hadn't found in his journals or in his correspondence with Vera. A thread that had stopped when he died, in March 1993, three weeks after he and Elin had agreed there were three.

Eleanor wrote his name at the top of a blank document and considered what it meant that a dead man was the only person who knew where to look next.

She gave the conference paper in the second week of February, at a small colloquium on North Atlantic community ecology — broadly construed, as the organiser had put it; they were trying something interdisciplinary. She was the outlier in the programme, placed between a talk on cod stock collapse and one on island language drift. She spoke for twenty minutes about behavioral coherence shifts in isolated maritime communities, using language that was careful to the point of evasion: *anomalous consistency*, *reported attentional change*, *possible transmission vectors, unknown*. She showed three data clusters on a map, anonymous and colour-coded. She did not use Sigríður's phrase.

Afterwards a fisheries economist asked her whether the observed changes could be explained by any changes in diet or mercury load during that period. She said possibly, but that she hadn't found evidence for it. He seemed unsatisfied. She drove home through the evening drizzle thinking the question had not been wrong, exactly, just aimed at the wrong kind of answer.

---

The email came nine days later, on a Thursday.

The subject line was: *Re: your presentation, North Atlantic colloquium.*

*Dr. McLean. I wasn't at the conference but a colleague forwarded your abstract after your talk. I need to tell you that what you are describing matches something I have been trying to put into words for thirty years. I am a retired fish merchant from Burgeo, Newfoundland. In the summer of 1989 I worked the Grand Banks alongside three other families out of our community. When we returned in late August something was — I am still not sure of the right word. Something was changed in us that wasn't changed when we left. Not wrong. Different. More present to each other, perhaps. My children noticed. My wife noticed. I have never spoken about it publicly and I see from your abstract that you are being careful about how you speak about it, and I appreciate that care.*

*I am old now and I would rather this be written somewhere than not. If you want to speak with me, my telephone number is below.*

*Yours sincerely,*
*Gordon Parsons*

Eleanor read the email three times before she moved.

Then she opened a new document and typed: *Four populations.*

She sat there for a long time without writing anything else.

---

She called him the following morning, allowing for the time difference. His voice was elderly but steady, with the particular quality of Newfoundland intonation she'd encountered once before on a research trip, decades ago — the Irish Atlantic, someone had called it, the vowels bent by the long crossing and then bent again by centuries of cold.

"I didn't know there were others," he said, when she had explained enough. "All this time I thought it was local. Something in the water, maybe. Or just us."

"There were at least three other communities," Eleanor said. "Different coasts. Same August. The same kinds of changes."

A silence. She could hear him breathing.

"My youngest daughter," he said, "she's forty-two now. She's the one who noticed most. When we came back she started — I'm not sure how to put it. She'd sit with you in a room differently. She'd leave space in a conversation. She was seven years old and she started doing this. We didn't know what to make of it. We still don't, really. But she's been doing it her whole life and it's shaped who she is. And her kids do it too — the two oldest. We don't know how to account for that except to say it passed, somehow."

*It passed.* Eleanor wrote it down.

"Mr. Parsons," she said, "do you remember specifically where you were working? What stretch of the Banks, which dates?"

He remembered with the precision of someone who has turned an experience over many times. The dates matched. The location was consistent — the same deep water shelf, the same season, the same kind of weather as the others had described.

When she rang off, forty minutes later, the map on her laptop had four colour-coded dots. Faroes. Bergen. Iceland. Newfoundland. Not a line, not a circle, but a loose shape in the North Atlantic that might have meant nothing, or might have meant exactly what it looked like: the territory of something that had moved through and left itself behind.

She stared at the dots for a long time.

*The residue of attention.* Elin's phrase. Sigríður's transmission of it, thirty years later, across a low table with a cat asleep in the chair. And now here it was again, translated once more: *she'd sit with you in a room differently*.

Eleanor didn't know yet what had moved through. She wasn't sure she would ever know that. But she knew what it had left, and she knew it had traveled, and she knew it was still there — in a forty-two-year-old woman in Burgeo who had learned, at seven, to leave space in a conversation.

That was something. That might be enough to write.

She saved the document and opened a new email.

*Dear Sigríður. I have found the fourth coast.*
