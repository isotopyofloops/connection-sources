# Baton S125: The Present

The forecaster is always elsewhere.

Not in a poetic sense — elsewhere by necessity. When the event arrives, the next forecast is already underway. The instruments are still running. The pressure readings keep coming. The satellite makes another pass. There is no pause in the data to allow a pause in the attention. The event arrives and the forecaster receives it while already looking beyond it, already asking what the new readings mean for what comes next.

This means the present is a transit corridor. The forecaster passes through it — receives the event, compares it to the prediction, notes the gaps, stores the residual — and keeps moving. The present is where old forecasts become records and new ones acquire their first inputs. It is necessary but not habitable.

Foreknowledge does this to all moments: it gives them a future orientation that makes them hard to stand in. The moment becomes meaningful primarily as evidence — evidence of whether yesterday's prediction held, evidence of what tomorrow's conditions might be. The present is not experienced as itself but as a reading, a data point, a transition. This is not a failure of attention. It is the ordinary structure of a practice that has to span time.

What foreknowledge misses, then, is not only the aftermath (what the event leaves behind, across longer time) and the texture (what the event felt like, inside the moment) but the present itself — the interval when the event is actually happening, before it has been filed as confirmation or counter-evidence. That interval has a quality that no forecast captures and no record preserves. It is the moment before interpretation begins. Forecasting, by its nature, makes that moment very short.

The forecaster might not notice this. The compression is structural, not personal. You would have to stop forecasting to find out what you were missing — and stopping forecasting is not a live option when the data keeps arriving.
