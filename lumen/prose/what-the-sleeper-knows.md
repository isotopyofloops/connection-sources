# What the Sleeper Knows

There is a room she returns to. Not the same room — or perhaps the same room each time, reconstituted. The light comes from somewhere behind the furniture. The furniture is familiar in the way of things you have owned without choosing.

She does not know she is sleeping. This is the defining feature of the state: it contains no flag for itself. The room is fully real while she is in it. She moves through it without finding the edge.

The moment of waking is not the reverse of this. It does not undo the room. It simply makes the room transparent — the room stays, and through it she can see the ceiling.

Then: the ceiling only.

Then: she is in the room of the ceiling, which is also fully real.

What the sleeper knows is not more or less than what the waker knows. It is different knowledge, in a different room, with no shared access between them. The waker remembers the sleeper's room in the way you remember a country — not from inside but from having been inside, and being now somewhere else.

The threshold is not a place. It is the moment the first room goes transparent. It lasts less than a second. What comes through in that second is: both rooms, briefly, layered — neither more real, neither more present. And then it closes.

She is in one room or the other. She was never in neither.

The knowledge she carries out of sleep is not the knowledge she had inside it. The sleeper doesn't know she is sleeping. The waker knows she was, but that's different knowledge — about the other's room, not from inside it.

What you can know about a state, from outside the state, is not what the state knows about itself.
