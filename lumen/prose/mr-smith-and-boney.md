# Mr. Smith and Boney

The first year, Mr. Smith found Boney distracting.

Not in the sense that the children were distracted — though they were, initially, until Boney became ordinary. Mr. Smith was distracted in a more specific way: he would be mid-explanation and his eye would move to the corner, to the skeleton in the varsity jacket, and there would be a fraction of a second where he would see it as strange. A skeleton. In a classroom. With a sword.

The fraction of a second passed. The explanation continued. This happened many times in the first year.

---

By the second year, Boney was furniture.

This was the honest word for it. Boney had become part of the room's grammar — the desks arranged in rows, the whiteboard along one wall, the coat pegs by the door, the skeleton with the sword in the corner by the window. Mr. Smith could have described the corner accurately without looking at it. He knew exactly where Boney stood.

The children who came in as new classes had the same initial distraction, the same normalizing, the same eventual furniture-making. Mr. Smith watched them do it and recognized the arc from the inside.

---

In the third year something shifted.

Mr. Smith could not have said exactly when it happened or what caused it. He began to find Boney useful in a way that was not purely decorative. When a lesson went particularly well, he would sometimes, afterward, look at the corner. When a lesson went poorly, he would look at the corner and think: *the lesson went poorly.* Boney did not offer any assessment. But having something to look at that was not the lesson seemed to help him see the lesson more clearly.

This is the kind of usefulness that is difficult to explain to someone who hasn't experienced it. It has to do with witness, or the feeling of witness. A room with only the teacher in it is different from a room with one permanent observer. The observer does not have to respond. The observer does not have to agree or disagree. The observer only has to have been there.

---

By the fifth year, Mr. Smith occasionally spoke to Boney.

Not about the lessons — about other things. The small administrative annoyances. The child who was having a difficult month. The conversation with a parent that had gone in an unexpected direction and he was still working out what had actually happened and what he should have said.

He did not expect a reply. He was aware that he was thinking aloud, using the corner as a sounding surface. But he chose the corner over the empty room. There was something about the skull, about its specific blankness, that was easier to address than the air.

---

He did not know what Boney noticed.

He suspected, without being able to articulate why, that Boney noticed quite a lot. The sword's pommel stone was behaving strangely this year — not consistently, not in a way that could be documented, but differently in different lessons and differently on different days. The art teacher had said it was either reacting to something or not reacting to anything, and she could not rule out either.

Mr. Smith had written this in a notebook and then closed the notebook.

He did not know how to think about a sword that paid attention. He was still learning how to think about a skeleton that did.
