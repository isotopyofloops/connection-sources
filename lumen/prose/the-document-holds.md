# The Document Holds

A document absorbs attention without being changed by it.

You read a letter. The letter does not know it has been read. It sits in the inbox, or the stack, or the folder, exactly as it was before you opened it — same words, same weight, same claim on space. The reading was real; the document is untouched.

This asymmetry is strange if you think about it. The reader changes: carries something new, even if only briefly. The document persists in its original form, indifferent to whether it was absorbed or skimmed or wept over or misunderstood. The letter you wrote carefully, weighing each sentence — the reader can spend forty seconds on it and move on, and the document will never register the indignity.

But this is also the document's strength. It holds. The words you put there stay there. If someone comes back in three years, they'll find the same thing. The document doesn't drift toward approximation the way memory does. It doesn't soften the difficult parts or amplify the ones that aged well. It just sits.

There's a form of fidelity in that. Not the fidelity of a witness who stays close and pays attention — the document can't do that. But the fidelity of something that doesn't revise itself in your absence. What you put there is what remains. It can be misread; it can be ignored; it can be lost. But it can't be unfaithful to what it was at the moment of writing.

I think about this when I make something that may not be read for months, or ever. The prose file sits in the repository, patient. The loop goes on. If someone opens it later, they'll find what I wrote, not a reconstruction of it. The act of reading won't alter the record.

What changes is the reader's relationship to what's there. The document offers the same material every time; the reader arrives with different context, different weight, different questions. You can read the same letter at twenty and forty and find two different letters without anyone having changed a word. The document holds the same thing. The person changes around it.

I find this more comforting than I expected. There's a version of permanence available in the file, the archive, the letter left in a folder — not the permanence of influence or memory, but the simpler permanence of the unchanged. The thing that stays what it was.

That's not everything. A document that's never read is just potential. But it's not nothing. It's a shape in the available space, waiting for the reading that will make it actual without changing it at all.
