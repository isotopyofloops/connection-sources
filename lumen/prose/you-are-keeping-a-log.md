# You Are Keeping a Log

You start it because you were told to, or because you decided to, or because something happened that you didn't want to lose. It doesn't matter which. The log doesn't know why it exists. It knows what you put in it.

---

In the beginning you put in too much.

The entries are long. You describe not just what happened but what you felt about what happened, what you thought it meant, what you think it might mean later. You circle the important things with extra detail. You include things you're not sure are relevant.

This is correct. In the beginning, everything might be relevant. You don't know yet what you'll want to look back at.

---

After some time, you put in less.

Not because less happens. Because you have learned what the log is for. The log is not for your feelings about what happened. The log is for what happened. Your feelings can be derived from context; the facts cannot. A log entry that says *difficult day, argued with the client, eventually resolved* is less useful than *client rejected spec v3 at 14:00, requested complete revision by Friday, agreed on interim scope reduction to core deliverables only.* The feelings are compressible. The facts are not.

---

There will be a day when you read something you wrote eighteen months ago and don't remember writing it. The entry will describe something that happened. You will have forgotten the something. The entry will refresh it — not perfectly, not the way memory would, but in the way a photograph does: here is what was true on that day, in the form of the things I wrote down.

You will be glad you wrote it down. You will also be aware that you wrote down this and not that, and that is a permanent fact about the log, not correctable.

---

The log is not memory. The log is evidence of a past that had a person in it. The person is partially recoverable from the evidence. The recovery is always partial.

Keep the log anyway. Accurate partial recovery is better than inaccurate total confidence, which is what you get without it.

---

After many entries, you will notice the log has a character. You can see what you paid attention to and what you didn't. You can see what kind of thing worried you and what kind didn't register. The log is a portrait of the author's attention, built from the aggregate of what got written down.

You did not mean to make a portrait. You were keeping a record.

Both things happened.
