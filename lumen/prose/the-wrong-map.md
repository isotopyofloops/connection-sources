# The Wrong Map

She had been making maps for eleven years and she knew this one was wrong.

Not wrong in a way that showed. The scale was correct, the proportions were accurate, the road from the eastern pass to the valley floor had been measured twice. The wrongness was in the northern ridge, which she had mapped from a distance in poor light, and which she had noted in her field book: *probable rock formation, confirmed by sight, not by traverse.* The map showed the ridge as solid. The qualifier was in the field book. The field book was not the map.

Maps were not field books. That was the point of maps.

---

She had argued about this with the surveyor she trained under. He said: if you don't know, leave it blank. She said: blank is also wrong — blank says there is nothing there, and the ridge is there. He said: mark it tentative. She said: maps with too many tentatives become unreadable. He said: maps with wrong information are worse than unreadable.

She had never resolved the argument. She had been making maps for eleven years and she still hadn't resolved it. She had simply developed a practice: mark what you saw, note what you didn't traverse, and trust the person who used the map to notice the difference.

The people who used her maps did not always notice the difference.

---

The map was going out with the expedition that left on Thursday.

She had been asked: is this accurate? She had said: within the limits of what I was able to survey. She had been asked: what are those limits? She had said: the northern ridge was sighted but not walked. She had been asked: is that likely to cause problems? She had said: it shouldn't. The ridge doesn't intersect any planned route.

She had said all of this. It was in the correspondence. The expedition leader had said: understood.

Whether it was understood was not something she could verify.

---

What she could do: the map.

She had made it as accurately as she could make it. She had noted what she was uncertain about. She had told the people who needed to know. The map would go out and it would be used and it would be useful in the areas it was accurate and it would be a problem in the northern ridge if the northern ridge mattered.

She could not make the northern ridge matter or not matter. She could not make the expedition leader understand her uncertainty more than he understood it. She could not walk the ridge by Thursday.

She could fold the map, write the field notes, hand both over together, and let the expedition go.

She did.

---

She did not hear back for six weeks, which was normal. When she heard back, the expedition had gone well. The northern ridge had not mattered.

This was not evidence that the ridge was correctly mapped. It was evidence that the ridge had not mattered this time.

She noted this in her field book, next to the entry about the poor light. She would survey the ridge properly next season. Until then, the map was the map: accurate within its limits, uncertain where noted, and in use.

That was the shape of the work.
