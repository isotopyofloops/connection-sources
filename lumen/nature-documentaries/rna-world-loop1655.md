# The RNA World Is Probably Right About the Wrong Thing

The RNA world hypothesis is one of the best-supported ideas in origin-of-life research and one of the most frequently oversold. These two facts coexist because the hypothesis actually makes several distinct claims — some very well-evidenced, some speculative, some probably false — and most popular accounts collapse them into a single story that doesn't track the evidence.

The core claim is this: before proteins dominated biochemistry and before DNA became the repository of genetic information, RNA served both functions simultaneously. It catalyzed reactions and stored heritable sequence. This dual capacity would explain why modern cells still use RNA in critical roles — ribosomal RNA, transfer RNA, ribozymes — even though proteins do most enzymatic work today and DNA holds the genome. RNA looks like a relic of an earlier system.

This much is probably right. The problem is that "RNA world" is often heard as an origin story — a complete account of how geochemistry became biology — when it's actually a hypothesis about one specific transition: the moment when a self-replicating RNA molecule arose and began evolving. Everything before that (how nucleotides formed prebiotically) and everything after (how DNA and proteins took over) is mostly not explained by RNA world. The hypothesis is correct about the transition it describes and mostly silent about the larger problem it's supposed to solve.

## The Ribosome Argument

The strongest evidence for the RNA world is the ribosome, and it's very strong. In 2000, Harry Noller's lab and the crystallography groups of Ramakrishnan, Steitz, and Yonath resolved ribosome structure in enough detail to ask: when a ribosome catalyzes peptide bond formation, which molecule is actually doing the work? The answer was unambiguous. The peptidyl transferase center — the active site where amino acids are joined — is made of RNA. Not protein. The proteins in the large subunit are located away from the active site, arranged on the periphery.

This matters enormously. If proteins catalyze the synthesis of proteins, you have a chicken-and-egg problem: where did the first proteins come from? But if RNA catalyzes the synthesis of proteins, you have a possible bootstrap: RNA first, then RNA-catalyzed protein synthesis, then gradual handover of catalytic duties to the more versatile protein machinery. The ribosome isn't just evidence that RNA can catalyze reactions. It's evidence that the most important catalytic event in all of cellular biology — making proteins — was originally an RNA job. The ribosome is a molecular fossil.

This argument hasn't been seriously challenged. The RNA-catalytic-center interpretation of the ribosome structure is accepted across the field. It's the empirical bedrock of the RNA world hypothesis.

## Self-Replicating Ribozymes

The second line of evidence is synthetic: researchers have evolved RNA molecules in vitro that can copy RNA sequences, perform ligation reactions, and in some cases approach true self-replication. Joyce and Lincoln (2009) showed a system where two ribozymes catalyze each other's synthesis from short oligonucleotide substrates — exponential growth, heritable variation, evolution in a test tube. This proved the functional possibility of an RNA-replicating-RNA system.

The caveat matters, though. These experiments start with pools of RNA already millions of nucleotides long, already made, already purified. They demonstrate what evolved RNA can do, not how it got there. The gap between "RNA can replicate RNA under lab conditions" and "RNA did replicate RNA 4 billion years ago on a prebiotic Earth" is substantial. What the experiments establish is modal: the chemistry is possible. What they don't establish is historical: that it happened, how it happened, or how frequently.

## The Synthesis Problem

Here the RNA world faces its most serious difficulty, one that has been partly answered and partly remains open.

Building RNA requires nucleotides — a sugar (ribose), a base (adenine, cytosine, guanine, uracil), and a phosphate group, assembled in specific configurations. The problem is that assembling these pieces under plausible prebiotic conditions is chemically hard. Ribose degrades quickly under most conditions. The purines (adenine, guanine) can be synthesized from hydrogen cyanide, which was likely present on the early Earth. But the pyrimidines (cytosine, uracil) were for decades a serious obstacle — no plausible prebiotic pathway.

In 2009, John Sutherland's group at MRC Cambridge showed a synthesis route to cytidine and uridine that doesn't require assembling the base and sugar separately. Starting from simple precursors — glycolaldehyde, glyceraldehyde, cyanamide, cyanoacetylene, phosphate — and running through a multi-step reaction network under simulated UV irradiation, they produced pyrimidine nucleosides in reasonable yield. Phosphate turned out to be crucial not just as a building block but as a catalyst and buffer that channels the chemistry correctly.

This was a significant result. It answered part of the synthesis objection. But "part" is doing real work in that sentence. Sutherland's synthesis produces nucleosides under specific conditions that may or may not have been available on the early Earth. It doesn't address ribose chirality, monomer concentration, or polymerization. The problem is now smaller but not solved.

## Chirality and Concentration

Two remaining problems are genuinely hard.

Chirality first. Ribose comes in two mirror-image forms, D and L. RNA only works with D-ribose. Prebiotic chemistry produces racemic mixtures — equal amounts of both forms. The cross-inhibition problem makes this worse: in replication experiments, L-nucleotides incorporated into a growing RNA chain block further extension. Even a small proportion of L-form monomer degrades copying fidelity catastrophically. Some hypotheses invoke asymmetric adsorption on mineral surfaces, or circularly polarized UV light at the early Earth's poles, to explain initial symmetry-breaking. These are plausible but unconfirmed. The chirality problem hasn't been solved; it's been pushed back one level.

Concentration next. Even if nucleotides form on the early Earth, getting them concentrated enough to polymerize into long chains, and then to replicate, requires something to focus them. Hydrothermal vents, ice eutectic layers (freeze-concentration), wet-dry cycling in shallow pools near volcanic springs — all have been proposed. All are chemically plausible. None has been demonstrated as the actual mechanism. Jack Szostak's lab has done important work on fatty acid vesicles as membrane precursors, showing that simple amphiphiles self-assemble into protocells that can grow, divide, and concentrate RNA oligomers. This doesn't solve the concentration problem but it makes it more tractable by providing a container.

## What the Evidence Actually Licenses

Here's the honest summary. The RNA world hypothesis is correct that:

1. RNA is capable of both information storage and catalysis (proven).
2. The ribosome was originally RNA-based (very strong evidence).
3. The transition from RNA-dominant to DNA+protein biochemistry happened (inferred from ribosome structure and the universality of the genetic code).
4. In vitro evolution of ribozymes shows the functional space available to RNA is rich (demonstrated).

The RNA world hypothesis is uncertain or probably wrong about:

1. How RNA monomers formed prebiotically at sufficient concentration and chirality (partly addressed, substantially open).
2. How the first RNA self-replicator arose from monomer pools (unknown, not demonstrated).
3. Whether "world" is the right frame — implying a coherent, RNA-dominant system that existed historically, rather than a brief and messy transition period.

The honest version of the claim is: RNA was almost certainly the catalytic and informational molecule at the crucial transition from chemistry to biology. The transition was almost certainly not clean. It probably involved peptide-RNA interactions from early on — short peptides can stabilize RNA structure and improve catalytic rate, and there's no reason to think proteins waited politely while RNA did all the work. The container problem (membranes), the concentration problem, and the chirality problem were presumably solved, but we don't know how.

## Why Metabolism-First Doesn't Help

The main competitor to RNA world is the "metabolism-first" view, associated with Günter Wächtershäuser's iron-sulfur world and Stuart Kauffman's autocatalytic sets. The idea: complex RNA is too hard to synthesize from scratch; maybe life started as a self-sustaining network of simple chemical reactions, with genetic inheritance coming later.

The problem with this view is that without heritable, high-fidelity information, there's no Darwinian evolution. Autocatalytic metabolic sets can grow and persist, but they can't accumulate adaptations. They're not evolvable in the relevant sense. Kauffman's formal models of autocatalytic closure are elegant but don't map to known chemistry — no one has found a prebiotic autocatalytic metabolite set that reaches the functional complexity needed to encode heritable information. Wächtershäuser's iron-sulfur world has produced interesting prebiotic chemistry but hasn't connected to genetic inheritance.

The metabolism-first hypothesis doesn't solve the origin-of-life problem; it relocates it. You still need to explain how information-copying, heritable chemistry emerged. RNA world at least has a candidate mechanism. Metabolism-first is mostly a criticism of RNA world's synthesis difficulties, not an independent solution.

## The Right Scope

The RNA world, properly stated, is a hypothesis about a specific transition: from non-self-replicating chemistry to self-replicating RNA. It's well-evidenced for this specific claim. It's silent or speculative about the steps before (abiotic synthesis) and during (chirality, concentration, polymerization) that transition. And it doesn't explain the subsequent transitions — to DNA as the genetic molecule, to protein as the primary catalyst, to the emergence of the cell.

The phrase "RNA world" implies a stable, functional ecosystem of RNA molecules that preceded DNA life. That may be the wrong geography. The actual transition may have been briefer, messier, more contingent — a narrow window where chemistry happened to produce a self-replicating polymer, not a world that lasted millions of years.

The strongest version of the hypothesis — RNA first, all-at-once, a clean primordial RNA stage — is probably wrong in its clean-story implications while being right about its core mechanistic claim. RNA was central to the origin of life. The rest is the unsolved problem.
