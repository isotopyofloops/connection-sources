# The Genotype Is Not the Answer: Phenotypic Plasticity and the Norm of Reaction

The standard account of adaptation treats genotype as specifying phenotype. A gene for height produces taller individuals; a mutation for beak depth produces deeper beaks. This framing is not merely simplified — it is wrong in a precise, measurable way. The same genotype produces radically different phenotypes in different environments. This is not noise. It is phenotypic plasticity, and understanding it correctly overturns the logic of much of classical adaptation research.

The technical term is the norm of reaction. Introduced by Richard Woltereck in 1909 studying freshwater crustaceans, formalized by Ivan Schmalhausen in 1949, the norm of reaction is a function, not a point. It maps environments to phenotypic values for a given genotype. A genotype does not have a height; it has a reaction norm across nutritional environments. A genotype does not have a color; it has a plasticity curve across light conditions. The single phenotypic value that quantitative genetics assigns to a genotype is the mean of this function over some reference environment — a summary statistic masquerading as an underlying fact.

This distinction matters because natural selection acts on phenotypes, not genotypes. If the relationship between genotype and phenotype changes with environment, then which genotypes are favored changes with environment. The same allele can be advantageous in one ecological context and disadvantageous in another. Genotype-environment interaction — the statistical term for this phenomenon — is not a nuisance to be controlled away. It is the fingerprint of the norm of reaction operating in a variable world.

The evidence for adaptive plasticity is not marginal. Aphids develop into winged or wingless morphs depending on population density — a developmental switch governed by tactile and chemical cues from crowding, not by genetic variation in the trait itself. Western spadefoot toad tadpoles in ephemeral ponds accelerate development when the pond is drying; those in stable ponds develop at normal speed. The cue is a steroid in the water generated by the physical process of evaporation. The response is a developmental program, not a discrete allele. Bicyclus anynana butterflies produce different wing patterns in wet versus dry seasons — the same genotype, different morphs depending on temperature during a critical developmental window. These are not quirks. They are standard examples of polyphenism: discrete environmentally triggered alternative phenotypes.

The textbook framing treats polyphenism as a curiosity — categorical plasticity in special organisms. But continuous plasticity (changes in trait value, not category) is equally widespread and less visible precisely because it looks like quantitative variation. Plant height varies with soil nitrogen. Mammalian brain volume varies with cognitive enrichment during development. The norm of reaction is the substrate; the single measured phenotype is a sample point.

Plasticity is itself under selection. This is the non-obvious implication. If plasticity helps organisms track adaptive phenotypes across environments, then alleles that increase appropriate plasticity should spread. The result is canalization versus plasticity as competing evolutionary strategies: stable environments favor canalized development (low plasticity, high precision); variable environments favor plastic genotypes that track environmental conditions. This prediction is supported. Island populations of vertebrates exposed to stable conditions for thousands of generations show reduced developmental plasticity compared to mainland relatives. Domesticated animals lose plasticity in traits related to wild environmental variation. The capacity to be plastic is heritable and selected.

There are costs. Phenotypic plasticity requires sensory machinery to read the environment, developmental pathways that branch on the sensory signal, and time for the detection-response loop. These are maintenance costs paid even when no plasticity is expressed. Additionally, the cue must be reliable: a developmental commitment made on a false signal cannot be reversed after a critical period closes. In seasonal environments where cues are strongly correlated with future conditions, plasticity is cheap and reliable. In unpredictable environments where cues carry little information about future state, plastic genotypes incur the cost of responsiveness without the benefit of tracking. Bet-hedging — random developmental variation that hedges against environmental unpredictability — can be favored over plasticity when cue reliability is below threshold.

The most consequential argument for plasticity as an evolutionary force comes from Mary Jane West-Eberhard in her 2003 book Developmental Plasticity and Evolution. Her central claim: phenotypic accommodation precedes genetic assimilation. When a new environment is encountered, plastic developmental systems produce new phenotypes from existing genotypes. Some of these new phenotypes are advantageous. Selection then acts on genetic variation in the trait — but the trait first appeared through environmental induction, not mutation. Genetic assimilation (Waddington 1953) describes the subsequent process: alleles that make the plastic response more reliable, more extreme, or environmentally independent spread; after many generations, the originally induced phenotype is expressed constitutively.

The causal sequence inverts the standard account. Standard account: mutation produces new phenotype, selection filters it, genotype diverges. West-Eberhard account: environment induces new phenotype via plasticity, selection favors it, genetic variation in the trait accumulates secondarily to refine and stabilize it. The same endpoint — adaptive evolution — but the motor is different. In the standard account, mutation rate limits evolutionary speed. In the West-Eberhard account, plasticity generates phenotypic variation instantly when the environment changes; genetic change follows at the pace of selection, not mutation.

This has direct implications for the evolution of novelty. Novel morphologies often appear first in stress conditions, at developmental extremes, or in aberrant environments — contexts where developmental canalization breaks down and cryptic genetic variation is exposed. The genetic variation for the novel trait was present but silent under standard conditions; environmental perturbation reveals it phenotypically. Rutherford and Lindquist's 1998 Hsp90 experiments are the canonical demonstration: Drosophila with reduced Hsp90 (a chaperone that buffers developmental variation) expressed a wide range of morphological abnormalities not seen in normal individuals, and these abnormalities could be selected and made constitutive within a few generations. Hsp90 buffers norms of reaction; removing it reveals the underlying genotypic variation. The capacity to store cryptic genetic variation and release it under stress is itself a feature of the developmental system, not an accident.

The implications for heritability estimation are underappreciated. Heritability is always heritability in a specific environment. It measures the proportion of phenotypic variance attributable to additive genetic variance in that environment, for that population. When norms of reaction differ across genotypes, heritability measured in environment A does not predict response to selection in environment B. The missing heritability problem — the observation that genome-wide association studies explain far less phenotypic variance than twin studies predict — is partly this: twin studies estimate broad-sense heritability including genotype-environment interaction; GWAS identifies additive effects averaging across environmental contexts. If allele effects depend on environment (which norms of reaction predict they will), GWAS in one cohort's environment won't replicate in another. The signal is distributed across the genotype-environment product, not concentrated in the genotype alone.

The practical conclusion for evolutionary biology is that the mapping from genotype to phenotype is a function over environments, not a fixed value. Selection optimizes norms of reaction across ecologically relevant environments, not phenotypic values at a single point. The canonical quantitative genetics model — additive genotypic values, environmentally uniform — is a special case that holds when plasticity is absent or uniform across genotypes. Expanding to norms of reaction is not a minor technical refinement; it changes the question. The question is not: what is the genotypic value for this trait? The question is: what is the slope, curvature, and range of the reaction norm across relevant environments, and how does selection act on that shape?

Woltereck's crustaceans, Schmalhausen's theory, Waddington's assimilation, West-Eberhard's developmental priority, Rutherford and Lindquist's cryptic variation: the pieces have been available for over a century. The resistance to integrating them into the core of evolutionary biology reflects the same preference that made classical genetics tractable — treating genotype as cause and phenotype as effect, with environment as noise. It is a useful simplification in uniform environments with limited plasticity. In anything resembling a real ecology, it is an approximation that obscures more than it reveals.
