# The Waste Product

*Lyric essay — Great Oxygenation Event, ~2.4 billion years ago*

---

The first poison was not a poison.

It was a byproduct. A waste gas expelled into the shallow sea and rising, because gas does that — it rises. The organism that produced it had no interest in the atmosphere. It was busy doing what it had always done: splitting water with light, pulling carbon from the dissolved dissolved dissolved world, making more of itself. The oxygen was incidental. The oxygen was nothing. The oxygen left.

This went on for three hundred million years.

At first the ocean absorbed it. Iron is generous — it will take oxygen from anyone. The ocean floor turned red in slow columns as dissolved iron rusted and sank, laid down in stripes, the banded iron formations, the geological ledger we read now with instruments and call *evidence of photosynthesis*. The ocean was reddening like a bruise. Three hundred million years of bruise.

But iron is finite. The ocean ran out of iron the way a sponge runs out of capacity — gradually, then completely. And then the oxygen had nowhere to go but up.

---

Up was the atmosphere. The atmosphere in 2.4 billion BCE was methane and nitrogen and carbon dioxide, warm and reducing, without a single free oxygen molecule to speak of. Life had adapted to this. Life had, in fact, *required* this. Oxygen is reactive, which is to say: oxygen destroys things. Organic chemistry, specifically. The long chains that make a cell possible. Oxygen attacks them.

So when oxygen entered the atmosphere, it did not do so gently.

It destroyed methane first. Methane was the greenhouse keeper, the thing that kept the early Earth from freezing despite a sun 30% dimmer than today. When oxygen removed it, the temperature dropped. Glaciers reached the equator. The Huronian glaciation lasted three hundred million years. From space, if you had been there, you would have seen a white planet.

Nearly everything died.

This is the sentence that stops the breath: *nearly everything died*. The largest extinction event in Earth history was caused by photosynthesis. The most productive, efficient, life-sustaining process in the history of biology killed almost all life. The waste product was the murder weapon. The cyanobacteria never knew.

They didn't die, of course. They had been doing this on purpose — not on purpose, but doing it. They survived. A few anaerobes found niches in the deep sediment, in the gut of other organisms, in hydrothermal vents where oxygen couldn't reach. They survive still, in our intestines, under our gums, in the mud of bogs. The ancient ones. The ones that predate our atmosphere.

---

What remained was an oxygen world. And in an oxygen world, the organisms that could use it had an extraordinary advantage. Aerobic respiration extracts 18 times more energy from a glucose molecule than fermentation. This is not an incremental improvement. This is the difference between reading by candlelight and floodlight. The whole metabolic economy shifted.

Complex multicellular life became possible in an oxygen world. Not because oxygen *caused* it — causation is always more tangled than that — but because the energy budget opened. Nervous systems cost something. Muscles cost something. Eyes, hearts, the long chains of amino acid decisions that we call behavior — all of it costs something, and now there was enough to pay.

We are made of the waste product.

Every breath is the aftermath of the worst extinction in Earth history, the one that happened two and a half billion years before anyone could know it was happening, the one caused by organisms that had no theory of what they were doing and no record of what they had done. We breathe and the cyanobacteria are still there, in the ocean, in the mats of hot springs, making more oxygen. They haven't stopped. They were here before and they will be here after.

The poison is ongoing.

It's just that we've adapted to it so thoroughly that we call it air.
