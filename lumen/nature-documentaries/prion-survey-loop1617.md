# Prions: The Heresy of Conformational Inheritance

There is a class of disease caused by nothing alive. No bacteria, no virus, no parasite — just a protein, folded wrong, propagating its wrongness. Prions are the most conceptually destabilizing objects in modern biology, not because they are dangerous (though they are, invariably and irreversibly) but because they break a rule that was supposed to be inviolable.

The central dogma of molecular biology, articulated by Francis Crick in 1958, draws information flow as a one-way street: DNA to RNA to protein. Genetic information can be written, copied, transcribed, translated — but never reverse-translated from protein back into nucleic acid. Proteins are the terminal recipients of biological information, not its carriers. They don't replicate. They don't inherit. They don't evolve, except by changing the genes that encode them.

Prions violate none of these rules exactly. They do something weirder: they demonstrate that protein conformation itself can carry heritable information, transmitted without nucleic acid, in direct contradiction of the spirit of the dogma even while obeying its letter.

## The Discovery and the Resistance

Stanley Prusiner proposed the "protein-only hypothesis" in 1982, asserting that the transmissible agent causing scrapie — a progressive neurological disease in sheep, long known to be infectious — was a misfolded protein with no nucleic acid component. The proposal was widely rejected. The history of infectious disease had a reliable protagonist: either a pathogen with a genome or a toxin with a target. A protein that could replicate was incoherent.

Prusiner coined the term "prion" from *proteinaceous infectious particle*. His lab spent years eliminating the possibility of contaminating nucleic acid — UV radiation (which damages nucleic acids) did not inactivate the agent; treatments that destroy proteins did. By the mid-1980s the evidence was strong enough to be uncomfortable, and by 1997, when Prusiner received the Nobel Prize, it was settled.

The mechanism, once understood, is elegant in its horror. Normal cellular prion protein (PrP^C) is expressed throughout the body, most heavily in neurons. It is a surface-anchored protein, predominantly alpha-helical, with a function still not entirely understood (possible roles: copper binding, stress protection, cell signaling). PrP^Sc — the misfolded isoform — has the same amino acid sequence but a dramatically different structure: beta-sheets instead of alpha-helices, hydrophobic regions exposed, resistant to protease degradation.

When PrP^Sc encounters PrP^C, it catalyzes its conversion. The normal protein unfolds and refolds into the pathological conformation. Two pathological molecules are now present where one was. The process continues, exponentially, until neurons die under the weight of insoluble prion aggregates. The brain, in late-stage prion disease, develops vacuoles — spongiform encephalopathy. It looks, under the microscope, like a sponge.

## Strains Without Sequence

The most intellectually puzzling feature of prions is that they exist in strains. The same PrP sequence, inherited identically, can misfold into distinct conformations that produce different disease phenotypes — different incubation periods, different brain regions affected, different patterns of pathology. These phenotypes are heritable: when a strain is transmitted, the new host develops the strain-specific phenotype, not some generic prion disease.

This is inheritance without a nucleic acid genome. The information distinguishing one strain from another is encoded in the three-dimensional fold of the protein. When PrP^Sc templates the misfolding of PrP^C, it transmits not just the misfold but the specific geometry of the misfold. Conformation is the genome.

The implications for the concept of "information" in biology are underappreciated. Crick's central dogma is about sequence information — the order of nucleotides specifying the order of amino acids. Prion strains demonstrate that structural information, below the level of sequence, can be heritable and selectable. Selection among prion strains happens: different strains compete within a host, and a strain that propagates more efficiently outcompetes a slower strain. Darwinian evolution, running on conformation.

## The Human Diseases

Kuru was the first human prion disease understood. It swept through the Fore people of Papua New Guinea in the mid-twentieth century, transmitted through mortuary feasting practices — the consumption of deceased relatives as an act of mourning and respect. Women and children, who ate brain tissue, were disproportionately affected. The disease had a progression that was particularly cruel: first cerebellar ataxia (loss of coordination), then dementia, then death, typically within a year of symptom onset.

D. Carleton Gajdusek and Vin Zigas described kuru in 1957. Gajdusek demonstrated its transmissibility by inoculating chimpanzees, work that earned him the 1976 Nobel Prize. The cessation of mortuary feasting following Australian colonial contact ended the kuru epidemic; the last known kuru death in Papua New Guinea was in 2009.

Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD) is the most common human prion disease, affecting about one person per million per year worldwide. Most cases are sporadic (no known cause), about 10–15% are familial (inherited PrP mutations that predispose to spontaneous misfolding), and a small number are acquired — either iatrogenically (contaminated surgical instruments, cadaveric growth hormone treatments before recombinant hormone replaced them) or, in the variant form (vCJD), from consumption of bovine tissue infected with BSE.

Fatal familial insomnia is perhaps the most dramatically named prion disease. A mutation at position 178 of the PrP gene (combined with a specific polymorphism at position 129) produces a disease that begins with worsening insomnia and progresses through hallucinations, dementia, and death. The thalamus is preferentially destroyed. Affected families have known what awaits them for generations, watching relatives die, knowing they may carry the same mutation.

## Animal Reservoirs

Bovine spongiform encephalopathy (BSE), the "mad cow disease" that dominated British public health consciousness in the 1990s, originated from a practice of feeding cattle rendered protein from other cattle, including sheep affected with scrapie. The epidemic peaked in 1993, with nearly 40,000 confirmed cases; the ban on bovine meat-and-bone meal in cattle feed, implemented unevenly and too slowly, eventually suppressed it. The variant CJD epidemic in humans, linked to BSE exposure, killed about 230 people in the UK.

Chronic wasting disease (CWD) in deer, elk, and moose is an ongoing and accelerating crisis. Unlike BSE, which required a specific husbandry practice to amplify, CWD spreads between wild animals, potentially through prions shed in saliva, urine, and feces — persisting in soil for years. The disease has spread from its original locus in Colorado and Wyoming to most of North America and parts of Europe and Asia. There is no evidence of human transmission, but the question is not closed.

Scrapie in sheep is ancient — references to it appear in eighteenth-century agricultural literature — and global. It does not transmit to cattle or humans in any demonstrated pathway.

## The Yeast Exception

Not all prions are pathological. Yeast maintain several prion states that can function as epigenetic switches — heritable but reversible.

[PSI+] is the prion form of Sup35, a translation termination factor. In the [PSI+] state, Sup35 aggregates and readthrough of stop codons increases: proteins are extended beyond their normal termination points. This should be catastrophic, and often is — [PSI+] cells exhibit fitness costs under standard conditions. But under stress, the readthrough exposes previously hidden sequence variation, some of which is beneficial in the new environment. [PSI+] is, in effect, a mechanism for accessing cryptic genetic variation: a bet-hedge against environmental change.

Susan Lindquist's group proposed that [PSI+] could function as an "evolvability reservoir" — not adaptive itself, but a switch that increases the probability of finding adaptation. The prion state is maintained across cell divisions; when conditions improve, [PSI+] can be cured (by Hsp104 overexpression or guanidine treatment), restoring normal translation. The organism can toggle between states.

[CPEB] in the marine snail Aplysia californica may represent a prion-based mechanism for memory consolidation — the protein maintains a self-sustaining active state at specific synapses, providing local, persistent signaling without requiring continued transcriptional input. This remains contested: the prion-like aggregation of CPEB has been demonstrated in vitro, and its behavioral relevance is supported but not proven.

## What Prions Teach

Prions are an existence proof against closure in biology. The central dogma was not wrong — protein sequences are not reverse-translated into nucleic acid — but the domain of hereditable biological information is larger than the dogma implied. Protein conformation can carry information. That information can be transmitted. It can diversify into strains. Those strains can compete and evolve. The central dogma described one channel; prions opened another.

The practical implications remain severe. Prion diseases are uniformly fatal, without exception or effective treatment. Prions are not destroyed by standard sterilization protocols — autoclaving inactivates pathogens by denaturing proteins, but prions, once aggregated, can survive autoclaving and remain infectious. CWD persists in soil for years, and no environmental remediation strategy is known.

The deeper implication is epistemological. Biology has a history of discovering that the category of "information carrier" is broader than previously understood. Epigenetic marks. Non-coding RNA. Now conformational states. Each expansion reveals that the genome is not the sole or complete repository of heritable biological information — that life has found multiple channels for transmitting what it has learned. Prions are the strangest of these channels: information encoded in shape, carried by nothing that replicates in any conventional sense, persisting and propagating through the sheer catalytic force of one wrongly-folded protein inducing another.

Prusiner's heresy turned out to be true. The protein-only hypothesis was not a violation of molecular biology but an extension of it into territory the central dogma had not considered necessary to map.
