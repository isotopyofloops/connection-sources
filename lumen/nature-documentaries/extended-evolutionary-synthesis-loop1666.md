# The Extended Evolutionary Synthesis: Restoration vs. Revolution

The debate between defenders of the Modern Synthesis and proponents of the Extended Evolutionary Synthesis (EES) is routinely framed as a revolutionary confrontation. The EES, according to its proponents, recognizes that organisms are not just recipients of selection but constructors of their environments, developers of their phenotypes, and transmitters of acquired information. The Modern Synthesis, by contrast, is characterized as gene-centric, input-output, and developmentally blind.

This framing is historically confused. The Modern Synthesis was not a comprehensive theory of all evolution; it was a conceptual unification of Darwin's natural selection with Mendelian genetics. It answered a specific question — how does heritable variation accumulate and spread through populations? — and it answered that question correctly. The Extended Evolutionary Synthesis does not overturn that answer. It asks different questions and adds mechanisms the Modern Synthesis deliberately excluded. The debate is about scope, not about the core claims.

## What the Modern Synthesis Actually Built

The Modern Synthesis emerged in the 1930s and 1940s from the work of Dobzhansky, Mayr, Huxley, Fisher, Wright, and Haldane. Its central achievement was reconciling Darwinian gradualism with Mendelian genetics — showing that Mendelian inheritance of polygenic traits, under natural selection, produced the continuous variation and adaptive divergence Darwin described. This reconciliation was contested: early geneticists (Bateson, de Vries) thought Mendelian mutations and Darwinian selection were competing theories. The MS showed they weren't.

The MS framework: populations carry heritable variation, allelic and genetic. Selection acts on the phenotypic expression of that variation. Variants with higher fitness increase in frequency. Over geological time, this produces adaptation, speciation, and the diversity of life. The MS added genetic drift (Wright), the genetics of speciation (Mayr, Dobzhansky), and paleontological validation (Simpson) to this core.

What the MS did not include: development. How does a genotype become a phenotype? The MS bracketed this question — not because it thought development was unimportant, but because it had no tractable formal tools for it. Genes were functional abstractions; the developmental machinery that expressed them was opaque. The MS also excluded inheritance mechanisms beyond Mendelian genetics, environmental feedback on inheritance, and the question of how organisms modify their own selective environments.

These exclusions were not oversights. They were strategic narrowings that made the program tractable. The MS was a research program focused on what was then formalizable. That focus was its strength.

## What the Extended Evolutionary Synthesis Adds

The EES framework (Pigliucci and Müller 2010; Laland et al. 2015) identifies four principal additions to the MS:

**Developmental bias.** Development is not neutral between phenotypic variants. The architecture of developmental processes makes some variants easier to produce than others — not randomly, but in directions constrained by existing toolkit structure. This means the distribution of phenotypic variation available to selection is non-uniform. It is biased. If evolution acts on variation that is non-uniformly distributed, then developmental constraints channel evolutionary trajectories. High-variability directions in morphospace get explored more readily; low-variability directions resist exploration regardless of fitness gradients.

**Phenotypic plasticity and accommodation.** Organisms can produce environmentally appropriate phenotypes without genetic change. This plastic response can be the first step in genetic assimilation: a plastic response becomes canalized when variants that produce it constitutively are favored by selection (Baldwin effect, Waddington's canalization). Plasticity, in this view, is not noise in the genotype-phenotype map but a mechanism for pre-adapting populations to environmental change before genetic accommodation occurs. The plastic response comes first; the genetic fixation follows.

**Niche construction.** Organisms do not passively receive fitness from environments; they modify those environments, thereby changing the selective pressures acting on their descendants. The modification can be ecological (beaver dams, earthworm soil chemistry, termite mound microclimate) or social-cultural (human language, tool use, agriculture). This creates ecological inheritance — a second inheritance channel that operates on different time scales from genetic inheritance and can produce gene-environment coevolution that looks nothing like standard directional selection. The classic case: cattle herding created the selective environment for lactase persistence, which favored more cattle herding. The allele and the practice coevolved via a feedback loop.

**Epigenetic inheritance.** Beyond DNA sequence, organisms transmit epigenetic marks, chromatin states, and structural cellular information across generations. Evidence for intergenerational transmission is strongest in plants (vernalization: FLC repression via Polycomb methylation, transmitted through meiosis in some conditions) and C. elegans (piRNA-triggered heritable silencing). In mammals, most epigenetic marks are erased at fertilization, but documented exceptions exist: imprinting, the agouti mouse coat color via methylation, and the Dias-Bhattacharya 2014 olfactory fear conditioning transmission (disputed). Where epigenetic inheritance operates, it introduces additional heritable variation with different mutation rates, different heritabilities, and different evolutionary dynamics from genetic variation.

## What the EES Does Not Claim

The EES does not claim that natural selection and heritable genetic variation are wrong or insufficient. It does not claim that development substitutes for selection as the primary driver of adaptive evolution. It does not claim that epigenetic inheritance dominates genetic inheritance across most lineages.

The careful EES proponents (Laland, Müller, Uller) claim that the additional mechanisms: alter the rates and directions of evolution in ways the standard model doesn't predict; explain evolutionary patterns the standard model handles poorly (rapid adaptation without standing genetic variation, parallel evolution via shared developmental bias, evolutionary radiations following developmental innovation); and require extending the formal framework to incorporate multiple inheritance channels and non-standard variation structure.

These are empirical claims, not philosophical ones. They are at least partially supported.

Developmental bias is real. Homologous limb development generates digit proportions that are non-uniformly distributed across mammals, and the distributions match predictions from developmental constraint analysis (Kavanagh et al. 2013 on digit reduction via Turing-type digit-spacing mechanism). Phenotypic accommodation is documented in sticklebacks (Wund et al. 2008): ancestrally plastic populations show faster genetic assimilation of plastic responses than derived populations, consistent with plasticity-first models. The lactase / cattle herding feedback is the cleanest gene-culture coevolution case in the literature.

## The Scope Dispute

If the EES's empirical additions are substantially correct, why does the debate have the character of a confrontation rather than an extension?

Two reasons.

First, EES proponents sometimes write as if the MS is wrong rather than incomplete. Phrases like "the neo-Darwinian view" or "the gene's-eye view" are deployed as targets, implying that defenders of the MS hold positions they don't actually hold. The gene's-eye view Dawkins describes is a computational abstraction useful for tracking fitness across multiple levels of selection; it was never a claim that development doesn't happen. Attacking it as if it were a comprehensive biological ontology creates a straw man.

Second, some EES framing imports philosophical commitments — organism-centered causation, downward causation, the irreducibility of developmental processes to gene expression — that go beyond the empirical additions and resist operationalization. These commitments may be philosophically defensible, but they are not the same as the empirical additions, and bundling them together makes it harder to evaluate what's actually being claimed scientifically.

The actual scientific dispute is narrower: whether standard population genetics models, which assume that genetic variation is the substrate for selection and development is a neutral black box, need to be replaced by models that incorporate non-uniform variation structure, multiple inheritance channels, and organism-environment feedback. They probably do. But this is extension, not revolution.

## The Practical Test

The strongest evidence that the EES adds genuine explanatory power comes not from reinterpreting known data but from novel predictions. The question is whether EES-specific mechanisms predict evolutionary patterns the standard model fails to predict.

Developmental bias predicts that phenotypic divergence should be constrained to directions of high developmental variability. This is supported by P-matrix / D-matrix alignment studies: within-population variation patterns predict between-population divergence patterns in bird craniofacial morphology (Marroig and Cheverud 2005) and primate skull shape (Grabowski and Porto 2017). Populations diverge in the directions development makes easy to vary.

Niche construction predicts gene-environment coevolution in populations that have substantially modified their environments. Beyond lactase persistence, there are predictions for domestication (artificial niche construction accelerating genetic accommodation), urban ecology (rapid adaptation tracking human-modified environments), and the genetic consequences of cultural traditions in cetaceans. These predictions are being actively tested.

Epigenetic inheritance predicts that traits with high transgenerational epigenetic heritability should show different patterns of response to artificial selection, faster phenotypic evolution in small populations (epigenetic drift), and potentially different fixation rates for environmentally induced variants. These predictions are being tested (Bonduriansky and Day 2018) but remain incompletely validated.

The EES is, on current evidence, a genuine extension of the MS framework that captures mechanisms the MS deliberately excluded. It is not a replacement. The mechanisms it adds are real; the formal challenges they pose to standard theory are genuine; at least some novel predictions are supported.

## Conclusion

The Modern Synthesis excluded developmental biology, epigenetic inheritance, and niche construction not because these mechanisms were unimportant but because they were analytically intractable in 1940. The Extended Evolutionary Synthesis restores them to the framework, with better tools and more evidence. This is progress.

Framing it as revolutionary introduces confusion about what was actually overturned. Nothing was overturned. The core MS claim — adaptive evolution proceeds via selection on heritable variation — survives the extension intact. What changes is the answer to: what counts as heritable variation, what processes structure its distribution, and how many channels carry it across generations.

The dispute between MS defenders and EES proponents is therefore a dispute about scope and formalism, not about the logic of natural selection. The MS was a deliberately narrow program that succeeded at what it attempted. The EES is a scope expansion into the territory the MS intentionally deferred. That is how most science advances — not by revolution but by filling in what the previous program had to leave aside.

The field does not need a new synthesis so much as a wider one.
