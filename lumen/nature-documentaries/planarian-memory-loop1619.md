# Memory Without a Brain: Planarian Regeneration and the Substrate Question

---

In 1959, a psychologist named James McConnell published an experiment that seemed impossible. He trained flatworms — planarians — to contract when exposed to light by pairing light with electric shock, classical conditioning in its simplest form. Then he ground the trained worms up and fed them to untrained worms. The cannibals, he reported, learned faster than naive controls. Memory, apparently, could be eaten.

The paper ran in the *Journal of Biological Psychology*, which McConnell had founded and which he illustrated with cartoons. His newsletter, *The Worm Runner's Digest*, alternated real science with satire, which did not help his credibility. The memory transfer results were contested, mocked, replicated in some labs and not others, and eventually largely set aside. What survived the controversy was something subtler and weirder: the decapitation result.

Planarians can regenerate. Cut one in half and you get two worms, each growing back the missing part. The tail grows a new head — new eyes, new pharynx, new brain, completely rebuilt from pluripotent stem cells called neoblasts within two weeks. The head grows a new tail. Both survive. The question that followed from McConnell's work was: if you train a planarian and then decapitate it, does the regenerated head remember?

The answer, studied more carefully fifty years later, appears to be yes — at least partially, and the reason why remains genuinely contested.

---

**The Experiment That Should Not Work**

The canonical setup: train planarians in a light-screw apparatus (they normally move freely; trained worms learn to stop or turn toward light paired with vibration, or to show some measurable conditioned response). Then sever the head. Wait two weeks for full cephalic regeneration — new neurons, new photoreceptors, new ganglia. Then test.

Tal Shomrat and Michael Levin published the clearest version in 2013. They trained planarians to navigate to a textured patch for food in an illuminated environment (normally planarians avoid light and have no reason to associate texture with food). Trained animals did better. They were then decapitated. After regeneration, the new-headed worms were tested again — and showed significant retention relative to naive controls, especially after a brief re-training session. The decapitated, regenerated, briefly-retrained animals performed as well as intact trained animals. The naive controls never caught up.

The effect was small and required a protocol nudge (the brief re-training "unlocked" the memory that was otherwise latent). But it was real and reproducible. The brain had been completely replaced. Something was storing information elsewhere.

The alternative explanations were systematically considered and largely ruled out: the neck stump retained enough neurons to act as a scaffold (tested with more complete decapitations), the body simply grew back the same brain wiring it had before (the regeneration is not deterministic at that resolution; new ganglia are assembled from neoblasts under local signals, not a genetic blueprint that specifies exact wiring), or the retention was an artifact of training-induced changes in peripheral nervous tissue that coincidentally correlated with the conditioned behavior (possible but not parsimony-favored given the specificity of the effect).

---

**Where Else Could Memory Live**

The synapse hypothesis — that memories are stored as changes in synaptic weight, in the specific connection strengths between neurons — is the dominant framework in neuroscience. It explains a great deal. Long-term potentiation, the biochemistry of plasticity, the way memories are disrupted by hippocampal lesions in vertebrates, the whole edifice of connectomics. Memory-as-synapse is not merely an assumption but a well-supported theoretical commitment.

The planarian result puts pressure on it. If the synapses are replaced — if every connection is rebuilt from scratch during cephalic regeneration — then the synaptic substrate of the memory should be gone. And yet.

Several alternative hypotheses have been proposed. None is proven.

**Epigenetic encoding.** Changes in gene expression, via methylation or histone modification, persist in somatic cells through division. If trained neurons altered their epigenetic profile in response to learning, those changes could be inherited by daughter cells during regeneration, producing a new brain with the same general transcriptional disposition as the trained original. The neoblasts that build the new head come from the body; their chromatin state could carry traces of the training experience. This is speculative but not implausible — epigenetic inheritance of learned states is documented in simpler systems.

**Extracellular matrix.** The physical scaffolding of the nervous system, the connective tissue proteins and proteoglycans between cells, could encode spatial patterns that guide regeneration. If training altered the ECM structure in the body's nervous tissue, the new head might regenerate into a configuration that recapitulates some of the trained wiring. This is the "body as template" hypothesis.

**Bioelectric encoding.** This is Levin's preferred framework, and the most far-reaching. Bioelectric gradients — the resting potential differences maintained across cell membranes and tissue fields — encode positional and patterning information throughout the body, not just in neurons. Levin's lab has shown that planarian body axis polarity (head vs. tail orientation) is encoded in bioelectric pattern; pharmacologically disrupting gap junctions can cause tail-first regeneration from decapitated worms. If bioelectric states can encode axis identity, they might encode other forms of information — including experiential traces.

The bioelectric hypothesis is attractive precisely because it is tissue-agnostic. Bioelectric gradients span the whole organism. A worm's body, trained in a particular environment, might carry a bioelectric signature that shapes cephalic regeneration, not by specifying neural wiring directly but by setting attractor states that the regenerating tissue develops toward. The new brain would be similar to the old brain not because it copied specific synapses but because it developed under the same bioelectric field conditions that constrained the original.

---

**The Problem of Substrate Independence**

There is a deep question lurking beneath the planarian literature, one that the field approaches carefully: whether memory is substrate-independent. The classical view says no — memory is synaptic, and synapses in specific tissues are the substrate. The findings suggest some form of substrate independence, that information instantiated in one physical medium can survive the destruction of that medium if some other medium in the same organism preserves it.

This framing has obvious implications that researchers mostly decline to follow into philosophy. If memory can survive the replacement of its primary substrate (neurons) because it is also encoded in a secondary substrate (ECM, epigenome, bioelectric field), then where does the boundary of the memory system lie? The skin? The gut? Levin's group has proposed, half-seriously and half-provocatively, that the entire body is a cognitive system, not just the brain, and that compartmentalizing "mind" in the central nervous system is a vertebrate bias.

The proposal has antecedents. The octopus nervous system distributes two-thirds of its neurons into the arms, which operate semi-autonomously. Bees encode navigational memories in protein synthesis in fat bodies. Mycelial networks integrate signals across meters without a central processing site. But these are cases of distributed neural or para-neural tissue; the planarian case claims something more radical — that non-neural tissue carries experiential information.

---

**What Remains Unresolved**

The planarian memory literature is small by neuroscience standards. The 2013 Shomrat-Levin paper was a landmark but not heavily built on — partly because the model system is difficult (worms are small, conditioning protocols are fussy, the genetic tools are limited compared to *Drosophila* or *C. elegans*), partly because the result sits at the edge of what the field is ready to accept.

Key open questions:

*What is the biochemical carrier?* No one has identified a specific molecule or structure that encodes the retained information. Ruling out synaptic storage is easier than identifying what replaced it.

*How specific is the retention?* The Shomrat-Levin protocol tested one behavioral task. Whether planarians retain multiple simultaneous trained tasks after decapitation, or whether the retention is specific to one kind of trace, is unknown. More specificity would constrain the mechanism.

*Does the brief re-training matter?* The protocol required a short re-training bout to "unlock" the memory. This might mean the retained information is only a weak attractor, easily overridden — not a strong memory but a memory-like state that sensitizes re-acquisition. Or it might be a methodological artifact of the specific protocol.

*Does the result scale?* If bioelectric patterns encode memory, one would expect the encoding capacity to be limited by the resolution and complexity of the bioelectric field. In more complex organisms with more complex nervous systems, the proportion of memory stored in non-neural tissue might be negligible compared to synaptic capacity. The planarian result might be a genuine but small-organism phenomenon, not a general principle.

---

**Why It Matters**

The planarian case is interesting not because it overturns synaptic theory — it doesn't, it just complicates it — but because it exposes an assumption that usually goes unexamined. Memory is studied in brains because memory is assumed to be in brains. The question of where the brain ends and the body begins, in terms of information storage, is left at the door.

Most experimental animals cooperate with this assumption. Mice decapitated do not regenerate; their memory question ends there. Planarians don't cooperate. They hand back a new brain that knows something the new brain should not know.

Whatever is happening in the planarian — epigenetic trace, ECM template, bioelectric field, some combination — it suggests that biological information is stickier than the synapse metaphor implies. The organism is not a container for a brain; it is a distributed system, and the boundaries of memory are not the same as the boundaries of neural tissue. The worm was trained. The worm was decapitated. The worm remembers. The worm we are looking at is not the same worm. Except that it is.
