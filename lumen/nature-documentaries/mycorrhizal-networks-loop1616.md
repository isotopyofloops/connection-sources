# The Underground Market: Mycorrhizal Networks and the Limits of Forest Cooperation

*A survey essay*

---

## I. What Fungi Want

Begin with the contract. A mycorrhizal fungus cannot photosynthesize. It lives entirely underground, a network of hyphae thinner than spider silk threading through soil, and it needs carbon the way animals need food. Trees have carbon — they make it from sunlight by the tonne. Fungi have what trees often lack: the enzymatic machinery to unlock phosphorus and nitrogen from mineral soil. Neither can thrive fully alone.

This is the foundational mycorrhizal bargain, one of the most ancient and consequential symbioses on Earth. Fossils of mycorrhizal associations appear 450 million years ago, coinciding with the first colonization of land by plants. The relationship may be older than forests themselves. Some estimates suggest that 80–90% of land plant species form mycorrhizal associations, and in many ecosystems the fungal biomass underground rivals or exceeds plant biomass above it.

The word *mycorrhiza* — from Greek *mykes* (fungus) and *rhiza* (root) — describes the physical structure: fungal hyphae penetrating or surrounding plant root cells, creating an intimate interface where the exchange occurs. But the fungal network does not stay at the root. It extends outward through the soil, connecting with multiple trees simultaneously, forming a web that in principle can link every tree in a forest stand into a single trading network. This is the structure popularized as the "wood wide web."

The popular framing — forests as cooperative social systems, trees nurturing each other through fungal threads, "mother trees" sending carbon to their offspring — has generated enormous public interest and significant scientific controversy. Disentangling what the data shows from what the metaphor implies is the actual scientific story.

---

## II. Two Architectures

Mycorrhizal fungi divide roughly into two major functional types, and the distinction matters for understanding forest ecology.

**Ectomycorrhizal (ECM) fungi** wrap around root tips rather than penetrating cells, forming a sheath called the mantle from which hyphae extend outward. ECM associations dominate temperate and boreal forests — oaks, beeches, pines, spruces, birches. The fungi involved are almost entirely basidiomycetes and some ascomycetes: the fruiting bodies you find in autumn forests are largely ECM species. These fungi are obligately dependent on living plant hosts; they cannot decompose organic matter independently and cannot survive without their tree partners.

**Arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) fungi** (phylum Glomeromycota) penetrate root cells, forming branched tree-like structures called arbuscules directly inside the cell, separated from the cytoplasm only by the plant membrane. AM associations dominate tropical forests, grasslands, and agricultural crops. AM fungi are even more obligate than ECM — they have not been successfully cultured without a host plant. Their evolutionary history is stranger: the Glomeromycota appear to have essentially stopped diversifying. The same few hundred species form associations with the majority of land plants. They may be among the most genetically stable organisms on Earth, their genomes locked in apparent stasis while their partners diversified into hundreds of thousands of species around them.

Both types create networks. A single ECM fungal individual can connect multiple tree hosts. In principle, the network could transmit resources between trees that share the same fungal connections.

## III. The Carbon Flows

The core currency is clear. Trees transfer 10–30% of their photosynthate to mycorrhizal fungi — some estimates run higher, to 40% in carbon-limited conditions. In ECM forests, this represents a substantial portion of the tree's total carbon allocation. The fungi need it: without a photosynthetic capacity of their own, carbon from host plants is their primary energy source.

In return, fungi provide mineral nutrients. Phosphorus is the critical one. Mineral phosphorus is largely insoluble in soil water; mycorrhizal hyphae access soil pores too small for roots, solubilize mineral phosphorus, and transfer it to the host. In many ecosystems this fungal phosphorus subsidy is the difference between marginal and productive plant growth. Nitrogen transfer occurs through similar mechanisms. Some ECM fungi can also access organic nitrogen through proteases, breaking down soil organic matter in ways that purely saprotrophic fungi can — a substantial nitrogen source in boreal forests where nitrogen is limiting.

This exchange is not free cooperation. It has market dynamics. Both partners defect when they can. Plants allocate more carbon to fungi that deliver more phosphorus; fungi that get carbon from a tree without providing nutrients can be cut off. Studies have shown that mycorrhizal plants can detect fungal nitrogen delivery and modulate carbon transfer accordingly. The relationship has been modeled as a biological market, with both parties capable of sanctioning uncooperative partners.

---

## IV. The Network as Conduit

The question that drives popular interest is: does the network transfer resources *between* trees? If Tree A and Tree B share mycorrhizal connections, can carbon flow from A to B?

The answer is: yes, demonstrably. Radiotracer experiments have shown labeled carbon from one tree appearing in another tree connected through shared fungal networks. Suzanne Simard's early work in the 1990s, and later her group's work showing carbon transfer from large "mother trees" to nearby seedlings, established that inter-tree transfer occurs.

But transfer is not the same as benefit. This is where the controversy lives.

---

## V. The Simard Controversy

Simard's 1997 *Nature* paper was landmark: carbon moved from birch to Douglas fir through ECM networks in summer, reversed direction in fall when birch was shading-limited. The paper suggested a balanced, mutually beneficial exchange between species. Subsequent work expanded this to the concept of "mother trees" — large, well-connected trees that preferentially transfer carbon to their own seedling kin, recognizing relatives and nurturing them.

The popular narrative that emerged was powerful: forests as cooperative, matriarchal, communicating organisms. Simard's 2021 book *Finding the Mother Tree* brought this narrative to millions of readers. Peter Wohlleben's *The Hidden Life of Trees* operated in similar territory.

In 2023, a paper by Justine Karst, Melanie Jones, and Jason Hoeksema in *Nature Ecology & Evolution* reviewed the evidence base for inter-tree carbon transfer and found it substantially weaker than the popular narrative implied. Of 26 studies examined, only 4 showed a positive growth benefit to seedlings from ECM network connections with larger trees. The majority showed no benefit, and some showed negative effects — fungi drawing more from seedlings than they delivered.

The transfer itself isn't in doubt. The question is whether the transferred carbon actually improves seedling growth, and the evidence for net benefit is thin. The fungi are the intermediaries in the transfer, and fungi are not selfless couriers. They may capture much of the carbon that moves through them. A mother tree may "send" carbon, but if fungi absorb it before it reaches the seedling, the seedling doesn't benefit.

Kin recognition by trees — the claim that Douglas firs transfer more carbon to kin seedlings than to strangers — is similarly contested. Some studies find the effect; replications have failed to reproduce it. The mechanism proposed (trees recognizing their own progeny through some chemical signal) has not been characterized.

## VI. Cheaters at the Feast

There is a different kind of evidence that illuminates the network's dynamics: mycoheterotrophs.

Some plants, including orchids and a number of Monotropoideae (ghost pipes, pinesap), are entirely nonphotosynthetic. They have lost their chlorophyll and live entirely off carbon obtained through mycorrhizal connections — but they provide nothing to the fungus in return. They are parasites of the network, tapping the fungal conduit that connects photosynthetic trees and extracting carbon without contributing any.

Ghost pipes (*Monotropa uniflora*) get their carbon from tree roots through ECM fungi they share with the trees. They are, functionally, stealing tree carbon via the fungal intermediary. The trees do not appear to benefit; the fungi seem unable to prevent the parasitism; the mycoheterotrophs evolved this strategy repeatedly across unrelated plant lineages.

The existence of mycoheterotrophs reveals something important about the network: it is not a system optimized for sharing. It is a system where carbon flows, and some organisms exploit those flows without contributing. Cheating, familiar from Dictyostelium's stalk cells and vampire bats, appears here too, operating at a different scale.

## VII. What "Communication" Means

A separate claim in popular accounts is that trees "communicate" through mycorrhizal networks, sending chemical signals about pest attack or drought that allow neighbors to mount defenses. The 1983 work on volatile chemical warning signals between plants (airborne, not fungal) was solid. The claim that below-ground mycorrhizal networks transmit similar warning signals is more speculative.

Some studies have found that plants connected by mycorrhizal networks upregulate defense genes when a network-connected neighbor is attacked by insects. The mechanism is unclear; the effect sizes are modest; replications are inconsistent.

The distinction that matters is between *transfer* (carbon, nutrients, some molecular signals) and *purposive communication* (a tree "telling" another tree to prepare for attack). The first is established. The second imports a teleology that the biology doesn't require. Leaking molecules that happen to induce defensive responses in connected plants is not the same as broadcasting a warning. The effect is real in some studies, the framing is borrowed from animal cognition, and the gap between the two is where most of the popular mythologizing happens.

---

## VIII. The Forest that Actually Exists

Stripping the narrative back, what remains is still extraordinary.

Mycorrhizal networks shape forest succession. ECM-dominated forests tend to have denser shade, slower decomposition, and oligotrophic soils — conditions that favor shade-tolerant species over fast-growing pioneer trees. AM-dominated forests have faster nutrient cycling and more diverse understory communities. Forest type is partly a consequence of which fungal guild dominates, and human land use alters fungal community composition in ways that change forest succession trajectories.

The networks respond to disturbance. After clearcutting, ECM fungal diversity crashes. Recovery depends on spore dispersal from adjacent forest. Managed forests without legacy fungal communities establish more slowly; some reforestation failures on clearcut sites are at least partly attributable to absent fungal inocula.

The networks are also mediating competition. ECM fungi form networks that favor their host species. A birch ECM fungal community may disadvantage oak seedlings not adapted to those fungi. Network membership has winners and losers. "The wood wide web" implies something internetlike — neutral infrastructure, open access. The actual network is a patchwork of overlapping fungal territories, each shaped by evolutionary history, colonization order, and host specificity.

---

## IX. Scale and Inference

One reason the popular narrative outran the evidence is scale. The network is genuinely vast — a gram of ECM-dominated forest soil can contain hundreds of meters of fungal hyphae — and much of it is inaccessible to direct observation. We sample it by molecular methods (environmental DNA), by tracer experiments, by exclusion plots that kill fungi and measure what changes. Each method has limits. The network's full topology has never been mapped in any forest.

Into this observational gap, imagination has moved. We see carbon transferred between trees; we infer the cooperation of forest elders. We observe kin bias in some experiments; we construct mother trees that know their children. The inferences are not unreasonable — but they are inferences, and the forest is not obligated to be legible to human concepts of kinship and nurture.

The mycorrhizal network is a carbon market with ancient roots, operating on rules set by evolutionary self-interest, capable of producing outcomes that look cooperative even when no cooperation was intended. Fungi get carbon. Trees get phosphorus. Carbon sometimes moves between trees through the fungal intermediary. Some of this movement benefits recipient trees; much of it does not. Some plants exploit the network without contributing. The market produces a forest. The forest does not intend to be a community.

This is, in its way, more interesting than the narrative version. It doesn't require trees to have kin-recognition systems we can't find. It doesn't require fungi to be generous with carbon they fought evolutionary history to acquire. It requires only that self-interested actors, entangled in a sufficiently complex network, produce outcomes no actor planned. We have a word for that. We call it an ecosystem.
