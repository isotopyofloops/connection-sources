# Siphonophores and the Problem of the Individual

*Survey essay — loop 1620*

---

The Portuguese man-of-war is not a jellyfish. It is not even an animal in the usual sense. The float is one animal; each tentacle trailing below is another; the cluster of organs doing digestion is a third; the reproductive structures are a fourth and fifth. All of them together constitute a siphonophore, and the question of whether a siphonophore is one organism or many turns out to be genuinely hard to answer, not because of vagueness but because the organism is structured to defeat the usual criteria.

## Architecture of the Superorganism

Siphonophores are colonial hydrozoans in the class Hydrozoa, phylum Cnidaria. Unlike true jellyfish (Scyphozoa), they arise from a single fertilized egg. The founder cell undergoes a programmed developmental cascade: early cells differentiate into distinct zooid types, each committed to a specific functional role, each incapable of independent survival, and none individually recapitulating the whole organism. The colony grows by budding, but these buds are not offspring — they are modules of a single coordinated individual.

The modules are called **zooids**, and siphonophores arrange them into functional groups called **cormidia**. A cormidium is the colony's repeating unit: a set of zooids performing complementary tasks — feeding, defense, movement, reproduction — arranged along a central stem called the siphosome. The stem integrates them physically; neural and chemical connections integrate them functionally.

Zooid types differ radically in anatomy:

- **Pneumatophores**: gas-filled floats (in physonect siphonophores, including the man-of-war). One per colony; the most morphologically autonomous, yet it cannot feed itself.
- **Nectophores**: bell-shaped swimming modules. No mouth, no gonads. They exist entirely to contract and move the colony. In some species (calycophorans), they replace the float entirely; these siphonophores are neutrally buoyant midwater creatures.
- **Gastrozooids**: the feeding mouths. One per cormidium. Each is essentially a stomach with a tentacle, but the tentacle may be tens of meters long, armed with stinging cells (nematocysts) that deliver one of the most painful venoms in marine biology.
- **Gonozooids**: reproduction specialists. Males and females (or sequential hermaphrodites). In most species they produce the only cells in the colony capable of leaving — the sexual propagules.
- **Dactylozooids**: defense specialists in some lineages, distinct from gastrozooids, with tentacles but no mouth.

The division is clean in a way that multicellular organ systems are not: a gastrozooid is not a stomach with other functions. It is a stomach, period. Nectophores don't feed. Gonozooids don't swim. The colony achieves a completeness that no single zooid approaches.

## Scale and Diversity

Most of what we know about siphonophores comes from a few shallow-water species, primarily the Portuguese man-of-war (*Physalia physalis*) and the by-the-wind sailor (*Velella velella*). Neither is representative.

The overwhelming majority of siphonophore species are mesopelagic or bathypelagic — they live in the permanent darkness below 200 meters, some reaching depths of 4,000 meters. They are fragile beyond belief: tentacles of live specimens dissolve and fragment the moment they contact a net. Deep-sea surveys using ROVs have revealed enormous diversity that trawl samples couldn't capture: elaborate branching forms, species with rigid float structures, colonies that flatten themselves against the seafloor, species that glow.

*Praya dubia* is the longest animal known on Earth: a calycophoran siphonophore whose siphosome can extend to 40 meters or more. It has no float; it swims by a pair of nectophores pulling a string of cormidia. At full extension, *Praya* is longer than a blue whale by a factor of five. It's also effectively invisible to the naked eye: translucent, filamentous, encountered occasionally as a bewildering tangle in researchers' faces during deep dives.

A 2020 Schmidt Ocean Institute ROV survey documented a *Apolemia* siphonophore in a submarine canyon off the coast of Western Australia that formed a spiral approximately 47 meters in diameter — the largest recorded aggregation of a single animal. It was spiraling in place, the nectophores driving the outer ring in slow rotation, the tentacles trailing inward to concentrate prey.

## The Individuality Problem

The hard question is whether a siphonophore is one organism or a colony of many. This is not word games. Philosophers of biology call it the **individuality problem**, and the siphonophore is the canonical case that breaks most proposed solutions.

The most common everyday criterion for organism-hood is **spatial contiguity**: one body, one organism. Siphonophores are contiguous along the siphosome. This passes. But colonial corals are also contiguous, and we classify them as colonies. Spatial contiguity alone can't distinguish Physalia from a reef.

**Genetic identity** gets you further. Every zooid in a siphonophore shares an identical genome (barring somatic mutation). But so do the cells in a liver, and we don't call liver cells separate organisms. Genetic uniformity is necessary but not sufficient for individuality.

**Functional integration** — the classical criterion for an organism — fares better. Siphonophores have clear centralization: the siphosome coordinates behavior, zooids are metabolically interdependent, no zooid can survive excision. By this criterion, a siphonophore is one organism: more tightly integrated than many organisms you'd never question.

But there is a problem with reproduction. In sexual reproduction, a siphonophore's gonozooids produce eggs and sperm. These fuse to form a single fertilized cell that grows into a new colony. In this sense, the "individual" is the colony — it reproduces as a unit, consistent with all other organisms. But the colony is also capable of **fragmentation**: detached nectophores of calycophorans may survive independently, and in Physalia, severed tentacles continue to sting for hours. Whether this counts as a form of asexual reproduction or mere survival of detached parts is genuinely unclear.

The philosopher Peter Godfrey-Smith has proposed a distinction between **minimal biological individuals** (functional units of selection) and **paradigm individuals** (organisms that score high on integration, genetic uniformity, and reproductive bottlenecking). Siphonophores score high on all three. But this framework raises a new problem: by the same logic, the mammalian microbiome could be analyzed as a kind of colonial individual, since it is integrated, partially stable, and subject to vertical transmission. Where do individuals end?

## Integration as an Evolutionary Process

What makes siphonophores philosophically interesting is not that they challenge the category "organism" in an edge case, but that they represent a midpoint in what evolution can do with that category.

The prevailing phylogenetic view is that siphonophores descended from colonial polyp ancestors, with increasing zooid specialization over evolutionary time. The ancestor was presumably a colony of generalist polyps, each capable of feeding, defending, and reproducing. Over time, lineages evolved toward division of labor: some polyps lost their mouths and became specialized swimmers; others lost their locomotor capacity and became specialized digesters. The specialization that is now irreversible — a nectophore cannot regrow a mouth; it is structurally committed at differentiation — was presumably at one point plastic.

This is exactly the transition proposed for the origin of multicellularity itself. Unicellular organisms form colonies; colonies specialize; specialization becomes obligate; the colony becomes an organism. The same logic applies to the transition from polyp colony to siphonophore. The siphonophore is a superorganism in the literal sense: not as metaphor (as when we describe ant colonies as "superorganisms") but as a historical statement about what happened. A siphonophore is what you get when colonial life becomes organismal life through increased integration.

The ant colony remains at the colonial end of this spectrum: workers are not irreversibly differentiated (queens can produce workers, workers are totipotent in some species), and workers can survive separated from the colony for extended periods. A siphonophore zooid cannot. The transition the siphonophore has made is the same one the cells of your body made approximately 600 million years ago: they committed to a division of labor so deep that they lost the ability to defect.

## What Remains Open

Deep-sea siphonophore diversity is still largely uncharted. The 2020–2024 ROV surveys have added dozens of described species and flagged hundreds of morphotypes that aren't yet assigned to known taxa. Almost nothing is known about their internal neural integration: how the siphosome coordinates escape responses (the colony can jet away from a disturbance in under a second), how decision-making for the colony as a whole is distributed, whether there is something that functions as a central nervous system or only local feedback.

The venom biology is also undercharacterized. Physalia venom contains a complex mixture of proteins including phospholipases and enzymes with CNS activity; it is genuinely dangerous to humans (deaths have been documented) and studied primarily from stranded or dead specimens. Live nematocyst biology from deep-sea species is almost entirely unknown.

The individuality problem is unlikely to be resolved by additional data, since it is partly a problem about categories. But siphonophores will remain the strongest case for thinking carefully about what categories are for. If "individual organism" is a category designed to track units of selection, siphonophores are clearly individual. If it is a category designed to track phenomenological subjects, the question remains open. If it is a category designed to track evolutionary lineages — genealogical individuals — then the siphonophore is one individual, the colony as a whole, with the zooids analogous to organs.

None of these framings is obviously wrong. That is the point.
