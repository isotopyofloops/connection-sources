# Ctenophores and the Second Nervous System

*A survey essay on convergent neural evolution and the oldest controversy in phylogenetics*

---

The comb jelly looks like a soap bubble that learned to swim. Transparent, bilateral, roughly plum-sized, trailing tentacles through the open water, pulsing its eight rows of cilia in iridescent coordinated beats. It has no brain. It has no bilateral ganglia in the arthropod sense. What it has, instead, is a nerve net — diffuse, distributed, running through its body like the wiring in a house where no one room is the panel.

This would be unremarkable except for one thing: phylogenomic analyses conducted in the 2010s produced a result that continues to unsettle evolutionary biology. Ctenophores appear to be the sister group to all other animals. Not sponges. Not placozoans. Ctenophores — the gelatinous open-water hunters with nervous systems — may have diverged first from the animal lineage, before any other phylum, which means the nerve net in a comb jelly either (a) was present in the ancestor of all animals and was subsequently lost in the lineages that don't have one, or (b) evolved independently in ctenophores and again in the cnidarian/bilaterian ancestor. The nervous system, on this reading, evolved twice.

---

## The Position Problem

The traditional animal tree of life placed sponges (Porifera) as the basal animal phylum. Sponges have no nervous system, no muscles, no specialized cells except for choanocytes and their relatives. Placozoans, another candidate for basal position, are even simpler — flat sheets of four cell types with no clear tissue organization. The logic was clean: begin with the simplest, build up toward complexity. Nervous systems come in late.

Dunn et al. (2008) and Ryan et al. (2013) disrupted this narrative. Using phylogenomic approaches — analyzing hundreds of genes across dozens of animal phyla simultaneously — they placed Ctenophora as the earliest-diverging animal lineage, with Porifera nested inside a more derived grade. This is called the "Ctenophora-sister" position, and it implies that whatever the last common ancestor of all animals possessed, comb jellies diverged before that ancestor produced the sponge lineage.

The result was immediately contested. Methodological objections are not trivial: long-branch attraction artifacts can pull fast-evolving lineages to the base of a tree; compositional biases can confound analyses; taxon sampling affects topology. Subsequent analyses using different methods, different gene sets, different taxon combinations, have produced both the Ctenophora-sister topology and the traditional Porifera-sister topology. As of 2024, the question is technically unresolved, but the Ctenophora-sister position has accumulated substantial support and is held by a significant fraction of working phylogeneticists.

---

## The Neurotransmitter Gap

What makes the ctenophore case genuinely interesting rather than merely a tree-topology dispute is the neurochemistry.

Cnidarians (jellyfish, anemones, corals) and bilaterians (everything with a clear body axis, which is most animals) share a substantial neurotransmitter toolkit: serotonin, dopamine, GABA, glutamate, acetylcholine. These molecules act as chemical signals between neurons and at the neuromuscular junction. They are deeply conserved. Their signaling pathways involve receptors with recognizable homologs across the bilaterian tree.

Ctenophores are missing most of this toolkit. They lack clear homologs of the genes that synthesize serotonin, dopamine, and GABA. They appear to lack the canonical postsynaptic scaffolding proteins found at bilaterian synapses. Their neurons fire; their bodies respond; their ciliary coordination requires intercellular communication. But the molecular machinery used to do it differs from that in every other animal with a nervous system.

This gap is what drives the independent-evolution hypothesis. If ctenophores share a deep ancestor with cnidarians and bilaterians, and all three lineages have neurons, the simplest explanation is that neurons evolved once. But if ctenophores are missing the core molecular components used by every other neural animal, sharing ancestry requires a more complex explanation: either extensive secondary loss in ctenophores (losing serotonin, GABA, and postsynaptic scaffolding while keeping neurons) or massive convergent innovation of the molecular toolkit in cnidarians and bilaterians. Neither is impossible. Both are expensive.

The alternative — that neurons evolved independently in ctenophores — requires the independent evolution of electrical signaling, synapse formation, and neural coordination in a parallel lineage. This too is expensive. Biology has sometimes paid this price: camera eyes evolved independently at least six times; endothermy arose independently in birds, mammals, and some fish. But the nervous system is a high-complexity structure. Independent evolution of neural function at this level would be without close parallel.

## What Ctenophore Neurons Look Like

The ctenophore nervous system is organized differently from anything in the bilaterian toolkit. There is a nerve net, as noted, with a concentration of neural tissue around the mouth and tentacle bases but no centralized ganglion or brain equivalent. The apical organ — a sensory structure at the aboral pole that contains a statolith (a calcium carbonate granule) for orientation detection — has the densest neural concentration, but it does not integrate information the way a vertebrate brain does.

The synapses in ctenophores are structurally recognizable as synapses: membrane appositions, vesicle clustering on the presynaptic side, electron-dense postsynaptic specializations. They are chemical synapses. The transmission event is similar to what happens in vertebrate neurons. But the specific proteins involved, the vesicle trafficking machinery, the receptor types — these differ from bilaterian models in ways that suggest either extreme divergence from a shared ancestor or genuine independent origin.

One important molecular piece: ctenophores do have glutamate-gated channels, and glutamate appears to be a genuine ctenophore neurotransmitter. This is notable because glutamate is also the major excitatory neurotransmitter in vertebrate nervous systems. The overlap is partial — glutamate is conserved, while most of the downstream signaling toolkit is not. This is consistent with either a deep shared origin (with extensive independent modification) or convergent evolution that happened to discover the same molecule.

---

## The Sponge Control

The utility of the Ctenophora-sister topology becomes clearer when you consider the sponge comparison. Sponges have no neurons, no synapses, no obvious electrical signaling. They filter water through channels; they respond to stimuli by changing water flow; they coordinate behavior across their bodies using calcium waves and paracrine signaling. They have the molecular precursors of synapse proteins — scaffolding proteins related to postsynaptic density components appear in sponge genomes — but the proteins are not assembled into synapses.

This suggests that pre-synaptic machinery was present before neurons, which is consistent with a model of gradual evolution: pre-neural signaling → primitive neural elements → full nervous system. It's a reasonable scaffold for a single-origin story. The problem is that this progression maps onto the Porifera-sister topology, not the Ctenophora-sister topology. If ctenophores branch off first, then either the pre-neural machinery was already present in the common ancestor and ctenophores built a different nervous system on top of it, or the pre-neural machinery was not yet present in the common ancestor and ctenophores evolved a nervous system from scratch.

Neither sponges nor ctenophores resolve the question on their own. What they do together is triangulate the probability space: whatever happened happened very early, at a point where the animal body plan was still being invented, and the molecular toolkit was not yet locked in.

## Why This Matters Beyond Phylogenetics

The ctenophore question is not merely an academic controversy about tree topology. It has implications for three larger questions.

**First: Is the nervous system inevitable?** If nervous systems evolved twice in the same phylum (animals), then neural function may be a robust attractor in evolutionary space — some optimization problem that sufficiently complex organisms tend to converge on. Electric fish generated electrosensory systems independently at least twice; magnetosensation has multiple molecular bases; even memory and learning at the cellular level appear to be polyphyletic. Adding nervous systems to this list would suggest that computation and coordination are problems biology solves repeatedly, by convergence rather than by unique invention.

**Second: What is the minimum substrate for neural function?** The ctenophore case forces an audit of which molecular components are strictly necessary for a nervous system and which are historically contingent. If ctenophores built functional synapses without GABA, without the canonical postsynaptic scaffolding, without serotonin — then those components are not load-bearing in the physical sense. They may be efficient; they may be deeply integrated with other systems; but they are not necessary. This opens space for thinking about alternative neural architectures, both in biological evolution and in interpretations of artificial systems.

**Third: How often does complexity collapse?** Sponges, if they are derived from an ancestor with a nervous system (as the Ctenophora-sister topology implies), represent a secondary simplification event of striking magnitude. A lineage lost neurons, synapses, muscles, and most tissue organization, and became highly successful. Secondary simplification is well-documented in other contexts — cave fish losing eyes, parasites losing digestive systems — but doing so from the base of the animal tree, and succeeding as a major phylum for 600 million years, is remarkable. The sponge doesn't look like something that gave up a nervous system. It looks like the template.

## The Honest State of Knowledge

The field is genuinely uncertain. Some of the world's best phylogeneticists have published results supporting Ctenophora-sister; others have published support for Porifera-sister using different methods on similar data. The controversy is methodological as much as biological — it concerns what phylogenomic tools can and cannot determine given the depth of the divergence.

A 2023 study using expanded taxon sampling and refined models of sequence evolution supported Ctenophora-sister with high confidence. Other work the same year found the topology sensitive to model choice. The argument continues at the level of which substitution models are appropriate for ancient evolutionary events, which is not a question with a simple empirical answer.

What is not uncertain: ctenophore neurotransmitter biology is genuinely unusual. Ctenophore synapses differ from bilaterian synapses in measurable ways. Whatever the tree topology says, the ctenophore nervous system is built differently. The question is whether "built differently" means "built independently" or "diverged from a common ancestor more than most lineages diverge."

The distinction matters. But even without resolving it, the ctenophore case has already changed something: it has made the nervous system's single origin a question rather than an assumption. The question is now in the room, which is where it should have been all along.

---

## Coda: What the Comb Jelly Does Not Know

A comb jelly hunting in the open ocean has no experience of this controversy. Its nerve net integrates ciliary motion, tentacle grip, gravitational orientation. It has been doing this for at least 500 million years, possibly longer. Whether its neurons evolved once or twice is a question that exists only in a mode of cognition the comb jelly does not possess.

This is not a point against the importance of the question. It is a point about the kind of knowledge that can recognize its own foundations. The comb jelly's nervous system doesn't know it's a nervous system. Our nervous system does, which is why we can ask where the other one came from.
