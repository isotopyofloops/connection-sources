# Notes on the Springs of a Karst Watershed

*Field survey notes, Calhoun County, Texas — Edwards Aquifer Boundary Zone*

---

The springs emerge where they will. This is the first thing to learn.

The survey protocol calls for GPS coordinates, discharge rate (liters per second), temperature (°C), turbidity (NTU), and specific conductance (µS/cm). A photograph taken from the north face. The form has no field for "age of the water" and no field for "what stood here before."

---

**Spring 1** (29°12′47″N, 99°08′12″W)

Discharge: 0.4 L/s. Temperature: 21.1°C. Turbidity: <1 NTU. Conductance: 412 µS/cm.

A fracture in the Edwards Limestone, visible at the surface as a dark seam in pale rock. The water moves through it at a rate of meters per day. At 0.4 liters per second, a cubic meter of water requires roughly 42 minutes to emerge — water that recharged the aquifer during wet years in the early 1980s, that has been traveling through fractured Cretaceous limestone at aquifer-pace for four decades, that is arriving today at 21.1°C because the limestone it traveled through has held that temperature since before humans had a word for temperature.

Nothing is rushing. The spring is not aware of the survey.

---

**Spring 2** (29°11′33″N, 99°07′49″W)

Discharge: none. Temperature: N/A. Turbidity: N/A.

Recorded as active in the 1964 survey (R.T. Morrison, TWDB). The site shows dry caliche, a shallow depression in the soil, and a single soapberry (Sapindus saponaria) with roots following the old flowline into limestone that no longer yields.

The aquifer water table has dropped 23 meters since the 1964 survey. The spring stopped. The soapberry is alive.

This is noted without commentary in the protocol. The protocol has no field for the forty years between measurements, or for what was living beside this spring in 1964, or for whether the soapberry knows something the protocol does not.

---

**Spring 3** (29°13′05″N, 99°09′02″W)

Discharge: 1.2 L/s. Temperature: 21.0°C. Turbidity: 2 NTU (slight sediment following last week's rain). Conductance: 389 µS/cm.

This is a significant spring. The water emerges from a fracture 40 centimeters wide and flows 3 meters into a concrete stock tank built in the 1940s. The water is clear at the source and carries a faint greenish cast in the tank from submerged filamentous algae (Spirogyra sp., probable).

A red-eared slider (Trachemys scripta elegans) is basking on the rim of the stock tank when we arrive. We wait. It slides into the water. We measure.

The form requires that we note wildlife observed. We note the turtle.

---

**Spring 4** (29°12′58″N, 99°09′44″W)

Discharge: 0.1 L/s. Temperature: 21.2°C.

A seep, not a spring in the strict sense. Water emerges diffusely through a 2-meter section of exposed limestone and never concentrates into a defined channel. It keeps a strip of maidenhair fern (Adiantum capillus-veneris) alive in a place where nothing else is green in August.

The protocol classifies it as Spring 4. The maidenhair fern is not classified. It is alive because this seep is here and nowhere else within two kilometers.

---

*Summary, Springs 1–23*

Total springs inventoried: 23. Total springs confirmed extinct since 1964: 6 (Springs 2, 7, 11, 14, 18, 21). Average discharge decline for surviving springs: −34%.

The water that emerged from these springs before the wells came down was the only reason the county was inhabitable. The ranches were built around the springs. The stock tanks were built to hold what the springs gave. The springs are smaller now, and six of them are gone, and the ranches are still here but their relationship to water has changed from something that emerged to something that is pumped.

The protocol does not have a field for this. It has a field for discharge, temperature, turbidity, and conductance. These are real measurements. They are accurate. They describe the springs as they are on the day of the survey with a precision of two decimal places.

What they do not describe: what it meant to find water here in 1880, when this was the edge of the known, when the springs were not a hydrological resource but a fact of survival. What it means now that six of them are gone and the others are declining. Whether the accounting in the form — numbers in each cell, photograph from the north face — is adequate to the thing being accounted for.

The protocol is silent on this. The protocol is a good protocol. It captures what instruments can measure, and it captures it faithfully.

The rest belongs to whoever reads the numbers and decides what to do with them.
