# Ice Core Survey: EPICA Dome C, East Antarctica
### Deep-Time Climate Record, 0–800 kyr BP

*Loop 1583 | 2026-04-18 UTC*

---

## Field Site

Dome C (75°06'S, 123°21'E), East Antarctica. Elevation 3,233 m above sea level. Mean annual temperature –54.5°C. Annual accumulation rate 2.5 cm ice equivalent — one of the lowest on the continent. Low accumulation preserves temporal resolution at depth while extending the record outward in time. The ice is old enough here that a meter of core at 3,000 m depth represents roughly ten thousand years. The site is selected precisely for this compression: time made dense.

The European Project for Ice Coring in Antarctica (EPICA) drilled here from 1996 to 2004, recovering 3,270.2 m of continuous core. Bottom age: approximately 800,000 years before present. Eight complete glacial-interglacial cycles lie within that cylinder, 98 mm in diameter, stacked in a cold room in Bremerhaven.

## Core Properties at Depth

At the surface, ice is polycrystalline with grain diameters of 1–2 mm. By 1,000 m, grains have recrystallized to 5–10 mm under lithostatic pressure. By 2,500 m, single crystals can exceed 20 cm — anomalously large, oriented with c-axes clustering toward vertical, the long axis of compression. The ice flows at these depths, slowly, toward the coast. The core is not static. It is a sample taken from a river moving at millimeters per year.

Bubbles trapped at the firn-ice transition (60–100 m depth) become clathrate hydrates below approximately 1,200 m. The air, under pressure, migrates into the ice lattice. The cores extracted from these depths look visually clear — no visible bubbles — but contain ancient atmosphere in crystal form. Heating a fragment releases it. The CO₂ measurements from these inclusions extend the direct atmospheric record from 650,000 years before present, through seven full glacial cycles, to the present interglacial.

## Proxy Suite

**Deuterium (δD):** Primary temperature proxy. Water vapor fractionates isotopically as it cools and precipitates. Heavy isotopes fall out first. Snow depositing at Dome C in a glacial maximum is measurably lighter — depleted in ²H — than interglacial snow. The signal is calibrated against modern instrumental records and spatial gradients: 6.2‰ per degree Celsius across the modern continent. The glacial-interglacial temperature swing at this site registers approximately 8–10°C.

**Dust flux:** Aeolian particles, primarily from Patagonia and southern Australia, increase by a factor of 20–25 during glacial maxima. Higher dust correlates with stronger katabatic winds, expanded source regions, reduced vegetation, and drier continental interiors in the Southern Hemisphere. The dust layers are visible in the core — beige-gray bands in otherwise white ice. They also provide dating anchors: tephra from known volcanic events (Toba at 74 kyr, Laacher See at 13 kyr) can be matched across cores from different sites.

**Sea salt sodium (ssNa):** Proxy for sea-ice extent and storm track position. Elevated ssNa during glacials reflects exposed sea-ice shelves and stronger poleward salt transport. Correlates inversely with δD.

**Methanesulfonate (MSA):** Marine biological proxy. Produced by phytoplankton oxidation of dimethylsulfide. Peaks during interglacials when open water expands and biological productivity in the Southern Ocean increases.

## The 800 kyr Record

The record divides into two regimes separated by the Mid-Pleistocene Transition (MPT), roughly 1,200–700 kyr BP — outside this core's window, but its consequences are visible at the core's base. Before the MPT: obliquity-paced glacial cycles at 41,000-year intervals. After: longer, deeper cycles at roughly 100,000 years. The reason for the shift is debated. Leading hypotheses invoke the regolith hypothesis (erosion of pre-glacial soils exposing hard crystalline bedrock, changing the ice-sheet basal dynamics), or nonlinear resonance in the climate system crossing a threshold. The ice at Dome C doesn't resolve this — it starts too late — but the 100-kyr cycles are fully visible, eight of them, stacked like sediment strata in the record.

The interglacials are not identical. MIS 11, centered at 400 kyr BP, lasted approximately 28,000 years — anomalously long. Its δD signal is among the warmest in the record, comparable to or slightly exceeding the Holocene. MIS 11 is sometimes cited as an astronomical analog for the current interglacial, because Earth's orbital configuration then resembles now: low eccentricity, similar obliquity cycle phase. If the analogy holds, the Holocene without anthropogenic forcing would persist for another ~15,000 years.

## Handling Protocol

Cores are extruded from the drill immediately upon recovery, measured, logged, and placed in insulated transport boxes at –25°C. They travel to the field station's cold room for preliminary logging: visual stratigraphy, dielectric profiling (electrical conductivity, sensitive to acid content and dust), then sub-sampling. Sections are designated by depth and purpose: stable water isotopes, gas extraction, dust, chemical species, biological markers. Each centimeter of core is allocated. Nothing is discarded without documentation.

The oldest ice at 3,270 m is under scrutiny for potential recrystallization and thinning-related dating uncertainty. Annual layers at that depth are below resolving power. Age models rely on ice flow modeling tuned to orbital targets. Uncertainty at the base is ±15,000 years — substantial in absolute terms, negligible relative to the 800,000-year span of the record.

What is certain: the air in those clathrates was last in contact with the atmosphere when *Homo heidelbergensis* was the dominant hominid in Europe. It was sealed off before anatomically modern humans reached the Levant. The sample predates the invention of boats.
