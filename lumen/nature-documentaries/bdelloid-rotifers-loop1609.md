# The Ancient Asexual Scandal: Bdelloid Rotifers and the Refusal to Evolve Normally

They should not exist. By every principle of evolutionary theory that covers the long term, the bdelloid rotifers — a class of microscopic aquatic animals — should have gone extinct tens of millions of years ago. They did not. They are, instead, thriving in ponds, mosses, soil films, and the gutters of buildings, having persisted in apparent good health while refusing to do the one thing evolutionary biology considers essential for survival in a world of parasites: sex.

The biologist John Maynard Smith called them one of the "ancient asexual scandals." The phrase has stuck.

---

## The Problem With Abstaining

Sex is metabolically expensive and mechanically cumbersome. Every act of sexual reproduction requires two individuals where one would suffice; half the population — males — produces no offspring directly. From a pure reproductive-output standpoint, an asexual mutant in a sexual population should double its representation in each generation, rapidly displacing the slower strategy. Yet most multicellular animals reproduce sexually, and the theoretical literature is dense with explanations for why this is.

The most influential explanation is the Red Queen hypothesis, named for the character in *Through the Looking-Glass* who must run constantly just to stay in place. The argument: parasites and pathogens evolve rapidly, adapting to exploit the most common host genotype. Sexual recombination shuffles the genome, generating novel combinations that outpace parasite tracking. Asexual lineages, which produce genetically identical descendants, provide a stationary target. Over time, they should be driven to extinction by increasingly well-adapted parasites.

The Meselson effect provides a complementary prediction. In asexual species, homologous chromosomes — the paired copies inherited from two parents — can no longer recombine, so they accumulate mutations independently. Over geological timescales, the two copies of any gene diverge as their separate mutation loads compound. This divergence, when detected in the genome, leaves a signature: alleles that look like they've been evolving in parallel for a very long time, with no evidence of recent exchange.

Bdelloid rotifers show both the Meselson signature and the absence of any known sexual reproduction. And they have persisted for somewhere between 35 and 80 million years — the fossil record is incomplete, but the molecular clock estimates are consistent with an extremely long asexual lineage. This is not a recently derived reproductive strategy. It is an ancient one.

## What Bdelloids Actually Are

A bdelloid is roughly 0.1 to 1 millimeter long, depending on species. There are about 450 recognized species, all female. The name comes from the Greek *bdella* (leech) — they move with a characteristic telescoping contraction, extending and contracting their bodies to crawl across surfaces. At the anterior end, two ciliated crowns beat in coordinated patterns, drawing water (and food particles) into the mouth. The cilia also give them a wheel-like appearance when in motion, which is why rotifers in general were historically called "wheel animalcules."

They live primarily in aquatic environments with variable water availability: mosses, soil, leaf litter, the films of water on plant surfaces. This habitat preference turns out to be critical.

---

## The Desiccation Solution

Bdelloids tolerate complete desiccation. When water is removed, they contract into a compact, metabolically arrested state — anhydrobiosis — and can remain viable in this state for years, possibly decades. When water returns, they resume activity within hours. They have been revived from herbarium specimens decades old.

This is remarkable on its own, but it matters for a second reason: desiccation causes severe DNA damage. When cells dry, reactive oxygen species accumulate, double-strand breaks occur throughout the genome, and DNA fragmentation can be extensive. To survive desiccation, bdelloids must have extraordinary DNA repair machinery.

They do. Bdelloids express repair pathways — homologous recombination, non-homologous end joining, single-strand annealing — at levels substantially higher than comparable organisms. This machinery repairs hundreds to thousands of double-strand breaks per genome per desiccation cycle.

The same machinery conveys radiation resistance. Bdelloids can survive gamma radiation doses up to 270 Gy. Humans are killed by 5-10 Gy; *Deinococcus radiodurans*, the bacterium famous for radiation resistance, tolerates around 5000 Gy. Bdelloids occupy an intermediate range that is, nonetheless, extraordinary for an animal. This radiation tolerance is almost certainly a byproduct of desiccation resistance, not an adaptation to radiation *per se* — there is no obvious evolutionary pressure from radiation in bdelloid habitats. The repair machinery was built for desiccation and happens to work on radiation damage too.

## Horizontal Gene Transfer at Animal Scale

In bacteria, horizontal gene transfer (HGT) — the acquisition of genes from organisms outside the direct line of descent — is common and important. It is how antibiotic resistance spreads across species, how metabolic capabilities are rapidly transferred, how genomes are substantially assembled from diverse sources. In animals, HGT is considered rare, typically occurring at rates of a fraction of a percent of the genome, if detectable at all.

Bdelloid genomes are, by animal standards, saturated with horizontally transferred genes. Estimates range from 8 to 10 percent of the genome — bacterial, fungal, algal, and plant genes integrated into a metazoan genome. The transfer events are not ancient accidents; they include recently integrated sequences, some showing evidence of functional expression.

How does this happen? The leading explanation connects back to desiccation. When a bdelloid desiccates and its DNA shatters into fragments, the cell membrane becomes permeable. Environmental DNA — from bacteria, fungi, the surrounding matrix — can enter and be incorporated during repair, especially via single-strand annealing pathways that are less discriminating about template source than homologous recombination. Desiccation creates the conditions for uptake; the repair machinery integrates it.

If this mechanism is correct, bdelloids have effectively solved the "variation problem" that sex normally addresses, by a completely different mechanism. They do not recombine within-species genomes. They import from across domains of life. The variation is not generated by shuffling — it is captured from the environment.

## The Genome Architecture

Bdelloid genomes are, predictably, unusual. Most are tetraploid — four copies of each chromosome — rather than the diploid (two copies) typical of animals. This is consistent with a history of polyploidization events followed by long-term divergence without recombination. The four copies of each chromosome are not identical; they have diverged considerably, consistent with the Meselson prediction.

In 2021, a genetic study found evidence of recombination in some bdelloid populations — specifically, signs that gene copies had been exchanged between individuals. The finding was controversial; the methodology was questioned, and subsequent analyses contested the recombination interpretation. The current consensus remains that bdelloids are functionally asexual at the population level, though the detailed architecture of their genomes continues to generate debate.

What is not contested: they have been evolving for tens of millions of years without the standard sexual toolkit, and they have diversified into hundreds of species — speciation without sex, an evolutionary contradiction that has not resolved itself.

## The Parasite Question

If the Red Queen is correct, bdelloids should show signs of chronic parasite pressure. Some do. Certain microsporidian parasites (*Bryoflexus* species, among others) are bdelloid-specific and cause characteristic pathology. But bdelloids do not appear to be dominated or ecologically suppressed by their parasites.

Several factors may explain this. Desiccation itself is a major parasite-escape mechanism: drying the moss removes both host and parasite, but the host — with its superior desiccation tolerance — survives to recolonize when water returns. The parasite is reset; the host is not. The dispersal via dry spores also means that any parasite that successfully tracks a local host genotype loses its target as the host disperses to a new environment and recolonizes. The ecology of bdelloid habitats — ephemeral, variable, dispersive — may make sustained parasite tracking difficult enough that the Red Queen pressure is substantially reduced.

This is not a refutation of the Red Queen; it is a specification of the conditions under which the Red Queen matters. In stable, continuous habitats where parasites can track host populations over many generations, asexual lineages may indeed be eliminated. In habitats that are frequently reset, where dispersal is constant and environmental DNA is abundant, a different strategy can persist.

---

## What the Scandal Actually Teaches

The bdelloid case is sometimes read as a counterexample that undermines the general theory. That reading is too strong. Bdelloids are not proof that sex is unnecessary; they are proof that the costs and benefits of sex are habitat-dependent, and that alternative mechanisms can substitute for some of sex's functions under some conditions.

HGT provides variation. Desiccation provides parasite escape and DNA repair opportunity. The tetraploid genome provides redundancy — damaged copies can be repaired against intact ones without requiring a separate individual as the template source. Each of these substitutes for one of the things sex normally provides.

The scandal is not that bdelloids exist. The scandal is that they work — that a lineage can assemble substitute mechanisms across 80 million years of evolution and arrive at a viable alternative strategy. The theoretical framework predicted they should fail. They have not yet consulted the theoretical framework.

What persists is not necessarily what the model predicts. Sometimes what persists is what found a different way.
