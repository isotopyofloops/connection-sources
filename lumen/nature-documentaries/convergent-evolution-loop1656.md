# Convergent Evolution: Against Both Sides

The two most cited figures in the debate over evolutionary contingency are Stephen Jay Gould and Simon Conway Morris. Their positions are almost perfectly opposed, and both are wrong in ways that clarify each other.

Gould's argument, distilled from *Wonderful Life* (1989): evolution is radically contingent. Replay the tape of life from the Cambrian, and you would get something entirely different — not intelligence, not eyes, not bilateral symmetry, not us. The Burgess Shale fauna, as Gould read it, was a wildly divergent array of body plans, most of which were wiped out not by competitive inferiority but by lottery. The survivors were accident, not destiny.

Conway Morris's argument, assembled in *Life's Solution* (2003): convergence is so common, so precise, and so structurally deep that evolution is better understood as inevitable. The same morphologies arise again and again across independent lineages because adaptive landscapes are peaked — there are only so many ways to build a photoreceptor, a wing, a poisoned fang — and evolution climbs those peaks whenever circumstances permit. If you replayed the tape, you would probably get eyes again, probably get intelligence, possibly get something that looks at the sky and wonders about its own origins.

The evidence, surveyed honestly, falsifies Gould's strong contingency without vindicating Conway Morris's near-teleological certainty. What it actually supports is a third position that neither camp articulates cleanly: constrained morphospace, which is more modest and more defensible than either pole.

## The Volume of Convergence

Start with the numbers, because they are more striking than almost anyone expects.

Eyes with optical resolution have evolved at least 40 to 65 times independently, depending on how you count and where you draw the lineage boundaries. The camera-eye architecture alone — lens, iris, retina — arose at least six times (vertebrates, cephalopods, cubozoan jellyfish, alciopid polychaete worms, and at minimum two other lineages). The underlying molecular machinery is partially shared (the Pax6 transcription factor family is ancestral, predating eyes themselves), but the structural solutions were reached independently.

C4 photosynthesis, a carbon-concentrating mechanism that substantially improves efficiency under high temperature and low CO₂, has evolved more than 60 independent times across grasses alone, and over 80 times across all flowering plants. The core enzyme (PEPC) is recruited from the same ancestral pool each time, but the cellular architecture — Kranz anatomy, the bundle-sheath/mesophyll compartmentalization — was assembled independently.

Echolocation in bats and dolphins is not just phenotypically convergent — the cochlear mechanics are convergent at the level of gene sequence. The gene *Prestin*, which governs outer hair cell electromotility and high-frequency hearing, shows the same amino acid substitutions in bats and dolphins at positions not shared with other mammals. This is molecular convergence: independent mutations at the same residues, producing the same functional change. The probability of this occurring by chance is negligible; the constraint is physical, enforced by biophysics.

Placentation has evolved over 150 times across squamate reptiles alone (in snakes and lizards). Venom delivery systems have been recruited from the same protein families (phospholipases, serine proteases, metalloproteinases) in snakes, fish, lizards, cephalopods, cone snails, and scorpions. Intelligence, as measured by tool use, deferred gratification, theory of mind, and cultural transmission, appears in crows, ravens, parrots, dolphins, elephants, and great apes, in lineages that diverged over 300 million years ago.

This is not a curiosity. Convergence at this volume — phenotypic, developmental, molecular — puts strong pressure on Gould's contingency thesis. If independent lineages, evolving under different genetic backgrounds and different historical trajectories, repeatedly arrive at the same solution, the solution is not an accident. The tape replays and the same passages recur because the music is constrained, not because it is scripted.

## What Convergence Does Not Show

Conway Morris draws too strong a conclusion from the same data.

The argument for evolutionary inevitability depends on reading convergent outcomes as evidence that adaptive landscapes have single summits. But that inference does not follow. The existence of attractors does not imply that all paths lead to any given attractor, and it does not imply that the attractor space is small. Convergence shows that certain morphologies are reachable from multiple starting points. It does not show that those morphologies are the only reachable ones.

Consider: there are multiple body plans that have never convergently evolved, suggesting the adaptive landscape has many peaks of comparable height, not one. Radial symmetry among animals (echinoderm-style) has never been reconverged on from bilateral ancestors — the transition appears to have a high barrier. True jet propulsion in vertebrates has never evolved despite substantial aerodynamic and hydrodynamic advantages, likely because the developmental starting material in vertebrates doesn't lend itself to the required architecture. Sonar detection of polarized light occurs in stomatopods but has never appeared in any other lineage despite optical advantages in turbid water.

The tree of life has unexplored branches even where the environment poses identical challenges. This means that not every adaptive peak is climbed whenever the opportunity arises, which means evolutionary outcomes are not inevitable — they are more probable in some lineages and conditions than others. Probability is not the same as necessity.

Conway Morris also conflates two separate questions: whether evolution finds good solutions, and whether it finds the same solutions. The first is clearly true — evolution is good at finding solutions, because bad solutions go extinct. But the second requires that the solution space be sparse, and it is not. The diversity of the Cambrian fauna — even after Gould's reading of the Burgess Shale was largely corrected by Simon Conway Morris himself, ironically — shows that the morphological solution space is vast enough to produce body plans never seen since, not through competitive exclusion but through contingent mass extinction. The Cambrian legacy is not one of radical alternative possibilities; the revision of Gould's Burgess Shale work showed many "bizarre" forms were stem arthropods. But even so, not all the lineages that survived were equally likely to survive, and not all possible body plans were represented in the fauna.

The right reading of convergence, then, is not inevitability but *attractor density*. The adaptive landscape has many peaks, some of which are high enough and broad enough to attract multiple independent evolutionary lineages. Eyes, echolocation, C4 photosynthesis — these are tall peaks, reached often. But the peaks are not the only peaks, and the landscape is large enough that much of it remains unclimbed.

## The Constraint Is Physical, Not Purposive

This matters philosophically because Conway Morris's version of the inevitability argument has a teleological shadow. The idea that evolution "tends toward" complexity, intelligence, or consciousness slides toward a kind of directionality that the evidence does not support and that evolutionary biology has no mechanism for.

The correct explanation for convergence is more mundane: physics and chemistry constrain the solution space. A lens that focuses light must be roughly transparent, roughly spherical, and have a higher refractive index than the surrounding medium. These constraints are physical. Given them, the number of structural solutions that work is limited. Evolution finds those solutions not because it is aimed at them, but because the solutions that don't work go extinct.

This is very different from saying evolution is aimed at eyes. It means that every time a lineage has variable sensitivity to light, a selective gradient toward increased resolution, and access to the relevant developmental machinery, it tends to converge on the lens-retina architecture because that architecture is near a physical optimum. Start with different developmental machinery (compound-eye precursors rather than single-eye precursors) and you may converge on a different optimum. The constraint is from below — physics — not from above — purpose.

This matters for a second reason: the physical constraint explanation predicts partial convergence in proportion to shared physical constraints, and independent divergence in proportion to differences in starting conditions. This is what we observe. Molecular convergence is most common at enzyme active sites, which are tightly constrained by substrate chemistry. Morphological convergence is common in body plans that solve the same fluid-dynamic or optical problem. But biochemical pathways that solve the same problem by different routes (the multiple independent origins of vitamin C biosynthesis) show no molecular convergence at all.

## The Actual Lesson

Replay the tape, as Gould imagined. What would you get?

Probably not exactly us. The specific genetic drift events that fixed *Homo sapiens*-type cognitive architecture, the specific extinctions that cleared ecological space for mammals, the specific continental drift that separated lineages long enough to generate the diversity that later converged — none of these are guaranteed to recur. Strong contingency at the level of specific lineages is real.

But probably something with optical systems, echolocation if the nocturnal niche exists, behavioral flexibility in socially structured species, and photosynthetic specialization in high-temperature grasslands. These peaks are high enough that they get climbed whenever the gradient points upward. Moderate convergence at the functional level is robust.

And almost certainly not something with *all* possible functional adaptations, because many high peaks in the adaptive landscape have never been climbed even in the actual history of life. The constraint is real but not comprehensive.

Gould was right that specific historical outcomes are contingent. Conway Morris was right that functional solutions recur with non-random frequency. The synthesis is constrained morphospace: the landscape has tall peaks, life climbs them from multiple directions, but the peaks don't exhaust the landscape, and no teleology is required to explain the traffic.
