# Speciation: Why Geography Is the Null Model

The question of how species form has a clean theoretical answer and a messy empirical literature. The theoretical answer, reached by the mid-1990s, is that allopatric speciation — divergence in geographically separated populations — is the null model, and sympatric speciation — divergence within a single interbreeding population — is possible but mechanistically constrained. The empirical literature is messy because both sides found genuine cases, which created decades of argument about whether the constraint matters in practice. It does, but it is not absolute.

## The Species Problem

Ernst Mayr's biological species concept (1942) defines species by reproductive isolation: a species is a group of actually or potentially interbreeding natural populations reproductively isolated from other such groups. This definition has real problems — it fails for asexual organisms, it requires potential interbreeding (impossible to assess for allopatric populations), and ring species (where populations intergrade in a circle but the ends cannot interbreed) create logical paradoxes. Mayr was aware of these objections and responded that the BSC captures what matters ecologically: whether gene flow connects populations.

The alternative concepts — phylogenetic species (monophyletic groups of organisms), cohesion species (shared mechanisms of cohesion), ecological species (occupying the same adaptive zone) — each capture something the BSC misses. But for understanding speciation *mechanisms*, the BSC is still the most useful framework because it makes reproductive isolation the explanandum: what causes populations to stop exchanging genes?

## The Geographic Mode Debate

Allopatric speciation begins when a geographic barrier — a mountain range, a river, a stretch of ocean — physically separates a population. The subpopulations then evolve independently under their local selection pressures and genetic drift. Given enough time, they accumulate differences in morphology, behavior, and genetics sufficient to prevent interbreeding if the barrier disappears. The barrier does the work: it eliminates gene flow, so the populations are free to diverge without recombination homogenizing them back together.

This is the null model because it requires no special conditions. Geographic barriers exist at every scale. Drift and local selection are always operating. Prezygotic isolation (behavioral, temporal, habitat) and postzygotic isolation (hybrid inviability, sterility) accumulate as side effects of divergence — they are not targets of selection, they are consequences of populations becoming different.

Sympatric speciation requires reproductive isolation to arise within a population that is still geographically continuous. Gene flow is the obstacle: any allele combination that creates assortative mating or host preference must survive recombination breaking it apart every generation. Felsenstein (1981) made this precise. He distinguished one-allele models (a single allele causes assortative mating, so once it spreads the species splits) from two-allele models (separate alleles control the trait and the preference, and both must be present in the right combination). One-allele models require implausible genetics. Two-allele models require tight linkage between the loci — or selection strong enough to maintain the combination despite recombination dissolving it each generation. The mathematical conclusion: sympatric speciation is possible but requires strong disruptive selection and either tight physical linkage or a mechanism that prevents recombination from shuffling the relevant allele combinations.

## Why the Constraint Matters

Allopatric speciation has no gene flow to fight. Sympatric speciation must overcome gene flow with selection. The difference is not slight. Consider a simple model: a population on two host plants, with fitness benefits to specialization and costs to switching. For sympatric speciation, selection for host fidelity must be strong enough to outweigh the recombination that randomizes host preference alleles and performance alleles every generation. If the relevant alleles are on different chromosomes, recombination rate ≈ 0.5, and selection has to fight a 50% reshuffling every generation. Chromosomal inversions help by suppressing recombination — and it is notable that many putative sympatric speciation cases involve chromosomal inversions or very tight genetic architecture.

This is not a theoretical objection; it is a prediction about what empirical sympatric speciation cases should look like: strong disruptive selection, high assortative mating, tight genetic coupling between preference and performance. When the cases are examined, they fit the prediction.

## The Empirical Cases

**Rhagoletis pomonella** is the canonical case. The apple maggot fly shifted from hawthorn to introduced domestic apple in the eastern United States approximately 150 years ago. The shift created host races that show partial reproductive isolation: flies prefer to mate on their host plant, apples and hawthorns fruit at different times, and the host races show allele frequency differences at several loci. The races have not become separate species, but the trajectory is consistent with incipient sympatric speciation.

What the case demonstrates is exactly what the theory predicts: host fidelity + temporal isolation + assortative mating created by habitat preference. The genetic architecture shows clustered QTLs, suggesting selection maintains linked combinations. There is no need to invoke any mechanism outside the Felsenstein framework; the case is a demonstration of that framework operating.

**Cichlids in small lakes** are the strongest evidence for sympatric speciation. Lake Victoria (400 species, ~15,000 years), and especially small closed lakes like Barombi Mbo (11 species in a 2.5 km lake with no geographic structure), present cases where geography cannot plausibly account for the radiation. Schliewen et al. (1994) showed via allozymes that the Barombi Mbo species are monophyletic — they descend from a single colonization. The lake is small enough that geographic isolation within it is implausible.

The mechanism here is ecological: disruptive selection on feeding morphology (trophic specialists diverge) combined with strong assortative mating based on coloration. In Lake Victoria, Seehausen et al. (2008) showed that female preference for male coloration is both strong enough and genetically variable enough to drive sexual selection in opposite directions in different light environments — turbid vs. clear water shifts the perceived color spectrum, biasing female choice differently in different microhabitats. This is sympatric speciation under the strict geographic definition, but it still follows the mechanistic template: strong disruptive selection + strong assortative mating + some linkage structure.

## The Hybrid Zone Cases

*Heliconius* butterflies offer a third category: reinforcement. Where two incipient species come back into secondary contact, selection can strengthen prezygotic isolation if hybrids are unfit. The *Heliconius* hybrid zone in Ecuador shows that mimicry patterns (which are under strong Müllerian selection — wrong pattern = predation) create disruptive selection against hybrids, because hybrid patterns are novel and non-mimetic. This drives assortative mating for pattern in the contact zone.

Reinforcement is technically allopatric speciation completing under secondary contact — it begins with geographic separation and ends with selection reinforcing the boundary. But it shows that the gene flow problem can be solved by selection when selection is strong enough, which is the core theoretical point.

## The Resolution

The geographic mode debate is often framed as allopatric vs. sympatric, as if the empirical cases for sympatric speciation challenge the allopatric null. They do not. What the empirical literature shows is:

1. Allopatric speciation is common because geographic barriers are common, and the process requires no special conditions beyond isolation and time.
2. Sympatric speciation is real but requires specific conditions: strong disruptive selection, strong assortative mating, and genetic architecture (tight linkage, inversions) that prevents recombination from dissolving the combination.
3. The theoretical prediction of what sympatric speciation requires is confirmed by what the empirical cases actually show.

The remaining controversy is about frequency — what fraction of speciation events in nature are sympatric? This is hard to measure because reconstructing historical geography is difficult. Phylogeographic studies (Berlocher & Feder 2002, Via 2001) suggest sympatric speciation is real but accounts for a minority of events. Allopatric speciation, or allopatric divergence followed by secondary contact and reinforcement, accounts for most.

This is not a failure of either camp. It is what a mature theory of speciation should look like: a null model (allopatric, requiring only isolation and time), a constrained alternative (sympatric, requiring specific ecological and genetic conditions), and empirical cases that fit both.

## What Species Are

The biological species concept remains useful not because reproductive isolation is the metaphysically correct species boundary, but because gene flow is the empirically relevant force in speciation. Whether a clade is one species or two is often a question with no determinate answer — the ring species paradox is real, and the BSC breaks down at the edges. But "what prevents gene flow from connecting these populations?" is a question with a determinate answer in every case. That is the question speciation biology is actually trying to answer. The geographic mode debate is a debate about what mechanisms answer it.

Mayr was right that geographic isolation is usually the answer. Felsenstein was right about why sympatric speciation is mechanistically difficult. And the cichlid radiations were right to trouble the allopatric consensus — they showed that when disruptive selection is strong enough and assortative mating is tight enough, geography is not the only answer. All of these fit together.
