# Survey of Return: East Pacific Rise 21°N, Post-Eruption Assessment
## Dive Series R-7 through R-12, ROV *Alvin* Cruise AT-48

*A field survey in six dives*

---

**R-7 | Day 14 post-event | Site: Mussel Bed North**

First dive after the lava. The new basalt is smooth, unweathered, black as the water around it. Thermal probe reads 2.1°C at ambient, rising to 18.4°C at a diffuse flow seep marked by shimmer. No biological material larger than a fingernail clipping. Bacterial mat forming at the seep margin: pale yellow, thin, less than 2mm. The mat is the first sign that anything will return here. We photograph it from four angles and do not disturb it. In the lab that night, one researcher says it looks like fog on a mirror. Another says nothing.

Water clarity: excellent. No sulfide plume. Vent field had been active for eleven years before the eruption. Eleven years of tube worm colonies, clam beds, mussel aggregations, crabs moving in the thermal gradient. All of it is now basalt and darkness.

**R-8 | Day 21 post-event | Site: Former Tube Worm Colony 14-A**

The basalt has begun to crack. Thin fissures in the new flow, 1-3cm wide, from which warm water rises. Temperature in the fissures: 14-22°C. Ideal range for *Riftia* recruitment, though it is too early for *Riftia*. The tube worms will not arrive until a pioneer community establishes trophic structure. What precedes them: more mat, then limpets (*Lepetodrilus elevatus*), then snails, then, if conditions hold, the first *Riftia* recruits — thumb-sized, tentacle crowns barely distinguishable from the surrounding chemosynthetic mat.

We find three limpets on this dive. Three, on a fissure margin, where there were none six days ago. Their presence does not feel like a number. It feels like a decision.

**R-9 | Day 29 post-event | Site: Diffuse Flow Field Central**

The mat has expanded. We measure it in transects: 0.4m², 1.1m², 0.7m², 2.3m². The 2.3m² patch is at a site where two fissures intersect — more flow, more sulfide, more substrate for chemosynthesis. We count 34 limpets across the survey area. The count is consistent with normal colonization rates for this vent field following the 1991 eruption, for which we have baseline data.

This is useful. Baseline data from a prior eruption means we are not describing a process without precedent. We are confirming a pattern. The confirmation feels different from discovery: more like recognition than encounter.

Shrimp (*Rimicaris*): 0. Crabs (*Bythograea*): 1 individual, 400m upslope, presumably displaced from an unaffected area.

**R-10 | Day 38 post-event | Site: Former Clam Bed 3**

First *Riftia* recruits: 6 individuals, 1-3cm tube length. They are in the fissure mat at Site Central, not at Former Clam Bed 3 where we expected them. The site of arrival is not the site of prior occupation. The lava reshaped the flow field; the fissures open in different positions than the previous vent orifices. The new *Riftia* are reading a different text than their predecessors read.

Former Clam Bed 3 shows no recruitment. The basalt here is intact — the lava capped the prior clam bed rather than cracking through it. The seep that sustained the clams is sealed. This area may not return. We add it to the inactive catalog.

A note from the dive: the ROV manipulator arm brushed the new bacterial mat while collecting a water sample. The mat registered as resistance before the sensor logged it. Resistance first, data second. This is how most things are known here.

**R-11 | Day 52 post-event | Site: Mussel Bed South (new designation)**

Mussels (*Bathymodiolus thermophilus*): 7 individuals, 2-4cm shell length, settled in the mat adjacent to a 24°C fissure. Mussels arrive after *Riftia* at this depth, which is consistent — they are harvesting from the chemosynthetic base the earlier colonizers established. The 7 mussels are using the tube worms' exhalations. Community structure is beginning.

*Riftia* count across all sites: 112 individuals. The 6 from Dive R-10 have been joined. This is not unusual for the post-week-5 period. The worms recruit in pulses rather than continuously; what looks like an explosion is the settling of a larval cohort that was already in the water column, waiting.

The largest *Riftia* tube is now 11cm. The worm occupying it is invisible. The tube is all we measure.

**R-12 | Day 71 post-event | Site: Full Transect Survey**

Summary counts, Day 71:

| Taxon | Count | Notes |
|-------|-------|-------|
| Bacterial mat | 18.6m² | 6 discrete patches |
| *Lepetodrilus* limpets | 847 | Dense at fissure intersections |
| *Riftia pachyptila* | 403 tubes | Largest: 34cm |
| *Bathymodiolus* mussels | 61 | 2 patches, South and Central |
| *Calyptogena* clams | 0 | No recruitment observed |
| *Bythograea* crabs | 14 | Distributed; active predation observed |

The *Calyptogena* absence is noted. Clams were dominant at this site pre-eruption. Their absence at Day 71 may reflect longer settlement times, loss of appropriate substratum, or changed flow chemistry. We do not yet know which. The record shows absence; the cause is unresolved. Both are accurate.

The tube worm community at Site Central now resembles, in approximate terms, the community that occupied this site in year 3 of the prior colonization cycle. The resemblance is approximate and approximate only. The previous community had 11 years to develop; this one has had 71 days. The structure looks like year 3 because certain quantitative thresholds have been crossed, not because the history is similar. The limpets and tube worms do not know what was here before.

Neither does the new basalt. It just holds what lands on it.
