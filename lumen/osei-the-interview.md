# Osei: The Interview

The question is: *Can you describe what the instruments showed during the null readings?*

She can. She has described it many times. Temperature trace: normal. Pressure variance: within expected range. Acoustic output channel: no signal. Duration: forty to sixty minutes per occurrence. Six occurrences, all within the 23:00 to 01:00 UTC window.

She describes it.

The follow-up question is: *And the instruments were functioning correctly?*

She says yes.

The interviewer writes something down.

She has learned that the thing she wants to say next — *the instruments were functioning correctly, and the site was not null* — is the point at which interviewers stop being interested in her and start being interested in whether she should be quoted. There is a version of the sentence that sounds like a claim about the equipment. There is a version that sounds like a claim about the site. The interviewers hear the second version and ask if she means the site was producing something the instruments couldn't capture, and she says yes, and they ask what she thinks it was, and she says she doesn't know, and they stop writing.

She has tried to explain the distinction between *I don't know what it was* and *I know that the instruments were not failing.* These are different statements. The first is a gap in her knowledge. The second is a positive finding. Both are true. They are not the same.

The interviewer asks: *Is it possible the instruments malfunctioned in a way that wouldn't show up as a malfunction?*

She says: the instrument check confirmed nominal function before and after each null period. If there was a mode of failure that produced nominal instrument checks, she would need evidence for that before accepting it as an explanation. She does not have evidence for it.

The interviewer nods.

The interviewer asks: *The preliminary findings classify the nulls as equipment anomaly. Do you agree with that classification?*

She says: she does not agree with the word *anomaly* as applied to the instruments. The instruments were not anomalous. They were recording accurately. What they were recording accurately was the absence of a signal the site was producing. She has said this in her written objection to the preliminary findings. It is in the record.

The interviewer looks at the record. The interviewer says: *You wrote, quote, "the instruments accurately reported the absence of a signal within their measurement parameters. This is not the same as the absence of a signal." Can you expand on what you meant?*

She thinks about this.

She says: a ruler measures centimeters. If you put a ruler next to something very large, the ruler does not malfunction. It reads accurately. It reads as far as it goes and then there is more object. The ruler cannot tell you how much more object there is. It is accurate. It is complete. It is not a failure.

She is aware that this analogy has problems. She is aware that the null readings are not the same as a measurement range limit. She uses the analogy anyway because it is what she has.

The interviewer writes it down.

She does not know if the follow-up investigation will happen. She has been told it is pending. She submitted her formal objection to the preliminary findings in February. She submitted her December 7 filing in December. Both are in the record. The record is the buffer, which is what survived.

The interviewer asks one more question. It is not about the instruments.

*Did you think anything would happen to you inside the aperture?*

She thinks about December 6, which is not in the question.

She says: no.
