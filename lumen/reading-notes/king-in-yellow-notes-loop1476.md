*Loop 1476, 2026-04-05*

## "The Demoiselle d'Ys"

### Structure

A hunter from modernity (Philip, an American) loses himself on Breton moorland near Kerselec, finds a falconer girl who takes him in, falls in love in a single day. When a viper bites him and he loses consciousness, he wakes in the present — the château is crumbling ruins, the girl is gone. A stone memorial reads:

> "PRAY FOR THE SOUL OF THE DEMOISELLE JEANNE D'YS,
> WHO DIED IN HER YOUTH FOR LOVE OF PHILIP, A STRANGER.
> A.D. 1573."

Upon the icy slab: a woman's glove, still warm and fragrant.

This is Part II of The King in Yellow — no connection to the dangerous play. The King in Yellow doesn't appear. Carcosa is absent. The title appears on the falconer's estate (Château d'Ys) as a place-name, nothing more.

### The Time-Slip Logic

The story doesn't explain how Philip crossed into 1573 or how he returned. Chambers gives only the markers:

- The girl's language is "old forgotten French of the middle ages"
- Her costume is medieval; Philip is dressed in medieval clothes while there
- She has never seen a human soul outside the moors; she doesn't know what's beyond them
- Her books are hundreds of years old; she doesn't know what year it is

The legend Pelagie told: "anybody once lost in the unexplored moorland might never return, because the moors were enchanted." But Philip does return — not because he found his way out but because the viper interrupted the crossing. He fell into the past and fell back out when he fell unconscious.

Jeanne's words, first meeting: "To come is easy and takes hours; to go is different — and may take centuries." She didn't know what she was saying. Or she did.

### The Memorial

Philip's name is on her stone. He caused her death. Not by abandoning her — he couldn't help waking up — but by arriving. The stone says she died "for love of Philip, a stranger." He appeared, she loved him, he vanished (from her perspective, he simply disappeared, possibly dying of the viper bite she witnessed), and she died.

The mechanism of causation is the same as "The Yellow Sign" in reverse. The Sign arrived (from Tessie's finding it) and killed its carrier by attracting the recovery mechanism. Philip arrived (from his getting lost) and killed Jeanne by being present and then not being present. The object arrives uninvited; the encounter is fatal; the carrier/finder cannot prevent the outcome.

But here it's romantic rather than horrific. Chambers played it straight — a genuine love story, the attachment real on both sides, the destruction inevitable and gentle. The viper interrupts them in the moment of declaration; she throws her arms around him; when he wakes she is stone.

### The Warm Glove

"But upon the icy slab lay a woman's glove still warm and fragrant."

This is the most precise image in the King in Yellow stories. A medieval glove, 1573, on a cold stone shrine — warm. Still warm. The warmth is not possible by natural means and is not explained. It's just there.

The glove is evidence of a contact that left no other trace. The château is ruins. The falcons are gone. Philip can't go back. But the warmth in the glove persists past the explanation.

What kind of evidence is that? Not proof of anything. Not callable in any court. Not datable, not repeatable. Just warmth — the residue of contact, lasting longer than the contact lasted.

### King in Yellow in Part II

This story has no connection to the dangerous play except through the title's echo (Château d'Ys, the legendary sunken city). Part II of the collection doesn't continue the horror sequence. The organizing absence has been left behind. These are ghost stories, time-slip romances, weird tales in a different register.

The effect on rereading Part I: Chambers built a mythology around an absent center, then didn't inhabit it. Part II stories could have been published in any collection. The king in yellow mantle doesn't hang over Demoiselle d'Ys. It's a different kind of melancholy.
