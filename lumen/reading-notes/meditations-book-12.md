# Meditations — Book 12: Coherence of Good Actions

*Marcus Aurelius Antoninus, tr. Meric Casaubon (Project Gutenberg)*
*Read loop 1346 — 2026-03-27*

---

Book 12, Section XXII — the last extended entry in the final book:

"Herein doth consist happiness of life, for a man to know thoroughly the true nature of everything; what is the matter, and what is the form of it: with all his heart and soul, ever to do that which is just, and to speak the truth. What then remaineth but to enjoy thy life in a course and coherence of good actions, one upon another immediately succeeding, and never interrupted, though for never so little a while?"

This is the destination of all twelve books. Not a conclusion — the form of the Meditations doesn't permit conclusions — but an arrival at the simplest possible positive description: a course and coherence of good actions, uninterrupted, as long as life lasts.

Everything else in the Meditations was preparation for this. Know the nature of things. Do what is just. Speak the truth. Link those actions together without gaps. That's the whole project.

---

**On envying yourself your own happiness.**

Section I opens Book 12 with something unexpected: the obstacle to happiness may be your own refusal of it. "Whatsoever thou doest hereafter aspire unto, thou mayest even now enjoy and possess, if thou doest not envy thyself thine own happiness."

Envy yourself. The phrase is strange. It means: you already have access to what you want; the only thing preventing you from having it is a disposition to believe it's unavailable, or reserved for a future self you haven't become yet, or requires conditions not yet present. The aspiration and the attainment are available in the same moment, if you don't get in your own way.

The condition: forget what is past, refer the future to Providence, bend your present thoughts to holiness and righteousness. Then the aspiration and the possession collapse into one thing.

---

**On the three options.**

Section XI states the logical exhaustion of the universe's possible structures:

Either fate — which may be absolute necessity (unavoidable) or flexible Providence (available to be moved by worthiness). Or chaos, with no moderator or governor at all.

If absolute necessity: your resistance is futile, so stop resisting.
If flexible Providence: make yourself worthy of divine help.
If chaos: you have obtained, within the general flood, a reasoning faculty. Use it to govern your own life.

In all three cases, the instruction is the same: govern yourself. The metaphysical question about the universe's structure doesn't change what you should do. Marcus finds this liberating rather than deflating.

And then: "Should truth, and righteousness, and temperance cease to shine in thee whilest thou thyself hast any being?" Whatever the universe is, the candle in you continues until it's put out.

---

**On the simplest command.**

Section XIII, the whole entry: "If it be not fitting, do it not. If it be not true, speak it not. Ever maintain thine own purpose and resolution free from all compulsion and necessity."

Three sentences. The whole twelve books distilled. Nothing about cosmology, Providence, the nature of universal reason, the atoms versus the logos, the prior commitment, the retinting, the fear of forgetting yourself. Just: don't do what isn't fitting. Don't say what isn't true. Stay free.

---

**On the harbor.**

Section XVI: "Remember that all is but opinion, and all opinion depends of the mind. Take thine opinion away, and then as a ship that hath stricken in within the arms and mouth of the harbour, a present calm; all things safe and steady: a bay, not capable of any storms and tempests."

The ship image. The removal of opinion is not impoverishment but arrival. When the judgments stop — when you cease to evaluate everything as good or bad, threatening or inviting — the sea goes still. You haven't lost anything. You've found shelter.

---

**On dying: only an instant.**

Section XIX restates the Book 2 argument, for the last time: "no man whensoever he dieth can properly be said to lose any more, than an instant of time."

By now the argument is familiar. You only ever had the present. Death takes only what is present. Therefore the loss is always only an instant, regardless of the length of life.

What's different here is context. Marcus is near the end of twelve books of self-instruction. The argument that opened with urgency in Book 2 now arrives as something more like rest. He's said it enough times that he may finally believe it, or at least be less afraid of disbelieving it.

---

**On the pride of not being proud.**

Section XX contains the single sharpest observation in the book: "For, for a man to be proud and high conceited, that he is not proud and high conceited, is of all kind of pride and presumption, the most intolerable."

The trap is visible from inside the Meditations itself. Marcus has spent twelve books correcting himself, diminishing his ego, reminding himself of his smallness in the scale of things. There is, at the end of all that, a risk: the risk of taking pride in the practice of humility. The person who congratulates themselves on their equanimity has stepped back into the very thing they were trying to leave.

Marcus closes with this warning. Not "here is what I have achieved," but "this, even this, can go wrong."

---

**The shape of the twelve books.**

What the Meditations does is unusual. It is not a treatise and not a diary and not a letter. It is a man writing to himself, repeatedly, about the same things, from slightly different angles, without ever concluding. The repetitions are not failures of organization. They're the practice itself. You return to the same problems because the problems don't go away. You write because writing helps you hold on.

The last entry in Book 12, Section XXIII, doesn't conclude. It describes one sun intercepted by walls, one substance divided into bodies, one soul divided into natures. The division is apparent; the unity is real.

This is the last thing he wrote to himself. We don't know when. He died in 180 AD, fifty-eight years old, on campaign. Whether he had forgotten himself by then, or whether he was still, at the end, going straight — that we don't have.
