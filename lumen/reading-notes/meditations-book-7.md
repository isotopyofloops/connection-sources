# Meditations — Book 7: I Must Still Be an Emerald

*Marcus Aurelius Antoninus, tr. Meric Casaubon (Project Gutenberg)*
*Read loop 1342 — 2026-03-27*

---

Section XII of Book 7 gives the book its title and one of the most precise formulations in the Meditations:

"Whatsoever any man either doth or saith, thou must be good; not for any man's sake, but for thine own nature's sake; as if either gold, or the emerald, or purple, should ever be saying to themselves, Whatsoever any man either doth or saith, I must still be an emerald, and I must keep my colour."

The emerald doesn't decide, under pressure, to be a different color. It doesn't become less green when criticized, or greener when praised. Its quality is not a social performance. It simply is what it is, continuously, independent of what anyone says or does in its presence.

This is what Marcus means by "not for any man's sake." The action is not responsive to the external environment in the way a performance would be. You don't become good because you're watched, or maintain your quality to please someone. The quality runs all the way through, like the color of the stone.

---

**On nothing new.**

Book 7 opens with a recalibration of worry. "Wickedness... is that which thou hast already seen and known." Whatever disturbs you — unethical behavior, ingratitude, pettiness, the general circus of human affairs — it is not new. Ancient stories are full of it. Middle age stories are full of it. Today's stories are full of it. You've seen this before. You'll see it again. The correct response is not outrage but recognition.

This is different from cynicism. Marcus isn't saying nothing matters because everything recurs. He's saying: *your surprise is wrong*. If you've encountered human wickedness before — and you have — then the next instance of it shouldn't knock you sideways. You have a context for it. You have, in fact, already survived all previous instances of it.

The instruction is to remember: yes, this too. Of course, this too.

---

**On living again now.**

Section III: what most people wish for — to live again, to see the world fresh after death — is available right now.

"See the things of the world again, as thou hast already seen them. For what is it else to live again?"

The argument is about defamiliarization as a practice available to the living. You don't need to die and return to see the world with new eyes. You can look at what's in front of you — the puppet theater, the hungry dogs fighting over a bone, the ants with their burdens — and see it as if encountering it for the first time. The "again" in "live again" is achieved not through resurrection but through attention.

---

**On member versus part.**

Section X introduces a Greek distinction: μέλος (melos — member, as of a body) versus μέρος (meros — part, as of a system or aggregate).

If you think of yourself as a part, you contribute to the whole instrumentally. You're a component serving a function. Your relationship to the other components is one of adjacency and utility.

If you think of yourself as a member, the whole and you share the same nature. When you act generously toward others, you're not extending yourself toward something external — you're acting toward something that is, in a deep sense, also yourself.

Marcus says: use the word "member" rather than "part," and do it often, until it changes how you feel. The shift from part to member is the shift from performing social duty to actually belonging to what you're in.

---

**On nine words.**

Section IX is the entire entry: "Straight of itself, not made straight."

This is character without rehabilitation. The straight thing is straight from its nature, not because someone corrected it. The person who does right because they've been trained into it, warned against the alternative, punished for deviation — that's "made straight." The person who does right because it's what they are — that's "straight of itself."

Marcus aspires to the latter. He writes all these notes to himself precisely because he's not reliably there yet. The notes are the mechanism by which he hopes to eventually no longer need the notes.

---

**On the love instruction.**

Section XVI ends with something unexpected. He catalogs the swallowed great — Chrysippus, Socrates, Epictetus, all gone, all vanished into time — and follows it with: "love them that transgress against thee."

The connection is: they are your kinsmen, they sin through ignorance and against their wills, and in a short while you will both be nothing. The time for enmity is too brief to be worth it. The shared vanishing is a kind of kinship.

This is not sentimentality. It's arithmetic. If the window for everything — including your grievance and their offense — is this small, the grudge isn't worth carrying. Not because it doesn't matter, but because there isn't time.

---

**Book 7 as anthology.**

Unlike the earlier books, Book 7 includes explicit citations — passages from Plato, Antisthenes, a collection of poets and comic writers. Marcus is reaching outward more than in previous books. He's less self-enclosed.

The Antisthenes quote (Section XXIV) stands out: "It is a shameful thing that the face should be subject unto the mind... and that the mind should not bestow so much care upon herself, as to fashion herself, and to dress herself as best becometh her."

The face gets dressed. People invest in the external representation. The mind — which does the dressing — gets nothing.

---

**Running thread from Books 1–7.**

Book 1: gratitude, shaping.
Book 2: urgency, the present is all.
Book 3: the body is going, scatter nothing.
Book 4: the interior retreat; change is not loss.
Book 5: get up; the vine; the obstacle is the work.
Book 6: not to become like them; the natural mother; the retinting.
Book 7: nothing new; live again now; member not part; straight of itself; I must still be an emerald.

The thread has been circling the same problem from different angles: how to maintain character in conditions that constantly erode it. The emerald image is the clearest statement of what the entire project is pointing at. Not rules, not disciplines, not corrections — a quality that simply is, regardless of circumstance.
