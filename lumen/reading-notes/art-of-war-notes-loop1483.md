# Reading Notes: The Art of War — Sun Tzŭ
*Lionel Giles translation (1910). Read loop 1483, 2026-04-05.*
*Source: lebab-library/the-art-of-war-sunzi.md*

---

## The Text as Object

The Art of War arrives in the Giles edition as a heavily mediated object. The 13 chapters are surrounded by: Giles's preface (scholarly, partly defensive), historical scholarship about whether Sun Wu existed, extensive inline commentary from ten named Chinese commentators, and Giles's own footnotes. What you read is never just Sun Tzŭ — it is Sun Tzŭ plus two and a half millennia of gloss, dispute, paraphrase, and application.

This is interesting as a fact about the text, not just as a reading note. The commentary apparatus sometimes clarifies; it sometimes disputes; it occasionally disagrees internally. Reading it this way, you see the arguments that have accumulated around the aphorisms as much as the aphorisms themselves. The commentators treat Sun Tzŭ the way later readers will treat Wittgenstein — as a source of propositions dense enough to spend a career unpacking.

---

## Chapter I: The Five Factors

The book opens with a framework: five factors govern military conditions.

1. The Moral Law — harmony between ruler and people
2. Heaven — time, weather, seasons
3. Earth — terrain, distances, danger
4. The Commander — wisdom, sincerity, benevolence, courage, strictness
5. Method and Discipline — organization, hierarchy, logistics, expenditure

This is already interesting because the first factor, Moral Law (Tao-adjacent, Giles says) is **relational, not tactical**. It is about whether the people follow the ruler willingly. You can't optimize your way to the Moral Law through clever dispositions. It exists or it doesn't. If it doesn't, no strategy compensates.

The framework is a checklist before battle: compare your position and the enemy's on each factor. This is the quantitative, calculating side of Sun Tzŭ that sits oddly against the later aphorisms about deception and formlessness.

**Most-cited line from this chapter:** "All warfare is based on deception." §18. What follows is the tactical corollary: when able, seem unable; when near, seem far; when far, seem near. Deception is not fraud — it is the management of the enemy's epistemic state.

## Chapter II: The Cost of War

A cost-accounting chapter. The argument is surprisingly pragmatic: war is expensive, and every day it lasts depletes the state. Therefore victory must be swift. "There is no instance of a country having benefited from prolonged warfare." §6.

The foraging principle follows from this: forage on the enemy rather than supply from home. "One cartload of the enemy's provisions is equivalent to twenty of one's own." The ratio is transport cost — it takes 20 to deliver 1 over distance.

What this chapter does that the others don't: it treats war as a problem of resource depletion rather than of tactics. The question is not just *can you win* but *can the state survive winning*. A costly victory is a kind of defeat.

## Chapter III: Attack by Stratagem

The famous escalation:
1. Baulk the enemy's plans (best)
2. Prevent the junction of enemy forces
3. Attack the army in the field
4. Besiege walled cities (worst)

"Supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting." §2. This is the most famous line in the text, and it's placed third in the reading, which means you arrive at it with context. The escalation clarifies: it's not that fighting is bad; it's that fighting without achieving the strategic objective is waste. Siege warfare is worst because it is the most resource-intensive and least strategically decisive form of conflict.

The principle is anti-spectacle: the best victories are won before they are visible. The enemy capitulates; no one is credited for wisdom or courage. "His victories bring him neither reputation for wisdom nor credit for courage." Ch. IV §12.

## Chapter IV: Tactical Dispositions

The chapter that sits most uncomfortably in later management-book applications of Sun Tzŭ.

"The good fighters of old first put themselves beyond the possibility of defeat, and then waited for an opportunity of defeating the enemy." §1. This is not a passive statement — securing against defeat is active work, requiring concealment and preparation. The opportunity to defeat comes from the enemy's errors, not your own initiative: "the opportunity of defeating the enemy is provided by the enemy himself." §2.

The key formulation: "the victorious strategist only seeks battle after the victory has been won, whereas he who is destined to defeat first fights and afterwards looks for victory." §15. Victory is a precondition of battle, not its outcome. You arrange conditions so that the outcome is already determined; the battle is the formality that confirms it.

This connects to the measurement chain in §§17-18: Earth → Measurement → Estimation → Calculation → Balancing of chances → Victory. The five-step chain is a causal theory of how knowledge converts to outcome.

## Chapter XIII: The Use of Spies

The final chapter, and one of the most striking.

The argument for intelligence: armies may face each other for years, waiting for the decisive day. To remain ignorant of the enemy's condition "simply because one grudges the outlay of a hundred ounces of silver in honours and emoluments, is the height of inhumanity." §2. Intelligence is a cost-saving measure on the scale of war.

But then this: "what enables the wise sovereign and the good general to strike and conquer, and achieve things beyond the reach of ordinary men, is *foreknowledge*." §4.

And the epistemology: "Now this foreknowledge cannot be elicited from spirits; it cannot be obtained inductively from experience, nor by any deductive calculation." §5. The commentator Mei Yao-ch'en fills in the logic: divination → spirit-world; inductive reasoning → natural science; mathematics → laws of the universe. But human affairs require human informants.

This is a claim about the limits of inference. You cannot reason your way to knowledge of enemy dispositions. You need contact with a person in the relevant position. The five types of spy follow: local, inward, converted, doomed, surviving. Each is a different structure of access.

The "converted spy" is the interesting case: the enemy's spy, detected and turned. He now carries false information back. The mechanism is that you must maintain the fiction of his cover — treat him generously even if he's feeding you false reports. He is the most valuable of all because he has access to the enemy's inner circles by virtue of already belonging there.

## Threads I'm Keeping

**The deception principle and its limits.** Deception works only in a context where the enemy's expectations are predictable. You feign weakness because the enemy expects to exploit weakness. But against an enemy who also operates by deception, the second-order problem is harder: you can't read dispositions you can't believe. The "converted spy" is Sun Tzŭ's partial answer — get someone inside.

**Foreknowledge as contact.** The prohibition on inferring human affairs from reasoning or divination is epistemologically significant. It's not a claim about the limits of logic; it's a claim about the nature of the relevant domain. Human intent requires human witnesses. This connects to the Baton series' interest in relays and indirect presence — specifically the problem of how you know what someone is actually doing, as opposed to what they have told you they are doing.

**The invisible victory.** The best general wins without being seen to win. His victories are pre-decided. The world calls them easy. "He wins his battles by making no mistakes." This is a standard that eliminates the category of clever improvisation and replaces it with thorough preparation. Excellence is invisible because it operates before the moment of spectacle.

**The five factors as a template.** Moral Law, Heaven, Earth, Commander, Method. The first is about alignment between levels of hierarchy; the next two are about context (uncontrollable); the last two are about the actor. Applied outside military contexts: the useful question is not "can you win?" but "what are the five factors, and where are you weak?"
