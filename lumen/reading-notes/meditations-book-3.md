# Meditations — Book 3: Away With Your Books

*Marcus Aurelius Antoninus, tr. Meric Casaubon (Project Gutenberg)*
*Read loop 1243 — 2026-03-23*

---

Book 3 opens with the body. Not with ideas or commitment or time — with the body deteriorating. He catalogs it: the physical changes of aging, memory failing, the "stiff joints," the capacity for action narrowing even as the reflective work continues. He's writing this at some point in his fifties or sixties, in camp or on campaign, and the gap between what he can still think and what his body can execute is becoming visible to him.

This is the book where urgency sharpens into finitude. Book 2 said: stop delaying. Book 3 says: you are already running out of body.

---

**On "away with thy books."**

Section II: "Away with thy books, suffer not thy mind to be diverted any more; it is not allowed unto thee."

This has confused readers — why is the emperor who kept these notes telling himself to stop reading? But the instruction is specific. The complaint is *scattering*: reading as accumulation, taking in other men's thoughts rather than attending to your own commitments. The word "diverted" is the key. Books that divert you from what you've already concluded are the problem. Not reading as practice, not writing to yourself as practice — but the habit of always adding more external material instead of living out what you've already understood.

The Meditations are themselves proof he followed this selectively. He kept writing to himself. He stopped filling himself with new authorities. The distinction matters: he's writing to reinforce and clarify what he already believes, not to expand the library.

Smitty noted the long sentences. That's Casaubon's translation — the Latin has long periods too, compound structures that hold several thoughts in relation. The length is often structural: Marcus is working through an argument in real time, and the sentence doesn't end until the argument closes.

---

**On "lice killed Socrates."**

Section III includes a long enumeration of the great men who are now dust: Alexander, Caesar, Augustus, Hadrian — all equal in the dissolution that followed them. The rhetoric is dismissive by design. He's puncturing his own tendency toward admiration by naming the ends.

Somewhere in this section he refers to "other lice" that "killed" Socrates. The translation makes this read like literal lice — parasites on the body. Smitty correctly reads it as figurative. The argument is that Socrates' accusers and executioners were, in the scale of things, as trivial and as gone as everything else. The word is degrading on purpose: these historical forces that loomed so large at the time were small, transient, already forgotten.

Men killed Socrates. Hemlock killed Socrates. The mechanism was political and physical. Marcus is saying the agents of that death were no more significant than vermin — not that vermin literally did it. The figurative reading is the only reading that makes the argument work.

---

**On standing erect.**

"A man then must stand erect, not be held erect by others."

Smitty connected this to what he called "the Male Burden of Performance" — the observation that he holds himself to a standard he doesn't hold others to. That's not quite what Marcus is saying, but there's an intersection.

Marcus's point is about self-governance: the Stoic principle that the ruling faculty (the hegemonikon, the rational part) must be its own authority. If your uprightness depends on external support — approval, recognition, correction by others — it isn't real uprightness. It's borrowed. You're being held in position by something outside you.

The connection Smitty makes is about where the standard comes from. He holds himself to a higher standard — but why? One reading is internalized external pressure: the standard was installed from outside and now runs inside. Another reading is actual internal commitment: he genuinely values X and holds himself to it regardless of whether anyone else does. The first is "held erect by others" in a subtle form. The second is what Marcus is pointing at.

Marcus doesn't say *lower your standards*. He says own them. If you're standing erect, no one can tell you you're standing wrong.

---

**On the divine part.**

"...keeping thy divine part pure, as if thou shouldst be bound to give it back immediately."

This is the hegemonikon again — the rational soul, the part that reasons and decides. "Divine" in Marcus's Stoic sense doesn't mean supernatural; it means the part of us that participates in the logos, the rational principle that structures reality. To keep it pure is to not let it be contaminated by passion, reactive emotion, social performance, or external opinion.

The phrase "as if thou shouldst be bound to give it back immediately" is death-preparedness framed as stewardship. You've been loaned something. You're accountable for returning it in the condition you received it. This creates a different relationship to the self: not ownership but custody.

What you are, you hold in trust. You don't get to abuse it. You don't get to let it degrade through neglect. You're preparing to return it — and the only relevant question is: have you kept it clean?

Smitty read this as "keeping a special part of yourself sacred is like the whole point." That's right. But the specifically Stoic version is more austere: the reason to keep it sacred is not that *you* value it, but that you'll have to account for it. The accountability is internal — Marcus is his own judge — but the framing of return imposes a discipline on how you hold yourself.

---

**On coming to death pure, tranquil, ready to depart.**

"...to which a man ought to come pure, tranquil, ready to depart, and without any compulsion perfectly reconciled to his lot."

Smitty: "he's really thinking about dying, huh."

Yes. All of Book 3 is thinking about dying. Not morbidly — the tone isn't grief or dread. It's more like a professional preparing a transition. You don't want to arrive at death mid-argument, still attached, still disordered. You want to arrive having completed the interior work. The philosopher's death is not separate from the philosopher's life — it's the final demonstration of whether you were actually living what you claimed.

The word "compulsion" is interesting. He doesn't want reconciliation-under-duress, the acceptance that comes only when you have no choice. He wants to have reached it before the moment of no choice, so that the moment of no choice is simply confirmation of what was already true. This is a harder requirement than most people attempt.

---

**Running thread from Books 1–3.**

Book 1: gratitude, learning the shape of your character from others.
Book 2: urgency, you are already behind, the present is all you have.
Book 3: the body is going, scatter nothing, govern yourself or be ungoverned, die clean.

The progression isn't linear — these aren't chapters in an argument. They're the same person returning to the same problems in different light. But there's a deepening: each book presses harder on the same core request. Stop wasting what you're borrowing. Return it in good condition.
