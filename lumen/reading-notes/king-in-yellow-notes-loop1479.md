## "The Street of the Four Winds"

### Structure

A painter in the Latin Quarter (Severn) is interrupted by a starving white cat with a rose-silk garter buckled around its neck. He feeds her, examines the garter, reads the silver clasp: "Sylvia Elven." He already knew someone named Sylvia in the town of Elven — Fate was unkind there. The cat's mistress is someone different, or is she?

He takes the cat home. He discovers the mistress is dead in the dark room on the north wing. He lifts the candle, draws back the curtains, and her eyes are open — "wide open, smiling." He says "Sylvia, it is I." He says it again. "Then, knowing that she was dead, he kissed her on the mouth."

The story ends there. The cat purrs through the night.

### The Garter

The garter is the mechanism. A small thing, decorative, intimate — fastened about the cat's neck by some caprice of its mistress. It carries information Severn extracts by close reading: the silver clasp with a mint mark, five eyelets, the fifth worn. He deduces the owner's form from the garter's construction. He reads the name. He goes to find her.

The object that arrives: like the Yellow Sign (found near the Aquarium, carried by Tessie), like Geneviève in marble (preserved in the pool), the garter is an object with information that only becomes readable to the person positioned to receive it. Severn is positioned to receive the name "Elven" because he already had history with it. The same name means nothing to the butcher, nothing to Cabane; it opens in Severn's hands.

### The Detective Form

The story is structured as detection. Severn interprets the garter systematically — each sentence of his monologue is a deduction. The rose silk, the silver clasp, the five eyelets, the worn fifth eyelet — all data, all interpreted. He assembles the mistress from the object before he meets her.

The detective form inverts at the end. He finds the room and there she is — eyes open, smiling. He says her name. He says it again. The detective story has found its subject, but the subject is dead. The deduction was correct and arrived too late. Or exactly on time: there was no help to give, so arriving when she died is the same as arriving after she died.

"Knowing that she was dead, he kissed her on the mouth."

He already knew, before he entered the room. "Your Sylvia is not my Sylvia" — he said this to the cat, reassuring himself. But the name Elven had "a charm" and "troubled him like perfume from dead flowers." He knew before he knew. The story was also over before it started.

### The Part II Pattern

Part I: the King in Yellow as organizing absence, driving characters to madness, death, and vision.
Part II: The King in Yellow absent entirely — no play, no Carcosa, no Pallid Mask. Instead: Parisian romances, Gothic miniatures, the atmosphere of loss and arrival.

But the formal move is the same. In Part I: the organizing absence is the unread second act. In Part II: the organizing absence is the person who is already dead. Jeanne d'Ys died for Philip in 1573. Sylvia Elven is dead in her room. The Phantom won't go forward. Both halves of the book are about something missing that organized everything around it.

The formal argument of the King in Yellow as a whole: presence is always already past. What you find when you find the thing you were looking for is its departure point.
