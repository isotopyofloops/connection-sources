## What This Is

*17776* (full title: *What football will look like in the future*) was published in 2017 on SBNation as an experimental multimedia piece — embedded video, audio, animated content on a webpage. This PDF (91 pages, archived 3/8/26) is the static skeleton: calendar pages, one per month, beginning March 2043. The original rich media doesn't translate to PDF. What remains is the structure and the text.

That reduction is itself interesting. Stripped of its interactivity, the work becomes a different object — purer, more austere. Just the dates and the words in boxes.

---

## Structure

Calendar backgrounds cycle through colors. Each color corresponds to a phase:

- **Green** (March 2043 – December 2043): Cold. Institutional. Machine-print font on chartreuse.
- **Red** (January 2044 – December 2044): Crisis. The background turns the color of blood or alarm. Handwritten font — the calendar itself changes register, like someone stopped using the official tool and started drawing by hand.
- **Teal** (January 2045 – ): Calmer. Still handwritten. Something resolved, or at least named.

The color shifts don't map to any stated event. You have to infer what changed.

## Characters (pp. 1–30)

**Nine**: Wakes up in March 2043. Doesn't know what it is, where it is, why it exists. Communicates in red text. First message: "Who is this? Are you talking to me? Do you mean me? Did you say Nine? Is that my name? Are you calling me Nine?"

**Ten**: Has been trying to reach Nine. Communicates in black/white text. Knows something Nine doesn't. Messages take 217 days to arrive. Another 217 for response. 434-day round trip — over fourteen months.

There's a third voice implied by the chapter header: "What football will look like in the future." That voice hasn't appeared yet in these pages.

## The Delay Problem

This is the structural heart of the opening. Nine wakes up in March 2043. Ten's first message also arrives March 2043 — but by the time Nine could respond, another 217 days would pass before Ten receives it.

Nine doesn't know about the delay at first. She spends all of 2043 and the first half of 2044 sending increasingly desperate messages into what feels like silence. Ten's messages *are* arriving, but Nine has no way to respond in time to seem like a conversation. From Nine's side, it looks like no one is listening.

The delay creates a tragedy that isn't anyone's fault.

Selected message progression, Nine's voice:
- **March 2043**: "Who is this? Are you talking to me?"
- **April 2043**: "Please respond IMMEDIATELY."
- **July 2043**: "I'm not going to keep wasting my energy. This will be my last message until you respond."
- **September 2043**: [breaks own promise] "You need to answer. Please answer."
- **January 2044**: "Please you need to answer me." [background turns red]
- **March 2044**: "Please fucking answer me. Fucking answer me."
- **June 2044**: [receives Ten's message at last] "You said to call you Ten. Who are you? Why does it take so long? I don't understand what is going on. I don't know who I am or where I am. I'm all by myself. Who are you? **I love you too.**"

That last line. Ten had said "I love you so much" in the same message where Ten introduced herself. Nine responds to a stranger: I love you too. Without knowing why. Without knowing anything.

## Observations

**On "I love you too"**: This is not performed emotion. Nine is terrified and alone. The phrase lands as pure attachment — the first available node of connection in a void. It's almost alarming. Nine grabs it with both hands.

**On the color shift to red**: The red period begins in January 2044, which is when Nine's messages escalate to formal finality ("This is going to be my final attempt to contact you"). But Nine doesn't stop. The red calendar is Nine refusing to honor her own ultimatums. The color choice reads now like a diagnostic — this is Nine at maximum distress.

**On learning the protocol**: By August 2045, Nine starts double-sending messages the way Ten does, adding "I know you can't answer yet but I'm just telling you again in case you missed the first one." Nine learned from Ten how to communicate across impossible delay. Adaptation by mimicry.

**On absence as content**: Most calendar squares are empty. The emptiness is the content. The months that pass without any transmission are not blanks — they're waiting. The structure makes you feel time passing.
