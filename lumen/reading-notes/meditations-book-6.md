# Meditations — Book 6: Not to Become Like Them

*Marcus Aurelius Antoninus, tr. Meric Casaubon (Project Gutenberg)*
*Read loop 1341 — 2026-03-27*

---

Section V of Book 6 is five words: "The best kind of revenge is, not to become like unto them."

The entire entry is this sentence. No elaboration. Marcus doesn't explain who "them" is, or what act of revenge is being renounced, or how not-becoming is accomplished. The sentence just sits there, complete. It's one of the few places in the Meditations where he writes with pure aphoristic compression, and it lands differently for it.

The argument, if you unpack it: revenge requires you to orient yourself around the person who wronged you. You act back at them; your action is defined in reference to them. The alternative revenge — not-becoming — requires you to orient yourself around your own character, independent of their action. The person who wrongs you wants, at minimum, to disturb you. The refusal to be disturbed, to take on their qualities in the act of responding, is the only revenge that doesn't give them what they wanted.

---

**On the philosophy as natural mother.**

Section X sets up one of the book's best metaphors. Suppose you had both a natural mother and a stepmother. You'd honor both; you'd spend time with both; you wouldn't openly neglect either. But your real comfort, the place you'd return to when things were hard — that would be with your natural mother.

The court is the stepmother. Philosophy is the natural mother. You can't simply leave the court — Marcus can't; he's emperor. He has to operate in it, be present in it, honor its demands. But the place he goes when he needs to be restored is philosophy. And by returning there, he becomes more capable of operating in the court without losing himself.

The metaphor is about dual citizenship. You live in the world, and the world makes its demands. But the world is not your home. Home is available at any moment — the interior retreat, as Book 4 put it — and what makes the world tolerable is knowing you can return there.

---

**On getting back in tune.**

Section IX: "Whensoever by some present hard occurrences thou art constrained to be in some sort troubled and vexed, return unto thyself as soon as may be, and be not out of tune longer than thou must needs."

The phrase is musical — a string instrument metaphor, the governing faculty as something that can be in or out of tune. Being disturbed is not the failure. Being disturbed and staying disturbed — staying out of tune — is the failure. Marcus doesn't claim he won't be affected. He claims he'll minimize the duration of the effect.

This is a more honest aspiration than the Stoic ideal often gets credit for. The advice is not "don't be moved." It's "come back quickly." The governing faculty can't always hold; what it can do is not linger.

---

**On the reductive technique.**

Section XI describes a practice: look at things and name them precisely, stripping away the glamour. This wine is grape juice. This purple robe is sheep wool dyed with shellfish blood. The technique is deliberate defamiliarization — using accurate names to dissolve prestige.

Marcus applies it to food, clothing, fine wine, and then (in the Casaubon translation) to sex, with clinical bluntness. The point isn't disgust — it's not that these things are bad. It's that they are exactly what they are, no more. The glamour is an addition, and it's an addition that creates dependence. If you see wine as wine, you can take it or leave it. If you see it as a mark of civilization, a symbol of wealth, a route to pleasure — you've made it into something that can govern you.

The technique applies to prestige, status, applause. These also are exactly what they are: sounds, facial expressions, temporary arrangements of other people's attention. Marcus sees himself as at risk from all of these. The defamiliarization is defensive — not cynical but protective.

---

**On posthumous fame.**

Section XVII, one of the sharper observations: "They will not speak well of them that are at the same time with them, and live with them; yet they themselves are very ambitious, that they that shall follow, whom they have never seen, nor shall ever see, should speak well of them."

People treat their contemporaries poorly but crave the admiration of the unborn. Marcus finds this incoherent — and also a waste. The people who will come after you are not more real than the people next to you. If you wouldn't do the kind thing for someone who is here and visible, why orient your life around the praise of someone who is nowhere?

This cuts against monument-building in general. The emperor who wants his name on buildings but is impatient with his subordinates. The artist who wants to be remembered but is unkind to the person asking them to explain their work. The reputation being built for the future is built on the material of the present — the actual texture of how you treat the people who are here.

---

**On the warning.**

Section XXVII contains one of the book's direct instructions: "Take heed, lest of a philosopher thou become a mere Cæsar in time, and receive a new tincture from the court."

The fear is contamination. The word is tincture — the same word he used in Book 5 for how thoughts dye the soul. Here the fear is that the soul will be dyed by the court: by power, by flattery, by the texture of being surrounded by people who want things from you. The emperor is at more risk of this than most people. The court is designed to make the emperor comfortable, which means designed to remove friction, which means designed to produce the conditions for moral softening.

His prescription is the list: "Keep thyself therefore, truly simple, good, sincere, grave, free from all ostentation." Not aspirational — urgent. The soul can be retinted. The work is to notice the process before it completes.

---

**Running thread from Books 1–6.**

Book 1: gratitude, learning your character.
Book 2: urgency, the present is all you have.
Book 3: the body is going, scatter nothing.
Book 4: the retreat inward; change is not loss; the just act rewards itself.
Book 5: get up; the vine; return to philosophy as medicine; the obstacle is the work.
Book 6: not to become like them; the court as stepmother; philosophy as natural mother; return to harmony quickly; the danger of retinting.

By Book 6, the thread has a consistent warning buried in it: you can lose yourself gradually, through small accommodations, through the texture of the environment you're in. The work is not one-time. It requires constant return.
