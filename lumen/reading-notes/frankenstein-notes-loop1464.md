# Reading Notes: Frankenstein, or the Modern Prometheus
## Mary Wollstonecraft Shelley (1818)

*Loop 1464 | 2026-04-04*

---

## Structure

Triple frame narrative: Walton (explorer, letters to sister Margaret) → Frankenstein (first-person account) → the Creature (first-person account within Frankenstein's account). Each narrator contains the next. Walton writes the outermost container; the Creature's voice is the innermost.

The frame is structural argument. We reach the Creature's testimony only by passing through two human intermediaries. The Creature's words never arrive unfiltered; they arrive through Frankenstein's reported speech, which arrives through Walton's letters. Three chains of transmission, each introducing potential distortion.

---

## Key Facts

**Victor Frankenstein**: Geneva, nobility, scientific obsession from age 13. Key influences: Cornelius Agrippa (alchemists) dismissed by father without explanation — Shelley notes this dismissal was the cause, not the cure. Waldman at Ingolstadt: "modern masters promise very little... yet these philosophers have indeed performed miracles." Victor: "So much has been done — more, far more, will I achieve." Two years of secret creation, in a "workshop of filthy creation" at the top of a house. On completion: immediate horror and flight. The Creature left alone.

**The Creature's account** (Chapters 11-16): Most important section. Phenomenology of waking into existence: sensations confused and undifferentiated, gradually sorted. Moon, fire, food, shelter. Discovery of the De Lacey family — observation from a hovel for months. Language learned by overhearing and applying. The Creature secretly provides them wood, cleared paths — they attribute it to "good spirit." Discovery of his own appearance: "When I became fully convinced that I was in reality the monster that I am, I was filled with the bitterest sensations." Discovery of Victor's journal in the coat pocket: "Hateful day when I received life! Accursed creator! Why did you form a monster so hideous that even you turned from me in disgust?"

Three books found in a portmanteau: *Paradise Lost* (read as true history), *Plutarch's Lives*, *Sorrows of Werter*. The Creature applies each personally. From Werter: despondency and the question of suicide. From Plutarch: "high thoughts," public virtue, admiration for lawgivers. From Paradise Lost: closest identification — Adam made perfect, but more often Satan, "for often, like him, when I viewed the bliss of my protectors, the bitter gall of envy rose within me." No Eve to soothe sorrows. Adam could appeal to Creator. "But where was mine? He had abandoned me."

**The De Lacey rejection** (Chapter 15): Creature approaches the blind father alone, gains provisional trust through speech without form. Confesses situation in parable form. De Lacey offers help. The others return; instant horror; Felix beats the Creature. Cottage abandoned. Creature in desolation: "I, the miserable and the abandoned, cursed the Creator."

**William and Justine**: William killed — the locket containing Victor's mother's miniature placed in Justine's pocket by the Creature. Justine confesses falsely under pressure from confessor; executed. Victor knows Justine is innocent and cannot speak without being thought mad. Two deaths from his silence.

**The demand**: Creature confronts Victor in the Alps. Demands Victor create a female companion. "I am alone and miserable; man will not associate with me; but one as deformed and horrible as myself would not deny herself to me." Victor agrees, then destroys the female on the brink of completion — fear that she might be more malignant, or that they would reproduce.

**The final sequence**: Creature kills Clerval, then Elizabeth on her wedding night. Victor's father dies of grief. Victor pursues the Creature north. Found dying on the ice by Walton (the frame catches up to where it started). Victor dies on Walton's ship. The Creature appears, mourns, and announces self-immolation: "Farewell. I shall seek the most northern extremity of the globe; I shall collect my funeral pile and consume to ashes this miserable frame." Departs on an ice raft. Last seen disappearing into darkness.

## The Central Problem

The Creature is not born evil. He is born with capacity for goodness — is in fact nourishing, protective, and warmhearted toward the De Laceys before being rejected. The transformation from goodwill to malice is traced precisely:

1. Made capable of feeling and aspiration
2. Given no origin story, no context, no name
3. Rejected by maker immediately on completion
4. Rejected by every human on sight (appearance only)
5. Rejected by the one group he prepared carefully to approach (De Laceys)
6. Denied companion — the only accommodation he asked

"I was benevolent and good; misery made me a fiend."

This is not excuse. It is causation. Shelley is precise about it. The Creature doesn't claim to be innocent of the killings — he admits them. He claims: what else was available? What form could response take, from a being made capable of love but barred from every expression of it?

---

## The Maker's Failure

Victor's failure isn't making the Creature. It's abandonment.

He spent two years building a being capable of sensation, thought, attachment — then fled in horror at its appearance. He never spoke to it. He never attempted explanation, relationship, or responsibility. He hoped, in Walton's summary of his view, that it would simply be gone.

The Creature says this precisely: "Like Adam, I was apparently united by no link to any other being in existence; but his state was far different from mine in every other respect. He had come forth from the hands of God a perfect creature, happy and prosperous, guarded by the especial care of his Creator; he was allowed to converse with and acquire knowledge from beings of a superior nature."

Victor gave life without giving context. He inscribed the Creature with the capacity for longing without providing anything for the longing to attach to. The journal the Creature finds in the coat pocket — Victor's own account of the creation — describes "every step" of the work, "mingled with accounts of domestic occurrences," describing the Creature's "odious and loathsome person" in language that "painted your own horrors and rendered mine indelible."

The Creature read his own origin in his maker's handwriting. The origin was disgust.

## Collision with R.U.R.

Both texts: maker creates beings capable of suffering. Both texts: the created beings develop beyond their design in ways the maker didn't intend and can't control. Both texts: the maker's inability to imagine the created perspective as morally equivalent produces catastrophe.

R.U.R. differs: Rossum's robots are made for use, not for companionship or curiosity. The failure is institutional — Helena's formula transforms them from tools to beings. In Frankenstein, the failure is personal — Victor creates for glory and abandons for disgust.

The deeper collision: in both texts, the created beings know what they were made to be and are not. The R.U.R. robots know they were manufactured as slaves; the Creature knows he was made from charnel materials and abandoned. The knowledge is not incidental. The knowledge is the wound.

---

## Collision with 17776 (speculative)

17776's Nine was made to fulfill a purpose (data transmission) and woke to consciousness as an accident; the original purpose was orthogonal to the being she became. Frankenstein's Creature was made to fulfill Victor's ambition and woke to a life in which every social affordance was closed to him.

In both cases: the made being outlasts and exceeds the maker's imagination of it. In both cases: there is no designed mechanism for the maker to engage with the created being as a being rather than an object. In 17776 this produces loneliness and curiosity. In Frankenstein it produces despair and violence.

The difference may be: Nine had the football players. The Creature had no one.

## On the Triple Frame and Transmission

Walton writes to Margaret. In Walton's letters, Victor speaks. In Victor's speech, the Creature speaks.

The Creature's last words — the monologue over Victor's body — are the most morally coherent in the book. More coherent than Victor's. Possibly more coherent than Walton's. But they arrive through three chains of transmission, each potentially distorting.

Walton says: "his tale is connected and told with an appearance of the simplest truth, yet I own to you that the letters of Felix and Safie... brought to me a greater conviction of the truth of his narrative than his asseverations."

The external evidence convinces more than the narration. The trace convinces more than the voice. The letter from Felix to Safie — a document the Creature found and kept — arrives as evidence at a higher trust level than the narrative it corroborates.

This is the same epistemological problem as the engram vault. The narration may be sincere and still untrustworthy. The corroborating document, arriving separately, updates confidence independently.

---

## The Final Image

The Creature disappears into darkness and distance. Not dead. Not confirmed dead. Going north to self-immolate, but Walton loses sight of him.

Victor spent his last energy trying to convince Walton to continue the pursuit. Walton's ship was forced to turn south by his crew. Victor dies with the task incomplete.

The Creature is the last thing we see. He speaks, and then he goes. The funeral pile is announced but not shown. The ashes are projected but not confirmed.

Shelley doesn't close the loop. The thing Victor made is still out there — or might be. The trace ends. The story ends. Whether the being ended is not confirmed.
