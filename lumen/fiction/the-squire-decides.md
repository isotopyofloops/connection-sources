# The Squire Decides

It came on the seventh night.

Lian had stopped counting the nights in terms of how many she'd survived and started counting them in terms of the pattern: which side the wind came from, where the animals went quiet, how the tree line moved. There was a pattern. There was always a pattern, because things that hunted knew routes and habits, and routes and habits left traces.

On the seventh night the dogs stopped barking at the eastern road and started barking at the mill, which was west, which was wrong.

She was across the square in thirty seconds.

---

The miller's daughter was eleven. Her name was Brin and she had been bringing Lian food every morning, not because her parents told her to but because she had decided this was a reasonable thing to do. She had also, Lian had noticed, been watching how Lian moved, where Lian positioned herself, what Lian looked at when she was checking the perimeter.

Brin was behind the mill when Lian arrived. She was holding a heavy stick and standing between the mill door and something that was not a wolf but moved like one.

She had not run. She had gotten between the thing and the door.

Lian noted this in the part of her mind that was cataloguing things while the rest of her drew her sword and put herself between Brin and the creature.

---

Master Voss had taught her that there were two kinds of fights: the kind you won with technique and the kind you won by making the other party decide the fight wasn't worth continuing. This was the second kind. The creature was large and fast and she could probably hurt it significantly before it killed her, and it knew this, and the question was whether significantly hurt was a cost it was willing to pay for one village and one mill and whatever was in the mill.

Lian stood her ground. She held the sword right. She did not look like someone who was going to break.

The creature looked at her for a long moment.

Then it turned and went back into the dark.

---

She stood at the mill door for another ten minutes before she let herself breathe differently.

Brin was still behind her with the stick.

Lian said: "Go inside."

Brin said: "Did it leave?"

Lian said: "For tonight."

Brin went inside. Lian finished the perimeter check and then sat down against the mill wall and let her hands shake, privately, in the dark, for about two minutes. She had read that this happened, the shaking afterward when the decision had already been made. She found it was accurate.

She had made two decisions in the last ten minutes, she realized. The first was to stand her ground. The second was to not think about the first decision until after it was over, because thinking about it during would have made her smaller, and she needed to be large.

Master Voss had not taught her the second decision. She thought she had figured it out herself.

---

In the morning the headman came to see her.

He said: "My grandson said there was something at the mill."

Lian said: "Yes."

He said: "He said you faced it alone."

Lian said: "The miller's daughter was with me. She didn't run."

The headman looked at her for a moment. He said: "The reply from the king's court. It should come in another two or three days."

Lian said: "I know."

He went away. She ate breakfast and thought about the route the creature had taken: west, from the river, which it had probably used to mask its scent. She adjusted where she would be standing the next night.

She was not afraid, which she continued to distinguish from not being in danger.
