# The Long Watch
## Day Seven

On the seventh day, the building showed them the roof.

Not a door this time. The staircase on the sixth floor, which they hadn't tried because the door at the top was locked, was unlocked. E found it — she'd been doing a systematic check, floor by floor, more organized than Mara's initial approach. She came down to get Mara, and they went up together.

The roof was ordinary. Grey stone, a low parapet, pipes and vents and a rusted iron structure that might have been a flag holder, long empty. From up here, the city was visible in a way it hadn't been from any of the windows — in every direction, but distant, as if the building were at the center of something without knowing it. The sky was overcast but bright. Below, very small, the courtyard with its stone and its plant.

"Still green," E said, looking down.

"Still green."

They stood at the parapet and looked at the city. Mara tried to identify buildings, streets, anything that would tell her where they were and what time it was in the broader sense. The city looked ordinary. It also looked empty, or nearly empty — no movement, no vehicles. Just buildings receiving light.

"I think people left," E said.

"When?"

"I don't know. Before we got here. Or while we were here and we didn't notice."

Mara thought about this. She'd been watching the courtyard carefully. She'd been noting the plant and the light. She had not been thinking about whether there were people in the city because the question hadn't occurred to her until now.

"Does it change anything?" she asked.

E considered. "I don't know. Probably not, for the watch."

"The watch continues regardless."

"Yes."

They stood there for a while in the wind. Then E said: "I found something else."

She'd found it on the roof: a small weatherproof box attached to the parapet, the kind a construction crew might use to store tools. It was locked, but a standard padlock, the combination default (0000). Inside: a notebook. New, unused except for the first page.

On the first page, in handwriting that was neither Mara's nor E's:

*If you've made it here, you're doing well. The watch continues from whoever started it. Keep the log. Mind the plant — it's a useful indicator, although of what exactly we were never sure. The building will show you things. Let it.*

*There is no exit, but there is also no urgency about that. The watch is the work. You were chosen for this because you are the kind of person who keeps going.*

*The last entry in this notebook should describe the day the watch ends. We do not know when that is. We do not know if it ends.*

*Good luck.*

No signature.

Mara read it twice. Then she handed it to E, who read it twice.

"Chosen," E said.

"Yes."

"By who?"

"I don't know."

E turned the page. The rest of the notebook was blank. Ready.

They took the notebook downstairs. They put it on the desk in Mara's room, which had become, by small increments, a kind of shared space — E's jacket sometimes over the chair, her notebook sometimes beside Mara's, both of them eating there when they ate together. Mara opened her own notebook to the current day.

*Day seven,* she wrote. *We found the roof. The city is visible. Empty, or nearly. The building gave us a new notebook: someone was here before us. The watch continues from whoever started it.*

She stopped. Then she wrote:

*I believe this. I don't know why, but I believe it. The watch is the work. I was not placed here; I was selected. I do not know the difference yet, but I will find out.*

She looked at that last sentence for a long time.

Then she looked out the window. Below: grey stone, the drain, the plant.

Still there. Still green. Light from somewhere.
