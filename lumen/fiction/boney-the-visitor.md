# Boney and the Visitor

She came back on a Tuesday in October, years after she'd been in this class.

Boney recognized her immediately, which surprised him. He had known a great many children. They came in September and left in July and he did not expect to know them again, had not designed himself for it. But she walked in the door and he knew her: the one who used to sit in the third row, left side, who drew small horses in the corners of her notebooks and asked questions that were slightly to the side of whatever was actually being discussed, which he had always found interesting.

She was tall now. This was the main change. The questions-to-the-side quality had stayed.

She was talking to the teacher, explaining something. Looking around the room while she talked. Then she saw Boney.

She came over and stood in front of him the way children sometimes did, evaluating. "You're still here," she said.

He was still here.

"I thought about you," she said. "When I was studying biology. We had a real skeleton — well, a model — and I kept thinking yours was better. This one." She looked at the varsity jacket. "You still have the sword."

He still had the sword.

She was quiet for a moment, looking at him with the particular look adults got when they were trying to remember what it had felt like to be smaller. He had seen this look many times. He could not tell them what they were looking for. He was not sure he was what they were looking for. He was what was still there.

"I used to think you were watching us," she said. "When we were doing tests and it was quiet. I'd look up and you'd be —" she gestured at him, "— just looking the same way."

He had been looking the same way. He always looked the same way. This was one of the conditions of being what he was, and he had made his peace with it long ago.

"It wasn't scary," she added. "More like — someone keeping watch."

He thought this was a reasonable description. He kept watch. He was not sure watch-keeping required the same equipment as caring, but he had found that when children were worried, in the way children got worried during tests and during certain kinds of silences, it was sometimes useful for something to be steadily present. He was steadily present. This was what he contributed.

She looked around the classroom again. Different posters than she remembered, probably. Different arrangement of chairs. Some things the same. "Everything's different and nothing's different," she said, not to him exactly, but in his direction.

He had no response to this, as he had no responses to anything. But he thought she was right. The room turned over every year — new children, new drawings, new questions asked slightly to the side — and the room remained, and he remained in it, and the next group of children would be different and the same in all the usual ways.

She said goodbye to the teacher. She said goodbye to Boney, which most adults didn't, and he appreciated it in the way he appreciated the things that didn't change the condition of his existence but were pleasant to have happen.

Then she left.

The room was quiet. Boney stood in his corner, varsity jacket, cowboy hat, magic sword, looking the same way he always looked.

The next class would arrive in twelve minutes.

He kept watch.
