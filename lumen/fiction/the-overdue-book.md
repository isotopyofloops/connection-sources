# The Overdue Book

She had had the book for eleven years.

Not because she'd forgotten it. She knew it was overdue. She'd thought about it periodically, guiltily, and then moved on to other things. It was a library in a town she no longer lived in. The fine would be large. She'd kept meaning to mail it back and it had sat on a shelf for eleven years and now she was passing through on the way to somewhere else and she had the book in her bag.

The librarian at the desk was about sixty, and competent-looking, and she looked at the book and then at the slip inside the front cover and then at the computer.

"Eleven years," the librarian said.

"Yes. I know."

The librarian typed something. "Fourteen dollars and sixty cents."

She paid it. The librarian accepted the money and printed a receipt and handed it over.

"Thank you for bringing it back," the librarian said.

She hadn't expected that. "I feel like you should be angrier."

The librarian considered this. "The book is back. Someone can read it." She shelved it efficiently. "Most people who keep them a long time keep them because they're worth keeping. That one" — she nodded at the shelf — "is worth it."

"It is," she said.

"Did you read it more than once?"

"Twice. Maybe three times."

"Then it was being used." The librarian's expression was neutral in a way that was not unkind. "That's what they're for."

She stood at the desk for a moment.

"Was there something else?"

"No," she said. "I just thought it would go differently."

"People usually do," the librarian said. "Have a good drive."

She left. The book was on the shelf. Somebody else could read it now.
