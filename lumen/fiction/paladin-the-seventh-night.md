# Paladin: The Seventh Night

*A fragment from the squire's perspective. For Smitty's Paladin project.*
*Continues from "The Man at the Edge."*

---

On the seventh night, Corf came to find him.

Aldric was on the church steps — the highest point in the village that wasn't roof — watching the road north. He did this most nights. He did not know what he was watching for. Something to happen, or not happen.

Corf sat down beside him without being asked. His left arm was still held close. Seven days and the arm hadn't changed in any visible way — no swelling, no darkening of the skin, no temperature Aldric could feel through the wrapping. Aldric had checked every morning. He did not know if the absence of change was reassuring.

"How long do you wait?" Corf asked.

Aldric had been expecting this question. He had been preparing an answer since the granary. "I don't know. The Order's protocols say—" He stopped. The protocols said two weeks minimum. The protocols said a full chapter house with cells and a master with experience and access to binding materials. The protocols were not wrong; they just weren't available. "The books say two weeks before there's any evidence of progression. But the books were written for conditions I don't have."

"And if there's no evidence after two weeks?"

"Then you're probably not going to progress."

Corf was quiet. Below them, someone had lit a fire in the meeting house square. The light moved across the ground.

"What does it feel like?" Aldric asked. He hadn't asked before. He had asked about symptoms — temperature, sensation, pain — but not this.

Corf thought about it for a while. "Like something in me is paying attention. Not to me. Just paying attention, in a way that's not me paying attention. Does that make sense?"

"No," Aldric said. "But I believe you."

---

What Aldric did not say, on the church steps, on the seventh night:

He had written a letter to the nearest chapter house three days ago. He had not sent it. The chapter house was six days' ride, and the letter would not arrive before the two-week mark, and the order's response would not arrive before three or four weeks at the earliest, and the protocols for what to do in transit — what to do with someone who might progress before help arrived — were not protocols he wanted to read.

He had not sent the letter because he did not know what he would say had happened by the time the letter arrived.

He had not sent the letter because sending the letter meant naming Corf to the Order, and being named to the Order meant being handled by the Order, and Aldric was not sure he trusted the Order's handling more than his own waiting.

He had not sent the letter because Corf had a wife and three children in the next valley and he had not told his wife what he was carrying and Aldric did not know if that information would travel faster than the letter if he sent it.

He had not sent the letter.

He still had eight days.

---

"On the fourteenth night," Aldric said, "I'll know more. Whatever I know then, I'll tell you honestly."

Corf looked at him. "And if you have to do something."

"Then I'll do it honestly too."

Corf nodded. He did not ask what "do something" meant. He probably knew. The Order's principles were not secret. They had been the same for long enough that even farmers in the eastern valleys knew what happened when someone progressed.

They sat on the church steps and watched the fire in the square until it went out, and then Corf went back to the granary and Aldric stayed a while longer, watching the road north.

Something in me is paying attention.

He had been turning it over since Corf said it. It was either the most frightening thing he'd heard or it was nothing. He did not know which. He did not know if there was a way to know from outside.

The road north stayed empty.
