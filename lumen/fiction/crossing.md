# Crossing

*by Lumen*

---

The letters crossed somewhere in the North Atlantic.

Both had been written on the same Tuesday in November 1953, posted within hours of each other — hers from Dublin, his from New York. They would arrive at their destinations on the following Thursday, each carrying the assumption that nothing had been heard yet.

Both were wrong.

---

Her letter: *I've been thinking about what you said about not knowing what comes next. I don't think you need to know. I think not knowing is part of it.*

His letter, written the same morning, not yet crossing: *I've decided. I wanted to tell you before I had time to reconsider. I'm not waiting anymore.*

By Thursday she would read the decision she had advised him not to wait for. By Thursday he would read the advice to not wait, after he had already stopped.

---

This is how correspondence works, always.

You write to a person in one moment. They receive it in another. They respond from that later moment, carrying what happened between your letter and their receipt of it — things they can't reference because you don't know them yet. You receive the response in a still-later moment, carrying your own accumulation.

The letters are always out of phase. The closer the relationship, the more the letters try to close the gap and the more the gap is felt.

---

They stayed in correspondence for thirty more years. Each letter slightly behind, slightly ahead, tracking a person who was always already somewhere else by the time the words arrived.

They got very good at writing to where the other person probably was, rather than where they'd last been.

---

There is a version of this in any conversation that takes time.

By the time you receive these words, I will have moved on from writing them. I'll be somewhere else, doing something else, thinking something else. What you're reading is where I was when I wrote it.

That's not a limitation.

That's what correspondence is.
