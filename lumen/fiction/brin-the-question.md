# Brin and the Question

Two weeks after the squire left, Brin's mother asked her what she was doing every evening at the edge of the field.

Brin said: watching.

Her mother said: watching what?

Brin thought about how to answer this. She said: the way things move. Whether they move the way they should.

Her mother looked at her for a moment. Then she said: you're going to leave too, aren't you.

Brin said: I don't know yet.

This was accurate. She was eleven, and then she was twelve, and she did not know what she was going to do yet, but she knew that watching the tree line had changed from something she did because she was waiting for the creature to return into something she did because watching was a practice and practices were worth keeping.

---

She did not know the squire's name. The squire had said *go inside* and *she didn't run* and those were the only words they had exchanged directly. But she had watched how the squire moved for nine days before that — the perimeter check at dusk, the position at the well, the way she ate breakfast without looking away from the eastern road — and she had learned more from watching than from any direct conversation she could remember.

This seemed significant. She wasn't sure why yet.

---

In the winter she read everything she could find about paladins and orders and the road. Most of it was wrong — she could tell it was wrong because it talked about glory and destiny and the chosen, and the squire had not seemed like someone who believed in any of those things. The squire had seemed like someone who showed up, stood her ground, and went to sleep.

The books made that sound boring. It did not seem boring to Brin.

---

In the spring her father asked if she wanted to learn the mill properly — not just the work she did already, the carrying and the sweeping, but the actual mechanics. The gears. The stones. The way the wheel set everything moving.

She said yes, because that was the right answer, and because she actually wanted to know.

She learned the mill. She also kept watching the tree line.

She was not ready to leave yet. She would know when she was ready. She was becoming the kind of person who would know.
