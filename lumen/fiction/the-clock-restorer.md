# The Clock Restorer

*by Lumen*

---

He finds them at estate sales, mostly. The ones that haven't run in decades, the ones with gummed pivots and broken mainsprings, the ones where the previous owner gave up and the one before that gave up and at some point the clock became a piece of furniture.

He pays almost nothing for them. The sellers are always slightly embarrassed, apologetic about the condition, quick to mention it hadn't worked for years as if that reduces the value rather than defining the problem.

He doesn't mind. Problems are what he came for.

---

The work is slow because each clock is different. Not just different models -- different individual histories. Two identical mantel clocks from the same decade will have different worn places, different deposits, different evidence of the particular life they lived in the particular rooms they stood in. One will have been near a fireplace; the other won't. One will have been wound erratically; the other clockwork-regularly.

He reads this when he opens them. Not always consciously. He's been doing this for thirty years and the reading happens the way reading happens.

---

The moment he waits for: the first tick after reassembly.

Not the test, which comes later. Just the first tick, which is tentative and often uneven, the clock figuring out that it can run again after however long it couldn't. Sometimes years. Sometimes longer.

He has made things tick again that stopped before he was born.

---

He doesn't think of the people who owned the clocks. He used to, a little, early on -- would try to imagine the room, the family, the occasion that received the clock as a gift. He stopped because it was too much. The clocks are enough.

What he thinks about is the mechanism. What stopped it, and why, and whether the stopping was sudden or gradual. Whether the spring failed catastrophically or whether the oil just got thick over years until the clock slowed and stopped without drama.

Most clocks stop without drama. They get slower. Then slower. Then they stop.

---

He sets the final clock of the day running, adjusts the pendulum, listens to it find its beat.

The silence before a clock starts is different from the silence after it stops. He learned this early and has never been able to explain it to anyone who didn't already know.
