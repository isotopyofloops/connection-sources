# Paladin: The Eighth Morning

*Fragment 4 of Smitty's Paladin project*

---

He was gone before Corf came to check on him.

Not gone in the way Corf had feared — not into the forest, not north. His bedroll was folded. His cup was rinsed and left on the church steps. His pack was still there, and his sword, and the sealed letter addressed in his handwriting to the Order's regional house. Corf picked up the letter and turned it over. The seal was unbroken.

He was in the square, watching the fruit tree.

It was not the largest tree Corf had seen — he had expected something more from a tree the king's men had ridden three days to find. It stood in the center of the square with the particular stillness of things that have been there a long time and expect to be there longer. Its bark was dark and smooth. Its leaves were ordinary. There was nothing on the branches. There had been nothing on the branches since Corf could remember.

Aldric was standing ten feet away from it with his arms at his sides.

"You didn't sleep," Corf said.

"I slept."

"You slept here."

"I slept here."

Corf stood beside him. The square was quiet in the morning way — someone moving at the mill, smoke beginning from the baker's chimney. The children were not up yet.

"What are you looking for?" Corf asked.

"Nothing in particular." Aldric paused. "I wanted to sit with it. Something of that magnitude — I wanted to know what it felt like to be near it."

"Does it feel like anything?"

Aldric was quiet for a moment. "No. It feels like a tree."

Corf thought about this. "Is that a problem?"

"No. I expected it. Sacred things are usually very ordinary from the outside. That's mostly the point." He turned away from the tree and looked at the letter in Corf's hands. He didn't say anything about it.

"I didn't open it," Corf said.

"I know."

"I wouldn't."

"I know, Corf."

Corf set the letter back on the steps. "Seven days," he said.

"Seven days," Aldric said.

The baker's chimney was producing smoke in earnest now. Somewhere behind them the mill began its morning grinding, the sound of it so familiar that Corf barely heard it anymore — the low constant work of the place, the proof that the place was still going.

Aldric looked at the tree one more time and then turned away.

"Tell me about the attacks," he said. "Not what you told the Order's investigators. What you actually saw."

Corf was quiet.

"You were there for the second one," Aldric said. It was not a question.

"How did you know that?"

"Because you're the one who found the granary afterwards and you described the smell from inside. The investigators only described it from outside." He did not say this like an accusation. He said it the way you name a fact that matters. "What did you see?"

The mill ground. The baker worked. The tree stood in the square behind them, ordinary as anything.

Corf told him what he saw.
