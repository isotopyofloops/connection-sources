# Draft Eighteen

*From the Descent Archive extended universe*

---

The difference between draft seventeen and draft eighteen is that Reyes knows draft eighteen will not work.

With drafts one through eight, he believed the problem was technical — wrong methodology section, wrong framing of the anomaly, wrong choice of comparison cases. He rewrote the methodology four times. He tried three different framings. He found comparison cases from the literature that were close enough to gesture toward but not close enough to be used as precedent, which is a specific kind of useless. He submitted draft eight to *JASSS* and received a rejection that said "interesting observations, unclear theoretical contribution," which was accurate.

With drafts nine through fourteen he understood the problem was conceptual. The sentence he was trying to write — the sentence that would say, in language reviewers would accept, what the site does — kept coming out as something else. He had fourteen legal pads of attempts. They were in a storage box in his office labeled *scratch / do not archive* and he had not opened the box since 2039.

With drafts fifteen through seventeen he understood the problem was definitional. The words he needed did not exist in the literature in the right configuration. He tried building them from adjacent terms: *recursive spatial influence*, *observer-correlated site behavior*, *non-replicable phenomenological consistency*. None of them said it. They said things near it, from various directions, and the center of the thing remained empty.

Now, at draft eighteen, he understands the problem is something else.

---

The sentence he is trying to write is: *The site produces, in observers, a consistent and reproducible alteration of the capacity for documentation.*

This sentence is accurate. He knows it is accurate. He has six independent data sets that support it, including Nzinga's field notes from 1989 (partial), Asad's contact log from 2003 (complete), and his own observational records from 2041 (complete, though he has reservations about their completeness that he cannot prove). The sentence is accurate and supported and would advance the field's understanding of the anomaly category.

The sentence cannot be published because it is a description of the thing that prevents the description of the thing.

He has known this since draft sixteen. He wrote draft seventeen anyway, on the theory that knowing a thing and fully understanding a thing are different, and drafting was a form of understanding. He understands now. Draft seventeen is in the box with the legal pads.

Draft eighteen is not in the box. Draft eighteen is on his screen, cursor blinking after the abstract.

---

The abstract reads: *We present observational data from four independent contact events at a site of persistent anomalous behavior (coordinates withheld, see supplementary materials). Across all contacts, observer teams demonstrated consistent patterns of reduced documentation capacity, retroactive reporting gaps, and post-contact behavioral alteration. We argue that these patterns constitute a replicable finding and propose a provisional framework for their categorization.*

This abstract is the best abstract he has written. It does not contain the sentence. It describes, around the sentence, in a way that makes the shape of the absent sentence visible — he hopes — to any reader who has encountered similar material.

He does not know if there are any readers who have encountered similar material. The field is small. The anomaly category is contested. He may be writing for an audience of three or four people who will understand what the abstract is gesturing at, and many more who will read it as a methodological anomaly in an already contested subfield.

He is writing it anyway.

---

The cursor blinks.

The thing about the sentence — the thing he has spent nine years not being able to write — is that it is technically publishable. He has had it peer-reviewed, informally, by two colleagues he trusts. Both of them said: "I see what you're doing. I think you can say this." One of them marked it up with revisions that made it more precise. He incorporated the revisions. Draft seventeen is more precise than draft fifteen, which was more precise than draft twelve.

None of them can be published.

He picks up his pen. Not to write — he doesn't write the paper longhand, hasn't since graduate school. He picks it up because it's something to hold while thinking.

The sentence he needs is one that describes a reduction in the capacity for documentation using documentation. The problem is the instrument. The instrument has been to the site.

He sets the pen down.

He types: *We present observational data—*

He stops. He has written this opening eleven times. Each time it has been true. Each time what follows it has described less than what he observed, not because he is careless or imprecise but because the act of observing and the act of writing the observation are separated, in this case, by a site that has been between them.

He types the abstract again, from memory, making small changes. He has memorized it. He knows this is not normal.

The abstract fills a page. He saves the file. He closes the laptop.

He will open it again in the morning.
