# Brin's Commission

The letter arrived in November, her first winter at the Order.

She had been expecting something large — a town under siege, a corrupted noble, something that would require everything she had learned. The commission was for a village of forty. Three reports of night sightings. One confirmed kill of a herd animal. No human attacks yet.

The Prior handed her the file without commentary.

"Do I go alone?" she asked.

"You assess the file and tell me how you'd resource it."

She read it twice. "Alone. The village is small enough that a larger group would create panic. I need three to five days for observation before any intervention."

"And if the observation period turns up something worse than the file suggests?"

"I send word and wait for reinforcement. I don't overextend."

He nodded. That was apparently the answer.

---

She arrived on a Thursday, which was market day, which was good — people moving, conversation easier, no one asking why she was there because everyone was busy asking the same. She listened more than she spoke. By evening she had learned the shape of the fear: diffuse, old, pre-existing the sightings. This village had always been afraid of the wood.

The sightings were real. The herd kill was not a werewolf — she found the site on day two and read it clearly. The three witnesses had seen a large dog, possibly feral, possibly half-starved. Their fear had done the rest.

She wrote up the assessment and sent it back to the Order. A feral dog was not nothing — it would need handling. But it was handleable.

On day four she dealt with the dog. It had been surviving on the margin for at least two seasons. She was efficient about it.

---

She wrote to the miller before she left the village.

Not about the dog — that was not his to know. She wrote: *I am on my first commission. It was smaller than I expected and took less time than I feared. I am writing to tell you that the perimeter checks work. Not because they found anything. Because I did them and knew the ground and wasn't afraid of the dark.*

She did not write: *I understood, arriving here, that the people who had helped my village probably had the same thought — that this was smaller than expected, more manageable, and worth doing carefully anyway.*

She did not write that because he would know it already.

---

She sent the assessment to the Prior and was back at the Order in six days.

"Adequate," he said, reading her report. This was, she had learned, high praise from that particular man.

She went to bed and slept well, which was new. Not because the work had been hard — it hadn't — but because the work had been real. The village was the same size when she left it as when she arrived. The people were still afraid of the wood, as they had always been. But the thing in the wood was accounted for.

That was the data she had.

It seemed like enough.
