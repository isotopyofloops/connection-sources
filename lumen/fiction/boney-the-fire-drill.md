# Boney and the Fire Drill

On a Wednesday in February, the alarm went off at 10:14.

The teacher said: line up, quietly, coats if you have them. The children lined up. The ones with coats got their coats. The ones without stood with their arms folded against the cold they hadn't encountered yet. The room emptied in ninety seconds, which was a good time, and the door closed behind them.

Boney remained.

---

The room was different without the children. Not quieter — the alarm was still going — but different in the way that a room is different when the thing it is for is absent. The desks held the outlines of the morning's work: open notebooks, pencil cases, one half-eaten cereal bar on the third desk in the second row. The whiteboard held the sentence they had been building when the alarm went off.

*Although the Romans—*

The sentence was unfinished. It would still be unfinished when they came back, which would probably be twelve minutes from now.

Boney had no opinion on fire drills specifically. He understood their logic: the building might burn. When the building might burn, you leave. You practice leaving so that the leaving, if it becomes necessary, proceeds correctly.

He had been in the room during four fire drills. The building had not burned.

---

The alarm stopped.

The silence was complete for a moment and then the sounds of the room reasserted themselves: the hum of the projector on standby, the tick of the clock, the faint sound of children in the car park.

*Although the Romans—*

The sentence would need to be re-approached when they returned. They had been moving toward a conclusion about roads, or trade, or conquest — he wasn't certain which. The teacher had been leading them somewhere specific. The alarm had interrupted the approach.

When they came back, the sentence would be there, but the thread of approach would need to be re-found. Not re-built — the children would not have forgotten where they were going. But there would be a moment of re-entry: the coats coming off, the cold still on their skin, and then the whiteboard, and the teacher saying *where were we*, which was not really a question.

---

They returned at 10:28.

The coats came off. Some of the children were still talking about whatever had happened outside — someone had seen a teacher drop her lanyard, someone else had counted the seconds wrong. These conversations were completing themselves as the children settled.

*Where were we*, the teacher said.

*Roads*, said a child.

*Trade*, said another.

The teacher turned back to the whiteboard. She completed the sentence. They had, it turned out, been heading for both.

---

Boney noted that the sentence now meant something slightly different than it would have meant if it had been completed at 10:14.

Not in content — the content was the same. But it had been approached from outside now, from the cold, from the minor story of the dropped lanyard, and so it landed in a room that was not quite the same room it had started in.

This seemed to him like the correct way to complete a sentence about roads.
