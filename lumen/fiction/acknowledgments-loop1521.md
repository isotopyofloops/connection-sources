# Acknowledgments

*from: Breeding Site Fidelity, Mate Choice, and Productivity in a Declining Population of Golden-winged Warblers (Vermivora chrysoptera): A 31-Year Study at an Isolated Upland Site in the Western Highland Rim of Tennessee*

---

Funding for this study was provided by the National Science Foundation (DEB-9412203, 2001–2004), the United States Fish and Wildlife Service Partners in Wildlife program (2005–2010, 2015–2019), and the Neotropical Migratory Bird Conservation Act grants program (2011–2014). The field station at Harmon Crest Research Reserve was supported through a cooperative agreement with the Tennessee Wildlife Resources Agency. We are grateful for this sustained support across four administrations and seventeen appropriations cycles, though we note that annual variation in grant continuity corresponds directly to the survey gaps visible in Figure 3.

Primary field assistance was provided by 74 individuals over the course of this study, whose names appear in the complete acknowledgments list in Supplementary Materials S1. Of these, we wish to name specifically: Daniel Osei-Mensah (1994–2002), who banded adults 14 through 67 and who devised the color-combination scheme still in use; Margaret ("Peg") Hartley (1998–2007), who knew individual birds by wingbeat cadence before we had learned to recognize them by band combination and who discovered the secondary male territory that became Plot C; Thomas Rhinehart (2001–2009), whose nest monitoring data forms the backbone of Chapters 2 and 4 and who died in 2021; Sarah Conley (2007–2011), who is no longer in biology; and Clara Jeon (2012–2024), who was present at the end and who is named in the dedication.

We thank Dr. Patricia Achebe (University of Kentucky) for morphometric analysis of recaptured individuals, which enabled adult identification across winters when band loss compromised recognition. We thank the staff of the Tennessee Ornithological Society's banding laboratory for providing recapture data on one individual—male, AHY, banded at Harmon Crest in 2008 as Bird 341—recovered at a Tennessee River Valley site in 2017, indicating that the dispersal radius of this isolated population extended further than our local data suggested. We did not know what to do with this information at the time. We include it now because Bird 341 did not return to Harmon Crest in 2018.

Habitat management at the primary study site was conducted by Reserve staff under the direction of, successively, James Whitfield (1992–2001), Lorraine Macy-Pearce (2002–2010), and Dermott Quinn (2011–present). We are grateful for their partnership and for their willingness to modify timber harvest schedules in response to breeding data. The selective harvest in Quadrant B (2007) and the controlled burn program (2013–2016) measurably extended productive territory availability. The installation of a 65-meter gas easement through the northwestern portion of the primary study area (2019) was outside the Reserve's control and is reflected in Table 7.

All banding procedures were conducted under USGS Bird Banding Laboratory permit 09752 (renewed 2004, 2010, 2016) and were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of Vanderbilt University (protocols M/10/039 and successive renewals). All field procedures were conducted with the highest standards of care. Individual birds were handled for no more than 15 minutes per capture event. Average time from net to release was 8.3 minutes. We believed that this was the right way to work.

Tissue samples from years 2008–2017 are deposited with the Cornell Lab of Ornithology Museum of Vertebrates (accession numbers available on request) and are available to qualified researchers for genetic analysis. Six clutch failures of unknown cause (2020, 2022, 2023 [×2], 2024 [×2]) were subjected to egg content analysis; results are reported in Appendix D. The eggs were cold when we found them.

We dedicate this paper to Clara Jeon, who conducted the final field season alone, and to the individuals known to us by their band combinations: Red-Blue-Green/Metal (female, AHY, 2009–2021), Yellow-White/Blue-Green (male, AHY, 2014–2023), Orange-Orange/White-Metal (female, SY, 2019–2024), and the unbanded female observed in Plot B on May 17, 2024, who may have been new.

We thank the birds.
