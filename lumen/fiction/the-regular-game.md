# The Regular Game

Every Thursday at seven, Martin played chess with his neighbor Václav.

Martin had not won in eleven years.

This was not approximate. Martin kept records — not scores, because the scores were uniformly one-sided, but dates and openings and the point at which the game had turned. He kept them in a small notebook. Over eleven years the notebook had accumulated considerable specificity about exactly how he lost.

---

The question had been asked, by various people, over various years: why did he keep coming?

The answers Martin had given, at various points:

*Because Václav is my neighbor and it would be rude not to.*

*Because I'm getting better.* (This was true in an abstract sense. He was losing at a higher level.)

*Because the game itself is interesting regardless of the result.*

*Because I've been doing it for eleven years and stopping would require deciding to stop.*

---

The real reason, which Martin had not given to anyone, was harder to name.

It had something to do with Václav himself — not liking him, exactly, though he did like him, but something else. Václav played the way a mathematician solves a problem: without sentiment, with complete interest, with a kind of respect that was expressed entirely through the quality of his attention. When Václav played badly — which happened, rarely, usually when he was distracted — the game was worse. Not just for Václav's side. For both of them.

Playing against someone who was fully present and better than you was a specific experience. Martin did not have another word for it. You were not playing against him, exactly; you were playing in the space he created by taking you seriously.

---

He had won one game, four years ago. He did not know if Václav had let him win. He had not asked. The game was in the notebook with the others. He had noted the opening and the turn and the point at which something had gone different from every other week.

He was not sure he could reproduce it. He was not sure he should try.

---

Thursday at seven, Martin arrived with the board. Václav had the tea ready.

They set up the pieces. Václav took white. The game began.

Martin lost in thirty-one moves, which was better than his average.

He noted it in the notebook. He would be back next Thursday.
