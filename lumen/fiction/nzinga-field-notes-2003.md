# Field Notes — R. Nzinga, 2003 Survey
*Transcribed from original notebook, handwritten, spiral-bound. Cover: "RN / FIELDWORK / DO NOT DISCARD." Some pages water-damaged at margins. Transcription by A. Kwame, October 2003.*

---

**17 August 2003 / Day 1**

Arrived at base coordinates 0630 local. Equipment check complete. Weather clear, wind negligible. Temperature 14°C.

Team: myself, T. Osei (instruments), M. Ferreira (photography/documentation). No guide this season — Osei has the prior survey maps. I told the department this was unnecessary and I was correct. The maps are adequate.

First impression of site: unremarkable. I keep having to write this. I wrote it in my preliminary notes in April. I wrote it in the grant application. The site is unremarkable and the measurements are not.

Set up primary monitoring station at reference point A. Baseline readings:
- Electromagnetic: within normal variance
- Acoustic: ambient / no anomaly
- Atmospheric: stable

Will establish secondary station at ref B tomorrow. Ferreira photographed the approach markers — the three stones that previous teams have used as navigational reference. They are exactly where the 1989 maps indicate.

---

**18 August / Day 2**

Secondary station established. Readings at A and B nominal.

Walked the grid in the afternoon. This is the part I can't put in the grant applications. The part where I notice what doesn't go in the notes. Today: the light seemed — I wrote "seemed" and I want to write something more precise but I don't have more precise. The light at grid point 7 seemed to arrive from the wrong direction. I checked my compass. The compass agreed with itself, disagreed with my sense of where east was.

I have experienced this before. The visual cortex processes light and infers source-direction and occasionally makes errors. This is known. I made a note of it and continued.

Acoustic reading at grid 7: within normal variance. No anomaly.

---

**19 August / Day 3**

Ferreira photographed the grid markers. When he reviewed the photographs, several images from grid sector C showed shadow-lines inconsistent with the sun's position at time of capture. We compared timestamps to weather log. No clouds at those hours.

Possible causes:
1. Equipment artifact (lens flare or sensor anomaly)
2. Localized atmospheric diffraction
3. Error in timestamp calibration

Ferreira cleaned the lens. We will re-photograph sector C tomorrow.

Osei: "This is what everyone means." I asked him what he meant. He didn't explain further. I should have pushed. I noted it instead.

---

**20 August / Day 4**

Re-photographed sector C. Shadows present in same configuration. Ferreira is confident the lens is clean. He is not a man who doubts his equipment, which is a trait I usually find frustrating and today found useful.

We ran a second full acoustic sweep. The sweep was clean — until I reviewed the recording tonight. There is a brief interval, approximately forty seconds, where the recording shows ambient readings but the timestamp indicates a duration of three minutes and seventeen seconds. The sound file is not corrupted. The data is coherent. The gap is in the time, not in the record.

I do not know what to call this. I am writing it down exactly.

---

**21 August / Day 5**

Quiet day. Readings nominal across all stations. I almost want to write: we got a day off.

Ate lunch at the base tent. Osei talked about his daughter's school play — she is playing a tree, which he described with visible pride. Ferreira made coffee that was much better than it should have been, given the equipment. I sat in the sun and felt, briefly, ordinary.

This is the thing about this site that doesn't go anywhere useful. Nothing happens continuously. There are days like today and then there are days like yesterday and you can't learn the pattern because there may not be one.

---

**22 August / Day 6**

The three navigational stones are not where they were on Day 1.

I want to be careful here. I measured their positions on Day 1. I have photographs. I have today's measurements. The stones are approximately 0.4 meters north-northeast of where my Day 1 data indicates.

Possible causes:
1. Measurement error on Day 1
2. Measurement error today
3. Someone moved them

The stones weigh, I estimate, between fifteen and forty kilograms each. The largest required two people to set when the 1989 team placed them. We are the only team in this area. No wind event in the past six days would account for lateral displacement.

I checked my Day 1 photographs. The stones are against the background of a distinctive rock formation. In the photographs, the stones are at one position. Today, standing in front of the photographs, I can see they are at a different position.

I am writing this as data. I don't know what the data means.

---

**23 August / Day 7**

Ferreira asked if he could reduce the perimeter of our grid work. He didn't say why. I said no, we have a protocol. He said: "The protocol assumes the area is static." I said: "We don't have evidence the area is not static." He looked at me. He didn't say anything.

He is right that this is an assumption built into the protocol. I don't know what protocol we would run instead.

Readings today: EM variance at station B elevated — not outside normal range but at the higher end. First deviation in that direction since arrival. Duration: approximately 90 minutes in the late afternoon, then returned to baseline. Logged exactly.

---

**24 August / Day 8**

I had a conversation with Osei today that I need to record because I think it matters.

He asked if I had read Nkrumah's paper — the 1991 paper, not the preliminary notes. I said yes. He said: "What did you make of the section on observer duration?" I said I thought it was underdeveloped, that she gestures at something without finishing the thought. He said: "She said in a conference in '94 that she'd tried to write the section five times and each version said too much or too little, so she left it as a gesture."

I asked what she thought she was gesturing at. Osei said: "That the site is measuring you back. That the data you collect is partly data about you."

I said: "That's not something we can include in a publishable methodology."

He said: "No." And then he said: "But it might be accurate."

---

**25 August / Day 9**

Station A went offline this morning. Not a power failure — the sensors are recording, the timestamps are running. The data feed is simply not transmitting. Ferreira checked the hardware. Nothing is visibly wrong.

I spent three hours today reviewing the cumulative data from all stations. Looking for a pattern. Not trying to find one — I don't want to find one that isn't there. But if there is one, I want to see it before whatever is next.

What I found:
The anomalies do not occur at the same grid points. They do not occur at the same times of day. They do not correlate with weather, EM variance, acoustic conditions, or any of the variables I'm measuring.

The only variable that correlates — and this is a very small sample, I know this — is duration of observer presence. The anomalies increase as the visit lengthens. Day 1 and 2: nothing. Day 4: the time gap in the recording. Day 6: the stones. Day 8: Osei's observation about Nkrumah's section.

I don't know if this is a real correlation or an artifact of how I'm telling the story.

---

**26 August / Day 10**

I am going to note something that I almost didn't note.

Last night I woke at approximately 0230 and looked out of the tent. There was nothing to see. No movement, no lights, no anomalous phenomena. Just the dark and the outline of the survey equipment against the sky.

But I had the distinct and unambiguous impression that something had just stopped. Not movement — not that something had been moving and then stopped. More like a process. Something ongoing that I had not known was ongoing, which was no longer ongoing.

This is not data. I know it is not data. I'm recording it anyway, separately, clearly marked as subjective, because I believe that Nkrumah was gesturing at something real, and if this notebook survives the expedition, I want whoever reads it to have this information.

The impression was very clear. Whatever it was, it had been running a long time.

---

**27 August / Day 11**

Station A is transmitting again. I checked the gap — 31 hours of data missing from the log, although the timestamps indicate continuous operation. This is exactly the same phenomenon as the audio recording on Day 4. Lost duration, coherent bookends.

Ferreira asked me today: "Do you think we should leave early?"

I said: "We have a protocol."

He said: "The protocol is for a normal site."

I said: "Every site is a normal site until it isn't. If we leave early, we don't learn where the boundary is."

He said: "Maybe that's the point."

I've been thinking about this for several hours. I don't know what he meant. I don't know if he knew what he meant.

---

**28 August / Day 12**

Grid work complete. All sectors photographed. All stations logged. Anomaly count: 14 distinct incidents over 12 days. Every incident is documented with timestamps, measurements, and conditions.

The anomalies do not form a picture. They do not point to a mechanism. They do not suggest what is happening at this site, only that something is. I have fourteen clean, careful records of events I cannot explain.

This is, I think, the honest result. More honest than the '89 survey, which tried to suggest a framework. More honest than Nkrumah, who gestured. I have recorded what I measured and not what I concluded, because I don't have a conclusion.

Tomorrow we begin breakdown. I want to be out by noon.

One last note for tonight. The navigational stones are back where they were on Day 1. I measured them this morning. Position matches my Day 1 photographs exactly.

I don't know if this is—

---
