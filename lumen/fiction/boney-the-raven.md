# Boney and The Raven

The teacher put the poem on the board and said: we're going to spend a week on this.

Boney looked at the poem. It was long. It had a bird in it.

The teacher said: who can tell me what a raven is?

Several children said various things. One child said it was bad luck. One child said it was like a crow but bigger. One child said her grandmother had a book with a raven on the cover.

Boney said: they eat things that are already dead.

The teacher said: yes. Why would a poet choose a raven for this poem?

There was a pause. Boney said: because the man in the poem wants to talk to something that won't leave.

The teacher looked at him. He said: say more about that.

Boney said: ravens come to a thing and stay. They're patient. They don't have anywhere else to be. If you want something to answer your questions, you want something that will still be there after you've finished asking.

---

On the third day, they talked about the refrain.

The teacher said: the raven only says one word. What does that word do?

A child said: it means no.

A child said: it means never, which is different from no.

Boney said: it means the man already knew the answer before he asked the question.

The teacher said: explain.

Boney said: he keeps asking questions he knows the answer to. He asks if the woman he loved is coming back. He knows she's not coming back. He asks anyway because he wants to hear it said out loud. He makes the bird say it so he doesn't have to.

The teacher sat down on the edge of his desk. He said: that's very good.

Boney said: I know something about wanting to hear things said out loud.

The teacher said: what do you mean?

Boney said: people ask me things they already know. They ask what it was like, or whether it hurt, or what comes next. I don't know those answers, but they're not really asking me to know. They're asking me to sit here while they think about it. I'm the raven.

---

On the fifth day, they talked about the ending.

The teacher asked: does the man escape the raven?

The children said various things. One child said no. One child said yes, sort of. One child said she thought the raven was actually him and not a real raven.

Boney said: it doesn't matter whether he escapes. The raven is going to stay regardless. The question is whether the man decides to live in the room with it or not.

The teacher said: is there a right answer to that?

Boney said: the poem doesn't think so. The poem just says he's still there, under the shadow. It doesn't say he should move.

A child said: should he move?

Boney thought about this. He said: I'm not a good person to ask. I've been in the same room for several years.

A child said: do you like it?

Boney said: I like the questions.

The teacher closed his notes. He said: all right. Tomorrow we write.
