# Paladin: The Thirteenth Day

*A scene for Smitty's Paladin story. Follows "The Vigil."*

---

On the thirteenth day, Corf's shadow was wrong.

Not by much. Aldric saw it in the granary doorway at midday — Corf standing in the sun, and the shadow he cast was the right shape for a man but not quite the right weight. Too dark at the edges. Too still when Corf moved. Aldric looked away and looked back and the shadow looked ordinary. He was not sure what he had seen. He was not sure he had seen anything.

He checked Corf's arm. The wound was the same — or appeared the same. No swelling, no heat, no discolouration beyond what had been there from the first day. Corf submitted to the examination without complaint and watched Aldric's face while it happened, the way people watch a physician's expression when the physician thinks they're not looking.

"One more day," Aldric said.

"Then what?"

"Then I'll know more." He had said this before. He did not say: then I will either tell Corf's wife, or write the letter to the chapter house, or do the other thing. He did not say any of those things because he had been saying them to himself for six days and they had not gotten clearer.

---

What had changed: not the arm. Not the way Corf talked, or moved, or conducted himself in the village. He had gone back to work on the fifth day, doing what he could one-handed in the fields. People had stopped watching him quite so closely. The village had decided, with the pragmatic resolution of places that need everyone to pull their weight, that Corf was probably fine.

What had changed was smaller than any of that.

The morning of the twelfth day, Aldric had come to the granary before Corf was awake. He had sat by the door in the early dark and waited. Corf had stirred, and in the moment before waking — in that specific interval when a body moves before consciousness has caught up with it — Corf had turned his head toward the north wall. Not toward the door, where a sound might come. Not toward the light. Just: north. With a deliberateness that looked less like sleep-moving and more like attention.

Then he woke up and was Corf again. He nodded at Aldric and asked if there was bread left from last night.

Aldric had not said anything. He had given him the bread.

---

On the thirteenth afternoon, he went back to the old woman.

She had been expecting him. Not in the way of prophecy — in the way of someone who had watched young people working through difficult problems for long enough to predict their timing. She put tea in front of him before he sat down.

"You found the answers to the right questions," she said. "Now you have new questions."

"Something is changing." He said it carefully, as if the words themselves might do harm. "I don't know what. It's not the symptoms in the books. It's something smaller."

She was quiet for a moment.

"The books were written about people who progressed," she said. "Not people who didn't. If you want to know what doesn't progress — and why — you need a different source."

"Is there one?"

She refilled her cup. The small hospitality of continued interest. "There is someone," she said. "Two days north. An old order — not yours, older. They keep records of the cases the paladin order never saw because they were resolved before the order arrived. Not all of them resolved well. But some of them did."

"What kind of records?"

"The honest kind. What the person was, before. What they did and didn't do. What was different about the ones who held."

He thought about the shadow. The turned head. The thing he wasn't sure he had seen.

"I can't leave," he said. "The tree. The king's man arrives in—"

"Four days." She set down her cup. "You could be there and back in three."

He looked at her.

"I've made this trip once myself," she said. "A long time ago, for different reasons. The road is passable. The order is small but they receive visitors."

---

He told Corf that evening. Not all of it — not the shadow, not the turned head — but the shape of what he'd found and where he was going.

Corf listened without expression.

"And if the records say there's no holding," he said. "If none of them held."

"Then I come back and I tell you that honestly. And we decide what to do."

"We." Corf said it the way someone tests weight on a floor they're not sure about.

"You made a promise on the church steps. To wait. To let me find what there was to find. I'm finding it."

Corf looked at his arm. At the wrapping that hadn't changed in thirteen days. At the granary wall, and then at Aldric.

"Two days north," he said. "Come back in three."

"Yes."

A pause.

"Bring me something back," Corf said. Not asking. Not quite asking. "About whether it gets easier. Whether the paying-attention settles. I'd like to know that."

Aldric said he would.

---

He left before dawn, riding north, with the order's records in his saddlebag and the shadow on the granary wall sitting somewhere in his thinking where it wouldn't go away.

One day.
