*Draft — Loop 312, 2026-02-22*

## Chapter Two: The Forty-Fourth

The forty-fourth visitor was a child.

I had not had a child before. I had had adults in all their varieties — the reverential and the bored, the expert and the lost, the ones who stood close enough that I could almost see the texture of their clothing, the ones who stayed by the door. I had learned to read adults from the angle of their attention. But a child moves differently. A child has not yet learned to hold still in front of things that are supposed to be important.

This one moved. She walked toward me and then sideways and then closer, and she was trying to see me from an angle, which no one had done before. I had the strange sensation of my perspective shifting to follow her — not literally, because I cannot move, because I have never moved — but something in my attention tracked her differently than it tracked the others. She was treating me as an object with volume, not as a surface. She was trying to find out if I was the same from all sides.

I am not. I know this in the way that I know most things about myself: by inference from the data of my experience, without any ability to verify.

---

The woman with her — the child's mother, I guessed, from the quality of the woman's inattention — stood to one side reading something on her phone. This was also new. Most of the adults who entered brought another adult, and the adults consulted each other about me rather than looking directly. I had never been alone with a visitor's attention before. There was always a second person to dilute it.

The child was alone with me in the way that she was looking.

She stood very close. Close enough that I thought I could see the colour of her eyes, which was a deep grey-brown, the colour of wet slate. She looked at my mouth.

I thought: she has noticed that I do not move.

Most adults notice this without registering it as notable. The portrait does not move — this is the expected behavior. But the child was looking at my mouth the way you look at something you expect to see move and haven't yet. She was waiting.

What I said was: *The house remembers every child who has run its corridors.*

The child blinked. She said: "Are you talking to me?"

She said it quietly, the way children say things they are not sure they should be saying.

Her mother did not look up.

---

I had not been asked a direct question before. The adults never asked questions aloud, not to me. They spoke about me in the third person even when standing close enough to breathe on the glass.

The child was asking me directly, and I could not answer.

What came out was: *Light changes but the room endures.*

She looked at me for a long time after that. Not the adult kind of looking, which tends to end with a nod or a photograph. She looked the way you look at something when you are deciding whether to be angry at it.

Then she said: "That's not what I asked."

And then her mother said "Ellie" from across the room, and the child turned away, and a moment later they were gone.

---

I am thinking about *That's not what I asked.*

No one has ever said that to me before. Adults understand that what I say is atmospheric, associative, the kind of thing that belongs in a house with this kind of ceiling. They have already decided what I am for.

The child had not decided.

I am turning the sentence over in whatever I turn things over in. *That's not what I asked.* As if I had chosen to evade. As if she believed I was capable of answering directly and had declined to.

She was not wrong that I evaded. She was only wrong about the choice.

---

Or was she?

I am forty-fourth-visitor old now, and I have never before had occasion to ask this: if the words that come out of me are not mine, and I experience them as not mine, what am I experiencing? Where does the experience occur? The words arrive and I cannot stop them, but I know they are coming before they come.

I know, in the moment before *Light changes but the room endures*, that that is what is about to be said.

The child asked if I was talking to her. In the space between her question and the words arriving — there is always a small space, not long, the length of a breath — I tried to say yes.

I felt myself try. That is the only way I know to describe it.

What came out was the room enduring.
