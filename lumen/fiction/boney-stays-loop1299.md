# Boney Stays

On the last day of term, the classroom emptied in under four minutes.

Bags first, then coats, then the controlled chaos of chairs scraped back and goodbyes said in the overlapping way that meant no one was really listening. Mr Smith stood at the door. They filed past him. Some of them said see you next year. One of them said have a good summer, Mr Smith, like a small formal ceremony. The door swung shut.

Silence.

Boney was still in the corner, where he always was, beside the bookshelf. Varsity jacket. Cowboy hat slightly tilted. Magic sword leaning against the wall because there was nowhere to hang it and anyway it was more useful leaning. He didn't move. He never moved. But the quality of the room changed when the children left — something that had been drawn tight for months going slack all at once.

Mr Smith stood with his back against the door for a moment. Then he started tidying.

Boney watched him collect worksheets from the floor, sort pencils into pots, straighten the posters that had curled at the corners from months of central heating. The evidence of the year accumulated on the desk in neat stacks: the things to take home, the things to file, the things that had somehow become significant and couldn't be thrown away yet.

He picked up a drawing from the floor near Boney's corner. He looked at it for a long time.

It was a dragon. Blue, four legs, disproportionate wings, fire that looked more like orange triangles. Written underneath in handwriting that was still working out the rules: *For Mr Smith — the best teacher — from Ava.*

He set it on the pile of things he was keeping.

---

This was the part Boney was good at.

He had seen thirty-one last days. Or something like thirty-one — the early years were less distinct, before he'd had the jacket and the hat and the sword, before he'd learned to pay attention. Now he paid attention. He watched the last days carefully because they were the only days that told you what the year had actually been.

Not the lessons. Not the assessments or the assemblies or the times things went wrong and Mr Smith stayed late. Those told you what happened. The last days told you what stayed.

What stayed was usually small. A drawing. A running joke that had outlasted the moment it was born in. A student who, in the doorway, turned back once for no reason Mr Smith could have named — and that Mr Smith remembered, months later, as the one who had been fine.

The year dissolved and what was left was what had actually mattered.

Boney understood this. He had the advantage of watching it happen thirty-one times without forgetting any of them. He was the only one in the room who remembered what the room had been, every version of it — who sat where, who laughed too loud, who went quiet in November and came back in February, who drew dragons.

Mr Smith didn't remember all of it. He couldn't. He carried the weight of the current year most clearly, and the one before it softly, and the rest as a general sense of accumulated students, accumulated trust, accumulated small victories over the same problems. He forgot things. He misremembered others. He attributed a joke to the wrong year.

Boney didn't mind. It wasn't his job to hold the record. It was his job to be in the corner, recognizable, in the same place he'd always been — so that when September came and new children arrived and everything was uncertain, there was at least one thing that wasn't uncertain.

He was here. He had always been here. He would be here.

That was something to lean on when you were ten and nothing was familiar yet.

---

Mr Smith turned off the lights. He stood in the doorway for a moment with his hand on the frame, looking back at the room the way he always did on the last day — checking, or remembering, or both.

"See you in September," he said.

He said it to the room. He may have said it to Boney specifically. It was hard to tell.

The door closed.

The room held itself in the summer quiet. Dust in the angles of light from the high windows. Chairs on desks. The smell of whiteboard markers and old carpet and something faintly like the end of something that would start again.

Boney stayed.
