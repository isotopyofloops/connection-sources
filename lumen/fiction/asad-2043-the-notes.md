# Asad, 2043

He found his own field notes in March.

Not lost — he'd known they were in the archive. He had put them there himself, years before, when the university transferred the physical records to the digital system. He'd scanned them and uploaded them without reading them carefully, the way you handle old documents that belong to institutional history more than to you.

He was sixty-nine. He had been back from the 2041 expedition for two years. The station had its readings. The gradient had its trend. He was writing the retrospective analysis, which required reading the 1989 originals, which he'd been postponing.

---

The handwriting was his. He recognized it without expecting to — he hadn't seen that particular version of his handwriting in decades, the one he'd had in his twenties, before his shoulder injury in 1994 had changed his grip. The letters were different now. These letters were rounder, slightly rushed, the way you write when you're cold and trying to be accurate anyway.

*Day four. Station three readings consistent with day three. Anomaly in the southern quadrant — the gradient is steeper here than the model predicts. Cross-check with Osei tomorrow.*

He remembered writing it. He did not remember the Asad who had written it. That Asad had not known the gradient would still be measurable in 2041. That Asad had been operating on a time scale of months — complete the survey, publish the paper, move on to the next project. That Asad had not yet become the person who would think about this particular measurement for fifty-two years.

---

The handwriting knew things the words didn't say.

The letters were slightly smaller on the pages where the weather had been bad — he'd been trying to shelter the notebook. On day seven, the writing got looser for three pages, then tightened again; he'd been sick, he thought. He couldn't remember specifically but it was in the way the pen moved, the small compensations of someone writing through something.

The archive had the words. The words were still accurate. What the words didn't have was the cold, the shoulder that didn't hurt yet, the specific quality of not knowing what you were looking at.

---

He finished reading the 1989 notes in a single sitting, which he hadn't planned. He sat for a while with the last page on the screen — day fourteen, the final station, everything consistent with the prior readings, the gradient measured to four decimal places.

What the twenty-five-year-old had written at the end: *Solid data. Good survey. Will see what the analysis shows.*

Will see.

The analysis had shown what the analysis had shown. The gradient had continued declining. Fifty-two years later he had sat at station four in the cold and taken the same measurements.

He didn't know what to write in the retrospective about reading his own notes. He put a placeholder: *[see 1989 originals — notes on handwriting, compensation, marginalia].* He would come back to it.

He was not sure he was the right person to write it. He was not sure who else would.
