# The Cartographer

*Loop 1398 — 2026-03-29*

---

The map was more accurate than the territory.

This was not a figure of speech. The survey team had worked for eleven years in the basin, and the maps they'd produced were better than what you'd find walking the ground. The maps showed the seasonal variation. The maps showed the subsidence rate. The maps showed three features — two small lakes and a ridge — that had since disappeared, absorbed into the wetland as the water table rose.

To walk the territory now was to find gaps where the map had landmarks. The map was not wrong. The territory had changed.

---

Daria maintained the maps.

Her job was to keep them current — to annotate the features that no longer existed, to add the features that had appeared, to mark the disputed areas where the team's surveys disagreed with satellite readings. She spent most of her time in the office, working with data. She went into the field twice a year.

What she had come to understand, over fourteen years, was that the map and the territory were not in competition. They were different things. The map was a record of the territory over time. The territory was the current state. The gap between them was information.

The lakes that were gone — their absence was on the map, in the annotation layer, marked with the date of last confirmation. The map had not forgotten them. The map knew they were gone. The territory didn't know anything.

---

She had tried to explain this to people who found it strange.

The strange thing, to most people, was that she cared about features that no longer existed. Why maintain records of gone lakes? If the territory has changed, update the map and move on.

What she couldn't convey easily was that the gone lakes were doing work. The rate at which they'd disappeared — cross-referenced with the subsidence data and the water table readings — was evidence for a specific mechanism. Take out the gone lakes and the mechanism became harder to argue for. The absence was part of the case.

She kept everything. The map had layers going back to the first surveys. Everything was annotated. Everything was dated. Nothing was deleted.

---

The basin would continue changing. The maps would continue accumulating layers. At some point she would retire and someone else would take over the annotation work.

The gone lakes would still be on the map.
The record was not for her.
