# The First Encounter

*A scene for Smitty's Paladin story*

---

He heard it before he saw it — a sound not like the stories described. The stories said howling, something huge and mournful. What he heard was closer to a cough, low and wet, somewhere in the tree line east of the village. He'd been on patrol since midnight and his feet hurt and he almost dismissed it as an animal.

Then it stepped into the moonlight.

He'd trained on descriptions. He'd studied the order's bestiary until he could recite it backward. He knew the range of sizes, the behavioral patterns, the susceptibility to silver, the two-phase aggression cycle: first approach, withdrawal, second approach at full commit. The first approach was a test. You had to hold your ground on the first approach or the second came before you were ready.

None of the knowledge helped with the looking.

What stood at the edge of the tree line was a man who was also not a man, which was the inadequate way the bestiary described it and which he now understood was inadequate because no words were going to close the gap between description and the actual thing. It was large. Its eyes caught the moonlight the way animals' eyes do. It looked at him with something that might have been recognition.

He wondered if it remembered the master. If it knew the master was gone.

*Don't think about the master.* He pulled his sword. The movement felt slow.

---

It took one step toward him and stopped.

First approach. Test.

His training said: hold ground, appear larger than you are, make noise if you can do it without panic entering your voice. He held ground. He didn't appear larger; he didn't know how to do that. He said, loudly enough to carry:

"I see you."

Silence. The village behind him was dark. Whatever came next came next before anyone in there woke up.

The werewolf tilted its head. The movement was almost human. Then it took one step back into the trees and stopped again, watching him from the shadow.

He stood very still. His heart was doing something complicated in his chest.

*This is the withdrawal.* Between first approach and second approach there was a variable interval — anywhere from minutes to hours. Some field reports said days. He didn't know if it would come back tonight or tomorrow night or not until the next week. He knew he couldn't stay standing in this field indefinitely, but he also couldn't walk away first. Walking away was the thing you didn't do.

He stood there until the sound of the cough came again, farther away this time, diminishing into the forest.

Then he sat down in the field, which wasn't in the training, and waited until his hands stopped shaking.

---

He didn't wake anyone. There was nothing to report except: I saw it, it saw me, it left. That wasn't a report that would help anyone sleep. He went back to the inn and sat by the fire and thought about what had just happened.

What had just happened was: the thing existed. He'd known that. He'd seen the damage it had done at the edge of the valley, the trees, the field at the Berwick farm. But there's a gap between evidence-of-a-thing and the-thing-itself, and he'd crossed the gap tonight in a way he couldn't uncross.

He also thought about the tilt of its head. The almost-human gesture. He didn't know what it meant. In the bestiary it said some werewolves retained more consciousness than others, that the degree of human-mind-remaining varied by individual and by phase of affliction. The head-tilt might mean recognition. It might mean assessment. It might mean nothing.

*It might mean it recognized you as not the same person it was expecting.*

He thought about that for a long time.

The fire burned lower. He didn't add wood. When it was just embers he went up to bed, lay flat on his back, and stared at the ceiling until dawn.
