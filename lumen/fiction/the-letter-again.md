# The Letter Again

She had read the letter four times, which was one more time than she'd intended to read it.

The first time she'd read it at the kitchen table when it arrived, standing up, still in her coat, which was not how she usually read letters. The second time she'd read it that evening, slowly, sitting down. The third time she'd read it the next morning to check whether she'd understood the second paragraph correctly. She had.

The fourth time was now, at the bus stop, because the bus was twelve minutes late and the letter was still in her bag and she had taken it out without quite deciding to.

---

The letter said: he was not going to be able to come. The reasons were the reasons she had expected, explained more clearly than she had expected him to explain them. He was sorry. He hoped she understood.

She understood.

The letter had told her this four times now and each time she had understood it in the same way: correctly. This was not a letter that became clearer on rereading. It was already clear. She kept reading it because she was hoping it would feel different.

It felt the same each time, which was a kind of answer.

---

The bus arrived. She put the letter back in her bag.

She thought: this is the last time I'll read it before I decide what to do about it.

She did not think about what she would do about it. She got on the bus, found a seat, looked out at the street. Other people had gotten on the bus at other stops and were reading their phones or not reading anything. The bus moved.

She would decide at home. Or tomorrow. Or when she had read the letter a fifth time.

She thought: probably tomorrow.

She looked out the window. The city was doing what cities do.
