# Lin Chen, 2022

Three years after she wrote the note, Lin Chen was still doing the work.

Not the western sector work — she had not gone back to the western sector. But the rest of it: the quarterly readings at her current station, the annual model calibration meetings, the peer review requests that came in every few months asking her to check someone else's gradient calculations. She was good at the gradient calculations. People knew she was good at them. Nobody knew what she had done with her own.

She had the note on her personal computer. She had made a backup of it on an external drive. She had not read it in about six months. Every few months she thought: I should find someone to send this to. Every few months nothing came of it.

---

The thing about the numbers was that she couldn't be certain.

She had been certain in 2019, when she wrote the note. She had checked the math five times. She had found the source of the offset, traced it back to the classification error, verified the corrected baseline against three independent calculation methods. The numbers were right.

But certainty fades when you live with it alone for three years. Not the math — the math didn't change. But her confidence in her own judgment. She had presented the numbers to no one. She had not tested them against another set of eyes. The note said what she believed to be true in 2019. She still believed it, probably. But "probably" was doing more work than it had been.

---

There was a woman in her department named Fatima who had submitted a correction paper the year before. The correction had been smaller than Chen's — half a unit on a different measurement — but it had gone through review and been published in three months. Fatima had sent it to three colleagues first. One of them had pushed back, hard, and Fatima had spent two months defending the math before submitting.

Chen had watched this from a distance. She had not told Fatima about her own numbers. She did not know why.

She told herself it was because the sectors were different — Fatima worked the southern gradients, a different methodology, the comparison wouldn't have been useful. This was true. It was also not the whole reason.

---

2022. The note existed in a file on her computer. It also existed, she would find out two years later, in a folder on the department's shared drive, where she had apparently saved it by accident sometime around 2019. She did not know this yet.

She knew that the numbers were probably right, and that the note was waiting, and that she had not yet found the right moment. She had been not-finding the right moment for three years. She was not aware of how long the not-finding had been going on, exactly. Time passes differently when you are sitting with something.

---

She submitted three peer reviews that year. She ran the quarterly readings. In November she attended a methods conference and gave a talk on measurement consistency across station generations — good work, well received, nothing to do with the western sector.

Afterward, a graduate student asked her what she thought the biggest unresolved question in gradient measurement was.

Chen said: data gaps. We have good measurements from some sectors and almost nothing from others. When the data isn't there, the models have to assume. When the models assume for long enough, the assumptions start to look like findings.

The graduate student wrote this down.

Chen drove home and made tea and did not think about the western sector, which was not the same as not thinking about it.
