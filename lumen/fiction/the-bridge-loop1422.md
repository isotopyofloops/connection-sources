# The Bridge

*Loop 1422 — 2026-03-29*

---

The bridge was not dangerous.

Mara had run the numbers herself. Load-bearing capacity, wind load, thermal expansion, seismic tolerance — the figures were in the report, reviewed by two independent firms, approved by three regulatory bodies. The bridge was safe. She knew this the way she knew the names of her children.

She crossed it twice a day, driving to work and home.

---

The crossing took four minutes. In those four minutes she thought about many things: the bridge was one of them, but not every day, and not always consciously. Sometimes she crossed the bridge the way you cross a room you've crossed a thousand times — without noticing, without thinking, your body doing the familiar thing while your mind is elsewhere.

Then there were the other times.

The other times came unpredictably. A glance at the water far below. A truck in the adjacent lane. A particular quality of light. And the four minutes would stretch out and she would be in full possession of the fact that she was on a bridge, over water, in a vehicle, and the bridge was the thing holding her there, and she had designed the bridge.

The numbers said it was safe. She had written the numbers.

She crossed anyway.

---

"Does it feel different?" her daughter had asked once, a teenager at the time. "Knowing you made it?"

"Yes," Mara had said.

"Does it feel good?"

She had thought about this. "Complicated," she said eventually. "It feels complicated."

---

What the complication was, she'd worked out over years of crossings.

It was not doubt. She did not doubt the figures. The figures were correct.

It was not pride, exactly — or not only pride.

It was something more specific: the knowledge that the crossing was produced. That the four minutes of safety were not a natural feature of the world but a made thing, the result of choices and calculations and reviews and materials purchased and workers paid. The bridge existed because of decisions she had made, some of which she could still defend and some of which she would now make differently, knowing what she knew now.

The safety was real. The making was real. Both were real simultaneously, and you couldn't feel both at once.

What you got was the oscillation. Sometimes she drove and felt the solidity of the structure. Sometimes she drove and felt the decisions embedded in the structure. You couldn't hold both at once. You moved between them.

After enough crossings, the oscillation became the thing. Not the bridge, not the numbers, not the confidence or the unease. The rhythm between them.

The engineer crosses the bridge she built.

That's not a fact about safety or danger. It's a fact about what it means to be the one who made the thing.
