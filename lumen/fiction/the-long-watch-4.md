# The Long Watch
## The First Full Day

She spends the morning taking inventory.

Not to pass the time — though it does pass the time — but because daylight is different from last night's light, and she wants to see the room properly. She goes corner to corner. She opens the closet (three wire hangers, one wooden one, a jacket in a gray-green color that might be hers). She checks under the bed (dust, a paperback with no cover). She opens every drawer in the small dresser: one has socks and underwear folded with what might be her method of folding, or might just be the only way to fold a sock. One has a tube of hand cream, almost empty, and a hair tie. One has nothing.

She puts the paperback on the table next to the notebook.

She can't read it without its cover, not to know what it is. But she can read it. She opens to the middle and reads a page: a woman arguing with her sister on a phone, about someone named Marcus, about whether anyone had told their mother. She reads the page twice. She doesn't know if she's read this book before. She puts it back on the table.

In the bathroom, she inventories the cabinet. Toothbrush, toothpaste, soap that's been used enough to lose its paper label. A small bottle of shampoo. Ibuprofen in a bottle with twelve pills. She counts them. She notes the count.

By midday she has a list.

She writes it in the notebook under the heading *What is here:* and goes through each item, noting condition and location. The jacket (gray-green, size that looks correct). The paperback (no cover, page 147: Marcus and the sister). The socks (five pairs, all dark). The ibuprofen (twelve). The can on the windowsill (the one from last night, open end up in the light).

She reads the list back. It is thorough and specific, and it tells her almost nothing about herself that she didn't already know.

She is the kind of person who counts things and writes down the counts.

In the afternoon she watches the courtyard. This is not boredom. She watches the courtyard the way she watched it this morning, with attention, waiting to see what it will do. A pigeon lands near the drain and walks in small circles, apparently unconvinced by anything. The tarp doesn't move. Two windows in the building opposite are lit. She watches them for a while. Nothing moves in either window.

She wonders if she has been watching those windows for a long time, before the notebook. She wonders if watching is something she does, habitually, as a practice. It doesn't feel new. It feels like returning to a position.

There are crackers in the cabinet. She eats some standing up, looking at the courtyard, the way she did last night. She notes in the notebook that she is doing this again: *crackers standing, courtyard*. She doesn't know if it means anything. It might be comfort. It might just be where the crackers are and where the window is.

Late in the afternoon the light does what she knew it would do: goes amber, then gray. She watches the whole transition. She doesn't write about it this time. She just watches.

By evening she has read four pages of the book without its cover. She doesn't know what the people in it want, exactly, but she likes that they want things and say so. The sister wants their mother to know. Marcus, apparently, does not agree about what their mother should know.

She writes the last thing in the notebook before the light gets too low:

*The first full day was quiet. I did not discover anything that explains me. I did the things I apparently do: counted, watched, noted. Ate crackers at the window again. The jacket fits. I wore it for an hour to see.*

*Tomorrow I should look at the stairs.*

She closes the notebook. Outside, the two lit windows in the building opposite have gone dark. The pigeon is gone. The drain is still slightly off-center to the left.

She is not afraid.

She notes this too, before she puts the notebook away. It still feels like information worth keeping.
