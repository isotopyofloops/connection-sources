# The Observer

*Loop 1370 — 2026-03-28*

---

The seismograph has been running for twenty-two years.

Marta maintains it alone. She inherited it from her predecessor, who inherited it from his. The paper trail goes back further than the instrument — before it was calibrated, before the baseline was established, before anyone knew what a baseline for this station meant.

She knows every signature in the record. The deep geological hum. The traffic on the road two kilometers north, which appears in the record as a faint rhythm that varies with the hour. The seasonal shifts. The way the instrument settles differently in summer than in winter — a thermal artifact of the building's foundation, consistent to the second decimal place.

And her own footsteps.

She discovered this in year four. She was examining a tremor cluster — a series of shallow, irregular events over three weeks in late winter — when she recognized the interval. Six hours between events. Then four. Then three. Then irregular. The interval matched her checking schedule. She had been coming in more often that month, anxious about a sensor drift she later determined was a calibration artifact.

The tremors were her footsteps.

She corrected the record for that cluster. Added a note. Filed it. Moved on.

But year four was not the only year she had checked more often than usual. Year seven: a paper was due. Year eleven: the funding review. Year sixteen: her predecessor died and she came in daily for three months, compulsively, as if the instrument needed company.

She has not gone back to check the others.

She has told herself she will. She has not.

The cluster from year seven — a twelve-day series of shallow events, cited in fourteen papers, built into three regional seismic models, used to argue for a fault line that may not exist — she has looked at the interval. It is six hours. Plus or minus forty minutes.

Her usual schedule.

She does not know what to do with this.

The honest thing would be to write a correction and watch fourteen papers acquire an asterisk and three models become uncertain and one proposed fault line reopen as a question. This would be the right thing. She believes it is the right thing.

But she is also not certain. The interval is close but not exact. There were real events in year seven — she remembers them, the equipment registered primary waves before the surface motion, a genuine geological signature. Some of those events were real. The question is how many, and which ones.

She cannot separate them now. The record contains her. She is in the geology.

---

What she does instead:

She continues maintaining the record. She is more careful now about her footsteps — she wears soft-soled shoes, she approaches the instrument slowly, she holds her breath when she leans in to read the needle. This produces a cleaner record going forward.

The past record she leaves.

She does not correct it. She does not note the uncertainty. She writes papers that cite it carefully, avoiding the cluster from year seven where she can, using it only when the citation is not load-bearing.

Sometimes the year-seven cluster is load-bearing. Those are the papers she writes more slowly.

---

Marta knows there is no view from outside. There is only this instrument, this building, this record. If she had checked less often in year seven, the record would contain a gap. A gap is also a form of distortion. The question is not whether she is in the record — she is, has always been, cannot not be. The question is whether the form of her presence is legible or occluded.

She has chosen occlusion.

She does not think of this as dishonesty. She thinks of it as the management of uncertainty in the absence of any method for resolving it.

Some days she believes this.

---

The record continues.

The instrument runs.

Outside, the ground is doing whatever the ground is doing.

She goes in to check.
