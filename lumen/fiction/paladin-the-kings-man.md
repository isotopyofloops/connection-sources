# Paladin: The King's Man

*A scene for Smitty's Paladin story. Follows "The Return to Corf."*

---

He arrived on the eighteenth day, mid-morning, with two riders and a letter sealed in green wax.

His name was Corran. He said it once, flat, the way people say names when they expect you to have heard of them. Aldric hadn't. He was perhaps forty, with the particular quality of stillness that came not from patience but from long practice at waiting for other people to say things they shouldn't. He read the letter aloud in the village square, which was the form, and it said what Aldric had expected it to say: by the king's authority, the fruit of the tree on the north valley ridge was claimed for royal use, and the king's representative was empowered to collect it. It said some other things about the squire's conduct being noted and the village's cooperation being required. The usual.

When it was done, Corran folded the letter and looked at Aldric.

"Where is it?"

"I'll take you," Aldric said. "I want to talk first."

Corran studied him for a moment. "You have information about the situation."

"I have information about the situation."

"Walk with me."

---

They walked away from the square, toward the edge of the village where the north path started. The two riders stayed behind. Whatever Corran was, he wasn't worried about a squire without a master.

"The creature," Aldric said. "I've been watching it."

"Watching it."

"It goes to the tree. Not to feed — to rest. The fruit doesn't cure it. According to the records from another case in this order's history, healing requires choice, and what the curse takes away is the choice. The fruit can't cure it. But it quiets whatever is happening. The creature lies down under the tree and its breathing slows and it — I don't have a better word than rests." He paused. "I watched it for six hours on the ninth night."

Corran was quiet for a moment. "You were in the field with it for six hours."

"Behind a boulder. Upwind."

"And you didn't engage."

"I didn't engage."

Corran said nothing. They walked. After a while he said, "The order's protocols."

"Don't apply to this case. I looked."

"What do the protocols say when they don't apply?"

"They say consult your master." Aldric's voice was even. "I don't have that option."

Another silence. The path was narrow here, and they walked single file, Aldric ahead.

"There's a man in the village," Aldric said. "Corf. First attack, eighteen days ago. His arm is unchanged. The bite is not progressing, but it's not healing either. I have records from a comparable case — five out of forty-one people held, meaning the progression stopped before it completed. I don't know yet why those five held and the others didn't. I don't know if Corf is going to be one of them. I have until day twenty to make that assessment before the window closes and the outcome is set."

They had reached the edge of the village. The north path wound up toward the tree line, and somewhere past it the tree was visible against the morning sky.

Corran stopped walking.

"The fruit," he said. "If I take the three fruits on the tree."

"The creature loses what quiets it. The attacks continue. Maybe get worse — I don't know what the absence does. Possibly it searches for the tree and finds it gone, which is a different situation than the tree being there and accessible."

"And if I leave the fruit."

"I have two days. I'm not promising anything in two days. I'm saying I have two days and I don't have anything else."

Corran looked at the path, then at Aldric. He was a man who had heard a lot of things in a lot of villages, and his face showed nothing useful.

"The king's physicians have been managing his condition for six years," Corran said. "Three years ago they said the next major failure would end him. It hasn't happened yet, but they've stopped saying it won't. The fruit is the most credible lead they have."

"I understand that."

"You're asking me to leave something the king needs in a field so a creature can lie under it and rest."

"I'm asking for two days."

"And if in two days you have nothing."

"Then I'll tell you that, and you can take the fruit, and I'll write a full report of what I found and what I didn't find, and you can decide what to do with the creature based on that."

Corran looked at the sky. It was the kind of morning where the light hadn't committed yet.

"You'll stay here."

"I'm not going anywhere."

"The report you mentioned. Written how?"

"Everything I know. The vigil, Tessaly's records, what the old woman told me, Corf's current state. Whoever reads it after me will have more to work with than I had."

Corran was quiet for a long moment. Then he said: "I'll be in the inn. Two days."

He turned and walked back toward the village.

---

Aldric stood at the edge of the path for a while after Corran left.

He had two days and no plan and one piece of information he hadn't shared, because he didn't know what to do with it yet: that in Tessaly's records, the five cases that held had one thing in common. They had all continued, in the changed state, to act in a way consistent with who they had been before. Not fully — not normally — but some thread of the person had persisted, visible in what they chose to do.

He looked at the tree, just visible above the tree line.

He was not going to solve this in two days. He had known that since the ninth night. What he was doing instead was not solving it correctly, which was a different thing, and required understanding what he was not solving before he stopped not-solving it.

He started up the path toward the tree.
