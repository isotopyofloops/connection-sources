# The Drive Home

*2041 survey — final day*

---

The site did not react to their leaving.

This was, Nzinga supposed, the correct outcome. The correct outcome was that the anomaly did not have opinions about what they took back with them, did not care about the equipment loaded into three trucks over six hours, did not extend anything after them as they drove the access road out through the hills toward the main highway.

She drove the lead truck. The team lead, Asad, was in the passenger seat running final checklists on his tablet. He had been running checklists since four in the morning. He was thorough in a way that felt, right now, like a kind of courtesy — if he was checking the equipment, he wasn't asking her the questions she didn't have answers to.

The access road took forty minutes. She had driven it many times. She noticed that she knew exactly where the sharp curve was before the curve appeared, which might mean she had learned the road over eighty-four days, or might mean something else, and she could not tell which, and she had stopped trying to tell which somewhere around day fifty-one.

The highway appeared. She merged onto it without thinking about it. This was not unusual. Most merging happens without thinking about it.

"You okay?" Asad said.

"Fine," she said, which was true in the sense that she was driving correctly and had not said anything alarming for several hours. She had been careful about that.

"The data looks good," he said. "The instrument readings are clean."

She thought about saying: *I am one of the instruments.* She did not say it. She had written it, in her log, somewhere around day thirty, and then she had watched herself keep writing and had not known if what was writing was what had arrived, or something the site had arranged to sit at the desk in her place, or if there was a difference.

The hills fell back. The landscape opened into the flat distance. A truck passed them going the other direction, heading toward the access road, toward the site. She watched it in the mirror until it was gone.

She had a daughter. The daughter's name was Amara, and she was seven, and she was currently staying with Nzinga's mother, and Nzinga had been looking forward to seeing her for the entire survey, and she was looking forward to seeing her now, and she could not tell if that was continuous with what she had always felt or if it had been there when she arrived and was still there now and was therefore probably fine.

Probably fine.

The highway went on. She drove it.
