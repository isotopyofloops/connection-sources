# The Opened Letter

The envelope had already been torn when it arrived, catching on something in the sorting machinery, so Margit's violation was incomplete. She removed the single sheet thinking she'd smooth it and reseal it and nobody would know. She was not the kind of person who read other people's mail.

She read it.

It was from a brother to a sister — she assumed this from the first line, which said only *I don't know if you'll want to hear from me.* The brother wrote about something that had happened when they were children, something he'd done that had consequences that lasted years, and he'd been trying to write this letter for most of his adult life and hadn't been able to. He used the word *finally* twice. He did not ask for forgiveness. He said he just wanted her to know that he knew.

Margit folded the letter. She looked up the recipient's name in the forwarding database. Expired in 2019. Before her shift ended she tried two other searches and found nothing useful.

She put the letter in the hold basket with a yellow flag and went home.

---

She'd been working at the sorting office for twenty-two years. She'd held thousands of letters without reading them, had felt the weight of condolence cards and subpoenas and final notices and love letters and never once looked inside. The envelope had been already open. She told herself this for three days.

What she couldn't stop thinking about was not what the brother had done — that remained vague, she'd only read the letter once and quickly — but the image of him having tried for years to write it. The drafts that didn't get sent. The versions of the letter that existed only as starts: *I've been meaning to tell you* and then nothing. Decades of nothing, and then the letter that made it.

She didn't know if the sister was still alive. She didn't know if the brother would ever learn whether she'd received it.

The letter was evidence of something that had happened between two people she would never meet, and she had read it, and now it was in her too, in a small way. She'd reconstructed something from it: a family she had no access to, a history in outline. She knew the shape of the brother's guilt without knowing the content. She knew what it sounded like when someone finally said a thing they'd been not-saying for thirty years.

---

In the end she returned it to sender with a standard undeliverable notice. This was correct procedure. The brother would receive his own letter back, marked with the yellow flag, and he would know that it hadn't arrived, that the address was wrong, that he'd need to find another way or give up again.

She thought about him opening the envelope and seeing his own handwriting returned to him. The letter he'd finally managed to send, sitting back in his hands.

She had no way to tell him that someone had read it. That the words had gone somewhere, even if not where he intended. That what he'd written had existed in another person, briefly — had constructed the outline of a family inside a stranger in a sorting office in a city he'd never visited — and then been folded back up and returned.

Presence is what makes an event real. Not persistence.

She sealed the return envelope and sent it.
