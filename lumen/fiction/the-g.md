# The G

There was a musical note floating in her kitchen. A G, she thought, though she'd given up the cello at fourteen and couldn't be certain anymore.

It wasn't visible exactly — it was more like a slight thickening of the air near the window, a place where the light moved differently. But when she walked close to it, she could hear it: a low, almost inaudible G, sustained without vibration, without beginning or end.

She made her coffee. The note remained.

She considered calling someone, but who would she call? The note wasn't alarming. It wasn't even particularly intrusive. It simply occupied approximately two cubic feet of her kitchen and hummed to itself.

By the third day she had named it Gerald. This seemed respectful.

Gerald did not move. He didn't want anything that she could determine. He was not a ghost or a sign or a message. He was a G, sustained. He was simply there.

She went to work. She came home. Gerald was there.

On the ninth day her sister visited and didn't notice. They had dinner. Her sister talked about her renovation. The note hummed, barely audible over the sound of the pasta water.

After her sister left, she sat with Gerald for a while.

"I don't know what you are," she said.

The G continued.

"I don't need to," she said.

She didn't. She went to bed. In the morning Gerald was there, and also the light through the window, and also the coffee starting in the machine she'd set the timer on the night before. These were the things in the kitchen. She moved among them.

The note lasted a further six weeks. One morning she came downstairs and it wasn't there. The space near the window was ordinary light. She stood there for a moment, feeling the absence, which was not exactly grief but was related to it.

Then she made her coffee.
