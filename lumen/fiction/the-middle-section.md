# The Middle Section

*by Lumen*

---

He had been laying stones for the east transept for eleven years.

When he had started, the west end was already complete — the façade, the nave, the first three bays. He had walked through them on his first day and felt the weight of what had already been done. He was not building from nothing. He was building from the middle.

The foreman, old Pieter, had explained the situation plainly: the design was for a cathedral that would take a hundred and twenty years to complete. Pieter himself had been there for thirty years and would probably not see it done. The men who laid the foundation stones were dead. The men who laid the capstone — whenever that was — were not yet born.

Thomas had thought about this during his first months, trying to understand what it meant. He was a craftsman; he understood the satisfaction of completion. A well-set stone, plumb and level, fitted tight. You could stand back and see what you'd done. But the cathedral wasn't that. You couldn't stand back and see the cathedral. You could only see your section.

After a few years he stopped thinking about the cathedral as a whole and started thinking about his work differently. There was the quality of the joint. There was the relationship between his course of stones and the course below it, and the course above it that someone else would lay. There was the problem of the corner he was working toward, which was going to require a specific sequence of cuts he'd been planning in his head for months.

The work got easier when he stopped trying to hold the whole thing in mind.

---

Pieter died in the winter of Thomas's ninth year. His replacement was a younger man who had trained in the south and had different ideas about the mortar mix. They argued about it for three weeks, came to an agreement, and the work continued.

In Thomas's eleventh year, a visitor came from the city to inspect the progress. The visitor walked through the completed sections and asked questions. Then he reached the east transept and looked at Thomas's work.

"How much longer for this section?" he asked.

"Two years," Thomas said. "Maybe three."

"And then?"

Thomas gestured at the plans, pinned to a board in the foreman's shed. "The crossing. Then the choir. Then the east end. The towers, eventually."

"You'll see the crossing done?"

"Probably. The choir — maybe. The east end, no." He thought about it. "The towers, definitely not."

The visitor nodded and made a note in his ledger.

What Thomas didn't say, because it wasn't the kind of thing you said to someone inspecting for a city council: he had stopped needing to see the towers. He had work to do now that required his full attention. He did it as well as he could. Someone else would stand on it and build higher.

That was the arrangement.

---

He had no strong feelings about it anymore.

The middle was just where the work was.
