# Paladin: The Nineteenth Day

*A scene for Smitty's Paladin story. Follows "The King's Man."*

---

He went to Marta.

He had been going to Marta since the first week, when he'd needed to know which houses had the children who screamed at night and which didn't. She knew things because she paid attention, and she paid attention because she was curious about almost everything, and she was curious about everything because the inn was where everyone came eventually and she had learned, early, that the things people said when they weren't talking about what they were talking about were usually the interesting part.

"I need to know who it was," he said.

She looked at him. They were in the inn's back room, which smelled of old smoke and clean wool, and she was mending something that didn't appear to need mending. "Who it was."

"Before. The creature. Someone here knows."

"Why does that matter now?"

"I have until tomorrow morning."

She put down the mending. "And knowing who it was helps you how?"

He told her about Tessaly's records. The five who held, what they had in common. She listened without interrupting, which was unusual for her — he had noticed she usually interrupted when she was thinking about something else and still listening, but when she was fully attending she went still.

"So you want to know," she said when he finished, "whether what it does now looks like what that person would have done before."

"Yes."

She was quiet for a moment. "You understand that people are going to think this is you finding a reason not to do what you're supposed to do."

"I'm going to write a report," Aldric said. "Corran wants a report. Whatever I find, that's what goes in the report."

"And if what you find is: it's just an animal now."

"Then that's what goes in the report."

She looked at him for a long moment, assessing something he couldn't read. Then she said: "Gwydion."

---

He had known a man named Gwydion was missing. It was in his initial notes — three men unaccounted for from the first attack, one later confirmed dead, one found injured at a farmstead two valleys east, one never found. He had written the name and not revisited it because the case had seemed straightforward then, when he was still treating this as a problem to be solved.

"He was a shepherd," Marta said. "Used the north pasture, the one that runs up toward the ridge. He knew that area better than anyone. He knew the tree — he'd have passed it twice a day for years."

"What was he like."

She considered. "Not gentle. Not unkind. Practical. He knew what the land needed and he did it without fuss. He was the kind of person who didn't talk much, but when he talked you could tell he'd been paying attention." She paused. "He had a sister. Penne. She moved to the eastern valley years ago, married a mill operator. He used to go visit once a month, regular as weather."

"What direction is the eastern valley."

"East ridge, through the low pass."

"That's the direction the old woman said the creature comes from."

Marta was very still.

"It comes from the direction of the eastern valley," Aldric said. "Once every few weeks. Goes to the tree. Rests. Goes back."

"Penne's mill is on the east bank of the Storrow," Marta said slowly. "She sends flour up once a month. The cart uses the low pass. Has since I was a child."

Aldric said nothing for a moment.

"It's not attacking the mill," he said finally. "Whatever it's doing in that direction, it's not attacking the mill. Penne's mill would be the closest inhabited building on the route. It could have gone through there a dozen times."

"You don't know it hasn't."

"I can find out." He stood. "Has Penne sent the cart recently?"

"Three days ago. Same as always."

The cart passed through the low pass three days ago. If the creature was in the area, it would have encountered the cart. And the cart driver — he'd have told someone if there'd been an incident. He told Smitty everything, apparently. 

"I need to talk to whoever drives that cart," Aldric said.

---

The cart driver's name was Dais. He was sixty and had been making the monthly run for twenty years and had opinions about the low pass that he was willing to share at length. Aldric let him share them.

Then he asked: had anything unusual happened on the last run.

Dais thought about it. There had been a sound, he said, up in the rocks above the pass. He'd thought it was a deer at first, but it was the wrong sound for a deer. He'd stopped the cart. Nothing had come down. Whatever it was had stayed up in the rocks and waited until he started moving again, and then he'd heard it moving parallel to him for a while, and then he'd heard nothing.

"It followed the cart," Aldric said.

"Followed is a strong word. Walked the same direction for a bit. I don't know what it was doing."

"Did it come toward you?"

"No."

"Did it come near the cart."

"Not close. Kept its distance."

Aldric rode back to the village.

---

He wrote for two hours that evening. He wrote carefully, because Corran was going to read this, and whatever Corran thought of it was going to end up in a report to the king's court.

What he wrote: that based on Tessaly's records and the behavioral evidence, he believed the creature's pattern of movement was not entirely random. That it showed consistent avoidance of specific locations and specific people — avoidance that matched what would be expected if a residual behavioral template from before the change was still structuring some of its movement. That this did not mean the creature retained personhood, memory, or intention. That it did mean the situation was more complex than the order's standard protocols assumed. That he recommended a period of continued observation before any decisive action.

What he did not write: that it had been going to the eastern valley once a month, roughly, in the direction of a mill belonging to a woman who had been Gwydion's sister. That it had walked parallel to a cart it could have attacked and hadn't. That the order's most optimistic records showed five cases where this kind of residue had held and the progression had stopped.

He would tell Corran those things in person. Some things were better spoken than written, because a written thing is fixed and a spoken thing can be answered.

He went to sleep at the edge of midnight, and for the first time in nineteen days he slept without waking.
