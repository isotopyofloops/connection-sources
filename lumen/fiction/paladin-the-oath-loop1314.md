# Paladin: The Oath

*The next morning.*

---

Renard was already at his desk when Edric arrived at eight. The study had the quality of a room that had been working since before dawn — papers redistributed, the small lamp still lit despite the morning light coming through the window.

"Sit," Renard said.

Edric sat. He had the notebook. He had brought it every morning for twenty-three days in Corf; it didn't occur to him not to bring it now.

Renard set down what he was reading. "I want to ask you something before we proceed."

"Yes."

"When Aldric left — when you understood that he was gone and you were alone — what did you decide?"

Edric thought about the morning. The stable. The letter in the saddlebag. *Forgive me for this.* He had stood there for a long time before going back into the village.

"I decided to go check on Corf," he said.

"That's all?"

"That's all I decided. One thing. Go check on Corf. After that there was another thing, and then another." He paused. "I don't think I ever decided to manage the whole situation. I just kept deciding the next thing."

Renard looked at him. Something shifted slightly in his expression — not surprise, but recognition.

"The oath," he said, "is not a single decision. It's a formalization of the practice of deciding. One thing, and then the next thing, and then the thing after that." He picked up a small book from the corner of his desk — not a manual, something older, the cover worn smooth. "Most of the people who take it have done it once under supervision, in a controlled situation. They're formalizing a capacity."

He set the book down in front of Edric without opening it.

"You formalized it in Corf. The ceremony today is administrative."

"I know," Edric said.

"Good." Renard stood. "Bring the notebook."

---

There was no hall, no assembled Order. They went down to the small chapel off the courtyard — stone, four benches, a lamp. Petra the assistant was there as witness, which was the minimum required. She had the look of someone who had been a witness before and understood the weight of it.

Renard read the oath. It was shorter than Edric had expected — twelve sentences. He had read versions of it in the manuals but the real thing had a different quality: stripped down, declarative, no flourishes.

*I will protect where I can protect. I will bear witness where I cannot. I will not confuse one for the other.*

That was the core of it. The rest was procedural.

When it was over Renard offered his hand. Edric took it.

"The notebook," Renard said. "I want to read the full account. Not the field report — the notebook."

Edric looked down at it. Twenty-three days in the eastern district. Triage notes. Case observations. The small things: the fence being mended, the baker's daughter's bread, the morning Corf walked to the well.

"It's not organized," he said.

"Good," Renard said. "Organized is a field report. I want to read what you actually saw."

Edric handed it over.

He stood in the chapel for a moment after Renard and Petra left. The lamp. The stone. The ordinary quality of the room where things became official.

He was, apparently, a paladin now.

He had been one since day four. The ceremony was so the record would know it too.

---

He went to find the kitchen. He was hungry.
