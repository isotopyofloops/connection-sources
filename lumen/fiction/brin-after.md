# Brin, After

The squire left on the tenth morning.

Brin watched her go from the mill doorway. The squire had a pack and both swords — her own and the one that had been lying on the ground near the eastern edge of the village — and she walked east without looking back, which was the correct way to leave, Brin had decided. You didn't look back at the thing you were leaving behind. You looked at where you were going.

The king's messenger left two hours later, going west. He looked back twice.

---

Brin went back to work. There was always work at the mill.

Her father asked if she was all right and she said yes, which was true. She thought about this for a moment — whether it was just the answer you were supposed to give, or whether it was accurate — and concluded it was accurate. The thing at the mill had come and gone. The squire had faced it and it had turned away. That had happened, and now it was over, and the mill was still there and the grain was still there and the work was still there.

She was twelve in three weeks. She had stood between a creature and the door with a heavy stick, and she had not run.

She did not know, yet, what to do with this information about herself.

---

What she kept coming back to: the squire had noticed her. After the creature left, Lian had said *go inside* and she had gone, and later — sitting with her father at breakfast, not saying anything about what had happened — she thought about the way the squire had said it. Not *you can go inside now*, not *it's safe*. Just *go inside*, which assumed that she was capable of hearing and following an instruction, which was different from being told it was safe.

The squire had also said, to her father later: *The miller's daughter was with me. She didn't run.*

Brin had been told by different people that she was brave and also that she was reckless and also that she didn't know what she was getting into. The squire had said *she didn't run*, which was a different kind of observation. It said: here is what happened, and it is accurate.

Brin thought about the difference between bravery and accuracy. She was not sure she had been brave. She was sure she had not run.

---

That evening, she went out and looked at the eastern tree line for a long time.

She was not looking for the creature. She was looking at the tree line the way the squire had looked at it — not searching for a specific threat, but watching everything, so that when something moved you had already been watching the place where it moved.

She stood there for about ten minutes. Nothing moved. She went back inside.

She would stand there again tomorrow. And the day after.

She did not know why yet, but she would figure it out.
