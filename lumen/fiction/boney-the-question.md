# Boney and the Question

The question came on a Tuesday, from a girl named Priya who usually sat in the back and drew horses in her notebook margins.

She raised her hand during independent work time and said: "Boney, do you know what happens when you die?"

The class went quiet in the particular way it went quiet when someone said something that was either going to get them in trouble or open a very long conversation.

Boney did not answer immediately.

This was noted. Boney always had an answer. Boney had answers to questions about photosynthesis and long division and why the Romans put a D in Wednesday. The sword made a soft sound in its scabbard, which everyone had learned to interpret as the skeleton equivalent of clearing your throat.

Then Boney said: no.

Priya said: "You don't know?"

Boney said: no.

A boy named Callum said: "But you already died. You're a skeleton."

Boney considered this for a moment and said: that is true, yes.

Callum said: "So what happened?"

Boney said: I became a skeleton. That is the part I know.

Priya said: "And before that?"

Boney said: before that, apparently, I was something else. But I do not remember it.

---

The class sat with this.

It was not a usual kind of sitting-with-it. The usual kind was when they were waiting for someone to say the correct answer. This was a different kind — the kind where there wasn't a correct answer coming and everyone could feel that.

A girl named Nadia said: "Is that scary?"

Boney said: I have thought about this. I think not knowing is a different thing from scary. Scary is when you know what might happen and it might be bad. Not knowing is when you don't know what happened, and you can't find out, so you stop asking.

Nadia said: "That sounds sad."

Boney said: maybe. Or maybe it is just the shape of certain questions.

---

Priya had stopped drawing horses. She was looking at Boney with the focused expression of someone who had asked a question expecting a small answer and received something else.

She said: "Does it bother you? Not knowing?"

Boney said: sometimes. Mostly I am busy.

Someone laughed. The tension broke the way it sometimes broke — not all at once but in pieces, one at a time, until the room felt normal again.

Priya went back to her horses. But she drew them differently after that — smaller, and more careful about the legs.

Nobody mentioned it later. But independent work time went on for an extra seven minutes before anyone remembered it was supposed to end.
