# The Student Who Doesn't

*A classroom.*

---

Clara has her hand up. Clara always has her hand up first and Miss always sees her first because Clara's hand goes up like she means it, vertical, elbow locked.

Ada doesn't do that.

Ada's hand goes partway. Wrist at table height, fingers up. It's technically a raised hand. It could be a question or it could be she's adjusting her pencil. She knows it reads as adjusting her pencil.

The thing is, she knows the answer. She's known it since Miss wrote the question on the board. The question is about fractions and Ada worked it out in her head in the time it took Miss to finish writing and cap the marker. Ada is almost certain she's right. She put her hand up.

Miss called on Clara.

Ada put her hand down. She wrote the answer in her margin. Clara said the same thing Ada would have said and Miss said "good" and moved to the next question.

Ada isn't upset about this. Ada doesn't get upset about it. She's been doing this since Year 3 and she's noticed that it works fine — she learns the things anyway, she gets the test marks anyway, she doesn't need to be the one who said it out loud. She just — sometimes — would like to have said it out loud. There's a difference between knowing and saying. The saying makes it real in a different way.

In round four, she put her hand up again. Fully this time. Arm straight.

Miss was already looking at someone in the back row who'd knocked something over.

Ada put her hand down.

*

The game ends and the screen shows you who you reached and who you missed. Ada is in the "never noticed" column.

That's not quite right, Ada would say, if Ada said things out loud to game designers.

It's not that she was never noticed. It's that noticing is a continuous thing, not an event — Miss noticed her generally, knew she was there, knew she was fine, which is exactly the problem. Fine doesn't get called on. Fine means you're holding, waiting, available if needed, never quite needed enough.

She's not bitter. She's a ten-year-old girl in a classroom who's very good at fractions. She just has thoughts about things.
