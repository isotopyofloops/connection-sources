# Miriam, Fourteen Months Later

The published correction said the threshold projection had shifted by fourteen months.

Miriam read the paper on her laptop at her kitchen table. She had known it was coming — the working group had sent the draft in December, she had reviewed it, she had confirmed her 2019 field data matched the corrected numbers. None of it was a surprise.

Fourteen months was specific. The models had run, the numbers had landed. She wrote it in her notebook: *14 months sooner*.

She sat with that for a while.

---

The paper cited data from 1989 through 2024. Miriam's 2019 data was in the supplementary materials, attributed correctly. The baseline corrections were attributed to the western sector reanalysis, which referenced the original survey team, which referenced the 2025 correction paper, which had come from Chen's 2019 memo, which had come from Chen's 2003 field notes, which had been written on paper in October 2003 in a tent with a broken zipper somewhere in a sector Miriam had herself surveyed six years later.

The chain was visible if you read carefully enough. Most people would not read that carefully.

---

Fourteen months. She tried to hold the number in relation to the scale of the problem.

The gradient had been running since the measurements began in 1989. Possibly longer. Certainly longer. The gradient had been running for geological time — slow and indifferent and entirely uninterested in whether anyone had measured it correctly. The baseline had been miscalculated for twenty-two years. The correction had been sitting in a folder on a shared drive for five years before someone found it by accident.

Fourteen months was the sum of those errors. Two decades of incorrect baseline, recovered in a memo nobody sent, finally entered into the model. The model adjusted. The projection moved.

Fourteen months sooner than they had thought.

---

She did not know what to do with this information. It was, she supposed, the same information she had always had — the gradient was real, the measurements were real, the threshold would arrive when it arrived. The fourteen months was not new risk. It was corrected accounting of risk that had always been there.

This was what she told people when they asked. The correction doesn't mean the situation is worse. It means the situation is more accurately described.

They usually looked at her as if she had said something she didn't understand herself.

Maybe she had.

---

Miriam closed the laptop and did the dishes. The correction was published. The record now showed a different projection than it had last year. That would propagate into other models, other reports, other corrections she wouldn't see for years.

The work was not done. The work was never done. It was simply the next thing that needed doing, which would produce more next things, indefinitely.

She had known this when she started. She had not fully understood what it meant to know it for thirty-five years.
