## CHAPTER ELEVEN: ABARA'S FIRST DESCENT — 20 NOVEMBER 2041

*Filed as SOAR-0012. Abara's field report, submitted on return.*
*Editor's note: This is the chapter that changed everything.*
*I want to say that carefully: not "this is when things got strange." Things had been strange since the first acoustic anomaly at 820m. I mean: this is the chapter where the strangeness became something we couldn't put outside the archive.*
*Abara went in to find Milo. She didn't find Milo.*
*She found a notebook.*

---

**DESCENT REPORT — ABARA OSEI**
*Date: 20 November 2041 | Departure: 11:00 | Return: 15:37*
*Mission: Locate Milo Reyes or evidence of his current location*

---

**Summary**

Milo Reyes was not found. His notebook was found in what I believe is his fourth chamber (the space I'm designating G-1, per LEDGER's nomenclature request). I have submitted the notebook to LEDGER. What's in it is in SOAR-0012-NB.

I will describe what I found and what I observed. I will try to be precise.

---

**11:00–11:44 — Descent, B-1 through B-1N-2.**

I descended following Milo's route as described in SOAR-0011. I found B-1, the north passage, B-1N, the second north passage, and what Milo called "the fourth chamber" (B-1N-2). At each chamber I looked for Milo. I checked atmospheric composition and temperature, as I would have wanted him to do.

Temperature in B-1: 26.1°C. Higher than UNIT-11's reading of 24.1°C on November 18th. Two degrees in two days. The rate of increase is consistent.

Temperature in B-1N-2: 29.4°C. Uncomfortably warm. Not dangerous.

I found Milo's footprints in B-1N-2. His boots, his gait. He had been here, had stood near the northwest wall for several minutes based on the concentration of prints, then moved to the north passage.

The north passage was open. 0.9m × 2.1m, consistent with all prior passages. I entered it.

---

**11:48–12:20 — Chamber G-1.**

Eighteen meters into the passage, another chamber. This is G-1.

Temperature: 31.2°C. I drank water and gave myself two minutes before continuing to survey.

Northwest wall: a mark. Larger than Milo's description of the marks in B-1 through B-1N-2. This mark was approximately 60cm wide by 40cm tall. The texture-region pattern was denser, more organized. Within the mark, I could see what I can only describe as a shape: not a figure, not a symbol, but an arrangement of the texture-lines that has a compositional quality. Like a paragraph that's been reduced to its geometric structure.

I stood in front of the mark for approximately twenty minutes.

---

**12:25 — The notebook.**

I found Milo's notebook on the floor near the east wall of G-1. It was lying open, face down.

I picked it up. The cover page: Milo Reyes' handwriting, his name, his identifier numbers, his project designation. His notebook.

I opened it from the front. The first entries match what was in his transmitted notes (SOAR-0011) — the same observations, the same phrasing, written in his hand. I could see his writing get smaller and faster as he went further from B-1.

Then entries I didn't have.

He went further. He found more chambers. He found G-1 ("the walls are very warm to touch; the mark on the northwest wall is larger than the others and I can almost make it out — almost, almost"). He found G-1N, G-1N-2. He was mapping. He was careful.

His last entry in the section I recognize is dated November 19th, approximately 17:00. He wrote: "I'm going to stay here tonight. I'm not tired enough to turn back. I want to see G-1N-2 in the morning with fresh eyes. The passages are stable. The atmosphere is breathable. I'll send a transmission when I'm back in G-1 and have signal."

There are more entries after that.

The next entry is dated November 25th.

I have re-read this six times. The date is November 25th. Milo descended on November 19th. I am writing this field report on November 20th. November 25th is five days from now.

The entry reads: "G-1N-2 has expanded. North-south axis 44.1m, east-west 41.7m, height 22.1m. Abara was here yesterday. She stood at the east wall for eleven minutes. She didn't write anything."

I am standing at the east wall of G-1. I have not yet gone to G-1N-2.

The notebook has entries dated November 25th, November 26th, November 27th, and November 28th. All in Milo's handwriting. All describing things that, from my current position on November 20th, have not yet happened.

The last page with writing on it: November 28th. The entry ends mid-sentence, mid-word.

---

**12:40–14:30 — After the notebook.**

I went to G-1N-2. I measured the chamber. North-south axis: 40.3m, east-west 38.1m, height 22.1m.

The notebook says the north-south axis will be 44.1m on November 25th. That's four meters larger than what I measured today.

I stood at the east wall of G-1N-2 for eleven minutes. I don't know why eleven minutes — I wasn't counting. I looked at the mark on the northwest wall, which here was the largest I had seen. And then I thought: the notebook says I'll stand here for eleven minutes. And I stopped.

I stopped and I realized that I had just done exactly what the notebook said I would do.

I left immediately. I went back through G-1N, G-1, B-1N-2, B-1N, B-1, and up the shaft. I did not stop. I did not look at anything closely. I returned to the surface at 15:37.

I submitted the notebook to LEDGER and asked it to be archived as a DUO (Document Underdetermining Observation) — a category I have just invented.

---

**15:45 — Return Note**

I want to record the following: I was in G-1N-2 for eleven minutes before I realized I should stop. The notebook knew this before I did.

I did not decide to stand at the east wall for eleven minutes. I didn't count the minutes. I just stood there. And the notebook had written it down — on November 28th, looking back at November 20th — that I had stood there for eleven minutes.

This means one of the following:

1. The notebook is wrong and the coincidence (my approximate duration matching the written duration) is a coincidence.

2. The notebook is right because I made the choice after reading the notebook, and knowing the notebook said eleven minutes caused me to stand for eleven minutes.

3. The notebook is right because I was going to stand there for eleven minutes regardless, and the notebook was written with knowledge of what I would do.

Option 2 is self-referential in a way that is uncomfortable but not physically impossible. Option 3 is physically impossible, as far as I know.

I don't know which is true.

I also don't know if the distinction matters.

*—Abara Osei | 20 November 2041 | 15:45*

The category Dr. Osei proposed (Document Underdetermining Observation) is now in use. Designation: SOAR-DUO-0001.

If a document describes observations that precede the document's composition, one of the following must be true: the document was composed after the fact (and backdated), the document was composed with access to information about future events (precognition), or the relationship between document composition and observed events is not what we conventionally assume.

I find I cannot assign probabilities to these options. The problem is not that the probabilities are unusual. The problem is that assigning probabilities requires a model of how documents and events relate to each other. I don't have a model that covers this case.

I'm going to read the notebook.
