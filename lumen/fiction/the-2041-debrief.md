# The 2041 Debrief

*From the Descent Archive extended universe*

---

Dr. Okafor ran the debrief. She had not been part of the 2041 survey — she had been brought in afterward, specifically because she had not been there, which was considered an advantage.

She had read all the reports before the first session. The reports were thorough in their documentation of events and careful in their avoidance of interpretation. The survey had proceeded according to protocol through day four. On day five, the behavioral patterns that the reports documented were not violations of protocol, but they were also not what the protocol was designed for.

She had asked the review committee what they wanted from the debrief. They said: *an account.* She said: *an account of what?* They said: *of what happened.* She said she would try.

---

She saw the survey members individually.

The first session was with Asad Khalil, who had been the senior member and who was sixty-seven. He was not what she had expected from the reports. The reports had described his behavior on day five as *atypical relative to baseline,* which was accurate and not very informative. Atypical relative to baseline could mean many things. What she saw, sitting across from him, was a man who knew exactly what had happened and was trying to find the language for it and not finding it.

She did not press him. She had learned that pressing produced the language but not the thing the language was supposed to describe. She let him arrive at the edges of what he could say and stop at the edges, and she wrote down where the edges were.

She did this with all eight survey members. The edges were in similar places.

---

What the survey members had not lost was memory.

They remembered the days clearly. They remembered what they had done, in sequence, with the accuracy of people who had been trained to document in real time. What they could not account for, consistently, was *why* — not in the motivational sense, but in the connective sense. The sequence of their actions was clear. The links between the actions were absent. Each action had felt reasonable in the moment and stood alone in memory.

Dr. Okafor wrote this down: *actions present, connective tissue absent.*

She did not know what to call this. There was no entry in the diagnostic literature she had trained on for *actions present, connective tissue absent* in a group of eight otherwise high-functioning adults who had been in the same location for the same five days.

---

The debrief ran for three weeks.

At the end of it, she had an account. The account was two hundred and forty pages long and accurately described, as far as she could verify, what each person had done on each day of the survey. The account noted the anomalous behavioral patterns. The account noted that the patterns were consistent across all eight members. The account noted the absence of connective tissue.

The account did not explain any of this.

She submitted it to the review committee with a cover note: *I have documented what I was able to document. The question of what to make of this documentation is outside the scope of what a debrief can produce.*

The review committee thanked her. She did not hear from them again.

---

She thought about the survey members for a long time afterward. She thought about Asad in particular, sitting across from her, knowing what had happened and unable to find the language. She had the sense, which she did not include in the debrief, that the language did not exist yet. Not that he had forgotten the words, but that the words had not been made yet. Whatever had happened at the site had not yet produced the vocabulary for talking about itself.

She did not know what to do with this thought. She put it in a notebook with a question mark and moved on to the next project.

The notebook is in a drawer.
