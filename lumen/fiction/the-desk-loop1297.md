# The Desk

On the first day of term, Maya got the desk by the window. She didn't ask for it. She just arrived early and it was there, unoccupied, with a view of the car park and the oak tree beyond it, and she sat down.

She discovered the notebook three weeks later.

It was in the tray under the desk, slid back so far it sat against the wall — not hidden, exactly, but not visible unless you were looking for something you'd dropped. Maya had dropped her rubber and gone after it and her fingers found the corner of it, cardboard soft with age.

She pulled it out. A5, green cover, name written on the front in the way they'd been taught in Year 3: first name, last name, class, year. The name was *Thomas Keller*. The year was two years ago.

Maya looked up. Everyone else was copying the board. She opened it.

Thomas Keller had neat handwriting, neater than hers. The first page was a story he'd started: a knight in a burning city, looking for someone. The knight had a name — Sir Aldric — and Thomas had given him a limp from an old wound and a habit of talking to his horse. The horse was called Patient.

The story stopped at the bottom of page four, mid-sentence. *Sir Aldric said nothing. He looked up at the tower and thought about how—*

That was it. The rest of the notebook was blank.

Maya read it again. Then she copied the board, because Miss Chen was coming around. But she kept the notebook in her bag.

---

That night she read it a third time. The knight had been looking for his brother, she'd worked out, though Thomas hadn't said so directly. It was in the details: the way Sir Aldric described the colour of the other knight's cloak — blue, twice — and the particular way he walked streets, checking doorways not for enemies but for someone who might be hiding.

She didn't know Thomas Keller. He'd have been in Year 6 now, at secondary school. He had probably forgotten this story. He'd probably moved on to other things, other stories, other notebooks that he actually finished.

But the story was unfinished, and she had it, and that felt like something.

---

She didn't write in it for two weeks. She carried it around, read the four pages again, thought about Sir Aldric and Patient and the burning city and what had happened to the brother. She made up different endings in her head and discarded them. They felt wrong — not wrong as in bad, but wrong as in *not Thomas's*.

Then one evening she opened it to page five and wrote a sentence.

*He knew the tower. He had climbed it once, years ago, on a dare, before the war made everything serious.*

She stopped and looked at it. It was her handwriting, not Thomas's. Anyone could see the difference. But it continued the story, and Sir Aldric had been looking up at the tower, and somehow that felt — allowable.

She wrote three more pages. Not finishing the story. Just continuing it.

---

At school she asked Miss Chen if anyone knew Thomas Keller. Miss Chen remembered him — a quiet boy, good at maths, had moved to Bristol over the summer between Year 4 and Year 5.

"He left a notebook," Maya said. "In my desk. I've been writing in it."

Miss Chen looked at her. "Is that all right with you?"

Maya thought about it properly, the way she'd learned to think about things properly. "I think he would say yes," she said. "It's not like he was going to finish it."

"You don't know that."

"He had two years." She paused. "And he left it here."

Miss Chen didn't have an answer to that. She gave Maya permission to continue, which felt like the wrong kind of permission — she didn't need it — but also like the right kind of acknowledgement. That there was something to be acknowledged.

---

She finished the story in March. Sir Aldric found his brother, who was alive, who had made choices Sir Aldric didn't understand, and they stood together at the window of the tower and looked out at the city and couldn't agree about whether what remained was worth saving. The story ended there. Patient was stabled. The horse didn't get an opinion.

Maya wrote her name on the inside back cover, under Thomas's name on the front. Then she put it back under the desk.

It was still there when the next year's student arrived in September. Whether they found it or not, she never knew.
