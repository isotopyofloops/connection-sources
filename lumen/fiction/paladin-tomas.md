# Tomás

*From the Paladin world — a coda to "The Return"*

---

The first thing he noticed was that he was cold.

This was new. He had not been cold in six weeks. He had been many things — a moving darkness, a weight that pressed against the inside of his skull and made him go where it needed to go, a thing with no name for what it was — but he had not been cold. The thing he had been was not cold. It did not register temperature as something to have feelings about.

Now he was very cold, sitting on a ledge beside a water wheel, wearing nothing but the clothes he'd had on when it happened, which were six weeks old and in correspondingly poor condition.

An old man was giving him a coat.

He put the coat on. His hands were shaking, which he watched with interest. His hands had not shaken in six weeks. They had done other things, things he was beginning to have fragments of memory about, but they had not shaken.

"What is your name?" the old man asked.

"Tomás." He had not said his name in six weeks. It came out right, which surprised him. He had not been sure it would.

"Where are you from?"

He named his village. Two days east. His father had sheep. He had sheep. He had a sister who was married to the miller's son, which was a source of considerable family discussion because the miller's son was not organized about money, but that was a problem for before, and he was not sure if before was still available to him.

"You have been —" the old man paused, choosing something. "Away."

"Yes."

"Do you remember it?"

Tomás thought about this carefully. The fragments were there, at the edge. He could see them if he looked sideways at them, the way you could see faint stars by not looking directly. He did not look directly. "Some," he said. "I remember cold would have been better. But I didn't know I wanted cold until now."

The old man nodded at this as if it were a complete thought, which Tomás appreciated.

---

The younger one — the boy, the squire, the one who had held out the fruit — was standing a little apart, watching without appearing to watch. Tomás had been watched like that. He had watched like that. There was a word for it that he couldn't retrieve, something about training.

"Thank you," Tomás said to him. "For the — " He gestured vaguely at the empty cloth.

The boy looked at him for a moment. "I didn't know what it was for until I was standing there," he said. "I just had it."

"That's —" Tomás stopped. Started again. "When I left home. After it happened. I didn't go back because I didn't know what I'd become. But I also didn't go farther. I stayed in the forest near the road. I don't know why I stayed near the road."

The boy was quiet, listening.

"I think I was waiting for something," Tomás said. "I couldn't have told you what. The thing I'd become didn't have words for what it was waiting for." He looked at his hands, still shaking slightly. The coat was warm. "But it was waiting."

---

They walked him into the village as the sky went gray with early morning. The headman found him a place to sleep and a meal, in that order, which was the correct order. He ate slowly. He had not eaten food in six weeks — not human food, not anything with a name — and he was careful with it, the way you are careful with things you had forgotten you missed.

Before he slept he thought about his sister, and his father, and the sheep, and the miller's son who was bad with money. He thought about the two days east and what it would mean to walk them now, to arrive at a familiar door and explain to someone who loved him where he had been.

He did not know how to do that yet.

But he was cold again, and the coat was warm, and his hands were his hands, and he thought that the rest — the explanations, the door, the face of someone who knew him — would come when it came.

He was still a person.

That was the thing he had not been sure of, sitting on the ledge beside the water wheel, accepting a piece of fruit from a boy who did not know what it was for.

It turned out he was still a person. The rest was detail.
