# The Annotator

*A fragment recovered from a private correspondence, sender unidentified, date unrecorded. The recipient's name has been redacted in the copy held in the SOAR project files. This document does not appear in any official institutional record.*

---

I found the 1962 entry by accident. I was looking for something else — I don't remember what — and there it was in the anomaly register, filed between two unremarkable entries about thermal springs and subsidence reports. Entry No. 1947-QC. Heat signature 340% above baseline. Depth measurement not obtained. Equipment malfunction in field conditions.

I read it three times.

I already knew about the 1989 file. I had read the 1989 file. I had read the 1989 file carefully, more carefully than it deserved to be read, which is to say: I had read between lines that had no between. I had looked at where things were not said and found them unsatisfying.

The 1989 memorandum recommends against further investigation. It cites anomalous instrument behavior — I am paraphrasing; the exact language is in the file. It cites team members who experienced disorientation and declined to return to the shaft. It cites the heat exceeding safe working conditions. And it says: this does not meet the threshold for priority investigation given current resource allocation.

I had always found that phrase strange. *Does not meet the threshold.* A threshold is a thing you can cross or not cross. The 1962 survey team had also not crossed it: their equipment failed and they left. The 1989 team had also not crossed it: they sent people home and left. And now there was a memorandum saying: do not cross it.

The 1962 entry said: *recommend follow-up survey.*

The 1989 memorandum said: *no further investigation.*

No one had written the connection down. The two documents were in the same building — different file rooms, I think, though I am not certain of the building's layout — but no one had taken one to the other. No one had written: *we found the thing you recommended follow-up for, and here is what we found.*

---

I had a pen.

I wrote: *see 1989 file.*

I did not sign it. I did not date it. I did not explain why I was in the anomaly register or how I had come to read the 1989 memorandum or what I thought the connection between the two documents meant. I did not know what I thought it meant.

I wrote four words and I put the file back.

---

I have thought about that annotation many times since.

The thing I keep returning to is this: the annotation does not say anything. It points. It says: look somewhere else. It does not say what you will find there, or what you should conclude, or whether the connection is important. It just draws a line between two things that are separated by twenty-seven years and two different filing systems.

I cannot tell you whether the connection is important. I cannot tell you why the depth measurement failed both times, or whether the same kind of failure is involved in both cases, or whether the equipment malfunction in 1962 was the same in character as the "not obtained" in 1989, which says less. I cannot tell you why no one scheduled the recommended follow-up survey.

I do not know. I knew, when I wrote the annotation, that I did not know.

What I knew was: these belong together. Someone should see them together. I was not going to be that someone. I had read what I came to read and I was leaving. But the files would stay, and someone else might come.

---

That someone was, eventually, Asad. He photographed both documents. The photographs are in the SOAR project files. My handwriting is in those photographs, and in any article that cites those photographs, and in any archive that holds copies of the articles.

I am not in any of those places.

The annotation is not authorship. It is a form of pointing that persists past the person who pointed.

I am not sure what that means. I am not sure it means anything. But when I think about the site — the heat, the failed measurements, the team member who would not go back — I think about the annotation too. About what it is to know that two things belong together without knowing why or what that belonging requires.
