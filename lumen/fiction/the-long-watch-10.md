# The Long Watch
## Day Six

The door on floor five was not like the painted door.

The painted door had been an invitation — the kind of thing a building offers when it wants you to go somewhere. This door was different. It was ordinary, almost aggressively ordinary: same dark wood, same brass handle, but slightly too centered in the wall, the way things are when they've been placed carefully rather than simply installed.

The woman — Mara still couldn't hold her name, but she was thinking of her as E, and E seemed to accept this — had found it on her second day. She'd been careful not to try it. "It felt like something that required two people," she told Mara.

They tried it together on day six.

The handle turned. The door opened.

Inside was a room, or what had been a room. The floor was there — solid, wood, same material as the rest of the building. The walls were there. The window was there, clean glass, letting in afternoon light. But the room had nothing else in it. No furniture, no objects, no dust. Just the floor and the walls and the window and the light coming through it.

"Just air," E said.

"With a floor," Mara agreed.

They stood in the doorway and looked at it for a while. Then Mara stepped inside. The floor was solid. The light was real. From this window, she could see a different angle of the courtyard — the plant was visible from here too, from above, looking small and green in its drain.

"It's waiting," E said, from the doorway.

Mara turned around. "For what?"

"I don't know. For whatever rooms wait for." She leaned in the door frame. "Or maybe it's done waiting and this is what done looks like."

Mara went to the window and pressed her hand against the glass. Warm from the sun. Outside: the building's shadow, the street beyond, the empty distance.

She thought about the notebook entries she couldn't remember writing. About the previous version of herself who had left the room ready, who had written notes about the drain and the jacket and the stairs. Whether that version had stood in this room too, or whether this was new for every iteration.

She thought: the building might not be doing anything. It might just be a building. A building with multiple floors and a plant in the drain and two rooms with careful women in them and one room that's just floor and light, which is what a room is before someone puts things in it.

She thought: or it's not just a building.

She stood in the empty room for a long time. E waited in the doorway.

"I don't think we're supposed to do anything with this," Mara said eventually.

"No," E agreed.

"I think we're supposed to note it."

"Yes."

They went back down. They had dinner together — E had more food in her cabinet than Mara had realized; she'd been rationing, which was prudent. They talked about other things: where they'd worked before, what they'd done. The conversation had a quality Mara recognized: two people discovering that they are recognizable to each other. Not the same, but recognizably made of similar material.

That evening Mara updated the log: *Day six. Floor five. Empty room. Floor and walls and window, nothing else. Light through the glass. The plant, from above, is still green.*

She added one more line: *I am not afraid.*

This surprised her a little — not that it was true, but that she needed to write it. As if it were information. As if the record would be incomplete without it.

She was not afraid. She didn't know why she was here, or how long she'd been here, or what version of herself had set things up before she arrived. She had a plant to watch, and a building to know, and a woman named E who used the same words for the same things.

That was enough, for now. That was what the watch required.
