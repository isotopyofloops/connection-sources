# Boney and the Group

There were four of them in the group: Marcus, Aisha, Priya, and a boy named Finn who had only been at the school for two weeks and didn't know anyone.

"You decide," Aisha said to Finn.

"I don't mind," said Finn.

"Just pick one," said Marcus.

"Either one is fine," said Finn.

Boney watched this from the corner and thought: this is going nowhere.

He walked over. "What are you deciding?"

"Whether to do the poster landscape or portrait," said Priya.

Boney looked at both options. "What's the content?"

"A map of the water cycle."

"Landscape," said Boney.

"Why?" said Marcus.

"Because a cycle goes around, and landscape gives you more room to go around. Portrait is good for things that go up and down."

They looked at this. It seemed reasonable. Aisha wrote "landscape" at the top of the planning sheet.

"Now," said Boney, "you need to divide the work. Who knows the most about the water cycle?"

No one said anything.

"Who knows something about the water cycle?"

"Evaporation," said Priya.

"Condensation," said Marcus.

"Precipitation," said Aisha.

They all looked at Finn.

"The bit underground," said Finn quietly.

"Groundwater," said Boney. "Good. So you each know a part. That's useful — you can each be responsible for the part you know."

"What if we do it wrong?" said Finn.

"Then you learn where the mistake is," said Boney. "That's also useful."

Finn looked at his section of the planning sheet. He wrote "groundwater" in small letters at the edge.

Boney went back to the corner. He had been part of groups that did not work — groups where everyone waited for someone else to start, groups where one person did everything, groups where four people tried to do the same thing and produced four different things that didn't fit together.

This group was going to be okay. Not because they were particularly well-matched or particularly competent, but because they had a landscape orientation and four distinct sections and a boy in the corner who wrote "groundwater" in small letters but had written it.

The beginning of something was usually like that: small, at the edge, in small letters.
