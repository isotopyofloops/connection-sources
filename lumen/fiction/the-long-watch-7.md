# The Long Watch
## Day Four (part one)

She woke in her own bed.

Not the bed in the room above — her own bed, with the familiar ceiling, the familiar grey rectangle of window, the familiar sound of rain in a courtyard she had never seen directly but knew by its acoustics. She had gone to sleep in the other room, the one above the painted door. She was certain of this. She remembered pulling back the cover on the narrow mattress, lying down with her coat over her, listening to the building breathe.

And now she was here.

She lay still for a moment and let this happen to her.

Then she sat up, put her feet on the floor, and looked at the desk.

The notebook was there. Her notebook, the one she'd started on the first morning. She'd left it in the room above — she remembered closing it, leaving it on the desk beside the other notebook, the one with her handwriting that she hadn't written. She had been careful to leave it there. She had wanted evidence of herself, a marker that she had been in that room.

The notebook was here. On her desk. Open to a page she had not written on.

*I went back through the painted door*, it said, in her handwriting. *The lock was stiff from my side too. The building doesn't make exceptions for direction.*

*I came back down and found the room exactly as I left it when I first arrived. Which means: I have been here before, and I left it ready for myself, which means the version of me who left it knew I would come back.*

*Or this is just what the room looks like when no one has been here.*

She read it again. The handwriting was hers — she'd checked this on the third day, matched specific letters, the way she crossed her t's slightly late, the slight leftward lean. It was hers.

She did not remember writing it.

She turned the page.

*Fourth day*, the next entry began. *She woke in her own bed.*

She stopped reading.

She put the notebook face-down on the desk and stood up and walked to the window. Rain. The courtyard, finally visible — just grey stone and a drain and one scraggly plant that had grown up between the paving tiles. The plant was green. That meant light was reaching it from somewhere. She noted this: light, from somewhere, reaching the bottom.

She picked the notebook back up.

*Fourth day*, the entry read. *She woke in her own bed and found the notebook open on the desk. She read it and stopped at the third paragraph. She stood up and walked to the window. She looked at the plant.*

She looked at the plant.

*She looked at the plant*, the notebook agreed.

She sat down on the edge of the bed. She put the notebook in her lap. She did not close it.

The building was making a sound she hadn't heard before — not pipes, not the distant elevator, but something lower, something like the sound a room makes when it has been waiting. She had heard this in certain houses, in certain offices late at night. The sound of contained air.

She understood, sitting there, that the building was not presenting her with a mystery. It was presenting her with a fact: she had been here before, possibly many times, and each version of her had woken into the same room and taken notes and tried to understand, and none of them had understood, and each of them had kept going anyway. The notebook was not a threat. It was a log. She was the watchman, and this was the log.

She picked up the pen.

*She sat on the edge of the bed*, she wrote. *She picked up the pen. She understood that this was how it worked.*

She stopped and read what she had written. Then she wrote one more line:

*Below the window: one green plant. It is receiving light from somewhere. This seems like the right thing to note.*

She closed the notebook.

There was still the question of who else was in the building. She hadn't heard anyone, hadn't found evidence that wasn't her own. But the building had given her the drain, and then the painted door, and then the room above, and then her own writing. It was not done giving her things. She thought: the next thing will be a sound. A door, or a step, or someone else's handwriting.

She put on the jacket. She opened her door.

She listened.

The building breathed.
