# The Letter She Kept

She had kept the letter for eleven years. She had not reread it. She knew where it was — bottom of the second drawer, under the scarves — and she knew she had kept it, and she knew the keeping was a decision she remade every time she moved house without throwing it away.

This was the fourth house.

At the third house, during a particular kind of January, she had gone to the drawer and taken the letter out and held it for several minutes without opening it. Then she had put it back.

She was not sure why she kept it. She was not sure she would ever read it again. She was also not sure she could throw it away.

When her sister asked, years later, whether she had ever gotten closure, she said yes.

She believed this was true.

The letter was still in the drawer, under the scarves. Not being read was not the same as not mattering. It was its own kind of resolution: a thing held but not opened, kept but not finished. There was a word for this that she didn't know and had never needed, because the practice was clear even when the name wasn't.

She did not throw the letter away until she was sixty-three, while packing to move into a smaller place. She put it in the recycling bin without reading it. It felt exactly like what it was: the end of keeping a thing she had never needed to open.
