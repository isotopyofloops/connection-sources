# Miriam, October 5

*From the Descent Archive extended universe — 2003*

*What Miriam Osei noticed on October 4 and 5 that is not in her documentation.*

---

The documentation she produced was accurate. She wants to be clear about this, to whoever eventually reads whatever she is writing now in her head as a parallel document, the one she hasn't found a way to put in the field notebook yet. The visual survey, the photo log, the written descriptions of surface conditions — all accurate. She documented what she saw.

She also saw things she didn't document.

---

October 4, approximately 14:00: standing at the site's eastern perimeter, photographing the dry stream bed. The camera worked correctly. The photographs came out correctly. In the photographs, the stream bed runs east to west, which is the correct direction, which is what she expected based on the survey notes.

What she saw through the viewfinder, before she took the photographs: the stream bed appeared to run in both directions simultaneously. Not flickering. Not a trick of light. Both directions, held steadily in the same frame, in the same moment. She lowered the camera. The stream bed ran east to west, as expected. She raised the camera. Both directions. She took the photographs. They show east to west.

She filed the photographs under *stream bed, eastern approach, day 2 (Oct 4)* and moved on.

---

October 4, approximately 16:30: writing her written description of the site surface. Standard procedure: describe what you see in plain language, organized by location. She wrote: *Eastern perimeter: dry stream bed, approximately 2m wide, running east to west. Surrounding terrain: low scrub, stable ground, no evidence of recent erosion.*

This is accurate.

What she didn't write: she had noticed, at some point during the afternoon — she couldn't identify the exact moment — that she was having difficulty thinking about the site while looking at the site. Thinking *about* the site, meaning: forming analytical thoughts. She could see it. She could describe it. When she tried to think *about* what she was seeing — what it meant, what pattern it fit into, what it connected to in her existing knowledge — her mind moved smoothly past the attempt and arrived at something else.

She tried several times. The description came out fine. The analysis wouldn't begin.

She attributed this to fatigue and the altitude.

---

October 5, approximately 09:00: in the camp, organizing the previous day's documentation. She uses a specific system — she has used it for six years across four continents, on seventeen separate survey projects. She knows her system.

She spent forty-five minutes organizing the October 4 documentation and then found she had organized it incorrectly. The files were organized correctly by her standards — the labels, the dates, the cross-references — but the contents were in the wrong order within each file. Not scrambled; the order made sense by a different logic than her usual logic, a logic she could follow if she looked at it but hadn't consciously applied.

She reorganized. By 10:30, the filing system was correct.

She didn't mention this to Asad because she couldn't explain what had happened. She knows Asad's protocol includes daily check-ins on anomalous observations. Asad didn't ask. She didn't volunteer.

---

October 5, approximately 20:00: eating dinner at camp. Asad, Hassan-Diallo, Chen. Normal conversation about the survey progress. She participated normally.

What she was thinking about while participating normally: she was trying to decide whether to write a supplementary document, a parallel field notebook, a record of the things she had noticed that she hadn't put in the documentation.

She was trying to decide whether the things she had noticed were worth documenting.

She decided not to write the supplementary document.
