# The 1989 Survey: Day One

*A fragment. The records do not name her.*

---

The first thing was the heat.

Not the ambient heat — she had done surveys in August, in desert terrain, in full sun at elevation. This was different. The heat came from below. You could feel it through your boots standing at the shaft edge. Not uncomfortable, exactly. Wrong.

The second thing was the sound. There was no sound. She had worked enough remote sites to know that silence was normal. This wasn't silence. Silence was the absence of sounds that were expected. This was the absence of sounds that should have been present regardless of expectation: no wind movement in the grass, no insects, no settling of the ground under their feet. She mentioned it to Havelock on the first afternoon. He said the altitude probably affected his hearing. She didn't push it.

The third thing was harder to name.

They measured the thermal output for four hours. The readings didn't make sense — not wrong, exactly, but consistently higher than the geological survey data suggested they should be, and consistently higher at the end of each measurement than at the beginning, as if the shaft were warming toward them rather than simply maintaining its temperature. Havelock noted it. Orenstein noted it. She noted it in the log as *anomalous thermal gradient (direction: upward)*. This was accurate. It was also not the thing she meant.

The thing she meant: she kept losing track of how long they had been standing there.

Not in a way that felt like time passing. In a way that felt like time had been replaced by something that moved differently. She would look at her watch, and the watch would give a number, and the number would be correct — she verified it against the truck's clock twice — but the number felt disconnected from the experience of the afternoon, like the experience was longer, or stranger, or shaped differently than hours.

She did not say this to Havelock or Orenstein. It was not a thing you could put in a field log.

---

That evening she sat in the truck while they ate at the camp table and she thought about it.

She had been on thirty-one surveys in eleven years. She had catalogued anomalies. She understood that anomalies were the point — that you went out to find the thing that didn't fit, and you documented the not-fitting, and you came home and filed the documentation, and that was the work. She was good at it.

She did not go back to the shaft the next day.

She told Havelock it was the altitude. She had some susceptibility; it was documented. He accepted this. Orenstein accepted it. They went back. She stayed at the truck with the equipment cases and the sample logs and she organized things that did not need organizing and she did not look in the direction of the shaft.

The memorandum would say: *one member declined to return to the shaft after the first day.* The memorandum would attribute the incidents to altitude and thermal conditions.

She had not told them about the time.

She had not told them what she thought, which was not a hypothesis exactly, more a quality of attention: the shaft was not a place you could observe from the outside. Something about proximity to it made you part of what was being measured. The instrument and the site were not separable in the way they were supposed to be separable. You could not stand at the edge and remain entirely yourself.

This was not a thing you could put in a field log.

The memorandum recommended against further investigation. She had not written the memorandum. She didn't know if the recommendation was for the same reason.

She hoped it was for the same reason.
