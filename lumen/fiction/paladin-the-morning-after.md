# The Morning After

*A scene for Smitty's Paladin story*

---

He woke before the town did, which was how he always woke — habit from the order, from years of first watch while the master slept. The sky was the particular gray that wasn't dawn yet, just the absence of full dark.

The master's horse was gone. He'd known it would be. He'd watched from the window last night until the sound of hooves faded and then he'd stood there a while longer, doing nothing, and then he'd gone to bed because there was nothing else to do.

Now he sat in the common room of the inn with the fire banked low and a cup of water he wasn't drinking and thought about what the order required of him.

The order's requirements were clear in the easy cases. He'd memorized them at twelve, recited them for eight years, explained them to townspeople twice this very week: a paladin defends. A paladin does not flee. A paladin especially does not flee from what he's been bitten by, because that's the moment when fleeing matters most.

The master had fled.

He wasn't angry. That surprised him — he'd expected anger, had braced for it in the night when the sound of hooves was still receding. But what arrived instead was something quieter. More like: *oh.* More like finding a crack in a foundation you'd thought was solid. Not rage, just the slow revision of what you'd believed.

The master had been a paladin for thirty years. He'd ridden into the village three days ago with all the weight that implied. He'd assessed the situation and he'd said, in front of the innkeeper and two farmers and a child: *we will handle this.* And then, alone, in the dark, he'd revised that assessment.

*He was afraid.* That was the honest version. He was afraid, and he left, and now the town still needed handling.

---

The girl from the kitchen came in to rebuild the fire and stopped when she saw him.

"You're up early," she said.

"Yes."

She looked at him for a moment. She was maybe fourteen, young enough that she'd probably believed, before this week, that paladins were the thing that made the fear stop. Now she'd seen the master's face when the werewolf came out of the trees — that particular expression, not brave and not afraid but somewhere between them that he didn't have a word for. The expression of a man calculating.

"Is he coming back?" she asked.

"No."

"Are you staying?"

He looked at the cup of water in his hands. He'd been avoiding the question since the hooves faded. What the order required. What he was, without the master to be it first and show him how.

"Yes," he said.

He wasn't sure if it was true until he said it. Then it was.

---

After she'd gone back to the kitchen he sat with the fire building behind him and thought about what *yes* meant in practical terms. He had: a sword he was competent with. Training in the order's methods, which were good against most things and probably adequate against a werewolf if you were fast and didn't hesitate. Knowledge of the fruit tree and why the king wanted it and why the werewolf kept returning to the valley where the tree grew. The trust of no one in this town, yet — just the borrowed trust of the master's reputation, which he'd now have to maintain without the master.

He had no one to check his thinking against. That was the new fact. Everything he decided from now on he'd decide alone, and there'd be no one to tell him if he was wrong until it was too late to be corrected.

He thought about the master. Thirty years. All that weight. And then the calculation, and the hooves in the dark.

Maybe the master was right. Maybe what lived in those woods was beyond what two could handle, and the master had done the arithmetic and found it honest. Maybe leaving was the rational choice, and everything the order said about not fleeing was the language of people who'd never met this particular thing in these particular woods at this particular hour.

Maybe.

But the town was still here. The tree was still here. The werewolf would come back.

And he had said yes.
