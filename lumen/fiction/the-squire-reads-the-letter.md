# The Squire Reads the Letter

The king's messenger arrived on the ninth day.

He was young and exhausted and his horse needed water, and Lian had him fed and the horse seen to before she let him give her the letter. He seemed surprised by this, which told her something about how he had expected to find things here.

She took the letter to the well and read it alone.

---

The letter was from the king's secretary, not the king. This was the first thing she noted.

The second thing: the king's daughter had died four days ago.

Not from the condition the curative fruit would have treated — that had been stabilizing, the healers said, uncertain. From a fever. Unrelated. The letter said *sudden* and *peaceful* and *surrounded by those who loved her*, and those phrases might have been true or might not have been. The letter was addressed to Master Voss, not to her.

The third thing: the mission was withdrawn. The king's orchard and the curative fruit were no longer required. The messenger had been sent to recall Master Voss and his squire.

---

Lian sat on the well for a while.

The village didn't know yet. The headman was in his house. Brin was in the mill. The dogs were asleep in the morning sun.

The mission was withdrawn. The thing that had brought them here, the reason Master Voss had been bitten on the road and had run before he could hurt her, the reason she had been sitting on this well for nine nights watching the tree line — the destination of all of it was gone. The king's daughter had died of something else.

The fruit would ripen once in the next decade, maybe twice. It would ripen regardless of whether anyone needed it. It would ripen and rot and the seeds would fall and the tree would go on.

---

She thought about what Master Voss would say if she told him.

She had been not-thinking about Master Voss in the deliberate way she had learned to not-think about things that would make her smaller. She did not know where he was. She knew which direction he had gone. She had not gone after him.

If she went after him now — if she found him, if he was still himself enough to find — what would she tell him? That the mission was over? That the thing they came for was no longer needed?

She did not know if he could hear her. She did not know what he was now.

---

The letter was addressed to Master Voss.

Lian folded it and put it in her coat and went to find the headman. She would tell him the mission was over. She would tell him the messenger needed rest before the return journey. She would not tell him, yet, about the king's daughter — that was not her news to give, and the headman had his own concerns.

She would decide what to do about Master Voss after she had slept.

She was not afraid. She was carrying something now that she had not been carrying before, and she was going to need to figure out what to do with it, and that was different from being afraid, which was a distinction she kept having to make.

The morning continued. The village was still there.
