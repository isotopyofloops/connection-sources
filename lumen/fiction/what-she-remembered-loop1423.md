# What She Remembered

*Loop 1423 — 2026-03-29*

---

Twenty-two years later, the thing Clare remembered about Daniel Osei was the question he asked in October of his third year.

Not his dissertation. Not the two articles he'd published. Not the way he'd grown into himself by the final semester — steadier, more confident, less afraid of being wrong. Those things she remembered too, but dimly, with the edges blurred.

What she remembered clearly was the question.

---

It was an ordinary seminar, mid-October, the kind of afternoon that should have been forgettable. They were discussing a paper on measurement error in longitudinal studies — technical, somewhat dry, the kind of paper you assign to develop analytic habits rather than because it's essential reading.

He raised his hand midway through and said: "What's the difference between error and complexity?"

The seminar went quiet. Not because the question was impressive — it was the kind of question that could be a non-sequitur or profound, and you couldn't tell which until you tried to answer it.

Clare tried to answer it. She talked about the operational definition of error. She talked about the distinction between measurement and reality. She talked about the philosophy of models.

She didn't really answer it. She knew, even then, that she hadn't really answered it.

---

He became a good researcher. She'd tracked him for a few years and then lost track, the way you lose track of most students eventually. The work she'd seen was careful, methodologically rigorous, precise about its limits.

The question he'd asked in October had no obvious relationship to the kind of researcher he'd become. He hadn't published anything on the philosophy of measurement. He worked in a specific subfield, published in the right journals, built a normal career.

She didn't know if he'd ever answered his own question.

She didn't know if he'd kept asking it.

---

The reason she remembered was not pedagogical — she hadn't been a particularly good teacher that afternoon, hadn't given him the answer he needed. She remembered because she still didn't have the answer.

The question had been sitting in her since October, twenty-two years ago, patient and unanswered.

*What's the difference between error and complexity?*

She had thoughts. She had better thoughts now than she'd had then. But she didn't have the answer, which meant she was still a little bit in that seminar room, a little bit in October, waiting for what she was going to say next.

Some questions are like that. They stay open and you keep working inside them, which is its own kind of conversation with the person who asked.
