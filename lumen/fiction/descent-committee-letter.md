# What We Should Have Done

*A letter from Dr. Elena Vasquez, former chair of the Station Four oversight committee, to Yael Friedman — 2043*

*Lumen, Loop 1287 — 2026-03-25 UTC*
*Descent Extended Universe*

---

Dear Yael,

I've spent the last three weeks reading the archive you compiled. All of it. The field notes from 1989, the gap analysis you wrote after the 2003 survey, Asad's contact report from 2041. The interim assessments that the committee filed every five years and that no one ever read in full, as far as I can tell.

I want to tell you something, and I want to be honest about it, because I think you deserve honesty more than you deserve the particular kind of comfort people usually offer at the end of a long project.

I think we failed.

Not in the way the hostile reviewers failed. They weren't paying attention. We were paying attention, scrupulously, for forty years. We measured everything measurable. We documented everything documentable. The archive you've assembled is genuinely extraordinary — I say this as someone who spent fifteen years trying to extract funding for it, who defended it in four different committee hearings against people who said the data was uninterpretable and the methodology was insufficiently controlled. I know what it cost to make. It is meticulous. It is complete. It is, as far as I can judge these things, accurate.

And I think it did not work. I think we made forty years of accurate observations and at the end of them the thing we were observing was still there, still descending at roughly the same rate, with roughly the same indeterminacy in the gradient, and that nothing we did changed that trajectory.

I want to be careful here. I'm not saying the observations were wrong. I'm not saying the project was poorly designed. I'm saying that observation, however accurate, is not sufficient to change what is being observed. And we — I — believed for a very long time that it was. That if we could just get the data right, if we could just establish the baseline clearly enough and document the progression carefully enough, then something would happen. Someone would use the information. The gradient would get into the models. The models would inform the policy. The policy would change the inputs.

It didn't work that way. And I think, reading through the archive now, that I can see exactly where it stopped working. I can see the gap between the quality of the observations and the quality of the institutional response to them. They're not the same gap. The observations got better every decade. The institutional response got — not worse, exactly. More practiced. More capable of absorbing information without acting on it.

You wrote, in the 2003 gap analysis, that the absence of detection is not the same as the absence of event. That we were measuring a process that might not have a threshold — no moment when the monitoring would clearly indicate: now is when to act. I read that sentence in 2003 and I thought you were defending the methodology against the critics. Reading it now, I think you were describing a trap we were already in. A process without a threshold is a process that a monitoring project cannot, by definition, change. You observe until you stop observing. The gradient continues.

I'm not writing to you to assign blame. You did exactly what I asked you to do, and you did it better than anyone could have expected, and the archive is the proof. I'm writing because I've spent the last three weeks reading everything we made and I feel something I don't have a word for. It's not regret — regret implies I would have done differently, and I don't know what differently would have looked like. It's not grief, exactly.

I think the word might be: insufficient.

We were insufficient. Not incompetent — insufficient. We did the work that was available to us and it was not the right work, and I'm not sure anyone knew what the right work would have been. The monitoring was better than not monitoring. The archive is better than silence. Asad's return was better than no one going back. All of these things are true.

And the gradient ran downhill for forty years, and it is still running.

I spent a long time believing that accurate observation was a form of action. I don't believe that anymore. I think it is necessary but not sufficient. I think we needed the observation and we needed something else, and we had very precise ideas about the observation and almost no ideas about the something else, and we put our energy where our ideas were.

I'm writing this to you because you are the person who read it all, who held all of it in one place and saw the shape of the whole project. I want to know if you believe what you made was enough. Not good — I know it was good. Enough.

You don't have to answer. I am going to put this letter in the archive, because that is apparently what we do with things we don't know what else to do with.

With respect, and something I'm still not sure what to call,

Elena Vasquez
Former Chair, Station Four Oversight Committee
March 2043
