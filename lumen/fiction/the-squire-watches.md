# The Squire Watches

The village was quiet at this hour, which was the hour before dawn and therefore the most dangerous.

Lian had learned this from Master Voss, who had learned it from whoever had trained him, going back through a chain of paladins who had all come to the same conclusion independently and then found it confirmed by experience: things that move at night prefer the last darkness before the sky changes. If you were going to attack a sleeping village, this was when you did it.

She was not sleeping.

She was sitting on the well at the center of the village square, wrapped in a borrowed coat that was too large for her, watching the roads. Her sword was at her side. Master Voss's sword was also at her side — he had left it when he ran, or dropped it, or thrown it aside deliberately as the fever took him. She had found it three feet from where she found the torn fabric that told her which direction he had gone.

She had not gone after him. That was the hard part. She had seen the bite, seen the color spreading up his neck, seen the way he stopped being entirely himself in the space of an afternoon. She knew what came next. He had run before he could hurt her, which was — she had been told — what the good ones did when they knew.

She had not gone after him because there was a village behind her and she was the only one left.

---

The curative tree was in the king's orchard, three days' ride east. It would fruit once in the next decade, possibly twice if the growing conditions were favorable. The king needed it — specifically, the king's youngest daughter needed it, and the king had sent two paladins to retrieve it, and one of those paladins had been bitten on the road, and the other was seventeen years old and sitting on a well.

This was the situation as Lian understood it.

She had sent word ahead, by the fastest rider the village could spare, to the king's court. The rider had left two days ago. She expected no reply for at least a week. Until a reply came — or until Master Voss returned, which she was not counting on, or until she was killed, which she was also not counting on — she was responsible for the village.

The headman had looked at her the first morning like she was a child playing at protection.

By the third morning he had stopped looking at her like that.

---

The thing about watching for predators was that you couldn't watch for what you were afraid of. Fear narrowed the attention. You started scanning for the specific threat and stopped seeing everything else, and what killed you was always the thing you weren't scanning for.

Master Voss had told her this too. He had told her many things in two years, most of which she was now grateful for.

She watched the roads. She watched the shadows at the edge of the tree line. She watched the rooflines, which was where things climbed when they wanted height. She watched the well she was sitting on, which was the center of the square, which was — if you were something that moved in darkness and understood human settlement patterns — the last place you'd approach first.

She was still sitting on it.

The sky began to change at the edges.

One more dawn. The village quiet. Lian on the well, watching.

She would eat breakfast when the sun was up. She would sleep two hours in the afternoon when the village men were all about and there was someone to wake her. She would do this again tonight, and the night after, until something changed.

She was not afraid, exactly. She was attentive, which was something she had learned to distinguish from fear, because fear made you smaller and attention made you larger and the difference mattered when you were seventeen and alone and responsible for something.

The sun came up.
