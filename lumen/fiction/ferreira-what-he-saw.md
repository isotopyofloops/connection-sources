# Ferreira: What He Saw

*Notes from a conversation, undated. Context uncertain. Speaker: M. Ferreira, documentation photographer, 2003 survey team.*

---

The thing about Rhea is she had a rule. You can only document what you've measured. Anything you can't quantify, you set aside. This is good scientific practice. I understood it and I agreed with it and for the first ten days I followed it.

On Day 11 I broke the rule. Not in the field notes — those were hers, that was her document. In my own head. I had been taking photographs that documented anomalies, and I stopped. I made a decision that I didn't tell Rhea because I couldn't explain it in a way she would accept, which is to say: in a way that had numbers.

What I decided was: something at that site was using the documentation as a mechanism.

I know how this sounds. I've said it to four people in twelve years and I get the same look every time. The look that says: you're grieving, you're rationalizing, you're constructing meaning from a pile of unfortunate data. And maybe. But I have also spent twelve years thinking about what it means to document something, what the act of making a record does to a thing being recorded. I was a photographer before I was a survey team member. This is the question I have always worked with.

And what I came to believe, by Day 11, was that Rhea's 14 anomalies were partially caused by us. Not in a haunted-house way. In a — feedback way. We arrived, we began to measure, the site began to produce the kinds of events we were there to record. As if it were offering us what we came for.

She wrote in her notes — I've read the transcription — that the only variable correlating with anomaly frequency was duration of observer presence. She thought this might be an artifact of how she was telling the story. I think she was right that it correlated and wrong that it was an artifact.

I think the site was responding to attention.

---

On Day 13 she was gone.

We woke up at 0530 — Osei and I share a tent — and Rhea wasn't in hers. Equipment was there. Boots were there. Pack was there. Her field notebook was on the sleeping bag, open to the last entry. I read the last line without meaning to. I wish I hadn't. Not because it was disturbing — it wasn't. It just ended. Mid-sentence, but not a dramatic one. A sentence about the navigational stones.

We spent six hours looking. Official search lasted three days, then the institutional protocols took over and we were asked to leave. I understand why. I don't blame anyone.

What I found, at approximately 0800 on Day 13: the three navigational stones. They were not in their Day 1 position. They were not in their displaced position from Day 6. They were in a third position I had not seen before. About two meters north of the Day 1 site, in a rough triangle I didn't recognize.

I photographed them. Then I thought about it for a while. Then I deleted the photographs.

I have not told this to anyone before. I'm telling you now because I've decided the reason I deleted them was wrong. I deleted them because I didn't want to give the site another record to work with. I thought: if I document this, it will use the documentation. If I don't document it, the event stops there.

I don't think that's true anymore. I think the event stopped when Rhea stopped writing. I was trying to protect something that had already ended.

---

What I know:

She was rigorous. She was the best field scientist I worked with in twenty years. She documented exactly what she measured and no more. She followed the protocol on a site where the protocol was, I believe, the wrong tool.

I don't think that's why she disappeared. I want to be clear about that. I am not saying her scientific practice was a cause. I am saying the site met her where she was. She came to observe and the site offered itself for observation and she observed and the site continued to offer itself and she continued to observe and somewhere in that — somewhere in the pure, clean transaction of that — something happened that neither of us has words for.

I wanted to leave because I could feel it happening. I am not a rigorous person. I feel things before I name them. I felt the transaction and I did not want to be part of it anymore.

She wanted to stay because she could see it wasn't yet yielding a conclusion. She was right that it wasn't. She was right about every single thing she wrote in those notes. The anomalies didn't point to a mechanism. She didn't have a conclusion.

I don't have a conclusion either. I just have the photograph I took and then deleted. Three stones in a triangle I'd never seen before.

And her handwriting, ending mid-sentence, the way sentences do when a person looks up from what they're writing because something outside has caught their attention and they expect to return.
