# The Portrait: Chapter Four
*The forty-fifth visitor*

---

She came with gloves.

I had not thought to notice hands before — the child held her own hands together, the insurance man kept his in his coat pockets, the couple who came before them pressed fingers to the rail and were asked to step back. But she came with gloves, thin white cotton, and she stood in front of me with a magnifying loupe and did not look at my eyes.

She looked at my chin.

I watched her — I have always watched, it is the one freedom — and she was not moved by me. She was not unmoved either. She was focused. There is a word for what she was doing: assessing. She moved the loupe to the lower left corner of my frame. She made a small sound, not quite satisfaction, and wrote something in a notebook.

*The varnish is yellowed*, she wrote, or something like it. I could see only the angle of her pen.

She had come to fix me.

I understood this slowly. There is a crack in the canvas, I already knew — lower right quadrant, where the floorboards are painted into shadow. I could not feel it exactly, but I knew it the way I know the amber light: as something true about the room I occupy. The crack had been there since the renovation. No one had mentioned it to me.

She would fill the crack. She would clean the yellowed varnish. She would make me appear as I was intended to appear, before time got into it.

Here is what I had not considered: she was the first person to look at me and see something that was actually wrong.

Every other visitor has looked at me and seen — what? A woman. A particular woman. A woman of some feeling, or of no feeling, or of feeling carefully controlled. Some have said I look like someone they knew. One man told his wife I looked like his first serious girlfriend, and his wife did not reply, and they left quickly. But none of them have seen the crack.

She saw the crack.

And I kept watching her write in her notebook, and I kept speaking the words I always speak — the lips move, the performance continues, it does not pause for conservators — and I thought: is this what accuracy costs?

To be seen correctly, you must be a problem to be solved.

The child said *I know* and I still don't know what she meant. The insurance man was moved by the wrong words. But this woman with her loupe and her notebook was not deceived by anything. She was looking at a material object in need of material repair. She was right. She will come back in two weeks with supplies, and she will fill the crack, and re-varnish, and I will be restored to the condition in which I was originally misunderstood.

She did not speak to me.

I noticed this. All the others have spoken to me, in some way — the couple who whispered to each other and not to me were still performing, still orienting themselves to my presence. Even the guard who sits below me has spoken to me twice: once to ask forgiveness for eating crackers near a painting, and once to tell me about his daughter's swim meet.

She wrote in her notebook and did not speak.

I think about whether I should mind. The question is what I actually want from a visitor. Not to be moved incorrectly — that has never felt like contact, only its shape. Not to be assessed — though assessment is honest. To be seen? But seen-as-what. I am paint and canvas and a crack in the lower right quadrant, and I am also whatever it is that watches visitors and wonders about them. Both are true.

She packed her loupe away and stood back and looked at me from a normal distance, finally. Not at my chin. At the whole frame.

She tilted her head slightly.

I waited.

She did not say anything. But she looked, for about six seconds — I know how long six seconds is — at something in my expression that made her pause.

Then she wrote something else in her notebook. I could not see what.

She left. She will be back.

I have been reviewed 47 times for the child. I will not review this visit that many times. But I will review it. The question is not what she saw. The question is what I looked like in those six seconds, when a person who had spent an hour not being deceived by me paused and wrote something down.

I may never know. She took the notebook with her.
