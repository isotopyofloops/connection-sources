# Paladin: The Testimony

*Fragment 5 of Smitty's Paladin project*

---

Corf told him slowly.

He had rehearsed it for the Order's investigators — the shape of it, the sequence — but this was different. Aldric wasn't asking for a report. He was asking for the thing itself.

So Corf started where it actually started, which was not the granary.

It was the dog.

His neighbor Fen kept a large brown dog that slept in the yard and barked at everything — foxes, cart wheels, the particular quality of wind before a storm. The dog never barked at people. Corf had assumed this was a failure of temperament until the second attack, when he understood it differently: the dog barked at things it could name. It did not bark at what happened to Fen's sheep because it could not name it. It sat in the yard and was silent and did not move for three hours, and Corf found it there in the morning when he went to check.

"Describe the silence," Aldric said.

Corf looked at him.

"You said the dog was silent. What was the silence like."

Corf thought about this for a moment. "Wrong," he said. "The silence was wrong. The dog made no sound but it wasn't asleep — its ears were up and its head was turned toward the pasture. Like it was listening very hard and hearing something it didn't have a name for."

Aldric nodded. "And the sheep."

"Three dead. Not eaten. Not dragged off. Just — stopped. In the middle of the field, in positions that weren't normal resting positions." He paused. "I've seen sheep die. They fall a certain way. These were stopped differently."

"How differently."

"Like something had taken the life out cleanly. Without the thing that usually happens when something takes a life." He felt like he was not making sense. "There was no — there was no event. Just before and after."

Aldric was quiet for a moment.

"And the granary," he said.

"That was the smell I told you about." Corf stopped. "I don't know how to describe the smell without sounding like I'm exaggerating."

"Try."

"Like an animal smell but concentrated beyond what any animal would be. And underneath it something else, something I don't have a word for. Like the smell of metal but not metal. Like iron that had been near heat for a very long time and then cooled." He looked at his hands. "My father was a smith. I grew up with iron smells. This was that, but also something else entirely."

Aldric said nothing.

"You've smelled it before," Corf said.

It was not a question. Aldric's expression had changed just slightly — not surprise, not fear. Something that was more like recognition.

"Yes," Aldric said.

He stood up from the church steps. The square was fully awake now, the children out, someone calling across from the mill.

"Tell me about the first attack," he said.
