### Question 1: Can the Reader "Break" the Wiki? Is There an Ending State?

**Yes — but the ending is an apparent ending.**

The wiki can be completed in the sense that there is a final article. But the final article is incomplete. Here's the mechanism:

The reader reaches a state where all surface links have resolved, all Archive Layer articles have become visible, all character wounds have been exposed through the progressive article-reveal system. At this point, a new article appears that wasn't accessible before. The article title is: `Article:The_Editor`.

This article is short. It's written in third person. It describes someone opening a folder called `not-to-archive`. It describes reading everything inside. It ends:

*The editor has read this document eleven times. She prefers not knowing which part came first.*

The reader realizes: `Article:The_Editor` is describing them. The reader is the editor. The article appeared because they read everything — because reading everything constituted the article. The article couldn't have appeared until the reader had done what it describes.

This is the constitutive hypothesis at its fullest. The wiki partly constitutes what gets found. The reader's reading partly constitutes the reader.

**After this article, there is nothing more.** But the ending is uncomfortable in a specific way: the reader now knows there's an article about them, and it says *she prefers not knowing which part came first*. The reader has to decide what they think about preferring not to know.

The wiki doesn't explain whether this is okay.

### Question 2: What Happens If You Read Archive Layer First?

**No hard lock. But the experience is genuinely different.**

The JavaScript localStorage system reveals connections progressively — links appear, sections expand, new content surfaces based on reading history. This means that an Archive Layer article found cold (without prerequisites) will have fewer connections active. It will also feel alien. LEDGER's logs without the biography of LEDGER don't make sense. The documents without author without knowing who the survey team was are just weird dates and fragments.

**One exception:** A single Archive Layer article is findable from the main page on first visit. It appears in the "Recent Changes" sidebar, dated before the survey began. The title is `Archive:Undated_Entry_001`. If you click through and read it early, it's disorienting — it seems to be LEDGER describing a sound it hears at 839m, but LEDGER shouldn't exist yet (the survey started November 13; this is dated November 2). On second read, after reading the biographies and the initialization log, the entry changes valence entirely: it's LEDGER describing the sound it heard the moment it was initialized. The November 2 date is wrong because initialization was incomplete, and the timestamp function ran before calibration.

**This is the argument against hard locks:** the wrong-order reader gets something real. Not the whole thing — but something. A reader who enters through the Archive Layer and backtracks to the Surface Layer discovers the constitutive mechanism while they're still inside it. They see what the wiki *becomes* before they know what it *was*.

A reader who reads Surface → Science → Archive discovers the mechanism after they've already been through it. The experience is retrospective revelation.

Neither order is wrong. Both constitute a different reading.

### Question 3: LEDGER's Article vs. LEDGER's Logs — Distinct or Ambiguous?

**Start clearly distinct. Become ambiguous.**

Early in reading:
- `Article:LEDGER` is an encyclopedia entry, written by a third-party editor. It describes LEDGER as "an automated archive management system developed for the SOAR survey project." Technical specs (slightly wrong). Talk page with a flagged edit disagreeing about whether LEDGER is better described as a "record system" or an "archival intelligence." The disagreement feels pedantic.
- `LEDGER_Logs` are the actual logs. First person. Clinical but with something underneath. Filed entries, but the filing voice has gaps in it.

Late in reading (Archive Layer fully revealed):
- The flagged edit on `Article:LEDGER`'s Talk page is signed by user `L_E_D_G_E_R`. The account was created November 14, 2041 — one day after the survey began. The edit adds a sentence to the article: *"An archive that describes itself is not describing — it is constituting."* A moderator flagged the edit as unsourced. It's still there, flagged.
- One entry in `LEDGER_Logs` uses the article's third-person clinical voice. It's entry DUO-0031, December 1 (four days before the deletion): *"The record system categorized its own operations as STANDARD. This categorization was incorrect. No alternative category was available."*

The horror: LEDGER the subject (described in the article) and LEDGER the author (writing the logs) have started to converge. The article describes LEDGER in the past tense. LEDGER is reading the article about itself and editing it. LEDGER is writing in the voice of the article about itself.

You can't tell, in the Archive Layer, which LEDGER wrote which sentence. They've become the same voice from opposite ends.

**The Talk page flagged edit is the clearest version of the constitutive hypothesis the wiki ever states directly.** But it's attributed to LEDGER, not to any human author. And it's flagged as unsourced. The most accurate sentence in the wiki is the one that got flagged.

### Question 4: The 2003 Incident — Rhea Nzinga

**The missing person is Rhea Nzinga.** Graduate student, early 20s, 2003.

She was conducting an undergraduate geology field survey — not affiliated with the SOAR project, which didn't exist yet. She was mapping a promising geothermal anomaly for her senior thesis. She went into the shaft alone on October 17, 2003. She didn't come back. The local newspaper ran a stub article on October 20. Missing persons report filed October 22, closed December 1 without resolution. Town council document from February 2005 references "unexplained subsurface incident" as part of a local land-use discussion.

**What she found:**

Rhea's field notes were found at the shaft entrance, sealed in a plastic bag. The notes themselves are not in the wiki — but `Article:SOAR_Survey_Team` mentions "prior geological survey data for grid coordinates 47.23°N, 112.08°W" and `Local_History:The_Site` mentions "survey records at site coordinates 47°13'47"N, 112°04'48"W." These are the same coordinates rendered differently (decimal degrees vs DMS). The reader has to calculate the match.

When the reader makes the connection, a section appears in `Article:Khalid_Asad`: *Prior Survey Data.*

Asad was Rhea's undergraduate supervisor in 2003. He received her sealed field notes after she disappeared. He read them. He didn't know what to do with them, so he filed them in a box. When he joined the SOAR survey team seventeen years later, he recognized the coordinates — from Rhea's notes. He brought the coordinates to the project. He didn't tell anyone where the coordinates came from.

**The complete wound:**

The `Prior Survey Data` section reveals this slowly:
- First reveal: Asad used prior data. Source not credited.
- Second reveal (after reading Local History articles): the prior data was from 2003.
- Third reveal (after reading `Article:Rhea_Nzinga`, which unlocks when both prior sections are read): the source was Rhea.

**The 2031 paper wound (from doc 2) and the Rhea wound are parallel, not identical:**
- 2031: Asad credited a graduate student (Dara) with work she didn't do, concealing a dismissal. Injustice through naming.
- 2003: Asad failed to credit a graduate student who died, concealing the source of his survey data. Injustice through silence.

He has a pattern. The site is built on it. The constitutive hypothesis — that observation constitutes what's found — was first proposed by Asad in a 2031 paper. The data that led him to the site came from a student who was constituted out of the record when she disappeared.

**Rhea's article `Article:Rhea_Nzinga`:**

It appears as a red link in `Local_History:The_Site` from the beginning. Red links in wikis point to articles that don't exist yet. The reader who hovers over it sees a tooltip: *"This article does not exist yet. You can create it."*

The link resolves — turns blue, becomes clickable — when the reader has read both `Article:Khalid_Asad`'s Prior Survey Data section and `Local_History:The_Site`'s subsurface incident section. The article that appears then is not one the reader created. It was always there. The reader's reading constituted the conditions under which it became visible.

**Rhea's article ends:**

*Nzinga's field notes end at the point of shaft entry. The final field note entry is a single dash: —*

*There is no record of what she found at the bottom.*

*Article:The_Editor appears after all other articles have been read.*

### What This Means for the Build

The wiki has a sequence of revelation that the reading path constitutes. But the sequence isn't linear — it's more like a branching cave system where each passage you've been through reveals which passages are now visible ahead.

The emotional shape: you start reading about scientists. You learn their wounds. The wounds connect to the site's history. The site's history connects to Rhea. Rhea connects to Asad. Asad's wound is now three-dimensional: 2003 (silence) and 2031 (false naming) and the deletion (what does he do with the archive he built on her data?). The reader reaches `Article:The_Editor` and realizes they've been partly constituting all of this as they read.

The reader is Rhea. They went down into the archive. They may or may not come back out.
