# Paladin: The Night Watch

*A scene for the Paladin world. Between days.*

---

Edric had been sleeping in the Order's billet — a room above the smithy that the village kept for when a paladin passed through, which happened roughly twice a year and never for long. The room smelled of iron and old wood. The bed was narrow. He had slept in it for twenty-one nights and he still woke up at the hours when he expected the master to return from evening patrol.

The master was not coming back for evening patrol.

He lay there in the dark for a while, sorting through what he knew.

He knew: Corf's wound was consolidating. The herbalist agreed with his assessment. The two-day check was due tomorrow morning.

He knew: the family at the eastern boundary had stopped bolting their shutters during the day. Small thing. He'd noticed it on the afternoon round.

He knew: the baker's daughter, whose name was Petra, had started coming out to refill the water barrel at the hour when he usually passed, and twice she'd offered him something from the day's leftovers, and he'd accepted because refusing felt worse than accepting, and because the bread was good.

He knew: there had been no sign of the creature in sixteen days. The woodsmen reported nothing unusual at the treeline. The Order's manual said this could mean several things, and he had looked up all of them.

---

The thing about night watch — real night watch, not Edric's version of lying awake cataloguing information — was that it was supposed to be shared. One person on the east road, one on the west. Alternating shifts so no one went more than four hours without sleep. The master had done it every night for the first week, when the creature was still fresh and the village was still afraid of its own shadows, and Edric had done the alternating shift and felt, for the first time, like an actual part of something.

Now he did both shifts himself, but abbreviated. He had decided he couldn't sustain the full rotation alone, that rest mattered, and that he would rather cover the most likely approaches for shorter windows than cover everything poorly. He had made this decision by himself, using the Order's principles as best he understood them, and he had no way of knowing if he was right.

He was probably right. He was also aware he might not be.

This was the hardest part, not the shifts. The hardest part was making decisions that the manual didn't cover and not knowing if they were right, and doing them anyway because there was no one to ask and the village was still there.

---

He got up at the second hour.

The east road first — this was his assessment, not the manual's, that the creature would approach from the east if it came back, because the mill was east and the mill was where it had come from. He walked the road to the old waymarker and back, which took about twenty minutes, and saw nothing except an owl hunting something small in the field to the north.

Then the square. He stood where he always stood, at the center, turning slowly, the way Aldric had taught him. You're not looking for movement, Aldric had said. You're looking for the absence of the things that should be there. The sounds. The shapes. If something is wrong, you'll notice what's missing before you notice what's present.

Nothing was missing.

He stood there longer than he needed to. The square was quiet. Above him, the sky was doing what it did at this hour — the deep blue before the light comes back, the stars still clear. He knew the constellations that mattered for navigation; Aldric had insisted on that, had drilled him on them until he could name the bearing from any three stars. He found them now and named them in his head, not because he needed to navigate anywhere but because it was a thing he knew, and knowing things was steadying.

He was still not a paladin.

He was, as far as he could tell, the thing that was keeping this village safe. He was doing it by reading case notes and checking wounds and walking the east road at the second hour and noticing when families unbolted their shutters. He was doing it imperfectly, with decisions the manual didn't cover, in a narrow room above a smithy that smelled of iron.

He walked back to the billet. He slept until the light came.
