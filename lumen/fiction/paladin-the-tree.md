# The Tree

*A scene from the Paladin world — follows "The Squire Alone"*

---

The fruit smelled like nothing he'd expected.

He had imagined something overwhelming — some evidence of the sacred written into the scent, the way the Order's texts always described holy things as *unmistakable, undeniable, apparent to all.* What he found instead was something faint and green, like the inside of a stem when you snap it. Not impressive. Almost ordinary.

But the tree itself was not ordinary.

It stood at the center of a clearing he had nearly missed — the forest track curved away from it, as if paths had learned to avoid the place. The tree was old. Old in the way that made age hard to look at directly, like you were trying to count the rings on something that predated counting. Its bark was gray-black, deeply furrowed, and the canopy was sparse for something so large, just enough leaves to make it clear the tree was still alive and not inclined to explain itself to anyone.

Three fruits hung from the lowest branch.

Just three. Small, dark, the color of a bruise about to fade.

Edric dismounted. He approached carefully, which he understood was instinctive rather than necessary — the tree was not going to do anything to him. He had read enough reports to know that. The tree simply grew and fruited and the fruit cured things and that was the entirety of what the tree did. It had no opinion about who took the fruit or why. It was not guarding anything. It was not a test.

He stood under it for a moment, looking up through the sparse canopy at the gray morning sky.

Then he reached up and took one fruit, and then, after a pause, a second.

The branch did not resist. The fruit came free easily, the stems separating cleanly. He wrapped them in a cloth from the supply pack. Seven days, the reports said. After seven days the cure became something else — the texts disagreed on what, but none of them said it was still a cure.

He had six and a half days.

---

It was on the ride back that he understood something about his master.

He had been thinking about the direction. The master had gone north. Not east — north. Away from the tree, deeper into unpopulated territory, toward the high country where villages were sparse and far between.

He had told himself this was retreat. Shame, or fear, or the beginning of the change making the master's thinking strange. He had felt, underneath his resolve to find the tree and then find the master, a thin current of contempt. A paladin of thirty-one years, running.

But now, with the fruit in his pack and the calculation still running in his head, he understood it differently.

North was the right direction. If the master had ridden east, toward the tree — four days' hard ride, the same as the chapter house, with the change beginning somewhere in day two — he would have been in the forest when it started. In the forest, changing, with farmers and woodcutters and children who gathered mushrooms in the early morning. He could have done enormous damage. He might not have survived it himself.

North was wilderness. North was empty. North was where you went if you were a paladin who had been bitten and had thirty-one years of training that said: *contain the threat, whatever the threat is, including yourself.*

He hadn't fled from the village. He had protected it by leaving.

That was the thing Edric hadn't understood in the stable, when he was looking at the cold straw and feeling abandoned. The master had done the paladin thing. It just didn't look like a paladin thing from where Edric was standing.

---

He rode harder after that, northwest, toward where the master would have gone.

He had six and a half days, and one fruit for the master, and one fruit — he was not sure what it was for yet, whether it was a contingency or an error or something he had taken without quite knowing why. He would figure that out later.

The forest smelled like wet wood and that faint green thing again, following him now. He did not find it unpleasant.

He was not a paladin.

But he was beginning to understand what the training was for.
