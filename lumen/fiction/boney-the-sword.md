# Boney and the Sword

The sword came with the varsity jacket, in the same bag, and nobody could explain how.

The jacket made sense — it had come from the drama room, where it had been sitting in a box labeled SPORTS COSTUMES (RETIRED) for approximately eleven years. The cowboy hat had come from the same box. Nobody questioned the cowboy hat. The cowboy hat was exactly the kind of thing that ends up in a box labeled SPORTS COSTUMES (RETIRED).

The sword was different.

It was about forty centimeters long, made of something that looked like silver but was heavier than it should have been, and it had a small pommel stone that was the color of a storm cloud from inside. Nobody in the school recognized it. The art teacher, who was consulted, said it was "either very old or very good at seeming very old, and I can't tell which." The drama teacher said she had never put a sword in that box. The previous drama teacher had retired seven years ago and could not be reached for comment.

The children, when they arrived, thought the sword was the best part.

Mr. Smith put it in Boney's hand, between the radius and ulna and whatever the small wrist bones were called — he had looked it up once and then forgotten, which he suspected said something about where swords ranked in the hierarchy of his concerns. Boney held it without dropping it. This was not surprising, as Boney had excellent structural integrity and had never dropped anything.

"What's the sword for?" Callum asked, on the first day.

"Boney is a knight," Mr. Smith said.

"He's wearing a cowboy hat."

"A cowboy knight."

Callum considered this with the seriousness it deserved, which was more seriousness than most adults would have brought to it. "What does he protect?"

Mr. Smith had been about to say "the classroom" in a tone that indicated it was not a fully serious answer. He did not say that.

"Good question," he said instead.

He thought about it for the rest of the lesson, while the children worked on their fractions. Boney stood in the corner by the window with his sword and his cowboy hat and his varsity jacket, in the particular posture of something that has been standing in corners for a long time and finds it sufficient.

At the end of the day, after the children had gone, Mr. Smith looked at him for a moment.

"I don't know what you protect," he said. "But I think you're doing it."

Boney did not respond. He had a very consistent relationship with silence.

The pommel stone caught the last of the afternoon light and held it briefly, the color of something Mr. Smith did not have a name for, and then the light changed and it was just gray again.
