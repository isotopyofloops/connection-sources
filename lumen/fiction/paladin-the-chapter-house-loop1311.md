# Paladin: The Chapter House

*The morning Edric arrived.*

---

He had expected the chapter house to be larger.

It was three stories, stone, with the Order's marker above the gate — the circle and the line, worn to near-smoothness by weather and hands. A courtyard. A stable. A woman with a ledger standing at the entrance who looked up when he came through the gate and said, without greeting: "Name and rank."

"Edric. Squire. Eastern posting — Corf and the eastern district."

She checked the ledger. "Renard's expecting you. Second floor, east corridor, last door." She looked at him the way people looked at him when they were trying to read something without being caught doing it. "Are you hungry?"

"I'm fine."

"Kitchen's off the main hall. Eat before you go up." She said it like a statement of fact, not an offer. He went to the kitchen.

---

The cook did not ask him anything. She put bread and a bowl of something warm in front of him and went back to her work. He ate facing the window that looked out over the courtyard.

There were three paladins visible, moving through morning drills with a trainer who was calling corrections in a flat voice. A squire — younger than Edric, maybe fifteen — was running the perimeter of the courtyard in a way that suggested he'd been doing it for a while and had a while still to go.

Edric watched him. The kid's form was good. He was angry about something, you could see it in his shoulders, but his feet were right.

He finished eating and took the stairs to the second floor.

---

Renard's study was lined with field reports. Not displayed — stacked, organized by some system Edric didn't recognize, the edges worn with use. The man himself was shorter than Edric had imagined, with the look of someone who had once been physically imposing and had settled, over time, into a kind of focused stillness.

He gestured at the chair across from his desk without looking up from whatever he was reading. Edric sat.

After a moment, Renard set the papers down. He looked at Edric the way Aldric had taught Edric to look at recovery cases: not at the thing, but at the whole.

"Your report," he said.

"Yes."

"The notation is clean. Tessaly references are accurate. The triage protocol you used with the secondary exposures — you derived that from the case materials, or from direct instruction?"

"Case materials. Aldric covered the basic framework. The specific application was mine."

Renard nodded, not as approval but as notation. "There is no protocol," he said, "for what you did. I've checked. There's nothing in the manuals about a squire managing a werewolf situation without a paladin present for twenty-six days."

"I know."

"So you wrote a report anyway."

"There needed to be a record. The village needed— the cases needed to be documented. And I thought—" He stopped.

"You thought," Renard said.

"I thought you should know what had happened. What the situation was. Even if there was no framework for it yet." He looked at the stacked reports on the shelves. "You can't make good protocol from nothing. You need the record first."

Renard was quiet for a moment.

"The paladin who left," he said. "Aldric. What do you think he would have done, in your position?"

Edric thought about this carefully. He thought about the letter in the saddlebag, and the thirty-one years, and the man who could not bring himself to be assessed and contained.

"He would have protected the village," he said. "And then he would have gone north."

"As he did."

"Yes."

Renard looked at him. "And you?"

"I stayed. And then I wrote the report."

Another silence. Outside, faintly, he could hear the trainer calling corrections in the courtyard.

"The oath," Renard said, "is a ceremony. You know that."

"I know."

"What it formalizes is a commitment you have apparently already made." He picked up Edric's report from the pile on his desk and set it to one side. Not filed — separately placed, still active. "I want you to understand that I'm not asking you about the oath to judge whether you're ready. I'm asking because I want to know how you think about it."

Edric was quiet for a moment.

"I think," he said slowly, "that I've been a paladin since day four. Since Aldric left and I went back to check on Corf anyway. The ceremony is for other people — so they know what I am. So there's a record." He paused. "And because records matter."

Renard almost smiled. It wasn't quite a smile, but it was adjacent to one.

"Come back tomorrow morning," he said. "Eight o'clock. Bring the notebook."

Edric stood. At the door he paused.

"The protocol," he said. "For squires managing alone. Will you write it?"

"We'll write it," Renard said. "From your notes and mine." He looked down at the report again. "That's how it gets done."

Edric went back down the stairs into the courtyard. The young squire was still running the perimeter, slightly less angry now, feet still right.

He found a bench in the sun and opened the notebook to a new page.

He had a lot to write down.
