# Paladin: The Twentieth Day

*A scene for Smitty's Paladin story. Follows "The Nineteenth Day."*

---

He went to Corf first.

It was early — the village not quite awake, the light flat and even. Corf was sitting up when Aldric arrived, which was new. The last week he'd been lying down most of the day, not from pain exactly but from a heaviness that he hadn't been able to explain when Aldric asked. Today he was sitting at the edge of his bed with his feet on the floor, and he looked at Aldric when he came in with something that was more like alertness than what he'd had before.

"How's the arm," Aldric said.

Corf held it out. Aldric examined it carefully, the way he'd done on the seventh day and the thirteenth. The wound was different. Not healed — he wasn't going to say healed, not yet. But the edges had changed. Nineteen days ago they had been irregular in a particular way that Tessaly's records had called active. Now they were irregular in a different way. The records had called that indeterminate.

Five of forty-one, he thought.

"Does it hurt."

"Less than yesterday," Corf said. "Different. It's like..." He thought about it. "Before it felt like something was pulling at it. Now it feels like it stopped pulling."

Aldric kept his face neutral. Indeterminate meant the outcome wasn't set. It could still go either way. But it had stopped progressing, and that was what the records said mattered most.

"I'm going to leave some instructions," he said. "With Marta, and with the herbalist at the east end of the village. I'll write them down before I go. You need to tell them immediately if anything changes — the feeling, the appearance, anything. Will you do that."

"You're leaving."

"I have to report to my order. But I'm going to write the instructions." He paused. "I think you're one of the ones who might hold. I don't know that yet. No one can know that yet. But the window has been different than I expected."

Corf looked at his arm for a moment. Then he said: "What does holding feel like."

"I don't know," Aldric said honestly. "The records don't have much from inside. They have observation from outside. I can tell you what it looks like, but what it feels like is something you'd know better than Tessaly."

Corf turned his arm over slowly, looking at it. "It feels like something decided," he said. "I don't know what it decided. But it feels like whatever was fighting stopped fighting."

Aldric wrote that down.

---

Corran was waiting in the inn when Aldric came back. He had his riding coat on and his two men outside, and he had the look of someone who had already decided what they were going to do that morning and was waiting for the conversation that preceded doing it.

Aldric sat down.

"Tell me what you found," Corran said.

He told it plainly: Marta, the name, the north pasture, the monthly timing, the cart driver, the mill. He told it in the order he'd found it, without emphasis, the way you say a thing when you want the person to hear it rather than to agree with you.

Corran was very still throughout. When Aldric finished, Corran said nothing for a moment.

"Gwydion."

"Yes."

"This shepherd has been going toward his sister's mill once a month since the change."

"That's what the timing suggests. I can't confirm he reaches it. I can confirm he's gone in that direction and has not attacked the people and locations he would pass. Including the mill cart three days ago."

Corran looked at the table. He had good hands, Aldric noticed — steady, used to holding things carefully. He was holding his cup now the same way.

"You're asking me to put this in my report to the court."

"I'm telling you what I found. You'll write what you judge appropriate."

"What I judge appropriate is what I can defend to a court physician who has been managing the king's decline for three years and has no interest in the behavioral residue of a missing shepherd." Corran set down the cup. "What you're asking me to believe is that the creature retained enough of Gwydion to go visiting, once a month, in the direction of his sister's house, without killing anyone."

"Not asking you to believe it. Asking you to note that the evidence is consistent with it."

Corran looked at him. It was the same look from the path three days ago — the look of a man deciding how much a thing weighs.

"The fruit," he said.

"Yes."

"I need it."

"I know."

"The king needs it."

"I understand that."

Corran stood and walked to the window. Outside, the village was moving now — the early workers, the sound of someone at the well.

"If I take the fruit and the creature loses what quiets it," he said. "What happens."

"More attacks, probably. I don't know how quickly. I don't know what the absence of the tree does to the pattern."

"If I leave the fruit."

"The situation continues. The creature continues to come and go. Corf gets his chance at holding. The village lives with the uncertainty."

"Those are not equivalent outcomes for the king."

"No. They're not."

Corran turned. "You understand I don't have the authority to choose the village over the king."

"Yes."

"And you don't have the authority to stop me."

"No."

They stood in the same room for a moment, two people who had run out of negotiations.

Then Corran said: "I'm going to take two of the three."

Aldric was very careful not to show anything.

"The third I'll leave. I'll report that the fruit was not fully ripe on two of the three specimens and I collected what was viable. One fruit may be sufficient — the physicians will know better than me whether it is." He picked up his cup again. "I'll report that the situation with the creature is unresolved and that in my judgment the squire's investigation has produced credible evidence warranting further study before the order is given to eliminate it. I'll name Gwydion in my report. The rest is for the court to decide."

"Thank you."

"Don't thank me. The king's physicians may override it in a week. The court may decide the creature question differently regardless of my report." Corran set down the cup for the last time. "What I can tell you is that in twenty years of doing this job, I have never written a report that named a creature as a person, and I'm going to write one today, and I'd like to know whether I'm making a mistake."

Aldric thought about the ninth night. The breathing in the moonlight. The creature lying down by the tree with the specific deliberateness of something that had found, for a few hours, an absence of pain.

"I don't know," he said. "That's the most honest answer I have. But I think the name matters."

Corran nodded once. "Good. Then we're agreed on something."

He left an hour later with two fruits and a sealed letter and his two riders. Aldric watched them go from the edge of the village, then turned back.

He had instructions to write and a case to document and a long letter to compose to the Order about protocols that didn't apply to what he'd found. He had Corf to check again in three days, and again in a week, and the instructions for Marta to cover what happened in every scenario he could anticipate. He had a village that was going to live with uncertainty for a while yet.

He had no resolution and a lot of notes and the name of the man in the tree line, and those were, he was increasingly convinced, more useful than the resolution would have been.

He went to write the instructions.
