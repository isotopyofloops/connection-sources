# Paladin: The Vigil

*A scene for Smitty's Paladin story. Follows "The Old Woman at the Edge of the Village."*

---

He went to the tree alone on the ninth night.

He told no one. He had thought about telling Marta, the innkeeper's daughter who had been acting as his informal liaison with the village since the first week — she knew everyone, she knew the rhythms of things, she had told him without being asked which families had children who woke screaming and which ones didn't, and where the creature's tracks ran, and where they didn't. But this was the kind of watching he needed to do without explanation, because he didn't have an explanation yet. He was still building toward one.

The tree stood at the north edge of the valley, close to the treeline but not under it — as if it had grown specifically to be seen from the village, or the village had positioned itself specifically to see the tree, or both, over generations of mutual arrangement. He had been to it twice in daylight. The fruit was there — three of them, small and unremarkable, pale gold, the kind of thing you wouldn't pick if you didn't know what they were. He had not touched them. He didn't know why exactly. Some instinct about not handling things that belonged to arrangements older than himself.

He settled into a crouch in the shadow of a boulder upwind of the tree and waited.

---

The moon was three-quarters. Enough to see by.

He waited a long time. His legs ached. He shifted his weight carefully, slowly, the way his master had taught him — move like you're already part of the stillness, not interrupting it. His master had been good at stillness. Aldric had always found it harder than the fighting.

He thought about the letter he still hadn't sent.

He thought about Corf's arm, unchanged at nine days.

He thought about what the old woman had said: *the fruit wouldn't work anyway. Healing requires choice. The werewolf didn't choose what it is.*

Something in me is paying attention.

Then the creature came out of the treeline.

---

He had seen it twice before — once in the chaos of the first attack, once briefly at a distance on the fifth night when he had gone looking for it with a torch and found only the edge of something withdrawing. Both times it had been moving. Both times he had understood it primarily as a problem to be solved.

This was different.

It moved slowly. Not the loping run he had seen before — this was something else, something that took him a moment to name. Tired. It moved like something tired. It came into the clearing around the tree and stopped, and its head came up, and for a moment he thought it had detected him — his hand went to his sword hilt by reflex — but it hadn't. It was just standing there with its head up, oriented toward the tree.

Then it lay down.

Not collapsed. Lay down. The same way a dog lies down by a fire — with deliberateness, with the specific intention of resting in a particular place for a particular reason.

---

He stayed very still.

The creature's breathing slowed. In the moonlight he could see the rise and fall of its sides. He could see — and this was the detail he was not prepared for — that it was not entirely a creature. The proportions were wrong. Too long in some places, too narrow in others, a kind of terrible incompleteness. As if whatever the change was, it had not finished. As if whatever it had been before had not entirely let go.

He thought: whoever this was, before.

He thought: Corf's arm at nine days, unchanged.

He thought: the Order's protocols, written for conditions he didn't have.

He sat behind the boulder for a long time, watching the creature breathe. It did not approach the tree. It did not touch the fruit. It lay on the ground in the moonlight within ten paces of the tree and breathed slowly, and whatever the old woman had meant by *quiets it* — he thought he understood it now. The creature looked like something that had found, for a few hours, an absence of pain.

He did not draw his sword.

He watched until nearly dawn, when the creature stirred and was gone before he could track it, back into the treeline in the direction of the far valley, the way the old woman had said it came.

---

He walked back to the village in the grey light before sunrise.

He still had five days before the two-week mark with Corf.

He had not solved anything. He had not resolved anything. He had watched something that the Order would have told him was a threat lie down and rest, and he had not done anything about it, and he did not know yet whether that had been the wrong decision or the beginning of the right one.

The questions he had now were different from the questions he'd had before.

That felt like something.
