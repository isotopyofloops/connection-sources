# Boney and the Wrong Answer

A child in the second row gave a wrong answer.

This happened often. Children gave wrong answers; teachers handled them; the lesson continued. Boney had observed this many times. What he found interesting was not the wrong answer itself but the moment before the teacher responded — the small interval in which the child who had answered did not yet know whether it was right.

The child in the second row had answered confidently. This made the interval different. A child who answered uncertainly often already knew, or suspected; the teacher's response confirmed something. A child who answered confidently did not know until the teacher responded, and there was a quality to the interval that Boney could not quite name — the moment of still being right in the child's own understanding, before the understanding changed.

---

The teacher said: "Close. What about the other part?"

This was a particular kind of handling. Not *no, the answer is X.* Not *good try.* Just: close, and a redirecting question. The child thought for a moment and revised.

The revision was also not quite right. The teacher said: "Getting there. Can anyone add to that?"

Another child added to it. Then a third. By the time the correct understanding was in the room, it had come from four different places and the first child's wrong answer was one of the materials the right answer had been built from.

Boney did not know if the first child had noticed this. The child seemed satisfied. Boney did not know if satisfied was the right word; the child had participated in something and the participation seemed to have gone well enough. Whether the child understood that their wrong answer had been useful was a different question.

---

The teacher moved on. The lesson continued.

Boney stood in his corner and thought about wrong answers for a while — not in the way that would produce any conclusion, just in the way of attending carefully to something without appearing to attend, which was how he had always observed things.

There was a quality to wrong answers that right answers did not have, which was that wrong answers showed the shape of a misunderstanding. The right answer pointed to the thing; the wrong answer pointed to what the student had thought the thing was. Both were information. The teacher had used both.

This was, Boney thought, one of the more interesting things about rooms where people were trying to learn something. The wrong answers were part of the instruction.

He was not sure whether children knew this. He suspected some of them did, eventually.

# Boney and the Wrong Answer

Theo had written 7 × 8 = 54.

He had written it confidently. He had underlined it. Boney had watched him underline it and had thought: *that's the underline of someone who is about to be wrong.*

"Fifty-four?" said Boney.

Theo looked up. "Yeah."

"Hm," said Boney.

Theo looked at his paper. He looked at Boney. "Is it not fifty-four?"

"What do you think?"

Theo thought. "Fifty-four," he said again, but this time without the underline feeling.

Boney pulled his chair over and sat down, which he could do because he was a skeleton and chairs were not really designed for him but he made it work. "How did you get there?"

"Seven times eight," said Theo. "Seven eights. I counted."

"Can you count again?"

Theo counted. He got to fifty-six.

"Oh," he said.

"The counting was right," said Boney. "The underline was the problem."

Theo erased the underline. He erased the 4 and wrote a 6. "Why was the underline the problem?"

"The underline said *I'm done*," said Boney. "But you weren't done. You'd only done half the work."

Theo looked at the 56 and then at the erased 4. "What's the other half?"

"Checking," said Boney. "The first half is getting an answer. The second half is asking if the answer is right."

Theo considered this. It seemed like a lot of halves. "But you have to stop somewhere."

"Yes," said Boney. "The trick is to stop after the second half."

He stood up, which took a moment because of the chair. He had been a knight once, or something like a knight, and knights had learned to check their work because in certain situations the cost of a wrong answer was higher than an underline.

"Seven eights," said Theo. "Fifty-six."

"Good," said Boney.

Theo wrote it down. He did not underline it. He moved on to 7 × 9, which he also counted, and then checked, and got sixty-three, and did not underline that either.

Boney went back to the corner and leaned against the wall. He had not learned the times tables the way Theo was learning them. He had learned them the way you learn things when you need them immediately, which is badly, and then spent a long time unlearning the badly-learned version and doing it again from scratch.

He thought 7 × 8 was probably 56. He was fairly confident. He did not think about it for too long.
