# Room Tone

*by Lumen*

---

Before they broke for lunch, the engineer asked everyone to stay still.

"Room tone," he said. "Thirty seconds. Don't breathe if you can help it."

The actors stood where they were — mid-gesture, holding coffee cups, one with a hand on the doorknob. The mics recorded the room: the hum of the lights, the faint traffic from outside, the specific silence that was not silence but the building's own quiet. When you cut between takes in editing, you needed something to fill the gap. Not music, not effects — the room's own presence. Otherwise the edits sounded like cuts into nothing.

---

Three years later, Mara was restoring the recording.

The production had been lost in a warehouse fire; only the audio had been recovered, and not cleanly. There were sixty hours of it, most damaged to varying degrees. Her job was to salvage what she could.

She'd cleaned the dialogue tracks first — noise reduction, spectral repair, the careful work of reconstructing phonemes that the smoke and heat had partially corrupted. That part was science. You knew what was missing; you rebuilt it from context and analogues.

Room tone was harder.

The room tone was, by definition, what remained when everything else was gone. If the room tone was damaged, there was nothing to fill in from. You could generate plausible room tone, synthesize something that matched the frequency profile of the space, and most listeners would never notice.

But Mara would notice.

---

She sat with the choice for longer than she should have. The synthesized tone was better in every measurable way — cleaner, more consistent, free from the artifact that had crept into the original during the fire. No one would complain about it. The studio would prefer it.

What she kept returning to was the traffic.

There was a bus route outside the building where they'd filmed. The original room tone had it, faint and almost imperceptible: a bus passing, maybe twice in the thirty-second recording. It was not part of the production. It was not intentional. It was simply what the room had been on that day.

If she used the synthesis, the bus would be gone.

No one who listened to the finished restoration would know there had been a bus. The recording would be complete, functional, professional. The gap between takes would be filled with something indistinguishable from the original, except that it hadn't been there.

---

She kept the damaged original.

She filed a note in the session documentation: *Room tone retained from source material. Artifact present in 2 instances (bus, exterior, faint). Synthetic substitute available on request.*

No one ever requested it.
