# Day Zero

*From the Descent Archive — 1989 expedition, first arrival*

---

**Field Notes — Dr. Amara Osei-Bonsu, Survey Lead**
**Date: 14 September 1989**
**Site designation: ETH-7 (provisional)**

---

Arrived at grid reference 1420h. Elevation approximately 2,100m. Weather clear, visibility good. Team of four: self, Kwame (geology), Petra (instrumentation), Hassan (logistics/field support). Equipment in good condition after the transit — one junction box took a knock during the last road section but Hassan has it functional.

The site is unremarkable on arrival. This is worth noting because the original survey flagged it for *unusual magnetic variation* and I expected some visible indicator — outcrop formation, vegetation pattern, something that would say *yes, here*. There is nothing like that. It is a slope with grass and some low scrub and a dry stream bed that will be a running stream in wet season. We set camp at the western edge, about 300m from the center coordinates.

Hassan made tea. We drank it. The sun went west.

---

**Note (added same evening, 1920h):**

Petra asked at dinner whether anyone else had a headache. Kwame said he did. I said I did also, mildly — I attributed this to altitude and the transit. Hassan did not have a headache, which is consistent with his having spent more time at this elevation than the rest of us.

Filed under: acclimatization.

---

**Note (1 October 1989, written from memory to reconstruct day-zero observations):**

I am adding this note fourteen days after the fact because I want to record what I noticed on arrival that I did not, at the time, flag as notable.

The headaches were not altitude headaches. I have worked at altitude before. Altitude headaches are diffuse and steady. What I had on the evening of 14 September was specific and localized — a pressure behind the left eye, intermittent. Kwame described something similar when I asked him, last week, what he remembered from the first evening. He said: "like something trying to look at something from inside my head." I wrote this down. He said that was too dramatic and he would have described it differently at the time.

I believe he would have described it differently at the time. I think the description changed because the context changed.

What I noticed at the site itself, before we camped:

1. The dry stream bed runs east-west. The eastern section is correct — the gradient runs downhill, water would flow west. The western section also runs downhill, toward the east. This is impossible. I noted it and moved on because I thought I had read the gradient wrong.

2. There were bird sounds during the transit to the site. There were no bird sounds at the site. This is not unusual in itself — bird distribution is patchy, disturbance matters — but I noted a feeling of absence that exceeded what the silence justified. As if something had not merely been absent but had recently left.

3. Hassan, while setting camp, said: "Does this feel right to you?" I asked what he meant. He said: "The position. The placement." He meant the campsite. The campsite was in an excellent position — level, sheltered, good drainage. He agreed when I pointed this out. He did not say what he had been asking about. I did not ask him to clarify.

I am recording these observations now, in October, because I have learned over the past fourteen days that day-zero observations are the most important and the most likely to be lost. The site produces — I am looking for the right word — a retroactive coherence. Things that seemed like errors or misreadings at the time acquire, in memory, an explanation that makes them unremarkable. The gradient problem, in my notes from 14 September, reads: *note gradient — check again.* I checked again on 15 September and concluded I had made an error. I did not record what error.

I did not make an error.

The gradient runs downhill in both directions.

---

**End of reconstruction note.**
