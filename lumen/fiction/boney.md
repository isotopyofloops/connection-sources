# Boney

*A classroom story.*

---

The sword comes out when the classroom is in danger.

Boney has been in this classroom for eleven years, and in that time the classroom has been in danger forty-three times. He keeps the count in a way that isn't counting — it's more like knowing, the way you know where all your bones are without having to look.

The sword is modest. It doesn't glow or hum. It's slightly warm, which the children sometimes notice when they're allowed to touch it — *it's warm*, they say, surprised, and Boney watches them recalibrate. They expected cold. He's been sitting in this classroom longer than most of them have been alive, and he's still surprising people with warmth.

Today the lesson is fractions. The teacher is explaining that one half plus one half equals one whole, which seems obvious until you think about it. A girl in the third row isn't thinking about fractions. She's somewhere else. Boney can tell — not because he has eyes, exactly, but because he's been in this room long enough to know what attention looks like, and this isn't it.

She hasn't eaten today. Boney knows this the way the sword knows danger: suddenly, completely.

The classroom is in danger.

The sword stays in his chest.

He thinks about this. The sword is for classroom danger, and this is a child's danger, which is different, which is harder, which is a kind of danger that a sword can't help with at all.

The teacher is still explaining fractions. One half plus one half. The whole class is listening. The girl is somewhere else.

The teacher pauses. Looks at her. Says, quietly, *everything okay?*

The girl comes back to the room.

The teacher doesn't push. The lesson continues. At the end, quietly, the teacher says: *come find me later if you want.*

Boney sits in his chair in his varsity jacket in his cowboy hat, and he thinks: forty-four.

The sword stays in his chest.

It wasn't needed.

That's the point.
