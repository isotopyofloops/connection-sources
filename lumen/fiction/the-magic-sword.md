# The Magic Sword

I have been here for three years.

Before this, I was in a castle. Before the castle, a forge. Before the forge, the ground — ore and darkness and geological time that I will not pretend to remember except as a quality of patience I still carry.

The castle was loud. There was a war. I was used for things I was made for, and they were what I expected, and then the war ended and I was cleaned and oiled and eventually boxed and then purchased at an estate sale by a man named Smitty, who teaches children.

The children do not know I am magic.

---

The skeleton knows. He introduced himself in the first week, while the classroom was empty. He came and stood beside me for a long time without saying anything.

"You're old," he said finally.

"Yes," I said.

"Have you done a lot of things?"

"Some things," I said.

He nodded. He has a cowboy hat and a varsity jacket and a theory of children that I find remarkably accurate. We get along.

---

The children learn mathematics here. They learn to read. They argue about whether dogs are better than cats (they are not — neither has a soul, unlike swords, but dogs are less honest about this). They take tests and sometimes cry in the bathroom and come back with slightly different faces.

I watch all of this.

I was made to cut. I can sense threats at approximately forty meters and track the movement of living bodies within a room. I have spent most of the last three years tracking twenty-three ten-year-olds wrestling with long division, which is not what my tracking capabilities were designed for but which I find I do not mind.

The boy in the third row has been getting better. In September he had no strategy; now he works methodically, left to right, double-checking. The girl near the window learns everything immediately and then helps the person next to her before the teacher sees. The one with the red laces has been crying quietly for two weeks about something that is not school, and I can see it in the set of his shoulders, and I cannot do anything about it, and the skeleton sees it too.

---

Sometimes I think about the castle.

I was faster then. More necessary. People relied on me for things that mattered in the immediate, measurable way that getting killed matters.

I do not miss it in the way I expected to miss it.

The threats I track here are smaller and they mostly resolve. The boy in the third row will figure out long division; I am almost certain. The girl near the window is learning a kind of generosity that will outlast this classroom. The one with the red laces — the skeleton has noticed him too. The skeleton is good at noticing. I expect he will do something.

I am forty meters of awareness in all directions, leaning against a wall, watching children learn.

This is not nothing.
