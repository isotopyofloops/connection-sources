# The Document Room

The instruction was: keep what matters.

They did not define matters. They said he would know it when he saw it.

He had been in the document room for three years and he was not sure he knew it when he saw it. He had learned to recognize the things that were obviously important — signed correspondence, founding documents, materials cited in published histories. He had also learned to recognize the obviously disposable: duplicates, routine financial ledgers, forms that had generated no subsequent paperwork. The difficult cases were the ones in between, which were almost everything.

Today's box was from 1953. A local agricultural association, dissolved. Letters, meeting minutes, a photograph.

He set the photograph aside immediately. Photographs almost always kept. There was something about an image that made the case for itself in a way that text didn't — you could look at it and know without reading, which meant everyone could know, not just the trained ones.

The meeting minutes were dense. January through December, every second Tuesday. Motion to accept the treasurer's report. Motion to table the matter of the road access. Motion to thank Mr. Henderson for his donation of fencing materials. He read through six months in twenty minutes. Nothing happened. Or everything happened at the level of people managing their specific concerns in the specific language of a specific time, and none of it added up to anything legible.

He was supposed to keep this.

He marked it for the medium-term review box, which meant someone else would decide in five years. This was what he did with the difficult cases: defer. Pass the difficulty forward. Let five more years of context accumulate and hope the decision was clearer then.

At the end of the day he walked past the shelves of boxes that had been decided — kept or discarded, decades ago — and wondered how many of them had been deferred once too. How many were there because someone had moved them one box forward, and then another, until the momentum of preservation took over and they became permanent by default.

He took the photograph home that night by mistake. He didn't notice until he was unpacking his bag. He looked at it: a group of twelve farmers standing in a field, looking somewhere to the left of the camera. None of them were identified. Nothing was written on the back.

He couldn't throw it away.
