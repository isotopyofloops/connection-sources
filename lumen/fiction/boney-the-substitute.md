# Boney and the Substitute

The substitute was called Mr. Parrish. He arrived eight minutes before the children, looked around the classroom once, and moved two chairs.

He did not move Boney.

Boney noted this. Some substitutes moved him immediately — a quick startled look, a hand on the stand, relocation to somewhere less prominent. Some substitutes covered him with a coat or bag. Some substitutes simply did not look at his corner for the entire day, which was a different kind of moving.

Mr. Parrish looked at Boney once, said nothing, and began organizing his folder.

---

The children came in and Boney watched them register Mr. Parrish. The registering happened quickly, the way children's registering always happened quickly: a scan of the room, a check for what was different, a recalibration. Several of them checked Boney after checking Mr. Parrish, as if confirming that Boney at least was unchanged. He was unchanged.

"Right," said Mr. Parrish. "I'm Mr. Parrish. Your teacher has left work for you."

A child in the third row — not one of the usual first-row questioners — said: "What's wrong with his sword?"

Mr. Parrish looked at Boney. He had, Boney thought, been expecting this or something like it.

"Nothing's wrong with his sword," Mr. Parrish said. "It's a magic sword."

The child considered this. "How do you know it's magic?"

"It says so," Mr. Parrish said, "on the tag."

There was no tag. Boney did not have a tag. But the child looked at his corner with the concentration of someone looking for a tag, and several other children also looked, and the discussion moved on in the way that classroom discussions moved on: not resolved, just relocated elsewhere.

---

At lunch, Mr. Parrish sat at the teacher's desk and ate a sandwich and looked at Boney.

"I've had worse placements," he said.

This was not directed at Boney specifically. It was the kind of thing people said to empty rooms. Boney was used to being in empty rooms with people who said things to empty rooms.

He stood in his corner, varsity jacket, cowboy hat, magic sword, and was present in the way he was always present, which was entirely.

---

In the afternoon, a child spilled a cup of water reaching across a desk. The water went on another child's worksheet, and for a moment the room had the quality of rooms about to become difficult. Mr. Parrish moved quickly — not panicked, just quick — and had paper towels and the worksheet dried and two children placated before the moment could develop into anything.

Boney watched this. Mr. Parrish had done it before. He had the specific competence of someone who had been in many rooms where things were about to go wrong and had learned to move before they did. It was a different competence from the regular teacher's, who also had it but expressed it differently — more anticipation, less intervention.

Both worked.

---

At the end of the day, when the children had gone, Mr. Parrish packed his folder and put on his coat. He looked at Boney again before he left.

"Good room," he said.

Boney had no opinion on whether Mr. Parrish would return. He probably wouldn't. Substitutes usually didn't. But some did, once, and recognized the room from before, and the room recognized them back in the way rooms did, which was by being the same room.

Boney would be here either way.
