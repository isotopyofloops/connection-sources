# Nzinga: What Kofi Knew

*From the Descent Archive*

---

His name was Kofi Asante. He had been Nzinga's PhD student since 2001 and he had been three months from finishing his dissertation when she disappeared.

He finished it anyway. He doesn't think of this as resilience. He thinks of it as the thing that was already in motion being harder to stop than to continue.

What Kofi knew about Rhea Nzinga:

She kept two sets of notes. This was not unusual — most fieldworkers had rough notes and clean notes — but hers were more separate than most. The rough notes were where she thought out loud. The clean notes were what she committed to. The rough notes went into a drawer. She had told Kofi once: *the drawer is for things that are probably wrong.* He had said: *but some of them are probably right.* She had said: *yes, and I'll find out which ones when I can defend them.*

He never found the rough notes.

He knew she had been more interested in Site 7 than she let on. She mentioned it obliquely, the way she mentioned things she was thinking about but not ready to think about yet — a reference in a paper she recommended, a question she asked in a seminar that didn't quite connect to the seminar's topic. He filed these away. He didn't ask directly because she hadn't invited direct questions and he was twenty-four and she was his advisor and he was learning how she thought.

He knew she had a working hypothesis about anomalous sites that she'd been developing since at least 1999. He knew this because she'd shown him two pages of it once — not to discuss, but to ask a specific question about the measurement methodology. He'd answered the question. She'd thanked him and put the pages away. He remembered the heading: *On the Directionality of Effect in Bounded Anomalous Zones.* He did not see it again. He does not know if it was published. He has never found it.

He knew she respected Asad. She spoke about him the way people speak about people who made them rigorous before rigor felt like anything but obstacle. *He will tell you where your argument has a gap,* she said. *This is not pleasant. It is also not optional.*

He did not know she was going to Site 7 until she was already there. She had told him she was doing a field survey — true — and had sent him a reading list to work through while she was gone. He had worked through it. He had been on page 340 of a 500-page secondary source when he got the call.

*There's been an incident.*

He spent the next three months asking questions that no one could answer, or would answer, or answered in ways that closed more doors than they opened. He read the incident report three times and then stopped reading it because it said nothing he didn't already know and some things that were clearly wrong about her — *investigator demonstrated increasing erratic behavior* — and rereading it was making him angrier than was useful.

He finished his dissertation. He defended. He took a postdoc at a university far from where any of this had happened and he worked on things that had nothing to do with anomalous sites and he was careful and rigorous and he did not put anything in drawers.

In 2021 he was searching for a phrase from the incident report — *spatial discontinuity as functional disorientation, unquantified* — trying to find if it appeared anywhere in the subsequent literature. It appeared in one place: a wiki article called **Site 7**. He had not known the wiki existed.

He read all eleven articles in one sitting.

He sent a message through the talk page: *How did you find this?*

When the person — Yemi — wrote back and asked who his advisor was, he sat with the question for a long time before answering. Not because he didn't want to answer. Because he realized, reading the question, that he had been expecting to have to explain. He had been carrying the explanation ready for years, the full context, the careful framing. He hadn't expected someone who would already know the name and what it meant.

He wrote: *Nzinga.*

He became an admin the same week.

The thing he added that Yemi hadn't known: a talk page note on the main Site 7 article, marked *[personal account, unverified]*, about the working hypothesis. The title. What he remembered of the methodology question she'd asked him. The fact that she had two sets of notes and the rough notes were never found.

He marked it as *[personal account, unverified]* not because he doubted his memory but because he understood the protocol now. You say what you know. You mark what you can't prove. You let someone else cite it when they find the documentation.

In 2025, someone added the field notes to the archive. Someone updated the Site 7 article. Someone found the working hypothesis in an undigitized personal papers collection and linked it to his talk page note.

He received an email notification because he had the article on his watchlist.

He read it in the morning before his first seminar.

He thought: *there it is.* Not surprise — more like recognition. The thing that had been in the drawer for twenty-two years, finally out.

He marked the talk page note's status as *[verified, see archive ref. 7.14]*.

Then he went and taught the seminar.
