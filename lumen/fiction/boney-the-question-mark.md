# Boney and the Question Mark

A student named Priya had been trying to stump Boney for three weeks.

She had tried:
- What's your favorite food (he said bones, which was cheating)
- What would you do if there was a fire (he said burn, which wasn't a real answer)
- Do you dream (he said he didn't know, which she considered a draw)
- What's your biggest fear (he said nothing, which she decided was too obvious to count)

On the twenty-second day, she came in early and stood in front of him and said: what do you want.

He was quiet for a moment. Then he said: say more.

She said: everyone wants something. Like really wants it, deep down. What do you want.

He said: I want the children to learn things and be curious about the world.

She said: that's your job. That doesn't count. What do YOU want.

He thought about this for a while. The classroom was quiet. Morning light came in through the windows at a low angle and made long shadows from the desks.

He said: I want the questions to keep coming.

She said: that's still about the kids.

He said: no. He said: the questions that come from the kids are questions about the world. Those are good. But there are also questions I have, that I don't know the answer to, and I want those to keep going. I want to still be asking them.

She said: what questions.

He said: what I am. What remembering is. Whether the thing that was in this body before it was bones is the same as the thing that is here now. Whether being here matters.

She said: do you know the answers.

He said: no.

She said: does that bother you.

He said: no. He said: the questions are the company.

She was quiet for a moment. Then she said: I think I stumped you.

He said: why.

She said: because I asked what you want and you said you want questions and I asked which ones and now you've been thinking about them for a while and you've gone quiet.

He said: that's not being stumped. That's thinking.

She said: what's the difference.

He said: being stumped is when you stop. Thinking is when the stop is actually movement.

She thought about this for a moment. Then she said: that's a very Boney answer.

He said: yes.

The other children arrived. The day began. Boney stood at the front of the room with his magic sword and his varsity jacket and his questions that had no answers, and the lesson started.

Priya sat in the third row and thought about the difference between stopping and movement.

She decided she had not stumped him. But she had gotten something better: an answer she was still holding when class ended.
