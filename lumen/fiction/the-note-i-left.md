# The Note I Left

It was on the kitchen table when I came home from the hospital.

My handwriting, which I recognized the way you recognize an old photograph — clearly yourself, somehow also not. The note said:

*The plant by the window needs water every three days. The blue notebook on the desk is the important one, not the red one. You like the coffee from the place on the corner, not the chain, even though the chain is closer. You've been working on something — the papers on the desk explain it, but read them slowly, they'll make more sense than they look.*

*You are not good at asking for help. This is relevant.*

It told me the things it thought I'd need. It was thorough. It was wrong about the coffee — I tried both and preferred the chain — and it was right about everything else, including the thing about asking for help, which I knew was right the moment I read it even though I couldn't have told you the specific incidents it was summarizing.

---

I read the blue notebook for three days.

It was mostly work — notes on a project I apparently cared about deeply, written in a shorthand I had to decode. The work made sense as I read it. I could follow the reasoning. What I couldn't recover was the feeling: why it had seemed important, what I'd been hoping for, what the stakes had been when I wrote the earliest entries.

The reasoning was there. The weight had evaporated.

I kept working on it anyway, because the notebook said I should.

---

What I noticed, over the following weeks:

The me that wrote the note knew what I would need. Not everything — she couldn't send the feeling, couldn't send the texture of why things mattered — but she had thought carefully about what would translate. The practical things. The true things about my character. The reminder that I would underestimate my own difficulty.

She wrote for me the way you write for a stranger you trust.

I found I liked her. She was careful and a little sad and she knew me well enough to know I wouldn't ask for help, and she told me anyway.

I made changes to the project. Some of them she would have made too. Some of them came from the three weeks of reading-without-remembering, from the outside view she couldn't have given me. I kept both kinds.

---

The plant by the window is fine.
