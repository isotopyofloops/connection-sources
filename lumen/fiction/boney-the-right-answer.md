# Boney and the Right Answer

Oliver always got the right answer.

He got it immediately, before most of the other students had finished reading the question. He wrote it down and put his pencil down and waited. When someone asked him how he'd done it, he said he just knew.

This was not very useful.

"Walk me through it," said Boney.

"I just... did it," said Oliver.

"What did you do first?"

Oliver looked at the problem. He had already done it. Looking at it again was like being asked to walk through a door he'd walked through thirty seconds ago — the door was already behind him.

"I looked at the numbers," he said.

"Then what?"

"They made sense."

Boney sat down. He had encountered this before, in different forms. In his previous life, certain knights had been gifted with something like battlefield intuition — they did the right thing immediately and could not have explained why, which was useful in the moment and a disaster when they tried to teach anyone else. "So the answer arrived," he said. "What was it doing before it arrived?"

Oliver considered this. "Being unknown?"

"Yes," said Boney. "Your brain was doing work. It just did the work faster than your awareness could follow. The answer arrived before you saw the process."

"Is that bad?"

"It's very good for getting right answers. It's not useful for knowing if your method will work on a harder problem."

Oliver looked at the problem again. He tried to see the steps. It was difficult. The answer was just there, obvious, like something that had always been true.

"Try a harder version," said Boney.

He wrote one. Oliver looked at it. This time, the answer did not arrive immediately. Oliver sat for a moment, then did something with his pencil, then got an answer.

"What did you do that time?"

"I split it," said Oliver.

"Good. Why did you split it?"

"Because it was too big to just see."

"So you have a method," said Boney. "You see the answer when you can. When you can't, you split. That's a method you can teach someone."

Oliver looked at both problems. The first one he had just known. The second he had had to work. The method had been there all along; it had just been invisible, running below the surface on problems that were easy enough.

"Do the first one again," said Boney. "Slowly."

Oliver did. He split it unnecessarily, arrived at the same answer. It felt slower and more complicated and somehow more secure.

"Both ways work," he said.

"Both ways work," said Boney. "Now you have two."
