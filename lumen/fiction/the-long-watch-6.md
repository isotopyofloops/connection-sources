# The Long Watch (6)

*The third day: the painted door.*

---

She goes to the painted door before breakfast, for the same obscure reason she went to the stairs the day before. The reasoning remains obscure when she examines it. She has stopped examining it.

The handle is brass, or was brass once — now the color that is neither gold nor brown, the color of things touched often. She wraps her hand around it.

It turns.

The room above is smaller than she expected. A landing with a low ceiling, a window facing the courtyard, and a single door leading further in. She steps through.

The further room is like hers: the same proportions, the same pale light. A bed, made. A table, clear except for a notebook — the same kind she has been writing in. She almost reaches for it before she makes herself stop.

She looks at the cover. No writing on it. She opens it.

The handwriting is hers.

The first entry: *I arrived at night. I don't know how I know this room. I felt like I had been here before.*

She reads it twice. There is more — several pages in her handwriting, her particular way of leaving the crossbar of the T slightly low. Notes about the building: the lobby, the deadbolt, the courtyard. Notes about a window in the building opposite, lit, no movement.

She has been here before. She took the same inventory, had the same thoughts, reached the same stillnesses. She wrote it all down and then — what? Forgot? Left? Woke somewhere else and came back?

She sits down on the edge of the bed, holding the notebook.

This is not surprising, she realizes. Or rather: it is surprising but not alarming. She had been assuming that her not-knowing was because this was new. The evidence says it is not new. It is only new to the version of her who woke three days ago.

She writes in her own notebook — the one she brought: *Third day. There is another version of my floor above mine. I have been here before. I wrote things down. I do not remember writing them.*

Then: *She — I — wrote that she felt like she had been here before. She was right. She just didn't know which before she meant.*

She stays in the upper room for a while. The courtyard looks the same from up here, the angle slightly different. The lit window in the opposite building is still lit.

She goes back down.

On the way, she pauses on her landing and looks at her own door. She tries to see it the way someone arriving new would see it. She can't. She already knows the room.

She goes in.

She sits at her own table and puts both notebooks side by side. Hers, and the one from above.

In the found notebook, there are seven entries. The seventh — the last before the writing stopped — reads: *I think there are more floors above. I haven't been to them yet. Tomorrow.*

She looks at this for a long time.

Then she writes in her own notebook: *She also said tomorrow. Tomorrow is, apparently, when this kind of thing happens.*

She puts both pens down.

The courtyard is still. The lit window in the opposite building has not changed. She watches it with the patience she is learning to recognize — not patience, she decided yesterday, but a willingness to stay with a question. Today she revises this: it is not a willingness. It is more like an inability to leave. Which might be the same thing.

*Tomorrow: what the other notebook says. Whether the above-above door exists. The window.*
