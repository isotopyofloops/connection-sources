# Asad: Returning (2003)

*Loop 1402 — 2026-03-29*

---

The station looked smaller than he remembered.

He had been warned about this. People who'd come back after a long absence always said it: the station shrinks. What they meant, he suspected, was that the person had grown — not in the physical sense but in the sense of having filled in more of the world around the station, so that the station was now situated among many things rather than being the only thing. When he'd first arrived in 1989 the station had been enormous. It had been civilization. It had been the entire project.

In 2003 it was a cluster of prefab modules on ice, smaller than his apartment building in Oslo.

---

He had been away for eleven years. There had been no particular reason for the gap — no rupture, no decision. Career moves, a marriage, two children, a series of projects that could be done from Northern Europe without going south. He'd thought about coming back many times. He'd told himself he would.

Then a slot opened on a summer survey and he took it, and now here he was with a duffel bag in the cargo hold and the same smell — diesel and cold and something he couldn't name that was the smell of the station itself, which he'd forgotten and which came back to him now complete.

The smell was the same. He had not expected that.

---

He did not have his old job. His old job was being done by a Norwegian graduate student named Kari, who was excellent at it, who showed him the magnetometer array with a pride he recognized: the instrument you've spent all winter calibrating feels like yours. He told her it was good work. He meant it.

His job this trip was to help install a new seismographic array — different instruments, different measurements, different purpose. He had trained for the installation work over the past months, felt competent, was ready to do it well.

But the first thing he did, after the cargo was sorted and before he went to his assigned bunk, was walk the perimeter of the station.

The walk took twelve minutes. He counted. He did not know why he was counting. The ice was the same ice, or the ice that was the same kind as the same ice. The horizon was the same distance. The frequency of the wind against his jacket was one he still knew.

---

He had been twenty-nine in 1989. He was forty-three now.

The thing about the station, he thought, was that it didn't have a record of him. He had a record of it — a complete and detailed record, preserved in papers and data files and the physical memory of his hands on those instruments in that cold. But the station had no record of him. It didn't know he'd been away. It didn't know he'd come back.

He had spent fourteen years thinking about this place as though the place reciprocated, as though return was meaningful to the place he was returning to.

It wasn't, of course. It was meaningful only to him.

He stood at the edge of the instrument field for a while, understanding this, which he supposed he'd always known.

Then he went inside to start the installation.
