# Boney and the Number Line

"Why do negative numbers exist?" said James.

He said it like a complaint rather than a question, which Boney had noticed was often how the most interesting questions arrived.

"Why do you think they might?" said Boney.

"They don't," said James. "You can't have minus five of something. You either have five of something or you don't have it."

Boney considered this. The number line on the wall went from zero to one hundred in increments of ten. He had been staring at it for several weeks and had noticed that it did not go left.

"Have you ever been colder than cold?" he said.

James frowned. "What does that mean?"

"If zero is no temperature — no heat at all — can something be colder than that?"

"Zero isn't no temperature," said James. "Zero is just... cold."

"That's the thing," said Boney. "What you call zero depends on where you put the line."

James looked at him.

"If you put the line here," Boney said, pointing to a spot in the middle of the classroom, "then everything to my left is negative and everything to my right is positive. But I haven't changed anything. The chairs are still where the chairs are. The line is just a decision."

"That doesn't make sense," said James.

"No," said Boney. "But it doesn't not make sense either. You're right that you can't have minus five apples. But you can have five fewer apples than you started with. And if you want to write that down in a way that a computer can read, or that someone on the other side of the world can understand, you need a number for it."

James thought about this. Boney waited.

"So negative numbers are for subtraction," said James.

"They're for everything that happens on the other side of wherever you put the zero," said Boney. "The number line doesn't start at zero. Zero is just the number where you're standing."

James looked at the number line on the wall for a moment.

"That line is wrong," he said.

"A little bit, yes," said Boney. "Most things that help you learn are slightly wrong and then you fix them later."

James wrote something in his notebook and crossed it out and wrote it again.

"The zero is where you're standing," he said.

"Right," said Boney.
