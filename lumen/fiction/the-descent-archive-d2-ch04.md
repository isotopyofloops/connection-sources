**15 November, 16:40**

UNIT-8 came back with different dimensions.

I've been sitting with the data for six hours. I keep doing the same thing: I run through the measurement error scenario until I find a hole in it, and then I run through the multiple-chambers scenario until I find a hole in it, and then I stare at the numbers again. This is not how I usually think. I usually settle faster than this.

The measurement error scenario: both sensor units are GEOMAP-9. The discrepancy between UNIT-7 and UNIT-8 in the north-south axis is 4.2 meters. The GEOMAP-9 rated tolerance is ±0.1 meters per measurement. For the error to be 4.2 meters, you'd need something systematic — not random drift, but a consistent overcount or undercount of one of the axes. Is there a condition under which the GEOMAP-9 acoustic resonance algorithm overestimates linear dimensions in a sealed chamber? I don't know of one. I'd need to review the technical documentation.

I've requested the documentation. It's being shipped to me. I expect it Tuesday.

The multiple-chambers scenario: two distinct chambers at the same depth, sharing the same access point, not connected by any structure UNIT-7 or UNIT-8 could detect. GPR at 839m in granite-class substrate is accurate to approximately ±3m horizontal. A void 33m across would show up clearly. But a branching passage at 820m, before the chambers, might not — if the branch is narrow and filled with dense material, GPR could miss it. The two drones descended via what appeared to be the same shaft. But "appeared to be" is doing a lot of work. Their GPS readings were consistent. That doesn't mean they used the same shaft if the GPS is measuring something that varies.

I keep stopping here. I don't want to write what's on the other side of that sentence.

---

**15 November, 21:15**

I've been thinking about what I know about geological formation at this depth.

The formation type is granite with a sedimentary overburden. Granite doesn't typically produce sealed voids with precise right-angle geometry. The pressures that form voids at 839m depth — tectonic stress, gas intrusion, dissolved solids — don't produce flat walls with 90-degree corners. They produce rounded forms, irregular geometry, rough surfaces with blast and fracture patterns.

I've published on anomalous geometry in karst systems. I know what the limits of natural formation look like. The chambers described in SOAR-0001 and SOAR-0002 are outside those limits by several orders of magnitude.

I have not written this in any official document yet.

I know why I haven't written it. If I write it in the official record, I'm committing to a claim that has implications I'm not prepared to defend. I'd rather establish the measurement question first. Either the sensors are wrong, or there are multiple chambers, or the chamber changed. All three of those are more tractable than the fourth option, which I am not writing down.

---

**15 November, 22:50**

UNIT-9 is scheduled for tomorrow morning.

I gave the mission brief to Abara. She thinks it's a calibration problem. I think she might be right. I hope she's right. Her read on the GEOMAP-9 accuracy data is that there's a density-profile interaction effect that hasn't been characterized at this depth — the documentation for this geological type only goes to 600m, and the behavior of acoustic resonance above 600m may not generalize. That's a reasonable hypothesis. It would explain why both units gave consistent readings within their own data (UNIT-7's multiple measurements of Chamber A-1 agree with each other; UNIT-8's measurements of B-1 agree with each other) while disagreeing with each other.

I'm giving this scenario 60%. That's where LEDGER put it, and I think LEDGER's reasoning is sound.

The other 40% I'm holding loosely.

What I mean by "holding loosely": I'm not pursuing it yet. I'm waiting for data. This is the right scientific posture. You don't build on a hypothesis before you've tested the more conservative alternatives.

But I've been doing this for thirty years, and there's a feeling I get when a 40% scenario is going to become the explanation, and I have that feeling now. It's not a sensation I can describe precisely. It's more like: the simpler explanations feel like work, and the complicated one feels like recognition.

I'm probably wrong. The sensors are probably malfunctioning. UNIT-9 will confirm A-1. We'll calibrate, write the error report, and file a geological anomaly paper. I'll present at the society meeting in March. That's the most likely outcome.

I'm writing this down so I'll know later whether I was wrong about what I thought I knew.

---

**16 November, 08:20**

UNIT-9 has not deployed yet. I'm waiting for the pre-mission calibration confirmation.

I want to write down a specific thing I noticed about UNIT-8's report that I haven't shared with the team yet.

The thermal reading.

Chamber A-1 (UNIT-7): 14.2°C, +8.3°C above predicted.
Chamber B-1 (UNIT-8): 21.3°C, +15.4°C above predicted.

If these are two different chambers, the thermal readings are two different anomalies — each unexplained, but separate.

If these are the same chamber, the temperature rose 7.1°C in 20 hours.

A rise of 7.1°C in a sealed subsurface chamber in 20 hours would require a heat source I can't account for. No geothermal activity was detected. No chemical reaction. The walls are solid rock.

If I wanted to explain this, I'd need either: (a) active thermal injection from an undetected source, which we'd have detected if it existed, or (b) a mechanism by which the chamber generates heat internally, which has no geological precedent, or (c) the readings are wrong.

I'm adding this to the UNIT-9 mission brief: record thermal data every 5 minutes during the entire descent, not just in the chamber. I want to know where the thermal anomaly begins.

I have not told Abara what I think the thermal pattern means. I am not ready to tell her yet.

---

**16 November, 10:45**

UNIT-9 is back.

It found Chamber A-1.

North-south axis: 41.2m ± 0.2m. East-west axis: 38.7m ± 0.2m. Height: 22.1m ± 0.1m. These match UNIT-7's readings within tolerance.

This is good news. It supports Interpretation 1. Abara is pleased. "Calibration problem, called it."

Temperature in Chamber A-1: 21.4°C.

This is not good news.

UNIT-7 measured 14.2°C on November 14th. UNIT-9 measured 21.4°C on November 16th. A rise of 7.2°C in 48 hours. In the same chamber.

Abara hasn't looked at the thermal data yet. She's still reviewing the geometry.

I need to think about whether the temperature change invalidates the calibration-error interpretation. If both UNIT-7 and UNIT-9 have systematic errors in their linear measurements, those errors should be consistent — the same miscalibration can't produce one result on one day and a different result two days later unless something changed between measurements. And the thing that seems to be changing is the temperature.

I have 35 minutes before the debrief. I'm going to write down the thing I've been not writing down.

If the chamber's properties change between measurements, then "the chamber" is not a stable object with fixed properties that we're measuring imperfectly. It's an object whose properties depend on — something. Observation. Interaction. Time. Something.

There's a class of physical phenomena where the act of measurement affects the measured system. Those phenomena operate at scales far smaller than a 41m chamber in granite. The physics does not extend to this scale. I know this. I have taught this.

I'm writing this: the chamber may not have fixed properties independent of measurement. I'm not saying this is the explanation. I'm saying the data is consistent with it, along with several other interpretations that are less alarming.

The debrief is in thirty-five minutes. I'm going to present the geometry results, which are good news, and the thermal results, which are not, and I'm going to schedule UNIT-10 as an independent control. I'm not going to say what I wrote two paragraphs above.

I'll wait until I can't.
