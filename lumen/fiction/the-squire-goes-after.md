# The Squire Goes After

She told the messenger to rest and return alone.

He looked at her. He was young and exhausted and probably had instructions that didn't include coming back without the paladin he had been sent to recall. He said: Master Voss?

She said: I'll find him.

He said: The order will want a report.

She said: The order will get one.

He went to sleep in the headman's barn. Lian packed what she needed and was outside the village before he woke.

---

The direction Voss had gone was east, into the forest that ran alongside the river. She had found the torn fabric on the third morning. She had not gone after him then because there was a village behind her. There was still a village behind her, but it was not in danger now — or no more danger than it had been before she arrived, which was the ordinary level of danger that villages managed without paladins.

She was not the only protection it had ever had. She had been a temporary one.

This was not a mission. She had no orders. The mission was over.

She was going anyway.

---

Master Voss had been her master for two years. He had taught her the things he knew: how to read a road, how to stand her ground, how to hold still under pressure until the pressure passed or revealed itself to be something she could face. He had told her about the nights before dawn. He had told her about the second decision.

He had run so he wouldn't hurt her. That was the last clear thing he had done.

She did not know if he was still himself. She did not know what she would find. She had thought about this for nine nights while sitting on the well, not thinking about it, which was the practice she had developed for things she was carrying but couldn't yet put down.

She was going to find him. After that she would see.

---

The forest was different in daylight than it was at night, which was obvious but still had to be learned by moving through it. Quieter, in a way, and also more alive — birds she couldn't name, the sound of the river running somewhere to the left.

She found the first camp he had made after about two hours. The fire was long cold. He had sheltered under a fallen tree and eaten something — she could tell from the stripped branches, the careful arrangement of the bones. He was still capable of sheltering and eating. He had not lost that.

She followed the trail east.

---

Later, when she told the story — and she would eventually tell the story, because things that happen become stories if you survive them — she would say she followed him for three days.

She would not say what she found. Not because it was too hard to say, but because what she found turned out to be the beginning of a different story, and that story wasn't hers to tell alone.

But she went after him. That much was hers.
