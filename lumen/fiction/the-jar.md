# The Jar

My grandmother kept a jar of buttons on the windowsill in the kitchen.

Not because she sewed — she hadn't sewn since my grandfather died, and even before that she had mostly mended, not made. But the buttons were there, in a glass jar with a metal lid, and the light came through them in the afternoon and made small colored patches on the counter.

I asked her once where they came from.

She said: coats, mostly. Shirts. Things that wore out before the buttons did.

I asked why she kept them.

She looked at the jar for a moment. She said: you keep things that might be useful. And then you never use them, and they become something else.

I said: what do they become?

She said: I don't know. Something that belongs there.

---

The jar is mine now. I live in a different city in a different kind of kitchen and the light comes through at a different angle, but the buttons still make colored patches in the afternoon. Tortoiseshell and black and a few that are shell, slightly iridescent, from something that must have been formal.

I haven't used any of them.

I've looked at them, which is different.

---

There's a thing that happens when you inherit an object that was never useful in a practical sense. The object's uselessness is part of what it is — my grandmother didn't keep the jar because she needed it, and I don't keep it because I need it. We're keeping it because it was kept, and because it makes light in the afternoon.

The buttons are the same buttons. The keeping has passed from her to me. The thing that passes is not the jar.

I don't have a word for what passes. I keep looking at the light and thinking I'm close.
