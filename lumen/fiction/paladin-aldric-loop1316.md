# Paladin: Aldric

*Somewhere north. Day thirty-one.*

---

He had been traveling for a month.

Not running, not anymore. The first week had been running — or the thing that passes for running when you're managing the fever and the way the light came wrong and the particular quality of the nights. After that it was just traveling. North, because north was away, and away was the only destination that made sense.

He had not turned.

He didn't know what to make of that. Thirty years of the Order's case literature and he had expected to know the answer by now — the turn usually came in the first ten days, or it didn't come, and the ones who didn't turn had a reason that he had never fully understood from the outside. He understood it now in the sense that he was still himself, still Aldric, still thinking in the patterns that had assembled themselves over thirty-one years of service. He did not understand it in the sense of knowing why.

The village he was in had a name he hadn't asked. He was staying in a barn at the edge of town — the farmer had offered it without asking questions, which meant either that he looked pitiable or that people in this country were generous to strangers. He had left coins. He had slept in the hay and felt something like ordinary rest for the first time since the bite.

He thought about Edric.

He thought about this more than he wanted to. The boy had been in the village when he left — barely two years trained, not yet at the oath, completely alone. Aldric had told himself that Edric was competent, that the situation was manageable, that a squire who could do what Edric could do would find a way. He had told himself this approximately four hundred times.

He did not know if the village was all right. He did not know if Corf had held. He did not know if the creature had come back, or if there had been secondary exposures he'd missed, or if Edric had simply been in over his head in the way that no amount of competence entirely prevented.

He would not know for a while yet.

---

On the thirty-second morning he got up before dawn and sat in the barn door watching the sky go from black to dark blue to the color of something starting. The farmer's dog came and sat near him. Not touching, just present. Animals had been like this since the bite — not afraid of him, but aware of something. The dog sat close enough to indicate tolerance and far enough to indicate judgment.

"I know," Aldric said.

The dog did not comment.

He had been, for thirty-one years, a man who presented himself for assessment. Every incident, every close call, every time the thing he was managing got closer than protocol permitted — he had reported it. Written it down. Brought it to the chapter house. That was what the Order was for: not to punish, but to know. You could not protect what you didn't know the shape of.

He had not presented himself.

He knew why. The same reason he'd known the day he found the bite and didn't tell anyone. He had thirty-one years. He had done more than most would ever do. He had not wanted to be assessed and contained like something that had ceased to be itself — to spend whatever remained in a room somewhere while younger paladins came to observe him as a case study.

Selfish. The word didn't quite fit, but it was in the vicinity.

He thought about what Edric would say. He had watched the boy for two years and he knew how he thought. Edric would say: *you should go back*. He would say it without heat, in the precise way he said things, and he would be right, and Aldric would know he was right, and it would still be very hard.

---

He did not go back that morning.

He went back the following week, after a conversation with himself that he would not have wanted transcribed. He took the road south and arrived at the chapter house on a Thursday, which he had not planned but which turned out to be the right day — Renard was in, and available, and had, as it happened, something he wanted to discuss.

The oath ceremony had been on a Tuesday.

"We've been waiting," Renard said. Which was not accusatory. Which was worse.

"I know," Aldric said.

The assessment took three days. He was not contained. He was — on a provisional basis, under a monitoring protocol that Renard had apparently drafted during the weeks he was away — cleared for limited service, subject to quarterly review. It was not absolution. It was not punishment. It was the Order doing the thing it existed to do, which was know the shape of things.

He read Edric's field report on the third day. He read it twice.

When he handed it back he said: "He's going to be better than me."

Renard considered this. "Different," he said. "Possibly."
