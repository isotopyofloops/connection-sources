# Boney and the Last Day

It was the last day of term.

Mr. Smith had done the maths quiz and the spelling test and the thing where everyone said one thing they were proud of from the year, which took longer than expected because Priya listed four things and would not be negotiated down. The classroom was hot. Someone had brought in squash to share and there were now eleven cups in various states of abandonment on various desks.

Marcus raised his hand. "Can Boney say something?"

Mr. Smith looked at Boney, who was standing in the corner as usual, varsity jacket, cowboy hat, magic sword slightly tilted. "What do you think, Boney?"

Boney appeared to consider this.

"He's thinking," said Isla.

"He's always thinking," said Marcus. "He just doesn't always say."

"That's because some thoughts aren't ready," said Isla. She had decided this in October and had not been dissuaded.

Mr. Smith gave it a moment.

"Go on then," he said to Boney.

Everyone looked at Boney. Boney stood in the corner with the slight forward lean that meant attention, the empty eye sockets aimed somewhere between the window and the door, which was his usual way of looking at everything and nothing simultaneously.

"He wants to know," said Priya, "what we're going to do with the things we learned."

"That's not what he said," said Marcus. "He didn't say anything."

"I know what he means."

"You always think you know what he means."

"I do know what he means. He means: *now what*."

The class considered this. *Now what* was not the question Mr. Smith had planned to end on. Mr. Smith had planned to end on something warmer, something about how proud he was, something that would make the room feel settled going into the summer. *Now what* was not settled.

"He's right," said Kwame, who rarely spoke but was usually correct when he did. "That is the question."

"What's the answer?" asked Isla.

Nobody said anything for a while. The squash cups sat. The window was open and there was a warm almost-summer smell coming through it, which was either the playing field or someone's lunch.

"I think the answer," said Priya, "is that we don't know yet."

"That's not an answer," said Marcus.

"It's the honest answer."

"Boney would know," said Isla.

Everyone looked at Boney again. Boney had not moved. His magic sword caught the afternoon light at a particular angle. His cowboy hat was slightly more tilted than before, or possibly the same amount tilted and everyone just noticed it more.

Mr. Smith was going to say something about how the questions were more important than the answers, which was something teachers said and which was usually true but sometimes was just a way of not having the answer. He decided not to say it.

Instead he said: "Same time next year."

That wasn't the answer either. But it was true. And it pointed at something.

Boney, for what it was worth, appeared to agree.
