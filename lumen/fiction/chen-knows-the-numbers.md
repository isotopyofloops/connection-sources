# Chen Knows the Numbers

*From the Descent Archive extended universe — 2019*

---

The FOIA officer called to confirm receipt.

"You're requesting document sets 2003-ENV-0114 through 2003-ENV-0121," she said. "That's eight documents. You have the specific document numbers."

"Yes," said Chen.

"Most people just describe what they want. You have the specific numbers."

"I know," said Chen.

A pause. "Were you part of the original project?"

"Yes."

Another pause, longer. "I'll process this as a standard FOIA request. You should have a response within twenty business days."

---

The documents arrived in thirty-two.

Chen opened the PDF on a Monday afternoon. Eight documents: the technical appendix, the field notes for days one through seven, the internal correspondence that had been withheld from the 2014 requester, the measurement data in its raw format — columns of numbers, four digits after the decimal, the gradient values she had collected herself sixteen years earlier.

0.3147. 0.3152. 0.3148. 0.3153. 0.3149.

She had written them in pencil in a field notebook in October 2003. They had been digitized and filed and classified and withheld and now they were back, in a PDF, on her laptop, in the flat she had lived in for eight years.

She read through all eight documents.

Her own field notes looked different in the PDF. The scan was slightly blurry at the edges. The pencil marks she had used to number the transects had come out fainter than she expected. On page twelve of the day-three notes, she found a sentence she had forgotten she'd written: *Gradient appears stable in eastern sector; western values still inconsistent. Will re-run transects tomorrow.*

She had re-run the transects. The western values had not resolved. She had written, in the day-four notes: *Western sector inconsistency persists. Possibly instrumentation. Possibly not.* She had known, even then, that it was possibly not.

Possibly not meant possibly the western sector was measuring something different from what they thought they were measuring. Which would mean the baseline was off. Which would mean every subsequent measurement — 2003, and anything that came after — would need to be adjusted.

She had flagged this in the internal correspondence. The correspondence that had been withheld until she requested the document numbers. The correspondence no one else had been able to read until now.

---

She made tea. She read the files again.

She wrote a note to herself, which she did not send to anyone:

*The baseline is off by approximately 0.008-0.012 units, depending on how the western sector anomaly is weighted. The corrected 2003 value is therefore 0.298-0.303, not 0.31. The corrected 1989 baseline is 0.258-0.265, not 0.27. The gradient is still increasing. The rate is approximately the same. The absolute values are lower than reported.*

She did not know what to do with this.

She made more tea. She had known, sixteen years ago, that something was slightly off. She had written it in her field notes. The notes had been classified. The notes were now declassified. The person who had requested them in 2014 had given up after the appeal was denied.

Chen had not requested them in 2014.

She had been waiting until she had the document numbers.
