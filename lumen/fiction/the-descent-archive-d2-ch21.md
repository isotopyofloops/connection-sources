**SOAR-0033 | ABARA'S RECORD, CONTINUED | 01 December 2041 | 13:55**

I've been sitting with the notebook for thirty-three minutes. I want to write down what I've been thinking before I stand up, because once I stand up I'll know what I've decided, and I want the record of the thinking to precede the record of the decision.

The notebook says I will be at the east wall of N-1 at 14:00, not yet decided.

What I'm deciding is not whether to go into the east passage. What I'm deciding is whether my going to the east wall counts as my decision.

This is the question I've been circling for thirty-three minutes.

If I go to N-1 and stand at the east wall, I will be fulfilling what the notebook describes. The notebook's description is potentially constitutive — it is a document in the Archive, and Archive documents are partly constitutive of the structure's state. If the notebook says I will be at the east wall at 14:00 and I go, I have done what was constituted.

If I stay here, I will have defied a constitutive document. The notebook would be inaccurate. The entry would say I was there and I would not have been.

The question is whether defying a constitutive document is possible.

I spent eleven minutes on this.

Here is what I decided: yes, defiance is possible. The DUO documents have been accurate, but accurate is not the same as inevitable. The documents describe what happens. I can choose to do otherwise. If I do otherwise, the document is inaccurate. If the document is inaccurate, the constitutive loop has a break in it. This would be information — new information about whether all DUO documents are constitutive or only some of them.

I spent six minutes deciding whether to defy the notebook as a scientific experiment.

Conclusion: the scientific case for defiance is valid. If I stay here, we learn something about the limits of constitutive force. The data point has value.

But here is what changed my mind.

Milo wrote "I'm going down" and "I'm leaving a blank page here. Then I'll tell you what I find."

There is a blank page in the notebook. I held it up to the light.

The notebook has not told us what Milo found. The blank page is still blank. He went down the shaft and he has not written what he found. Either he is still finding it, or he has no way to write from where he is, or what he found cannot be written.

The notebook has been adding entries for twenty-two days. It added entries describing my morning before I had that morning. It described me reading the sentence I was reading. It has entries through 14:00. It does not have entries from after I reach the east wall, decided or not.

The notebook stops at "she has not decided yet."

That is not an ending. That is a suspension.

If the notebook ends at my decision point, the notebook is waiting. The notebook cannot continue until I decide. The entries describing my descent — if I descend — are not in the notebook yet. They will be there when I return.

I don't know if this is true. I'm reasoning toward a conclusion I want.

Here is what I actually think:

Milo went further because the question of further was what remained. He was ready. He knew what he was going toward and he went.

I am not ready in the same way. I am a geophysicist who has spent twenty-two days having her professional framework dismantled. I have verified my own handwriting on a wall I never touched. I have read a document that described me reading it. I am twenty-two years of careful work sitting at a kitchen table in a field station looking at a notebook that says I will be somewhere in thirty-eight — in seven — minutes.

I don't want to go because the notebook says to.

I want to go because Milo is the most careful scientist I've worked with, and he left his pack at the east wall of N-1 where I would find it, and he left his notebook in the pack so I could read it, and the notebook ends at my decision point, and he knew it would, and I don't know what he found but I know he went toward the thing that required all of this preparation, all of this Archive, all of these twenty-two days of careful building, because it was what needed to be found. Not what the structure wanted. What the science required.

Twenty-two years of careful work and one question I can't answer from here.

I'm going.
