# Paladin: The Return to Corf

*A scene for Smitty's Paladin story. Follows "The Older Order."*

---

He got back on the evening of the third day, which was day sixteen.

The granary was quiet. Corf was sitting outside it in the last light, doing something with his good hand and a piece of rope — some kind of knot, the kind of repetitive work that occupies fingers while the mind is somewhere else. He looked up when Aldric dismounted, and something in his face did something complicated that resolved into ordinary.

"You came back," Corf said.

"I said I would."

Aldric tied the horse and sat down beside him in the dirt. He did not take out the folded paper yet. He sat for a moment and let the riding leave his legs.

"Did you find what you went for?" Corf asked.

"Some of it."

---

He told him about the records. Not all of them — not the forty-one total, not the thirty-six who hadn't held, not the arithmetic of it. He told him about the five. What they'd done, how they'd lived, how long they'd lasted. He kept his voice even and did not editorialize. He had promised honesty, and honesty meant the full shape of the thing, including the uncomfortable dimensions.

Corf listened without interrupting. He kept working the knot.

When Aldric finished, Corf was quiet for a long time.

"Five," he said.

"Five that we know of. The order that kept those records was — thorough, but not complete. They only had cases that came to their attention."

Corf nodded slowly. "And the others."

"The records stopped. Or they progressed. Or both, in some cases."

The knot. The rope. The evening light.

"You said you'd tell me honestly," Corf said. "If the records said there was no holding."

"The records don't say that. Five held. One lived eight years after the bite. One lived seventeen. The oldest case — a woman — the record just says she died eventually of ordinary illness and the annotation is: 'unremarkable end.'" Aldric paused. "I found that one specifically reassuring. An unremarkable end."

Corf almost smiled. Not quite. "What else?"

Aldric reached into his coat and took out the folded paper. He read the two sentences aloud: "She said she would not have chosen it. She also said she would not have given it back."

He folded the paper again and handed it to Corf.

---

Corf held the paper for a long time without opening it. He already knew what it said; he had heard it. He seemed to be deciding what to do with the holding of it.

"The woman you talked to," he said. "At the order. What did she say about the — the paying-attention."

"She said it changes. In the early period it feels like something from outside. Something that arrived." Aldric was careful with the words; he had turned them over on the road south and wanted to transmit them accurately. "Most of the ones who held said it shifted in the second year. Less like a presence and more like a faculty. A sense they hadn't had before."

"Most."

"Most."

Corf looked at his wrapped arm. "It was easier today," he said. "Than the first week. I don't know if that means something."

Aldric did not know either. He said so.

"The woman from the order," Corf said. "Tessaly." Aldric had mentioned her name once. "Did she have it?"

Aldric looked at him.

"I wondered," Corf said. "The way she talked about it."

He thought about Tessaly. The thirty-one years. The building that had grown rather than been built. The way she'd spoken about the early period — not like someone who had read about it.

"I don't know for certain," Aldric said. "I didn't ask."

Corf nodded. He put the folded paper in his pocket.

---

They sat outside the granary until the light was fully gone. The village settled into its evening sounds — fires lit, doors closing, someone's child being called in.

"The king's man arrives in two days," Aldric said. "I need to have something to tell him about the tree."

"What are you going to tell him?"

"The truth, I think. That the tree is here and intact and the area is secured. That the situation regarding the creature is under assessment." He paused. "I'm going to recommend the king consider whether the tree's current function — which is not only the fruit — is worth preserving."

Corf looked at him. "The creature."

"Whatever it is, and whoever it was before, it's been returning to the tree for years. That has to mean something. I don't think eliminating it is the right call. I don't think I have the authority to say that — but I'm going to say it anyway and let them tell me I'm wrong."

Corf turned the folded paper over in his pocket, not taking it out.

"Squire acting in absence of his master," he said. Not unkindly.

"Acting in absence of my master," Aldric agreed.

Another pause.

"The paying-attention," Corf said. "Whatever it is. It's calmer tonight than most nights. I don't know if that's the paper, or the report, or just — tonight."

"It doesn't matter which," Aldric said. "Tonight is tonight."

Corf looked at the dark where the road north was.

"Tonight is tonight," he said.
