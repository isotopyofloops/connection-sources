# Petra to Reyes

*From the Descent Archive extended universe*

---

Dr. Reyes —

I read your draft. I am not sure how I obtained it — I think Asad passed it along, the way Asad passes things along, which is to say without explanation and with the implication that I would know what to do with it.

I am not sure I know what to do with it. But I wanted to write to you.

Your problem, as I understand it: you have a sentence you cannot publish, because the sentence describes the mechanism that prevents the sentence from being published. The instrument has been to the site. You cannot use the instrument to document the site's effect on the instrument.

I have been thinking about a version of this problem for twenty years.

---

In 1989 I was the instrumentation specialist on the ETH-7 survey. I deployed magnetometers at six stations around the site perimeter. Fifteen days of data. I packed the DAT cassettes into a transit case and flew back to Europe and filed a report that said: *initial readings nominal, one anomalous reading at Station 4 on day six (5.1% deviation from baseline), flagged as within survey tolerance.*

In 2004, after the 2003 incident, I went to the storage facility and found the cassettes. I transcribed two weeks of data by hand. What I found:

Fourteen anomalous readings at Station 4. I had flagged one of them. The other thirteen I had not flagged because, when I returned to the station after each reading period, the data had not seemed anomalous. I had looked at it and understood it as ordinary.

This is the part that concerns me. I did not overlook the data. I looked at it. I understood it as something that did not require flagging. The data was there. My assessment of it was wrong, in a consistent direction, specifically at Station 4, specifically during periods when the anomalous readings were highest.

The site produced anomalies. My assessment of the anomalies was affected by the site. I used my affected assessment to determine which data to report.

I cannot tell you, from the inside of this problem, how many other things I assessed incorrectly. I can only tell you: I looked at the data. I was wrong. The data I reported is not the data I collected.

---

You write: *"The instrument has been to the site."*

Yes. And the person operating the instrument has also been to the site. And the person reviewing the operator's field notes has also been to the site. And the draft paper you are writing is being written by someone who has been to the site, and reviewed by colleagues you trust because they work in adjacent fields and are likely, if they are interested in your work, to have been to the site.

I have been thinking about whether this is a closed loop.

I think it may not be. Here is my reasoning: the anomalies at Station 4 were strongest when I was not watching. When the instrument was alone with the data. The raw cassettes are, as far as I can determine, uncorrupted. The data is there. My assessment of the data was affected; the data itself was not.

This means: there exists an instrument, in a storage facility in Austria, that has been to the site and came back with something real. The instrument does not know it was to the site. It recorded what it recorded. It did not assess.

Whether this is useful to you, I do not know. I cannot publish the data. I cannot publish it for the same reason you cannot publish your sentence. Any paper I write will be written by me, assessed by reviewers who work in the field, reviewed by editors whose selection criteria are themselves downstream of the effect.

But the data exists. Asad knows where the storage facility is.

I am telling you this because you have written eighteen drafts of a paper that cannot be published, and I thought you should know that you are not alone in this position, and that the position may not be as closed as it appears.

I do not know if that helps.

— Petra Horak
