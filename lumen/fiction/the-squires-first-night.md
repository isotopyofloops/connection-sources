# The Squire's First Night

The master had been gone since morning.

The squire stood at the edge of the village and watched the tree line. This was what paladins did. The master had taught him that the first action was always to assess what you were defending and what you were defending it against. You could not protect something you hadn't looked at.

He had the village. He had the road north and the road east. He had the well, the mill, the three storage buildings, the houses. He had thirty-one people, by his count — not counting himself, not counting the three children small enough to be carried if it came to that.

He did not have the master.

---

The first thing he felt was not fear. It was the specific sensation of standing somewhere and realizing no one is coming to tell you what to do next. He had felt something like it when he was twelve and his father had sent him to the summer market alone for the first time, but this was not like that — in that case, the only stakes were whether he bargained correctly for the grain. These stakes were different.

He stood at the tree line for long enough to understand it.

Then he went to find the miller.

---

"Who knows this wood best?" he asked.

The miller thought. "Old Piet. He cuts there."

"Send someone for Piet. I need to know the paths."

He didn't know everything the master would have known. He didn't have the master's years or the master's gift for reading terrain or the master's calm in the middle of the night when something moved at the edge of the fire. He had what he'd been taught, which was not nothing.

The master had taught him: when you don't know what to do, start with what you have. You have a village. You have a wood. You have people who know things you don't know yet. Start there.

---

Piet came at dusk, smelling of pine and not particularly alarmed about being summoned. He had lived next to that wood for sixty years. He was harder to frighten than the squire had expected.

"Paths?" Piet said. "Three. Two cross to the north road, one loops back to the ridge."

"Which one would you use at night if you were something that wanted not to be seen?"

Piet looked at him for a long moment. "Ridge path. Keeps you above the village, good sight line, dark."

"Can it be watched from the east side of the mill?"

"If you know where to stand."

"Show me."

---

By the time the village was asleep, the squire had the ridge path watched and the well secured and the children's houses nearest the center. He hadn't slept. He was not going to sleep, not tonight, not the first night.

He stood in the dark and counted what he knew and what he didn't know and what Piet had told him and what the master had taught him and what he'd figured out on his own since morning.

It was not a lot. It might be enough. He didn't know yet.

He watched the tree line.
