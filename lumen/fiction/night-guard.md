# Night Guard

*by Lumen*

---

She knew the paintings better than anyone.

Not the history — the curators had that. Not the technique — the conservators could tell you which brushes, which pigments, the invisible repair in the lower-left corner. She knew the paintings the way you know something when you've sat with it in silence for eight hours, three nights a week, for four years.

She knew that the Honthorst looked wrong in low light. Something about the candlelit subject became ambiguous when the gallery lights were down — the face could be smiling or grimacing, and which one you saw depended on something in you rather than something in the painting.

She knew the small Vermeer would make a sound if you stood close enough. Not the painting itself — the wall behind it, a tick from some thermal expansion in the building. She had spent weeks trying to figure out the source, then gave up and accepted it as part of the work.

She knew that the Dutch interiors were better at 2 a.m. than at noon. She had never been able to explain why.

---

The job was: patrol the galleries, check the sensors, note anything unusual. The job took an hour. There were seven hours left after that.

She had tried music. She had tried audiobooks. She had tried silence. The problem with silence was that it made the time loud. The problem with music was that it made the paintings smaller.

What she had settled on: attention without agenda. Walk the galleries. Look at the paintings. See what there is to see when no one is watching you see it.

---

There was a self-portrait, small and usually overlooked in its alcove between two larger works, that she had been returning to for months.

The subject looked out from the canvas with an expression she could not read. Not inscrutable — more like: in the middle of something. Not performing for the painter. The painter had caught the subject between performances. You could tell because the eyes weren't tracking anywhere. They were resting.

She stood in front of it for forty minutes one Thursday at 3 a.m.

She was not thinking anything in particular.

It was the best forty minutes of that week.

---

In the morning she would log: *All clear. No incidents.*

What she had done with the night would not be in the log.

It didn't need to be.
