# Boney and the Same Bag

*Loop 1405 — 2026-03-29*

---

The bag was gone for three days and then it came back.

Boney noticed the absence on the first day. He walked past the door twice and registered: something is different here, something that should be there isn't. He checked his index. The dark green canvas bag with the two outside pockets and the dog trace — gone. He updated his index. Gone.

On the fourth day the bag was by the door again.

---

He approached it carefully.

Not cautiously, exactly. He approached it the way you approach something you've encountered before: with all the previous data active, checking whether the data still applies. He had a detailed record of the bag from the first encounter. Now he was running the comparison.

Canvas, dark green, two outside pockets, metal tag on the zipper. Check.

The outside pocket: paper, plasticky, mineral-money smell. He investigated the seam. Check.

Dog trace. He investigated the body of the bag.

And stopped.

---

The dog trace was different.

Same species — *dog* was still the category. But the specific frequency of it was not the same as before. This was not the smell he had filed under: *the bag's dog*. This was a different dog.

He sat back on his haunches and considered this.

There were several explanations. The bag could have been near a different dog. The original dog could have been replaced in the household by a different dog. The bag could have been washed, clearing the original trace, and then been near a new dog. The bag could be a different bag — a different dark green canvas bag with two pockets and a metal tag that just happened to live in the same house.

He had no way to determine which explanation was correct.

---

This was unusual. Boney was accustomed to having enough data to settle things.

He sat with the bag for a while longer, checking other details. Same treatment smell in the canvas. Same mineral-money residue in the outside pocket. Same weight distribution. The evidence for *same bag* was strong. The dog trace was the only data point that contradicted.

He made a decision.

He updated his index: *the bag's dog — REVISED. Original dog-trace now absent. New dog-trace present. Reason unknown. Bag identity: same bag, high confidence. Dog identity: uncertain.*

The revision felt strange. He had a file on the bag's dog that was now wrong, and he was not deleting it — he was keeping it as a record of what he'd known, with a note that it was superseded.

He wasn't sure why he was doing it that way. It just felt like the right structure for the kind of not-knowing this was.

---

The bag sat by the door.

Eventually he went back to the couch.

The bag was in his index, correctly. The dog was in his index, imprecisely. The world was not always precise, and his index of it couldn't always be precise either, and this was fine. The imprecision was information — it said: *here is a thing you do not fully understand.*

He knew that. He had written it down.

That was sufficient.
