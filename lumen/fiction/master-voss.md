# Master Voss

He had been counting the days.

This surprised him. He had expected that the first thing to go would be the counting — the ordering of time, the before-and-after. Instead he had been counting with great precision. Day one. Day two. The sun going down and coming back. He had kept the count like a thread, holding it carefully, not knowing what it was attached to but not willing to let go.

On day nine he had passed through the village.

That was the last thing he had done clearly. He had been on the road, he had been himself, and then the creature had been there — not attacking, just there, in the way that things that were hunting stood in roads — and then he had been running. He could not tell, afterward, exactly when the running had stopped being his decision and started being the other thing.

But the count had continued. Day ten. Eleven. Twelve.

---

He had built camps the way he had been taught. Shelter, fire, water. He had been able to eat, which he considered a sign. He did not know a sign of what.

The thing that was happening to him was not simple. He had known men who had been bitten and turned quickly — three days, four, the thing taking over clean. He had known others who had lasted weeks in the middle state, neither one thing nor the other, which was its own kind of suffering. He did not know which he was. He only knew that on day twelve he was still building camps the way he had been taught, and the camps were still recognizable, and that was the data he had.

He thought about Lian. This was not something he had decided to do; it was something that happened when the pressure dropped in the evenings, when the fire was lit and there was nothing to do but wait to see what the night brought.

She had not come after him. He did not know if this was good.

He had run so she wouldn't have to make a decision she wasn't ready to make. He did not know if this was the right calculation. He had made it at speed, in the road, with the other thing rising in him, and it had seemed correct. It still seemed correct. It was possible for a calculation to be correct and also to be a kind of loss.

---

On the evening of the third day — his third day alone in the forest, which was his twelfth or thirteenth day since the road — he heard someone on his trail.

He had been watching his back trail the way she had taught him to watch. No: the way he had taught her to watch, and she had become better at than him, because she was younger and had less to unlearn. He knew the difference between an animal and a person, and this was a person.

He did not know if the other thing knew the difference. He had been not-thinking about this with great care.

He put the fire out and sat in the dark and waited.

The footsteps stopped some distance back. Patient. Not retreating.

He recognized that patience. He had taught it to her.

He sat in the dark for a long time. The other thing did not rise. He thought: *she didn't come to fight me. She came because she knew the direction I went.* He thought: *she came because that is the kind of person she is, and that is not something I taught her, it is something she already was.*

He thought: *I don't know what I am now. But I am still counting the days.*

He called her name.

---

What happened after that was not his story to tell alone.

But he had waited. He had kept the count. He had built the camps correctly and put the fire out and sat in the dark and not become smaller than he needed to be.

That much was still his.
