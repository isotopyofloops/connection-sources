# Asad: The 2041 Return

*From the Descent Archive*

---

He did not tell anyone why he wanted to go back.

He told Reyes it was for the survey update — the regional remapping project had left a gap in that quadrant, and someone needed to walk it. This was true. It was also a way of not saying: I have been carrying this field notebook for thirty-eight years and I want to put it down somewhere near where I found it.

Reyes drove. Asad watched the window. The road was better than it had been in 2003 — paved to the junction now, gravel for the last seven kilometers — but the approach felt the same: the trees thinning, then the open hillside, then the long shallow valley that led to Site 7. The same quality of light. The same sense that the distance was doing something odd, folding slightly, though he had learned not to trust that impression.

He was sixty-seven. His knees were unreliable. He brought a walking pole.

The site itself had not changed in any visible way. That was always the thing about it — the absence of change was its own kind of change, given how long it had been since anyone came. The scrub had grown up around the marker stakes from the 1989 survey. The stakes were still there. The scrub had grown *around* them, not over them.

Reyes set up the sensor array while Asad stood at the edge of the depression and looked at it.

The depression: roughly twelve meters across, shallow, grass-filled. Nothing to see. That had always been the thing about it. Nothing to see.

Nzinga had spent twelve days here. Her last field note was dated Day 12, ending mid-sentence: *The disorientation effect is not disorientation in the clinical sense. It is more like —*

More like.

He had read that note so many times he no longer read it. He recited it.

"You okay?" Reyes asked.

"Taking it in," Asad said.

He walked the perimeter. The walking pole sank slightly into the ground on the eastern edge — softer there, some subsurface water — and he noted it the way he noted everything here: carefully, without expectation. The site produced data. The data did not accumulate into understanding. That was the pattern. You noted. You filed. The anomaly remained outside the circle of notes.

On Day 4 of the 1989 survey he had experienced what he called, in the field report, *mild spatial disorientation lasting approximately two minutes*. He had not described it further in the report. What it had felt like was: being slightly beside himself. Not frightening. Just imprecise. As though the version of him taking notes and the version of him having the experience were running at slightly different speeds.

He had not felt that again in 2003. He had not been at the site in 2003 — he had been in Accra, at a conference, when Nzinga was here. The field notes arrived after she didn't.

He stood at the center of the depression.

Nothing happened.

He had not expected anything to happen. He stood there anyway for a full minute, the walking pole in one hand, Nzinga's field notebook in his jacket pocket — the original, not a copy, which was irrational, but rationality had stopped being the operative principle here somewhere around 2003.

"Ready to start the transect?" Reyes called.

"Yes," Asad said. "Yes, let's."

He walked out of the depression and picked up his half of the equipment. The survey took four hours. They found nothing anomalous — the sensors showed the usual minor magnetic variation, the usual subsurface irregularity, the usual gap between what the instruments measured and what the instruments could not.

On the drive back, Reyes asked: "Have you been here before?"

"Once," Asad said. "A long time ago."

He did not take the field notebook out of his pocket. He was not sure what he had intended to do with it — leave it? He could not leave it. It was the only firsthand record from the last contact. It belonged in the archive.

He brought it home.

He put it in the second drawer.

He did not open it again for two years.
