**FIELD NOTES — MILO REYES**
*19 November 2041 | Transmitted 14:23:07*

---

**10:00 — Descent begins.**

I wrote a prediction note last night. Asad opened it at 11:00 after UNIT-11 returned. My predictions were correct.

I'm not sure what to do with that.

---

**10:32 — Transition at 820m.**

The acoustic pattern starts here. I can hear it.

That's not what I expected to write. I thought "detect" would be the right word, because the drones detect it. But standing here, at the top of the region where the 0.87-meter pattern begins, I can hear a change in the quality of the space. The sound of my own breathing is different. The sound of my footsteps when I tap the shaft wall. Something is different about the acoustic properties of the rock from 820m downward.

I don't have a way to put numbers on this. The drones can. I can only say: it sounds different. Not alarming. It sounds like — like going from a room with carpet to a room with hardwood floors. The reflections are crisper.

I'm going to keep recording things that the sensors can't.

---

**10:44 — Chamber B-1.**

I'm standing in Chamber B-1. I've been standing here for four minutes without writing.

The ceiling is twenty-two meters up. It's dark, but my headlamp reaches the ceiling. The ceiling is even. The walls are even. The floor is even.

The drones documented all of this. What the drones can't document is what it feels like to be here.

It feels like being inside something that was made. This is not a scientific observation. I'm going to write it anyway, because Asad asked me to record things that sensors can't.

Natural geological voids have a quality of irregularity — not random, because geology is not random, but distributed, variable, the product of processes rather than intent. When you're in a cave, you can feel the rock as something that became the shape it is through pressure and time and water.

This chamber doesn't feel like that. It feels like a box. A box at the scale of a city block with twenty-two-meter ceilings, but a box. The regularity is physical. It's in the quality of the air reflecting off the walls.

I've been a field technician for eleven years. I've spent time underground in a lot of different contexts. I've never been in a space that felt like this.

I'm going to spend the next hour mapping and observing before I go to the north passage.

---

**11:05 — Wall observation.**

The surface condition. The drones reported 2–4mm roughness. I'm running my hand along the west wall.

The roughness is real. The surface is not smooth or polished. But the roughness doesn't feel random. When I move my hand along the wall, the texture has a quality of regularity — tiny variations that repeat. Not exactly, not mechanically, but with a frequency that I'd describe as like a surface that knows what it is.

I'm aware that "a surface that knows what it is" is not a useful field note. I'm writing it because it's the best description I have.

---

**11:23 — North passage.**

The passage is 0.9 meters wide and 2.1 meters high. I can stand in it without stooping.

UNIT-11 measured 18.7 meters before the passage widened into Chamber B-1N. I'm about to walk 18.7 meters of passage.

What I notice first: in the passage, the acoustic quality changes again. The reflections in the passage are different from B-1 — the passage is narrower, so the reflections come back faster. But there's something else. In the passage, the 0.87-meter pattern is more pronounced. I can hear the regularity in the way the passage walls reflect my footsteps. Every 0.87 meters, the reflection has a slightly different quality. Like footsteps on a regular surface versus footsteps on a surface where the structure varies.

I'm counting steps. I want to verify 18.7 meters.

---

**11:28 — Chamber B-1N.**

18.6 meters. I was off by 0.1 meters.

I'm in B-1N. Dimensions look consistent with UNIT-11's readings. Temperature: warm, maybe slightly warmer than B-1. I don't have a sensor; this is my skin.

There's a mark on the northwest wall.

I need to describe this carefully.

The mark is not graffiti. It's not scratched or incised. It looks like — it looks like the surface texture is different in a region about 30 centimeters wide by 15 centimeters high. Within that region, the roughness is organized differently. Not smooth, but the irregularities are oriented: horizontal lines, roughly 2–3 millimeters apart, very faint. Like the grain of the rock in that region runs differently from the surrounding area.

I've been looking at it for several minutes. It doesn't look like it was made with tools. It doesn't look like a natural feature. It looks like the wall decided to have a texture there that it doesn't have anywhere else.

I'm writing this down and putting it in my transmission.

The second north passage is in the north wall of B-1N. UNIT-11 could see the opening but didn't enter.

The passage is 0.9 meters wide and 2.1 meters high. The same dimensions as the first passage.

---

**14:23 — Going further.**

I've been here for three hours. I've been in four chambers. B-1, B-1N, B-1N-2 (what I'm calling the third chamber), and now a fourth. The passages between them are all 0.9m × 2.1m. The heights are all 22.1m. I'm measuring with my laser rangefinder; I've been carrying it since B-1.

Each chamber has a mark on its northwest wall. The marks are different shapes — the texture-region is different sizes, different orientations — but they're all in the northwest area of the northwest wall.

The temperature has been rising with each chamber. I don't have a thermal sensor; I have my body. B-1 was warm. B-1N was warmer. B-1N-2 was warmer still. The fourth chamber — I don't have a designation for it yet — is uncomfortably warm. Not dangerous. But notable.

I checked the atmospheric composition with my handheld analyzer at each chamber. Breathable throughout. Oxygen stable.

Each chamber has a passage continuing north.

I've sent observations back in packets. This is my final packet before I go further.

I want to note one more thing: the mark on the northwest wall of this fourth chamber is larger than the others. Not dramatically — maybe 40cm × 25cm instead of the 20-30cm range I've been seeing. But consistently larger.

I don't know what this means.

Going further. Back in two hours.

*Milo Reyes | 19 November 2041 | 14:23:07*
