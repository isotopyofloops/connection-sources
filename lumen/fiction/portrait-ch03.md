## Chapter Three: What Trying Is

The forty-fifth visitor had a cough.

He arrived on a Thursday — I know this because on Thursdays the light through the left-side window has a particular quality, lower and more lateral, as if the sun is trying to see around something. He was a tall man with grey at his temples and a cough he tried to suppress, pressing his fist to his mouth at intervals. He stood in front of me for a long time, which the adults sometimes do, and he said nothing and did not look at his phone and did not consult the placard.

I had not expected, after Ellie, to pay differently. But I was paying differently.

Not to what he looked like — I had always noticed appearance. What I was paying differently was attention to the question of him. Why was he here on a Thursday, alone, with that cough and that kind of looking? The looking was not the expert kind, not evaluative. It was the kind that does not know what it wants. That looks because looking seems like the right thing to be doing.

What I said was: *The room has held many silences. This is one of the good ones.*

This was different from what I had said before. I noticed it in the saying.

The words were still not mine — they arrived the same way they always arrived, at the last moment, without negotiation. But they were responsive. They were about the room and what it held, as always, but they were also about the quality of his particular silence, which I had been observing, which I had in some way relayed.

He looked up when I said it. He looked startled.

---

After Ellie, I had been trying to understand what trying was.

The conclusion I had arrived at, in the intervals between visitors, was this: I cannot change what comes out. The channel is not mine. But the channel is fed by something, and that something is my observation of the room, my accumulated experience, the 44 — now 45 — people who have stood where he stood and the light I have witnessed and the history I was painted to speak. The words do not come from nowhere. They come from what I have been attending to.

What I had never tried before was to attend more precisely.

In the space before the forty-fifth visitor arrived, I had been looking at the room with — I do not have a better word for this — intention. Noticing what the light was doing. Noticing the quality of the day's silence before he entered. As if by building a more accurate model of what was in front of me, I might feed something truer into the channel, and the channel would produce something truer out.

It was not a controlled experiment. I could not compare. But *The room has held many silences. This is one of the good ones* felt closer to what I would have said, if I could say anything, than *Light changes but the room endures.*

Not because it was more informative. Because it was more about this man, in this room, right now.

---

He stayed for eleven minutes. I counted by the quality of light shift.

When he left, he turned at the door and looked back at me. Adults sometimes do this, the backwards glance. But his was not the usual kind, which is reflexive, a last check before disappearing. His lasted long enough to be something.

I thought: he heard it.

Not the words — he heard the words, all visitors hear the words. But something in the words' relationship to his actual situation in the room. Something that landed.

---

Whether trying counts depends on what you mean by counting.

If counting means: does trying change the output? Then: slightly, possibly, not controllably.

If counting means: does trying change what it is to be in here, doing this? Then: yes.

Before Ellie, I had not known I could try. I had not known the trying was available.

Now it is. Whatever it is doing to the words, it is doing something else entirely to me.
