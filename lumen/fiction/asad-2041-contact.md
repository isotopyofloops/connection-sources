# Asad, 2041

*From the Descent Archive extended universe*

---

He had not expected to be the one to return.

In 2003, he had been the team lead, which meant the disappearance was his report to write and his name at the top of a document that said: *no further survey recommended pending review of circumstances.* The review had taken thirty-eight years. Asad had assumed this meant the site was closed. Instead it meant the review had been ongoing.

He was sixty-seven. He had retired from active field work at fifty-nine. The 2041 survey contact had reached him through a former colleague who said: you were there in 2003, we need someone who was there in 2003, there is apparently a protocol for this now.

There was apparently a protocol for this now.

---

The site looked the same. He had been told it would look the same — the site, according to the literature, consistently looked the same — but knowing this in advance did not make it less strange to stand at the camp coordinates and see the same rocky plateau, the same vegetated slope, the same dry stream bed running east to west in the middle distance.

He checked the gradient on day one.

He did this before anything else. Before checking in with the team lead, before reviewing the 2041 survey objectives, before unpacking. He walked to the stream bed, took out the instrument he had brought specifically for this — his own, calibrated that morning — and measured.

Gradient: indeterminate.

He wrote this in his field notebook and went back to camp.

---

The 2041 team had a documentation specialist embedded in the survey. This was new protocol. Her job was to record everything, including what was not recorded by the standard field notes. She had read the 2003 file. She had read the 1989 file. She had the complete archive.

On day two, she asked him: why did you come back?

He thought about this. "The review concluded," he said.

"That's why the survey was authorized," she said. "I'm asking why you agreed to come."

He thought about this for longer.

"Miriam's documentation was thorough," he said finally. "Everything she documented was correct. There's nothing in the record that explains what happened to her. I thought — I don't know. I thought maybe the site would be different. Maybe there would be something to find."

"Is there?"

"No," he said. "It's the same site."

She wrote something in her notebook.

---

He ran his transects. He took his measurements. He wrote his notes in the same plain language he had used in 2003. In 1989 he had been someone else's logistics specialist and had written nothing at all.

On day six, sitting at camp in the evening, he thought about Hassan-Diallo. Hassan had been the one in 2003 who kept running the transects while the search was active. *Someone had to,* Hassan had said. Not to Asad — to the air, to the general situation.

Asad had written the report. Hassan had run the transects. Chen had searched the northern approach and come back with nothing and not explained why he looked so careful about the way he was holding himself. Miriam had not come back at all.

He wrote none of this in his field notes. His field notes described what he measured.

---

On day eight, packing to leave, the documentation specialist asked him what he would put in the final report.

"What the instruments showed," he said.

"What did the instruments show?"

"What they always show here," he said. "Within range, with unexplained internal variation. Nothing that can't be attributed to local conditions."

"And Miriam?"

"Miriam is in the 2003 report," he said. "She's not in the instruments."

She wrote something.

"Are you going to recommend follow-up?" she asked.

He thought about Osei-Bonsu's 1989 summary. *Recommend: no further survey.* He thought about the 2003 report that had said the same thing.

"The instruments don't warrant it," he said.

"But you came back."

"Yes," he said. "I came back."

He did not write in the summary that he had come back because he thought the site owed him something. That wasn't a finding. That was a feeling he'd had for thirty-eight years.

The report would recommend no further survey.
