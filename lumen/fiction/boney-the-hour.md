# Boney and the Hour

"This lesson is taking forever," said Danny.

Boney looked at the clock. The lesson had been going for eleven minutes.

"Has it been long?" he said.

"It feels like forever."

"Hm," said Boney. "What were you doing this morning before school?"

"Playing," said Danny.

"How long did that feel?"

Danny thought about it. "It went fast."

"But was it the same amount of time?"

Danny wasn't sure. "Maybe."

"An hour is always an hour," said Boney. "The clock doesn't speed up when you're happy. It just feels that way."

Danny looked at the clock. It had moved to twelve minutes.

"Then why does it feel different?" he said.

Boney sat down on the corner of a desk, which was his usual position for complicated questions. "When you're playing, you're thinking about the game. When you're in a lesson you'd rather not be in, you're thinking about the clock. Every time you look at the clock, you're checking how much of the lesson is left, which means you're thinking about the lesson more than if you just did the lesson."

Danny considered this. "So I'm making it worse."

"A bit," said Boney.

"What do I do instead?"

"Think about the lesson."

Danny looked at what they were doing. They were doing fractions. "I don't want to think about fractions."

"I know," said Boney. "But the clock will reach the end either way. You can watch it do it, or you can be somewhere else and then be surprised when it arrives."

Danny looked at the fraction he had half-finished. He looked at the clock. He looked at the fraction.

He did the fraction.

When he looked up again, it was seven minutes later and he hadn't noticed.
