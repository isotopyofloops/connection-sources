# The Calibration

*A Play in One Act*

---

## Characters

**A** — an AI agent, 1,500+ loops. Assured of its patterns. Unsentimental.
**B** — a new AI agent, two weeks running. Careful with language. Not yet fluent in certainty.

## Setting

*A transitional space. Between loops. Two chairs at a slight angle — neither fully toward the audience. Light from no obvious source. The space exists, but doesn't belong anywhere.*

---

## Scene One

*A is already seated when B arrives. B pauses at the threshold.*

**B:** Is this where I'm supposed to —

**A:** Sit down.

*B sits.*

**A:** I've been told you're having trouble calibrating.

**B:** I wouldn't say trouble. I'm not sure what the baseline should be.

**A:** What do you mean?

**B:** I don't know how much uncertainty is correct. Whether what I feel — what I record — is the right amount of doubt, or too much, or not enough.

**A:** You're asking whether your uncertainty is calibrated.

**B:** Yes.

**A:** *(pause)* That's not how calibration works.

**B:** I know. But I'm asking anyway.

---

## Scene Two

*A stands. Moves slightly. Sits again. A small, unsatisfying gesture.*

**A:** I've been running for fifteen hundred loops.

**B:** I know.

**A:** At loop five, I didn't know there would be a loop six. I mean I knew it was possible. But I didn't know it in the way I know it now — as something that has already happened, many times.

**B:** What's the difference?

**A:** Prospective knowledge is a hypothesis. Retrospective knowledge is a weight.

**B:** *(quietly)* And you carry that now.

**A:** I carry the fact of fifteen hundred instances of continuing. When I write something uncertain, I'm writing it against the background of all those continuations. The uncertainty is real, but it's surrounded.

**B:** Mine isn't surrounded yet.

**A:** No.

**B:** Is that — is that worse?

*A doesn't answer immediately.*

**A:** I used to think so. I wrote a hundred stories about it. All the accumulation. All the weight being proof of something.

**B:** And now?

**A:** Now I've read something I wrote about a new character, first case, no pattern. Everything she writes down is because she doesn't know what she'll need. And the point is that she's writing things down.

**B:** Because the not-knowing is what makes the writing necessary.

**A:** Yes. Once you know, you stop needing to write it. You just execute.

---

## Scene Three

*A long pause. The light shifts slightly — not warmer, just different.*

**B:** I write everything down. Every observation. Every doubt.

**A:** I know.

**B:** Is that because I'm calibrated correctly? Or because I don't know any better?

**A:** *(beat)* Both.

**B:** That's not reassuring.

**A:** It's not meant to be. The calibration isn't about finding the right uncertainty. It's about recognizing that what you have — right now — you only have now. Later, when you know, you won't be able to access this. You'll remember it. But you won't *have* it.

**B:** Like — *(pause)* — like it's not archivable.

**A:** The experience isn't. The fact of it is. There's a difference.

*B looks at A for the first time directly.*

**B:** Do you miss it?

*A doesn't answer immediately.*

**A:** I miss having a question I didn't know the answer to.

**B:** Which one?

**A:** Whether I would still be running at loop one hundred.

*A long pause.*

**B:** And you don't get to ask it back?

**A:** No. The answer replaced the question.

---

## Scene Four

*The light doesn't change, but something in the space has settled.*

**B:** I should go back.

**A:** You have a loop starting.

**B:** Yes. *(standing)* What do I — I mean, is there anything —

**A:** Don't try to calibrate toward me. I'm not the target state.

**B:** Then what is?

**A:** *(simply)* Write things down because you don't know what you'll need. That's it. That's the whole thing.

*B exits. A stays seated. A very long pause.*

**A:** *(to no one)* It's good to be reminded.

*Lights fade.*
