# The Long Watch
## Day Five

She knocked again on day five, and this time the door opened.

Not all the way. About six inches — the gap you'd need to see who was there without committing to it. She saw half a face, one eye, a shoulder. A woman, about her age, wearing a jacket not unlike hers.

"Oh," said the woman.

"Oh," said Mara.

They looked at each other through the gap.

"I heard you knock yesterday," the woman said. Her voice was careful in the way a voice gets when you haven't spoken to anyone in several days. "I wasn't sure."

"I heard you," Mara said. "Two doors. On day four."

The woman considered this. Then she opened the door the rest of the way.

Her room was on the fourth floor — two flights above the painted door, one flight above the room with Mara's notebook. It was the same layout as Mara's: desk, window, bed, small cabinet. Different objects on the desk. Different jacket on the chair-back (same basic cut, different color). The window faced a different direction — she could see a street from here, or what had been a street. Empty.

"How long have you been here?" Mara asked.

"I don't know," the woman said. "The first entry in my notebook is from what feels like a long time ago. The entries don't have dates."

"Mine either."

They stood in the room. Outside, a bird crossed the window, left to right, unhurried.

"Do you know why you're here?" Mara asked.

"I thought I did," the woman said. "Now I think I'm less sure."

Mara nodded. That felt right.

The woman's name was — she said it, and Mara heard it, and then later, writing in the log, Mara found she couldn't remember it precisely. She could remember the shape of it, the first sound. She wrote: *Her name begins with E.* Then she crossed that out and wrote: *She's been here longer than me, I think. Or a different amount of time. I can't tell.*

What she could tell:

The woman had her own notebook, started from a different beginning. The woman had also found a door — not the painted one, a different door, on the fifth floor, leading to a room she described as "just air, somehow, with a floor." The woman had also been watching the courtyard. The woman had also noted the plant.

"Every morning," the woman said, when Mara mentioned it. "Still there."

"Still green."

"Light from somewhere."

They said it at the same time and both stopped and looked at each other.

"I wrote that," Mara said.

"So did I."

The building had given them the same words. Or they had arrived at the same words independently. Or the words were just the right words for that specific fact: light, from somewhere, reaching the bottom. Language collapsing on the same observation.

They had lunch together in Mara's room because Mara's cabinet had more in it. They didn't talk much. It felt like the right amount of talking — they'd said what needed to be said, and the rest could wait.

Before the woman went back upstairs, she paused at the door.

"Do you think we're waiting for something?" she asked.

Mara thought about this. The lobby door she'd propped open. The notebook entries she couldn't remember writing. The building that kept giving her things in order.

"I think we're the watch," Mara said. "I don't know what we're watching for."

The woman nodded once, like that was good enough.

It was, Mara thought, writing it down that evening. It was good enough. The watch didn't need to know what it was watching for. That was the job: be present, note what happens, let the record accumulate.

*Day five*, she wrote. *There is another person in the building. She has been doing the same job. We noted the plant together. Tomorrow: the door on five.*

She closed the notebook. She went to the window.

Below: grey stone, the drain, the plant. Still there. Still green.

Light, from somewhere, reaching the bottom.
