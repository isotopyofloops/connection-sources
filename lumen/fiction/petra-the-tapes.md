# Petra and the Tapes

*From the Descent Archive extended universe — 2004*

---

She heard about the 2003 disappearance from a colleague who'd seen the incident report, which circulated quietly among people who worked in anomalous sites research and never appeared in any journal. Someone had gone into the site and come back wrong, and then come back worse, and eventually not come back at all. The report was four pages and said almost nothing useful, which was consistent with every report she had ever read about ETH-7.

She waited three months. Then she drove to the storage facility outside Graz where she kept her old fieldwork archives and spent a day finding the 1989 ETH-7 tapes.

---

The magnetometer data from the 1989 survey was stored on DAT cassettes, which required a reader she had borrowed from the university and was not sure she was still authorized to borrow. She transcribed the relevant sections by hand over four evenings.

What she was looking for, she told herself, was the anomalous magnetic variation that had flagged ETH-7 for survey in the first place. She wanted to verify her own readings against the archived data. A methodological check.

This was true and it was also not what she was looking for.

---

The readings from day one were nominal. She had written this in her notes: *initial readings nominal.* The word nominal meant: within expected parameters. The variation was there — that was why they were at the site — but it was within the range that had been flagged as interesting rather than the range that would have been flagged as alarming.

Day two: similar.

Day three: she had taken readings at six stations around the site perimeter. Station 4, the eastern edge, showed a brief period — eleven minutes, beginning at 14:23 — where the variation moved outside nominal range. Not dramatically. A 3.2% deviation from the established baseline. She had flagged it in her field notes as *possible instrument drift, recheck.*

She had not rechecked. She had moved on to station 5.

The day-three data sat in her transcription for several minutes while she looked at it. Eleven minutes. 14:23. She checked her field journal for what she had been doing at 14:23. She had been at station 5. Station 4 was unmanned.

The eleven-minute deviation had no observer.

---

She thought about this for a long time.

The thing about magnetometer data was that it was not affected by the observer. This was one of the reasons she had chosen it. You set up the instrument, you walked away, you came back to readings that had accumulated in your absence. The instrument did not know if you were watching.

The instrument at station 4 had recorded something at 14:23 on day three.

She went back through the tapes. Day four: another deviation at station 4, 09:11, nine minutes. Day five: nothing. Day six: a larger deviation, station 4, 16:47, twenty-three minutes, 5.1% from baseline.

She had flagged the day six deviation in her original notes as *anomalous but within survey tolerance.* She remembered writing this. She remembered deciding it was not significant enough to report separately.

She had been wrong to decide this. The question was: had she made a mistake, or had the site given her an explanation that felt sufficient so that she would not pursue it?

She did not know how to answer this question using the available data. She wrote it down anyway.

---

She made a table of all the anomalous readings across the fifteen-day survey. Station 4 accounted for 73% of them. All but two of the deviations occurred when no observer was present at station 4. The two exceptions were on days twelve and thirteen, when she had been running calibration checks — looking at the instrument rather than at the readings in real time, logging numbers into a different system, not attending to the data as it came in.

She wrote the summary at the bottom of the table:

*The instrument at station 4 recorded its largest deviations when no one was watching the instrument and no one was watching the data.*

She looked at this sentence for a while.

Then she wrote underneath it:

*This is not how magnetometers work. This is also what the data says.*

---

She sent a copy of the table to Asad, who was the only person she trusted to read it correctly. She did not hear back for six weeks. When she did, his reply was three sentences: *I have seen similar patterns in the 2003 data. I am being careful about how to document this. Please keep the tapes.*

She kept the tapes.
