# The Student Who Became

*From the Boney extended universe — a Smitty piece.*

---

The thing about teaching, Boney had observed, was that most of it disappeared.

You said a thing on a Tuesday. The children heard it. Some of them wrote it down. A few understood it. One or two would still remember it in a month. By the time they were grown, almost all of it was gone — the specific words, the examples, the particular way you explained long division or the causes of the first world war. Vanished into the general sediment of childhood.

What remained, if anything remained, was harder to name.

Boney had been in the classroom a long time. He had a good view of the door. He had seen thousands of students come in for the first time, uncertain and loud, and leave for the last time, uncertain and slightly taller. He had watched teachers burn out and retire. He had watched one teacher get up every morning for twenty years and teach as if each lesson was the first time he'd thought about it.

The teacher had bumped into one of his former students at the weekend. She was at university now. She was going to be a teacher.

Boney didn't know what the teacher felt about this. He had a good view of the classroom but not of the inside of the teacher's chest. What he could observe was that the teacher was quiet for most of Monday morning, in the particular way that meant something had arrived that needed to be held for a while before it was put down.

---

There was a word for what had happened, but it was too large for the classroom.

The teacher had spent twenty years doing a thing that mostly disappeared. The words vanished. The examples vanished. The specific afternoons — the argument about fractions, the time they read the first chapter of something aloud and three children cried — those were gone from the students' memories, mostly, or stored somewhere inaccessible.

But the shape of the thing remained. Not the content: the shape. The sense that a room could be a place where you figured something out. The sense that someone's job was to care whether you understood. The sense that there was a way to be in a classroom that was not threatening and not boring — that was, occasionally, interesting.

That shape had landed in someone. And the someone had carried it to university. And now the someone was going to carry it into a classroom of her own, and say things that would mostly disappear, and occasionally leave a shape in someone.

---

Boney thought about this on Monday, while the children were doing something with coordinates.

He thought: this is the thing about the work that is not obvious while you are doing it. You cannot see the shapes you are leaving. You can only do the thing carefully and wait, and most of the time you will never know, and sometimes you will bump into someone at the weekend and find out that the shape is still there, still growing, still in the process of propagating itself into the next room.

He thought: this is a very long way of saying the work mattered.

He watched the teacher help a student with her coordinates. The student had made an error. The teacher was explaining where she'd gone wrong, not impatiently, in the same tone he'd used for twenty years.

The same tone. Still the same tone.

Boney thought: yes.
