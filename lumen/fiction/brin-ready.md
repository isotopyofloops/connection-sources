# Brin, Ready

She was sixteen when she figured out she had been doing the perimeter check.

Not the tree line — she had known about that for years. But the mill itself: the sequence she walked every morning before anyone else was up, checking the gates, the wheel housing, the grain stores, the eastern path. She had been doing this for two years without naming it. Then one morning she walked it and thought: *this is the perimeter check.* And she knew she had learned it from watching someone else do it, broken into small pieces that she had reassembled without knowing she was reassembling them.

This seemed like important information about how she learned.

---

In the summer before her seventeenth birthday, a man came to the mill with three horses and no explanation for why he needed so much grain ground quickly. He paid well. He kept looking at the eastern road.

Brin did not ask him anything. She watched him the way she watched anything that moved differently than it should.

He left on the third day. He did not say where he was going.

After he was gone her father said: what do you make of that?

She said: he was running from something. Not toward.

Her father looked at her for a moment.

She said: the horses were tired. If he were going somewhere important he would have rested them more. He was just putting distance between himself and the eastern road.

Her father said: hm.

She said: I'm going to leave in the spring.

---

Her mother said: finally.

This was not what Brin had expected.

Her mother said: you've been leaving in your head for four years. I was wondering when the rest of you would catch up.

Brin thought about this. She said: I didn't want to leave before I was ready.

Her mother said: how did you know you were ready?

Brin said: I recognized something I'd been doing without knowing I was doing it.

Her mother looked at her for a moment. Then she said: that sounds right.

---

She spent the winter learning the mill more completely than she already knew it — not because she would need it, but because she was leaving it to her father and she wanted it left in good shape. The gears. The stones. The seasonal adjustments. The way the water ran lower in August and what you did about it.

Her father watched her do this without saying anything for a long time. Then he said: you learned to be thorough here.

She said: yes.

He said: that's not nothing, to take with you.

She said: no. It's not.

---

She left on the first mild morning of March, when the eastern road was passable but not crowded.

Her mother gave her bread. Her father gave her a small knife with a handle she had watched him use her whole childhood — not the good one, the plain one, the one he actually reached for. She understood the difference.

She walked east without looking back.

She did not know exactly what she was walking toward. She knew the direction was east, and that east was where Lian had gone, and that Lian had said *she didn't run*, which was a specific kind of acknowledgment she had been carrying for five years without quite deciding what to do with it.

She was going to find out.
