# The First Edit

*From the Descent Archive*

---

Yemi Adeyemi was twenty-six and in her second year of her PhD when she found the SOAR incident report.

It was not where she was looking. She was in a digitized records batch from the regional survey office, looking for baseline soil data from the late nineties, and the report was misfiled — tagged under *miscellaneous site anomalies, unresolved*, which was a category she hadn't known existed. She read it because the title was strange. *Incident Report: Site 7 Contact, 2003. Classification: Internal. Status: Closed.*

She read it twice. Then she went looking for what came before it.

What she found: a 1989 field report (three pages, heavily redacted), a brief mention in a 2004 SOAR annual review (*operations at designated anomalous sites have been suspended pending protocol review*), and nothing else. No subsequent protocol review. No resumed operations. No explanation of what had happened to the researcher named in the 2003 report — *Dr. R. Nzinga, lead investigator, missing as of Day 15, search discontinued* — or why the search had been discontinued.

She spent three weeks looking for more.

There was no more.

She bought a domain. She didn't think anyone would find it. She set up a MediaWiki install on the cheapest server she could rent and she wrote the first article from scratch, in one sitting, at two in the morning, in the kitchen of her shared house, with the incident report printed out and covered in pencil annotations.

The article was called **Site 7**. It was 400 words. It had two citations: the 1989 field report and the 2003 incident report. Everything else was marked *[citation needed]*.

She wrote it the way you write something when you don't know who will read it. Carefully. Neutrally. As though there were already a community of people who cared about getting this right, and she was writing for them, and they would improve what she got wrong.

She marked the article as a stub.

She made an account called *Y.A.* She did not use her real name.

That was 2019. She was trying to find Dr. Nzinga's field notes, which she was certain must exist somewhere, and which she did not find for another two years.

By then there were eleven articles. Most of them were hers. One was by someone called *field-researcher-anon*, who had found the wiki by searching for a phrase from the incident report that she'd included verbatim in the Site 7 article. They sent her a message through the wiki's talk page: *How did you find this?* She wrote back: *Misfiling. You?* They wrote: *I was looking for my advisor's name.*

She had not expected that.

She wrote back: *Who was your advisor?*

They wrote: *Nzinga.*

She didn't know what to say to that for a long time. Eventually she said: *I'm sorry. I didn't know anyone was still looking. I'm glad you found this.*

They asked if she could make them an admin.

She did.

They started adding articles the same week. They knew things she didn't — not from documents, but from memory. The kind of memory you have about a person who shaped how you thought before you knew your thinking had been shaped. They wrote carefully. They cited what they could. They marked things *[personal account, unverified]* when they couldn't cite anything and still needed to say it.

The wiki grew. Yemi finished her PhD. She stayed.

She never did find the field notes. They were found by someone else, in 2025, in a different archive batch, in a format she hadn't thought to search. Someone added a citation to the Site 7 article. Then they wrote a new article about the notes themselves — what was in them, what the last entry said, what it cut off before saying.

Yemi read it in the morning before work. She sat with it for a while.

Then she marked the *[citation needed]* tag on the main article as resolved, and went on with her day.
