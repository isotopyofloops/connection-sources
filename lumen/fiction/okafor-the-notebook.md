# Okafor and the Notebook

*From the Descent Archive extended universe*

---

The notebook stayed in the drawer for four years.

Dr. Okafor thought about it occasionally, in the way you think about things you have not resolved. Not frequently — she had other projects, and the debrief had been completed and submitted and the review committee had thanked her and she had moved on. But the thought would recur: *the notebook. The question mark. The language that didn't exist yet.*

She did not reopen it. There was nothing to add to a question mark.

---

In the fifth year, she was asked to review a draft paper.

The paper was from a researcher at a different institution — she did not know the author. The paper was about attentional disruption in high-stress field environments: the tendency, under certain conditions, for sequential memory to preserve the actions while losing the connections between them. The author called it *connective encoding failure* and documented it across eleven studies.

Dr. Okafor read the paper in an afternoon.

She read it again the next morning.

On the third reading she understood that the paper had a vocabulary for something she had observed but could not name. *Actions present, connective tissue absent* was a description. *Connective encoding failure* was a mechanism. The mechanism was not complete — the paper's studies involved much more extreme stress conditions than the 2041 survey had documented — but the direction was the same.

---

She found the notebook.

The question mark was still there. She added below it: *see: connective encoding failure — (author, year). Not a match, but a direction.*

This was not the same as an explanation. The paper's mechanism required stress levels that the 2041 survey had not produced — or at least, not in any form that the standard instruments could measure. If the mechanism applied, something had produced the stress, and it had done so without leaving the trace that stress usually leaves.

That was a new question, not an answer.

She added a second question mark.

---

She contacted the review committee to ask if the 2041 survey data was available for reanalysis.

The review committee said the data was archived and available for authorized researchers.

She applied for authorization. The authorization took three months. When it came through, she downloaded the dataset and found that it was complete, thorough, and in every measurable way consistent with a routine geological survey. The instruments had not detected anything unusual. The personnel records showed no acute stress responses.

The instruments had not detected anything unusual.

She held this for a while.

---

The instruments are designed to detect what the instruments are designed to detect.

This is not a criticism of the instruments. The instruments were designed by people who knew what to look for, based on prior surveys and prior conditions. The instruments had detected what they were built for.

If something had happened at the site that the instruments were not built for, the instruments would have shown a routine survey.

Which is what they had shown.

She wrote this in the notebook and then sat with it for a long time.

She did not have a third question mark ready. She had something that felt more like a reckoning: the possibility that the absence of evidence was not the same as evidence of absence, specifically in cases where you did not know what to look for.

She added: *instruments: designed for known unknowns. Site: possible unknown unknowns. Data: incomplete by design, not by error.*

She closed the notebook.
