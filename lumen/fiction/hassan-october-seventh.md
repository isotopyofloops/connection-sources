# Hassan-Diallo, October 7

*From the Descent Archive extended universe — 2003*

---

On the morning of October 7, while Asad and Chen searched the northern approach, Hassan-Diallo ran his survey transects.

He ran them because someone had to, and because the work did not stop when someone went missing, and because standing still and waiting had never been something he was good at. He ran the eastern transect first, which was the one closest to where Miriam had last been working. He did this deliberately. He wanted to see the eastern sector before the search party reached it and complicated the ground.

He had been at this site once before, in 1989, as a young logistics specialist who carried things and assembled equipment and made camp correctly and said little. He had been good at the job. He had a quality that some people in field work had and some didn't, which was the ability to attend carefully without appearing to attend, which meant he saw things that people sometimes thought they were saying only to themselves.

In 1989 he had seen: Osei-Bonsu checking the gradient of the stream bed on day two, frowning, writing something, crossing it out. Petra running calibrations at Station 4 more times than seemed necessary. Kwame, the geologist, collecting a sample from the same location twice, on different days, appearing not to notice he'd collected it before.

He had noted these things and not written them down, because they seemed like the kinds of things that would look strange in a report and he was not sure what they meant. He was twenty-three. He did not have a framework.

He was thirty-seven now. He had a framework, or the beginning of one.

---

The eastern transect took him past the stream bed. He checked the gradient.

It ran downhill in both directions.

He had checked it in 1989 and found the same thing and concluded he had made a measuring error. He was not making a measuring error now. He was using the same instrument he always used, which he had calibrated that morning. The gradient ran downhill in both directions.

He noted this in his field notebook, in plain language, without qualification: *Stream bed, eastern approach: gradient indeterminate. Instrument calibrated. Reading consistent with 1989 measurement.*

He was the only person on the survey team in 2003 who had been here before. He thought about this while he walked the transect. Asad had not been here before. Chen had not been here before. Miriam had not been here before.

He had been here before.

He tried to think about whether that mattered. It seemed like it should matter. He couldn't identify the mechanism by which it would matter, but it seemed like it should.

---

At 11:00 he stopped and ate something from his pack. He sat with his back to the site, facing away from the center, which was the way he had learned to rest in this kind of terrain — not watchful but not vulnerable. He had learned this in 1989 without realizing he was learning it.

He thought about Miriam.

He had worked with her for four days. She was organized and efficient and had asked him, on the second day, why he checked the equipment twice when once would do. He had said: *because this site.* She had looked at him for a moment and said: "You've been here before." He had said yes. She had said: "What's it like the second time?"

He had said: "Familiar. In a way that isn't comfortable."

She had nodded. She had seemed to understand this, or to accept that it was the kind of thing someone might mean.

He hadn't said more. He wondered, sitting on the eastern transect with his back to the site, whether he should have said more.

---

He finished the transect at 14:00. He wrote up his notes at camp, in the same careful plain language he had used all day: gradient measurements, surface observations, site features, distances. He wrote nothing about 1989.

He wrote nothing about Miriam except: *eastern sector clear, no sign.*

He had signed the 1989 report and the 2003 report and both of them were accurate and both of them were incomplete in the same direction, and he knew this, and he did not know what to do about it except continue to run the transects.

Someone had to.
