# Asad: October 2003

He received them on a Wednesday. The officer handed them across the desk — a plastic bag, sealed, the kind used for outdoor equipment in wet weather. Asad's name was written on the outside in her handwriting.

He had not known she knew his name.

He carried the bag to his office and set it on the desk. It was eleven in the morning. He had a seminar to teach at two. He sat and looked at the bag for a while.

The university had been notified. The search was ongoing. He had spoken to her parents once, briefly, the day before. There was nothing to say that he was in a position to say. He said he was sorry. He had been her thesis supervisor for four months. That was the full extent of his position.

He opened the bag.

The field notes were spiral-bound, green-covered, the standard survey notebook. He had suggested the format. Most of his students used them. The cover had her name and the thesis title, written in the same precise hand that was on the outside of the bag.

He read them from the beginning.

There were forty-three pages of notes. Measurements, observations, cross-section sketches. The handwriting was consistent and clear up to the last few entries, which were made in lower light, probably inside the shaft, the pen pressing harder to compensate. The observations were careful. She had been doing real work.

The final entry was a single dash.

He sat with the notebook for some time after that. Then he closed it and put it back in the bag.

The university required him to file a thesis status form. The form had four options: *complete*, *in progress*, *incomplete*, and *withdrawn*. He considered the options. *Incomplete* was accurate but carried an administrative implication — that the student had failed to meet a deadline, that the work was in arrears. *Withdrawn* implied a voluntary act, a decision to stop, which was not accurate either.

He marked *withdrawn*. It was the better word for what had happened, or close enough to the better word that it would serve.

He did not reproduce the contents of the field notes in any report. The missing persons report asked whether she had left any materials at the site. He wrote: *field notes, sealed, now in the possession of the supervising faculty member.* He was asked whether the notes contained any relevant information. He wrote: *the notes document survey work consistent with the stated thesis topic.*

Both of these things were true.

He kept the notebook. He did not know what to do with it and so did nothing. It remained in his desk for the next thirty-eight years. The desk moved with him across three institutions. The notebook stayed in the second drawer on the left.

He did not open it again.
