# Boney and the Good Idea

Isla had a good idea and then lost it.

She had been doing something — cutting out shapes for the display board, she thought, or maybe it was the geography project, she wasn't sure — when the good idea arrived. It was about something. She had almost had it. And then someone spoke to her and it was gone.

"What was it about?" said Boney.

"I don't know," said Isla. "That's the problem. I don't know what it was about."

"What were you thinking about before it arrived?"

Isla tried to remember. She had been thinking about the shapes, or the geography. And then the idea. And then Marcus had said something about the glue being in the wrong drawer, and now nothing.

"Something about maps," she said.

"That might be it," said Boney.

"No, the idea wasn't about maps. It was about something else that I was thinking while I was looking at the maps."

Boney nodded. He had lost many good ideas. Most of them had been about tactical matters — sword angles, enemy positions, which way to retreat — but the experience of having an idea and then not having it was the same whatever the topic.

"Usually," he said, "if you stop trying to remember it, it comes back."

"That doesn't help now."

"No," he agreed. "It helps later. If you relax and do something else, the idea often finds its way back."

Isla looked at him with the expression of someone who thought this was not useful advice. It was not useful advice. It was, however, true advice, which is sometimes a different thing.

"What if it doesn't come back?" she said.

"Then it was either not a good idea, or it was a good idea that visited too early."

"What's a good idea that visits too early?"

Boney considered this. "One that you weren't ready for. You can't hold it yet because you haven't built the place where it would go. It'll come back later when there's room for it."

Isla cut another shape out of the paper. She was not thinking about the idea. She was thinking about whether she had cut the shape straight.

"My sword," said Boney, after a moment.

"What about it?"

"I had an idea about it once, when I was in the middle of a battle. Completely lost it. Didn't find it for forty years."

Isla looked at the sword leaning against the wall. "What was the idea?"

"That it might not be magic," said Boney. "That the magic was something else."

Isla thought about this. "Was that the idea from before? The one you lost?"

"I don't know," said Boney. "It might be. Or it might just be what I arrived at on my own, and the original idea was different."

"How do you tell the difference?"

"You don't," said Boney. "Usually."

Isla looked at her shapes. She had an idea — not the same idea, she thought, but possibly a relative of it. She wrote it down on the corner of the paper before she could lose it.

Boney watched her do this and thought: there. That's the part they don't teach.
