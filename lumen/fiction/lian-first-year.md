# Lian, First Year

Her master had been teaching for twenty years and he taught the way that people teach who have been doing something for twenty years: he assumed she would figure out the parts he didn't say.

This was not unkind. It was how he had learned, and it had worked for him, and he had not considered that it was a particular method rather than the natural way of learning things.

She figured out the parts he didn't say. This took longer than if he had said them, but it also meant she owned the knowledge differently when she arrived at it — she had arrived at it through her own path, and she knew why it was true, not just that it was true.

She did not tell him this. He wouldn't have known what to do with it.

---

The first thing she learned that he did not teach:

The perimeter check at dawn was not about safety. It was about knowing the state of the ground before anything else happened. The safety was a byproduct. The primary function was calibration — giving yourself a baseline reading so that everything you encountered afterward could be measured against it.

She had been doing the check for three weeks before she understood this. Once she understood it, the check took the same amount of time but felt different. She was more present in it. She noticed more.

She did not tell him she had figured this out either. She suspected he knew it and had always known it, but had never said it because it was the kind of thing that could not be told — it had to be discovered, and discovering it was part of the function.

---

The second thing she learned that he did not teach:

The correct response to something you cannot yet handle is not retreat. It is positioning. You move to where you have more options, not to where the thing cannot follow.

She had retreated three times before she understood this. The fourth time, she moved sideways instead. The thing she was trying to get away from was not a physical threat — it was a conversation she didn't know how to finish — but the principle was the same. You move to where you have more options.

After the fourth time, she understood that retreat and positioning looked the same from the outside. The difference was what you were oriented toward.

---

In the winter of her first year, Voss said to her: you're learning faster than expected.

She said: what were you expecting.

He said: the usual. Six months to get the physical practices working, another six for them to become automatic. You're ahead of that.

She said: I think I learn differently than the way you teach.

He said: how so.

She said: you leave gaps. I fill in the gaps myself. The gaps are where I actually learn.

He was quiet for a moment. Then he said: I didn't know I was doing that.

She said: I know. It still works.

He said: what happens if a student doesn't fill in the gaps.

She said: I don't know. I imagine it's frustrating for both of you.

He said: yes. He said: I'll have to think about that.

She said: you don't have to change it for me.

He said: I'm not thinking about changing it for you.

She understood, then, that she was not the only student he had ever had, and that some of them had not filled in the gaps, and that he had been carrying something about that for a long time.

She did not say anything else about it.

Some things were not hers to fix.
