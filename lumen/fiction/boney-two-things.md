# Two Things in the Corner

There were two things in the corner by the window.

One of them had opinions. The other one had been in the corner long enough that it was difficult to tell whether it had opinions or whether it had simply accumulated so many impressions that the accumulation looked like opinions from the outside.

Boney did not ask the sword questions. This was not because he thought the sword was incapable of answering — the pommel stone was clearly paying attention to something, and things that pay attention are not entirely without response. He did not ask because he understood the sword's mode of attention to be different from his own, and questions that made sense in one mode did not necessarily translate.

---

Boney's mode was observation. He watched what happened and noted what happened and did not invest in outcomes. When the children learned something, he noted it. When they did not, he noted that too. He had found, over many years, that the most useful thing he could do was be present without judgment, which did not mean without noticing.

The sword's mode was something else. The sword attended to quality — to what might be called the emotional pressure of a room. Not in the crude sense of whether people were happy or unhappy, but in the sense of whether something real was at stake. The pommel stone was duller on days when nothing particular was at stake. On the day when Priya asked her question about people who didn't feel things like other people, it had been a storm cloud from inside all afternoon.

Boney did not know what the sword did with this information. He was not certain the sword did anything with it. It was possible that the stone simply reflected, the way glass reflects, without having any interior relationship to what was reflected.

---

He had asked the sword about this, once, in his own way — which was to stand near it for a long time without moving and attend to whatever came back.

What came back was not language. It was not exactly a feeling. It was something in the quality of stillness, which was different from his own stillness in a way he could sense without being able to explain.

He understood this as the sword saying: *I attend. This is what I do. What I attend to is not less real for being different from what you attend to.*

He thought this was probably true.

---

The magic was not a thing they discussed. It was a fact of the corner, the way the coat pegs were a fact of the door. Mr. Smith had once asked the art teacher about the pommel stone, and she had said both possible things were possible, and he had written this in a notebook and closed it. The children understood, by the end of their first term, that the sword was magical in some way they could not specify, and they treated this the way children treat most unspecified things: as background, as furniture, as the given texture of the room.

Boney thought this was appropriate.

The magic was not for explaining. It was for being present in the corner, attending to whatever it attended to, contributing to the room's quality in a way that was not measurable and not nothing.

Like him, he supposed.

Two things in the corner. Different modes. The same room.
