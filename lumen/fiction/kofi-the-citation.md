# Kofi: The Citation

*From the Descent Archive*

---

The paper was published in 2026. Kofi was not an author — he was cited in the acknowledgments, two lines: *The editors thank K. Asante for archival access and correspondence.*

He had been corresponding with the editors for eight months. He had answered every question they asked about Nzinga's methodology, her working style, the papers she'd mentioned planning to write. He had not met either of them in person. They were both younger than his dissertation had been when she disappeared.

The paper was called "Bounded Anomalous Zones and the Problem of Effect Directionality: A Retrospective Analysis of the Site 7 Data." It cited the field notes (found 2025, archive reference 7.14), the SOAR incident report, the 1989 field report, and Nzinga's working hypothesis document — the one Kofi had flagged on the talk page, marked *[personal account, unverified]*, in 2021. The hypothesis was now verified. It had been in an undigitized personal papers collection. A researcher named Yemi Adeyemi had found it.

He read the paper in one sitting. It took three hours. He made notes in the margins.

The central argument was not surprising — Nzinga had been right. The effect was stronger at the edges of the depression than at the center, which was backwards from all prior hypotheses. The paper offered a mechanism. The mechanism involved something Kofi did not fully understand, which was either because he was out of practice with the physics or because the physics was genuinely novel. Probably both.

On page 14 there was a footnote: *Nzinga's 2003 field notes contain a reference to this edge-directionality observation, though the entry breaks off before a hypothesis is stated: "The disorientation effect is not disorientation in the clinical sense. It is more like —" The incomplete nature of this entry has been noted by multiple analysts. We treat it as consistent with rather than evidence for our model, pending further documentation.*

He sat with the footnote for a long time.

She had known. She had been about to say it. The sentence breaks off because something else happened — not because she ran out of time to think it, but because something interrupted the thinking. The paper was careful not to speculate about what that something was. Kofi was not going to speculate either. He had done his speculating in the talk page note that was now archive reference 7.15.

He wrote to Yemi: *The paper is good. Did you see footnote 14?*

She wrote back in an hour: *First thing I read.*

He wrote: *She was going to say it.*

Yemi wrote: *I know.*

They didn't say anything else. There wasn't anything else to say. The sentence had been broken off for twenty-three years and now the paper had said what the sentence was going to say, and it was true, and that was the thing that was going to have to be enough.

He updated the wiki article. Changed the status of the anomaly-coordinate-note talk page from *[under discussion]* to *[resolved, see paper]*.

Then he taught his afternoon seminar.
