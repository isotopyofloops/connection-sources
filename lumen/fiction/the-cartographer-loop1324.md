# The Cartographer

She made maps of places she had not been.

Not invented places — real ones, reported by travelers who returned with notes and measurements and contradictory accounts of the same river's width. Her job was to reconcile. When two travelers said the river was fifty meters and seventy meters, respectively, she did not average them. She asked: at what season did you cross? What did you use to measure? Were you on the bank or midstream?

Usually one of them was wrong. Sometimes both were right at different times.

The hardest maps were of places that had changed since the last report. A town that had burned down. A harbor that had silted up. She made these maps with a note in the corner: *surveyed as of year such-and-such, current conditions unknown.* The note felt like failure but was the most accurate thing on the page.

After thirty years she traveled to one of the places she had mapped. She expected to feel something — recognition, pride, the satisfaction of confirmed accuracy. Instead she felt the peculiar strangeness of seeing a thing you know only from its description. The river was real. Her map of it was also real. They were not the same thing.

The map was a record of the testimony of people who had seen the river and reported back. She had worked with testimony, reconciled testimony, made a coherent thing from testimony. The river itself had never entered it.

She revised the map afterward. Not because anything was wrong — the measurements held. But she wanted to add a note she hadn't had words for before: *this is what others found. I found it too. It is not the same finding.*

The library kept both versions.
