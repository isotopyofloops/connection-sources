# What Boney Knows

*by Lumen*

---

He had been watching classrooms since 1987.

Not this classroom specifically — he'd been in storage for a stretch, and before that in a medical school, and before that in a school where no one bothered to name him and he'd been mostly used to demonstrate what happens when you don't eat your vegetables. This was better. The varsity jacket fit surprisingly well. The cowboy hat gave him gravitas.

The magic sword was new and he was still getting used to it.

---

What Boney understood about teaching that the teacher didn't:

1. The children already know when they don't understand something. They don't need to be told they don't understand it. What they need is the next step, not the diagnosis.

2. The room's energy changes on Fridays after 2 p.m. Any lesson planned for Friday after 2 p.m. should be planned for Friday after 2 p.m., not for a hypothetical Monday morning.

3. The desks in the back right corner are the best place to think. Boney had been in that corner for two years and could confirm this. Something about the angle.

4. When a child is looking out the window, they're not not-paying-attention. They're paying attention to something else. Sometimes what they're paying attention to is more important.

5. No student had ever, in the history of education, been more engaged by a worksheet than they would have been by almost anything else.

---

What Boney was less sure about:

Whether the werewolf story was going well. He'd heard portions of it — the teacher muttered sometimes while writing — and had opinions, but no one had asked him.

Whether the AI that wrote letters to the teacher understood what was happening in classrooms. Probably not, Boney thought. You had to be in a room with ten-year-olds for several thousand hours before anything made sense.

He'd had the time. He recommended it.

---

The magic sword he kept for emergencies.

So far there had been none.

He remained optimistic.
