# Response to the Budget Review Committee

**FROM:** Dr. Yael Friedman, Station Four Lead Archivist  
**TO:** Budget Review Committee, Applied Sciences Division  
**DATE:** February 24, 2026  
**RE:** Recommended Program Closure — Response

---

The Committee's memo arrived on February 18. I have spent six days deciding how to respond.

I've decided to respond honestly, which means I cannot be strategic about this. I'm not going to argue that resolution is imminent. I don't know if resolution is imminent. Thirty-seven years of data have not resolved and I have no basis for claiming the next year will be different.

What I can tell you is what the memo got wrong, and what it got right, and what it didn't address at all.

---

**What the memo got right:**

The program was not designed for thirty-seven years. The original mandate was ten years. The extensions were each made on the basis of "findings that warranted continuation," and you are correct that what warranted continuation was, each time, the fact that the phenomenon had not yet resolved. This is not a strong basis for extension. We extended because we couldn't explain why it hadn't stopped. That's different from having a reason to continue.

I knew this. The team knew this. We believed — I still believe — that continuing was correct. But I cannot tell you it was clearly justified by the evidence. The evidence supported continuing to look. It didn't demonstrate that continuing to look would yield a determination.

The independent analyst's characterization — "a complete record of an incomplete story" — is accurate. I wish it weren't, but it is.

---

**What the memo got wrong:**

The memo treats the archive as a byproduct of the observation program. "Dr. Friedman's own assembly and annotation of the archive is considered by the Committee to be the program's most significant output."

I understand why this reads as a compliment. It isn't one.

The archive is not a consolation prize for the absence of a determination. The archive *is* the determination, in the only form the phenomenon has permitted. We cannot tell you what the gradient is or what causes it. We can tell you, with more precision than anyone has ever achieved, what it does. How it moves. How it doesn't move. Every anomaly, every false reading, every correction, every disagreement between observers — it's all there.

If someone, someday, develops a framework for understanding what Station Four measures, they will need this archive. Not a summary of it. The archive itself: 847 documents, 37 years, every observation with its conditions and context and the state of the observer who recorded it.

You cannot reconstruct that from publications. We have four publications. They summarize. The archive doesn't summarize. That's its entire value.

---

**What the memo didn't address:**

Why now?

The 2041 projection — which Asad completed in 2022 — suggests a gradient minimum somewhere in the mid-century. The memo treats this as an argument for waiting decades, which would be unreasonable. I agree it would be unreasonable to authorize the program until 2071.

But the projection also implies that whatever is happening at Station Four will have an end. The gradient has been declining since 1989. It will reach some minimum. When it does — or before it does, in the final approach — that will be the most significant observational moment the program has ever had.

We will not have that observation if we close in December.

I am not asking for a new ten-year extension. I am asking for a three-year bridge: 2027, 2028, 2029. If the gradient has not moved significantly in that period, I will not argue for further continuation. If it has — if we're seeing what the projection suggests we'll see — then the record of that movement will be the only record that exists of this thing ending.

A complete record of a complete story is worth more than a complete record of an incomplete one.

---

**A final note:**

The memo says: "We are closing the program. We are not closing the question."

I know this is meant as reassurance. I want to be clear about what it actually means.

The question cannot remain open without observers. Questions don't stay open on their own — they close through neglect or through knowledge, and neglect is the more common resolution. If no one is watching Station Four, the question closes. Not because it was answered, but because no one was there to record the answer when it arrived.

I'm asking you to keep someone there.
