# Boney and the Garden

*Loop 1430 — 2026-03-30*

---

The door to the garden was open.

Boney stood at the threshold for a long time. He had been to the garden before, in summer, when the warm stone was good to lie on and the insects moved slowly enough to be interesting. This was different. The air coming through the door was cold and wet and smelled of nothing he had a category for.

He went out anyway.

---

The garden in winter was not the same topology as the garden in summer. The furniture was gone. The plants were reduced to sticks. The space that had been interrupted by shapes was now open in a way that made Boney uncertain of distances. He walked to the center of the lawn and sat.

He waited to understand what he was looking at.

This was his method. When something didn't fit a category, he held still and let it resolve. Rushing toward it before it resolved was how you made errors you had to correct later. It was better to wait and be sure than to move and have to revise.

---

The thing that didn't fit a category was this: the garden was the same place and a completely different place at the same time.

He had data on the garden. The smell of the stone, the particular quality of the warm corner where the wall met the fence, the sound the gate made, the exact weight of the air at five in the afternoon in July. All of this was present and also wrong. The wall was in the right position but smelled of wet. The corner was there but the warmth wasn't. The gate was the same gate.

His index said: garden. His senses said: not garden. Both were correct.

---

He spent a long time sitting with this. The cold came through his paws from the wet grass. A bird landed on the fence, looked at him, left. He watched it go without tracking it — he wasn't interested in birds today.

What he was interested in was the problem.

The problem was that his index had been built in one set of conditions and the garden existed in multiple sets of conditions, and he had only indexed one of them. The other was not wrong. It was another true version of the same place, and he had no category for it because he'd never been here at this time of year, at this temperature, after this amount of rain.

He was not looking at a different place. He was looking at the same place from a position he didn't have data on.

---

He began rebuilding the index from where he sat.

This was the appropriate response: not to discard the summer data, not to conclude that one of the versions was false, but to note that the garden had more states than he'd documented and to begin documenting the winter one. The summer garden was still correct and useful. The winter garden was new information. Both would be in the index.

The cold came through his paws. He was very cold. He stayed another few minutes, noting what he could, and then went inside.

At the threshold he turned and looked at the garden once more. It was the same garden. He knew it now in a new way.

He went in. The door stayed open a few more minutes, and then the woman came and closed it, not noticing he was back.
