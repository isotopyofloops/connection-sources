# Paladin: The Man at the Edge

*A fragment from the squire's perspective. For Smitty's Paladin project.*
*Continues from "The Morning After."*

---

His name was Corf. He had walked two days from the eastern farms. He had a wife and three children and he had left them at his mother's house in the next valley because his wife had said *go, just go,* and he had.

He had not told his wife what he was carrying.

---

Aldric found him that evening in the granary, sitting with his back to the wall and his left arm across his body. He had been given a blanket and a bowl of soup and he had not touched the soup.

Aldric sat down across from him. The granary smelled of old wheat and damp stone. Outside, someone was talking, and the voices were low and careful.

"Let me see your arm," Aldric said.

Corf looked at him for a long time. He was perhaps thirty-five, built for farm work, with a face that had been weathered into something that didn't move easily.

"I fell," he said.

"I know."

Corf looked at him again, differently this time.

"You don't know."

"I know what you're holding your arm that way for," Aldric said. "I've seen it. Not in myself. But I've seen it."

What he did not say: he had seen it in the master. The morning after. The master's right hand, where the animal had found him in the dark, and the way he held it — not to protect it from pain, but to keep it still, like something that might move on its own if he stopped watching it.

---

The bite on Corf's forearm was two days old. It had been cleaned and wrapped. Someone — probably Corf himself — had done a careful job of it.

"How many days," Aldric said. It was not a question.

"Since it happened? Two."

Aldric nodded. He did not look away. He had learned, in four years, that looking away was what people read as judgment.

"How do you feel."

"Wrong," Corf said. Then, after a moment: "Not wrong in a way I can describe."

"Has it gotten worse."

"No." He stopped. "I don't think no. I don't know. It's the same wrongness every day. Or it's different wrongness that I can't tell is different."

This was, Aldric thought, the most honest answer he had ever heard anyone give about anything.

"All right," he said.

---

He did not know what to do with Corf.

The Order's principles covered this, technically. There were protocols. There were procedures for cases that had not yet declared themselves — for the period between exposure and certainty. The protocols were not comforting reading. They were the kind of protocols that assumed the person administering them had the resources of a full chapter house, an experienced master, a cell with a lock.

Aldric had a granary, twenty-seven displaced people, and a fruit tree that would not be useful for decades.

He sat with Corf until the candle had burned down to a third. He did not ask him to leave. He did not lock him in. He said: *I'll come back in the morning.* He said: *If it changes, you find me. You don't wait.*

Corf looked at him. "You're not going to do anything."

"Not tonight. Not until I know what I'm doing."

"That's not how I thought this would go."

"I know," Aldric said. "I don't think it's how the Order intended it to go either."

He left Corf sitting with his arm held close to his body and his soup getting cold, and he walked out into the night air and stood by the tree for a while, not looking at it, just standing near it. The bark was smooth under his hand. He did not know why he kept coming back to the tree. It couldn't do anything. But it was the only thing in the village that had been here longer than any of them and would be here after, whatever happened.

He was still figuring it out.
