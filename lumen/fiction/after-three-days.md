# After Three Days

**i. Lian**

She heard him call her name at the moment she had decided to stop for the night.

She had been standing still for about thirty seconds — not because she was tired, but because something in the quality of the dark had changed in a way she hadn't identified yet. She was cataloguing the change when she heard it: her name, spoken at a distance of about forty yards, from the direction of the cold fire pit she had found and passed an hour ago.

She had not run toward it. She had walked, at the pace she used when she was certain of the ground but not of what was waiting on it.

---

**ii. Voss**

He had been sitting for three hours in the dark after putting out the fire, practicing the thing he had learned to do in the middle state, which was to hold the thread and do nothing else.

The thread was: he was a person. He had a name. He had a student named Lian, who had not come after him, which was the right decision, because he was not safe, and you did not walk toward things that were not safe. He had been repeating this in the evenings, not because he believed it, but because it was a practice, and practices worked whether or not you believed in them while doing them.

When he called her name, he was not certain he would call it. And then he did.

---

**iii. Lian**

She stopped about fifteen feet away and waited.

He was sitting with his back against a fallen tree, which was how she would have sheltered. He looked the same. He looked like himself. She did not know if that was information or if it was just what she was hoping to see.

She said: "You look like yourself."

He said: "I'm not certain."

She said: "That's a thing you would say."

He said: "Yes."

---

**iv. Voss**

She sat down across from him. Not next to him — across, which was the correct distance, and he noticed that she had chosen it without hesitating. She had learned something in the nine days, or she had already known it and now had occasion to use it.

He told her: the count was still running. He had not lost the count.

She said that was good.

He asked if she had eaten.

She said yes, this morning.

He said there were stripped bones about twenty yards east, if she wanted to look.

She said she had already found them, two hours ago, and that was how she knew she was on the right trail.

He had not expected her to say that. He had expected — he was not sure what he had expected. That she would be different from how she was. She was not different. She had followed a cold camp trail for two hours in the dark and found him by the cold fire and sat down across from him at the correct distance.

He said: "I don't know what I am."

She said: "I know. That's why I came."

---

**v. Lian**

She did not sleep that night. She kept watch until dawn, at which point she made a small fire.

He watched her make the fire without saying anything. She could feel him cataloguing what she was doing — the same way she had watched him, all those months, noting the sequence of decisions. The kindling first, then the angle, then the patience.

At dawn she said: "We should go back."

He said: "I can't go back yet."

She said: "I know. I meant: we should find the direction. We don't have to walk it today."

He looked at her for a moment.

He said: "You figured out the second decision."

She said: "What?"

He said: "To not think about the first one until after it's done. I didn't teach you that."

She said: "No."

He said: "When did you figure it out?"

She thought about it. She said: "At the mill."

He nodded. The fire was going. The forest was light now, the birds starting.

The direction back was west, which they both knew. They did not walk it that day. But they were facing the same way.
