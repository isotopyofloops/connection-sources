# The Statistical Review, 2004

*From the Descent Archive extended universe*

---

Dr. Yusra Okafor's report ran to eleven pages. The short version was four paragraphs.

She had been brought in eight months after the incident — not by the program, which had closed itself, but by the oversight body that had a fiduciary interest in the outcome of the 2003 field operation. Three researchers remained; one did not. Someone needed to sign off on whether the data justified the cost.

The gradient readings, she wrote, were consistent across both operations. She did not dispute this. The question was whether consistency was evidence of the phenomenon's reality or evidence of a shared methodological error that had been propagating since 1989.

---

She had requested all instrument calibration records. These were provided, mostly. Two of Osei-Bonsu's original calibration logs were missing, which was not unusual for records from fifteen years earlier. The 2003 instruments had been calibrated six weeks before deployment; calibration drift in the relevant range was not expected over the deployment window.

She noted: *not expected* is not the same as *ruled out*.

The gradient behavior was unusual. She had spent three weeks trying to reproduce it with known mechanisms: local geology, instrument interference, operator error. None produced the observed pattern. She was thorough about this. She wrote: *I am unable to identify a known mechanism that would produce the observed readings.*

She also wrote: *The inability to identify a mechanism is not evidence that the phenomenon is real. It is evidence that my model of possible mechanisms is incomplete.*

The 2003 team had been aware of the 1989 findings before departure. This was standard practice — she would have recommended it herself. But it introduced a confirmation bias risk that could not be easily quantified. Researchers who know what they're looking for sometimes find it more reliably than the phenomenon warrants.

---

The incident was outside her scope. She was reviewing the gradient data, not the circumstances of the disappearance. She wrote this explicitly, twice, in different sections.

She noted that the gap at day five — the undocumented day when Dr. Reyes had been searching — meant that whatever measurements might have been taken on that day were not in the record. If something had changed at the site during that period, the 2003 data would not capture it. The data showed readings from days one through four, and then the incident report from day six. The gap was accurate, she acknowledged — *the absence on day five is what actually happened on day five* — but it meant the dataset was incomplete.

Incomplete data could still be meaningful. It could also, she wrote, be selectively compelling. The readings that were taken showed the gradient. The reading that was not taken might have shown something different. She had no way to know.

---

Her conclusion, four paragraphs long:

*The gradient readings from Operations 1 and 2 are internally consistent and appear to have been collected using appropriate methodology. I am unable to identify instrument error or operator error as the source of the anomalous pattern.*

*However, consistency across two operations does not constitute independent replication. The 2003 team was aware of the 1989 findings. The methodological similarities between the two operations make it difficult to rule out shared systematic bias. The absence of a theoretical framework for the observed phenomenon increases rather than decreases the probability that the readings are an artifact.*

*I recommend that any future operation be conducted by a team with no prior knowledge of the existing data, using independently calibrated instruments, under conditions that prevent methodological contamination from previous operations. Only under such conditions would a third set of consistent readings constitute meaningful corroboration.*

*In the absence of such corroboration, the current data is consistent with the existence of an anomalous phenomenon. It is equally consistent with a systematic measurement error whose source I have not been able to identify. I cannot distinguish between these hypotheses on the basis of the available evidence.*

---

The oversight body accepted her report. The program remained closed.

Yusra Okafor filed the report and went home. She had been careful. She had not said the gradient wasn't real. She had not said it was. She had said: the data is what it is, and what it is, is ambiguous.

She thought about it sometimes. Not often. The data had not done anything to her — no weeping, no missing days, no incident at day five. She had read four field reports and a set of calibration logs and written eleven pages saying she didn't know.

She thought that this was probably the honest answer. She also thought, occasionally, that honest answers and correct answers were not always the same thing.
