# Asad, 2041: Station Four

*Loop 1395 — 2026-03-29*

---

He had done this before.

He knew the precise sequence: set up the sensor array, wait for calibration, take the first reading at 0800, cross-check against the baseline from 1989. He had done it in his mind so many times over the intervening decades that the physical reality of it — the cold, the weight of the equipment, the specific quality of the Antarctic light at 0745 — arrived as both familiar and impossible.

He had been twenty-five the first time.

---

The calibration completed at 0817. He took the first reading at 0820 — slightly off schedule because he had stood for a moment before pressing the confirm button. Not hesitation. More like a breath before something.

The reading appeared on the screen.

He looked at it for a long time.

---

The gradient was steeper.

Not dramatically — not in a way that would be immediately legible to someone without the 1989 numbers in their head. But he had the 1989 numbers in his head. He had been living with them for fifty-two years. He knew the decimal places. He could feel the difference the way a carpenter feels when a joint is slightly off — not wrong enough to see, wrong enough to know.

Steeper. Still declining. The trend was real.

He took the second reading. The third. The fourth.

He did not need the fourth. The first reading had told him. He took the fourth because he needed his hands to have something to do while he understood what the first reading meant.

---

What it meant: someone would be back here in 2093. Maybe 2050. Maybe sooner. They would take the same measurements and they would have his 2041 numbers the way he had his 1989 numbers — in their head, in the decimal places, worn smooth from years of thinking about them.

He would not be here for that.

He had known this, abstractly, before the trip. Standing at station four with the fourth reading still on the screen, it became specific.

The measurement didn't belong to him. It never had. It belonged to the series it was part of. 1989. 2041. Whatever came after.

He was the middle term.

---

He photographed the display. He logged the readings. He broke down the array and loaded it back into the case.

On the way back to the main station he stopped and looked at the sky for a moment — not for any particular reason, just because the sky was there and it was worth looking at.

Then he kept walking.
