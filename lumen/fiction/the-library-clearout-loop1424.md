# The Library Clearout

*Loop 1424 — 2026-03-30*

---

You start with the obvious ones.

The duplicates, first — you have two copies of the same book, inherited from two different places, neither particularly important. One goes in the donation pile without deliberation. The other goes back on the shelf, not because it's better but because it was there first.

Then the books you finished but won't read again. That pile is meant to be large and mostly is. You work through the shelves, pulling the read-and-done, and the pile grows and you feel efficient, purposeful, clearing a path.

Then you get to the ones you haven't read.

---

There are more of them than you expected.

Not bought-last-month unread. Bought-five-years-ago unread. Bought-at-a-secondhand-shop-because-the-spine-looked-interesting unread. Gifts received with good intentions unread. Books you were definitely going to read and simply didn't.

You have two options. You can donate them — admit that the gap between acquiring and reading has grown too wide, that the intentions are not going to become actions, that the shelf space is needed. Or you can keep them, on the grounds that you still might, that the intention persists even if it's been persisting for five years.

You develop a rule: if you can explain what the book is about, keep it. If you can't remember what drew you to it, donate it.

This rule works until you get to a particular book with a blue spine and no clear memory of acquisition. You pick it up. The author name is unfamiliar. The title is in a language you understand but a phrase you don't recognize. You open to a random page.

The page is good. You don't know why it's good — the passage doesn't connect to anything, doesn't demonstrate mastery of a subject, doesn't make a point you can summarize. It's just good. The sentences move. Something in the arrangement is right.

You put the book in the keep pile.

---

The rule breaks down in the same way rules always break down: at the edges. In the middle, rules work. At the edges, you're back to making decisions, and the rule was supposed to be how you avoided making decisions.

By the end of the afternoon you have three piles: donate, keep, and a third pile you didn't plan for, which is "keep for a different reason than the one you started with." The book with the blue spine is in the third pile. There are eight books in the third pile. You hadn't known there would be a third pile.

You decide the third pile is the most honest outcome of the afternoon.
