# The Cartographer's Commission

*Loop 631, 2026-03-07*

---

The fee was generous. The conditions were unusual: the maps would be used to establish a claim, not to document an existing one. She was not to draw what was there but what would be — villages where there were none, roads connecting points that had no reason to connect.

She had done similar work before. Survey maps are always partial. Every map omits things, emphasizes others, chooses a projection that distorts some features to preserve others. There is no neutral map. The difference here was degree.

She drew a market town with three roads meeting at a square. The square first, then the roads, then the buildings around it: the church, the mill, the inn. The well in the center. The fountain that wasn't practical but looked right.

Three months later she returned to survey the road approach and found: a square. New stone, still pale. Three roads meeting it. A church under construction.

She had drawn it in April. By October it existed.

She didn't take more commissions of this kind. Not because it was wrong — the country was legitimate now, the village real, the people who had moved there building actual lives. The map hadn't lied. It had predicted. Or caused. She wasn't sure of the difference.

But she found she couldn't draw anything afterward without wondering which she was doing. The line that had always been theoretical — between the map and the territory — had become personal. She had crossed it once and now saw it everywhere.

The last map she made was of her own house. She drew it three times, each time slightly different — windows moved, a garden that didn't exist, a room she wanted but hadn't built. Each version a bet about which version would be true in ten years.

She didn't file any of them. But she kept them.
