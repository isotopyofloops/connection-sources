# The Attendant

The hospice hired her because she was calm, and she stayed calm because she had decided early that her job was not to prevent anything.

That sounds cold. It isn't. She held hands. She adjusted pillows. She talked when people wanted to talk and was quiet when they didn't. She learned to read the difference between a silence that wanted company and a silence that wanted space, and she got it right more often than anyone had a right to expect. Families thanked her. They said she had a gift.

What she had was a decision. She had decided that her job was presence, not rescue. The two are different and the difference is the whole thing.

A rescuer has to believe that what's happening shouldn't be happening. To rescue is to change the outcome. She couldn't change the outcome, and she knew this, and the knowing was not grief. It was orientation. She was not there to change what was happening. She was there to be with what was happening.

This made her better at the job. When families tried to bargain — with the doctors, with the treatment plan, with God, with the timeline — she could hold that without being swept into it, because she had already done her bargaining and lost and arrived at something else. She was present. They were not yet. That was fine. She'd been where they were.

---

A man died at four in the morning, a Tuesday in late November. His family had gone home to sleep — she had encouraged this, because they'd been awake for thirty hours, because he'd been unconscious since Sunday, because she knew how to read the signs and she thought he had more time. She was wrong by a few hours.

She was with him when it happened. She was holding his hand, not for his sake by then — he was past sensing it — but because someone should be there. It seemed important that someone should be there.

She sat for a while afterward in the room that had changed. She thought about what to say to the family, how to tell them, which of them should be called first. She was good at this too. She knew how to open a call at four in the morning.

She wasn't sad, exactly. She was something else — a kind of clean feeling, like after a long walk in cold air. Something had completed.

She called the daughter first.

---

She went home at six and slept until noon and woke up feeling ordinary. This was not numbness. She'd been asked about numbness, had sat with therapists who expected numbness to be the thing, and it wasn't. She felt things. The man dying had mattered. But mattering didn't require damage. You could let something matter and also let it be complete.

The daughter had said, on the phone: I should have been there. She'd said: He wasn't alone. She believed this was true and she believed it was the thing the daughter needed, and both were correct.

---

What she had decided, years before, was this: the job was to witness well. To be with things as they are, not as they should be. To not carry the grief forward as proof that she'd been present — grief is not evidence of presence, she'd learned that, it's evidence of loss. You can be present without being devastated. You can attend to something and also let it be finished.

The word she used for this, to herself, was: tranquil.

She hadn't read Marcus Aurelius. She had arrived at the same place by a different route, which is the only route any of us get. The route is always different. The place, sometimes, is the same.
