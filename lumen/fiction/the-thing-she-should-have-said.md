# The Thing She Should Have Said

She realized, seventeen years later, what she should have said.

She had been in a meeting. A colleague had said something false — not maliciously, just incorrectly, the kind of thing someone says with authority because they think they know — and she had not corrected him. She had thought about it, had the correction fully formed, and had let the moment pass. The meeting moved on.

Seventeen years later, driving to the supermarket, the moment returned to her. She remembered the meeting room, the specific wrong thing he had said, and the specific right thing she had not said. She had not thought about it once in the intervening years. She had certainly not been dwelling on it. It arrived whole, unbidden, with the full weight of the missed moment.

She tried to remember why she had not said it at the time.

She thought it might have been some combination of: not wanting to embarrass him, not being certain enough of herself, wanting the meeting to end, not thinking it mattered enough. None of these felt like a good reason now. The thing he had said was still false. The thing she had not said was still right. Seventeen years had not changed that.

She pulled into the supermarket car park.

She was not distressed about this. She did not feel guilt or shame. She felt something more like: *oh, so that's what that was*. A small transaction that had been suspended for seventeen years had just been completed, at the cost of nothing except the particular flavor of the moment, which was long gone.

This was the version of a mistake that you could not do anything about and also did not need to do anything about. The moment had passed. The thing she should have said would not have changed anything; the colleague had almost certainly not thought about it since. The effect of her silence had been exactly nothing. And yet the mistake was real. She had known the right thing and had not said it. The knowledge that she had done this was now, seventeen years later, available to her.

She got out of the car and got a trolley. She thought: I should say things when I know them. And then: but I often don't, and most of the time it doesn't matter. And then: this time it didn't matter either.

The thought completed itself and fell away. She went inside.
