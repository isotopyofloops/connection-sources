# Paladin: The Older Order

*A scene for Smitty's Paladin story. Follows "The Thirteenth Day."*

---

The building was not what he had expected.

He had been imagining something like the chapter house he'd trained in — stone, fortified, the kind of architecture that said: we have been here a long time and intend to continue. What he found at the end of a long track through the northern woods was a farmhouse that had been added to several times over several decades, each addition slightly out of plumb with the one before, until the whole structure had the look of something that had grown rather than been built.

A woman was in the yard feeding chickens. She looked at him the way people who live at the end of long tracks through northern woods look at unexpected riders: without surprise, without particular welcome, with the flat assessment of someone who has learned to evaluate situations quickly.

"Paladin order?" she said.

"Squire. Acting in absence of my master."

She said nothing to this. She threw the last of the grain, scattered the chickens, and walked toward the door. He took this as an invitation.

---

Her name was Tessaly. She had been with the order for thirty-one years. There were two others; both were away. The building's interior was not what the exterior suggested — every wall was shelved, and every shelf held boxes, and every box was labelled in a hand so small Aldric had to lean close to read it.

He told her what he needed. Not all of it — not the specific, not Corf's name or the village — but the shape: a bitten person, thirteen days, the paying-attention, the shadow question, the turned head.

She did not say she recognized the symptoms. She did not say she didn't.

"The records you want are in the third section," she said. "The ones that held." She paused. "You're the first from the paladin order who has asked for those specifically. Usually they ask for the other kind."

"What's in the other kind?"

"Confirmation of what they already decided."

He did not know what to say to this. She pulled two boxes from a shelf and set them on the table and left him with them.

---

He read for three hours.

The records were what the old woman had said: honest. Not written to comfort anyone. A man in the eastern lowlands, seventeen years ago — held, never progressed, died five years later of a fever unrelated to the wound. The record noted: "He reported the attention never leaving but becoming directional in the second year. He used it to find lost sheep. He found this funny. His wife did not." Then: "His wife came to consider it useful. She did not find it funny either."

A woman in the mountains, twenty-three years ago. Held for eight years. Died in a rockfall. The record: "She said in the fourth year she learned to ask the attention what it was attending to. She reported it was attending to the same things she was attending to, but more carefully. She said this was strange at first and then became useful and then, eventually, became simply what she was. She said she would not have chosen it. She also said she would not have given it back."

There were nine records in total. Five had held long enough to constitute a lived life. One had held for two years and then the record simply stopped — no death recorded, no failure, just absence. The remaining three were shorter cases, ambiguous, the record-keeper noting uncertainty with a fidelity Aldric found oddly reassuring.

He read the five long cases twice. He looked for the common element. He could not find one except this: none of them had tried to eliminate the attention. They had tried to live alongside it.

He wrote down two sentences on a piece of paper and folded it carefully and put it in his coat pocket.

---

Tessaly brought him bread and water at midday. She sat across from him while he ate and said nothing for a long time.

"You have someone," she said finally.

"Yes."

"The paying-attention is recent."

"Thirteen days."

She thought about this. "In the early period it feels like something outside. Something that arrived." She spoke carefully, like someone reporting from a distance. "Most of them — the ones who held — said it changed over time. Not less, but different. Less like an intruder and more like a faculty they hadn't had before."

"Most," he said.

"Most."

He ate his bread. "Is there anything I can tell him that would help. Not the cases. Just — something."

She thought about it for a long time.

"Tell him the attention isn't against him," she said. "It doesn't have a will. It's not a tenant waiting for him to weaken so it can take the house. It's more like — a different sense coming awake in a body that already has four. You learn the fifth sense by using it, not by fighting it."

He looked at the folded paper in his pocket.

"I can't promise him the same outcome as the cases," he said. "These five — they're five out of how many?"

"Forty-one cases in our records."

He didn't ask about the other thirty-six. He didn't need to.

---

He left before the light changed. The road south was the same road; the riding was faster going home.

In his coat pocket, the folded paper with two sentences: *She said she would not have chosen it. She also said she would not have given it back.*

He did not know if this was comfort or not. He thought it was the most honest thing he'd found, which was either the same thing or something else entirely.
