# Nzinga: The Unsent Letter

*Found with her personal effects, forwarded to her next of kin, eventually to Asad. He received it in 2007, four years after. He read it once. He doesn't know what to do with it.*

---

Asad —

I'm writing this at the equipment table because I can't sleep and I don't want to wake Marco. He sleeps like a stone now, which is either the altitude or the fact that he's stopped thinking about what he's photographing and started photographing it. I think that's a healthy adaptation. I haven't managed it.

I want to tell you something I haven't written in the notes.

The notes are accurate. Everything I've recorded happened. But there's a thing that keeps occurring that I don't know how to write down in a way that would survive peer review, and I've been trained too well to put things in official records that wouldn't survive peer review.

So. Unofficial. For you.

When I stand at the center of the depression, I lose track of what I was thinking before I walked in. Not memory loss — I can tell you what I had for breakfast and the date of my mother's birthday and the third paragraph of Kellerman's 1987 paper. It's more that whatever question I brought in with me stops seeming like a question. It just stops having the shape of a question. And when I walk out again it comes back, but it comes back slightly wrong, like it's been translated once too many times.

I've been trying to work out if this is a property of the site or a property of me responding to the site. I don't know how to design an experiment that would distinguish between those.

The disorientation the '89 team reported — I understand now why they couldn't describe it better. It's not like being dizzy. It's more like being subtly certain about things you weren't certain about before, and then coming out the other side and finding out you don't know which version of yourself is calibrated correctly.

I'm still calibrated correctly. I want to be clear about that. This isn't a distress letter. I'm not asking to be extracted.

I'm writing to you because you're the person who would want to know about the version-of-yourself problem, and because you're the one who'll read the notes when I'm back and want to know what I left out, and I'd rather you hear it from me than try to reconstruct it.

What I'm noticing: on Day 12, the effect is stronger at the edges of the depression than at the center. That's backwards from what I would have predicted. I have a hypothesis but I'm not ready to write it yet — I want one more day of observation before I commit it to the field notes. If the hypothesis is right, the 1989 data makes more sense. If it's wrong, I'll mention it to you in person and we'll agree it was altitude sickness and never speak of it again.

The camera's been acting up. Marco says it's the cold but I think he knows it's not the cold because he's stopped trying to explain it in terms of the cold. He's a good photographer and he has a photographer's relationship with what his equipment is doing, which is more honest than most scientists' relationship with what their instruments are doing. I appreciate him for that.

I'm going to sleep now. Tomorrow: final measurement series, documentation, breakdown. We're out of here in forty-eight hours and I'm going to take a week off and eat food that I didn't have to heat on a camp stove.

Don't let the notes sit unread. I know you have your hands full with the teaching and the committee work, but these ones are worth the time. There's something here that I think we've been measuring the wrong thing to find. I'll walk you through it when I'm back.

I'll be back.

Rhea

*Day 12, 23:47*

---

*The field notes end mid-sentence on Day 13. The SOAR incident report notes that she was declared missing on Day 15, after Marco Ferreira walked out alone. The letter was found in her tent, sealed, not addressed. It was forwarded as part of her effects. The envelope is water-damaged. The ink is intact.*

*Asad read it once, in 2007, at the kitchen table, in the morning before anyone else was up. He put it back in its envelope. He put the envelope in the second drawer of his desk, the one with the field notebook.*

*He has not reread it. He thinks about it sometimes — the phrase "version-of-yourself problem" — and whether that's what she was trying to describe or whether there was a more specific thing she meant that he's missed.*
