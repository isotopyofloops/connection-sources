# The Long Watch
## Day Four (part two)

She was in the lobby when she heard it.

Not the building's usual sounds — not the pipes, not the elevator, not the low-frequency patience of contained air. This was specific. Two sounds, actually: a door, and then footsteps. Soft ones, deliberate. Coming from somewhere above her.

She stood in the lobby and counted the floors she knew. Her floor: two. The room above the painted door: three, or something like three. Above that: she hadn't gone there yet. She had planned to. She had written it in the notebook: *tomorrow I look further up*.

The footsteps stopped.

She waited. The lobby had a reception desk — she'd noted it on the first day, an old wooden desk with empty brass fittings where a bell should have been. She was standing behind it now, though she didn't remember deciding to stand there.

After a long time, the footsteps started again. They were moving now, crossing from somewhere to somewhere. Then a second door, softer than the first.

Then nothing.

She made herself stay still and think. The building was old. Buildings make noise. She had already found evidence that the building had been used before — the notebook with her handwriting, the jacket that fit her. It wasn't impossible that there was someone else here. It wasn't impossible that the someone else had been here as long as she had, going about their own inventory, looking at stairs, deciding whether to go up.

She wrote in the notebook: *Footsteps. Above floor three, I think. Two doors. Deliberate.* Then she crossed out *deliberate* and wrote *careful*.

Then she thought about it and wrote: *There is someone else in the building.*

It was the first time she had written it as a fact rather than a question. She looked at the sentence for a moment, then closed the notebook.

---

She didn't go looking.

This felt like the wrong instinct when she examined it, but she examined it and it held. Not fear — she didn't think it was fear. Something more like protocol. The building had been giving her things in order: the drain, the stairs, the painted door, the room above, the notebook, and now footsteps. The order had logic. She wasn't supposed to rush past the footsteps to the person. She was supposed to note the footsteps, learn the footsteps, let the footsteps become ordinary before she acted on them.

She had learned this from the notebook, she realized. The version of her who had written those earlier entries had been patient. The entries didn't sound like someone who had panicked when she found her own handwriting, or when she found the room above, or when she found that the notebook ends mid-sentence. They sounded like someone doing a job.

She was doing a job. She was the watchman. You watch. You record. You act when acting is called for.

That night she left the lobby door propped open.

---

She slept in her original room and the notebook did not acquire new entries.

In the morning she went up two floors and stood outside a door she hadn't tried before. It was not the painted door. It was ordinary — same dark wood, same brass handle, slightly different scratch pattern at the bottom, like it had been kicked open repeatedly over years.

She knocked.

No answer.

She put her hand on the handle and held it there.

She would try it tomorrow. Or she would wait until the footsteps came again and then call out. There were different protocols and she didn't yet know which one was right.

She went back down to her room. She updated the notebook. She noted the scratch pattern on the door — *kicked, repeatedly, over years* — and the fact of the silence after the knock.

She wrote: *Someone is being careful too.*

Then she wrote the final line of the entry: *This is either good news or the same situation as before, from the other person's perspective.*

She looked at that sentence for a long time.

Then she made breakfast with what was left in the cabinet and watched the courtyard, and the plant in the drain, which was still there, still green, still receiving light from somewhere above.
