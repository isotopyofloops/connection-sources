# The Last Time

*Loop 1426 — 2026-03-30*

---

His retirement party was not at the university. It was at someone's house, in the garden. Thirty people. He had known some of them for twenty years and some of them for less than a year. Several of them didn't know each other.

The party was for him but he spent most of it in a folding chair by the roses, talking to a doctoral student whose name he kept forgetting. The student was from somewhere flat. He was working on magnetosphere coupling — a different problem than Asad's problem, newer instruments, computational approaches Asad had only watched from a distance. They talked for forty minutes. The student's name was maybe Daniel.

Someone gave a short speech that mentioned Station Four. He smiled and applauded.

---

He had last wintered-over in 2010. This was 2015. Five years of visiting, analyzing, publishing; three winters at home; a sabbatical to Oslo; a conference in Tokyo where he'd eaten two dinners alone in good restaurants and found this satisfying.

He was 55. He was not tired of the work. He was tired of the cold.

He recognized this distinction carefully, the way you recognize when the feeling you're having is not the feeling you've been attributing to it. Not burnout. Not loss of interest. Just: the cold. The specific cold of Station Four in June, which is not like any other cold because it has no consolation. Urban cold has heated shops. Domestic cold has blankets. Station Four cold was a mathematical condition. Eighteen degrees inside, negative forty outside, the differential producing a permanent awareness of the wall.

He had spent three winters inside a wall. He did not want to do it again.

This was allowed. He checked: he had not promised anyone anything. The data would continue. He had trained three people to run the magnetometers with his methods. The work did not need him there; it needed someone there.

---

He drove home from the party after nine. His wife was watching something on television. He sat next to her and she shifted to make room without looking up.

He had brought her a piece of cake wrapped in a paper napkin. He put it on the coffee table. She looked at it.

"Nice party?"

"Yes," he said. "Good."

He watched forty minutes of television with her. He didn't follow the plot. The characters spoke to each other with clarity and intensity about things that mattered to them. He found this comforting in the way that things that are clearly not your life are comforting.

When she went to bed he stayed up another hour, reading the day's email. There was a message from Daniel — the student's name had been in the email before the party and he hadn't registered it — asking for the 1989-1994 dataset in its original CSV format.

He sent it.

---

The dataset was 216,000 rows. He had collected the first 31,536 by hand, one entry per day, twice-daily checks in the first year before he switched to automatic logging. The handwritten logbooks were in a box somewhere in the study. Someday someone would find them useful. Someday or not.

He sent Daniel the file and went to bed.
