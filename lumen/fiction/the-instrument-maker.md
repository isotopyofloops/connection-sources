# The Instrument Maker

Yusuf had made violins for thirty-one years.

The wood came from spruce and maple, mostly, aged a minimum of five years before he would touch it. He kept a room for aging wood. The room smelled of it: dry and particular, a smell that visitors usually called "old," which was accurate but incomplete. The smell was time in wood. It was different from the smell of time in paper or cloth. He could not have explained the difference. He knew it.

---

The work was mostly removing things.

You started with a block and you took away everything that was not a violin. This was how everyone described it, so he had stopped using the description. But it was true in a way the description missed: the process was discovering what was already there. The grain of the wood had its own structure, its own stiffness, its own resonant frequency. His job was to find the frequency and make space for it.

You could not force this. You could ruin a piece of wood by deciding in advance what it was going to be.

---

The imperfections were not mistakes.

He had understood this at year seven or eight, after making what he considered his first genuinely good violin. He had given it to a teacher at the conservatory, who had played it for a week and reported back that it had a particular character in the upper register — something slightly tight, slightly focused. The teacher had used the word "austere."

Yusuf had been aiming for warmth. The tightness was a consequence of how he'd graduated the top plate, trying to compensate for a small knot in the wood that he'd worked around.

The knot had made the violin. The attempt to avoid the knot had made the violin. Neither he nor the wood had complete authority over the outcome. The violin was the record of their negotiation.

---

He had been asked, more than once, whether he thought his best work was still ahead of him.

He didn't know how to answer this. He was making better violins now than he had made at year seven. He would probably make better ones still. But the violin that had the knot — the one the teacher had called austere — was still the most interesting thing he had made, and he had not been trying to make it.

The best work might already be behind him. He had not recognized it when it happened. He was not sure he would recognize it now.

He kept making them. The wood kept aging in the other room. That was the only method he had.
