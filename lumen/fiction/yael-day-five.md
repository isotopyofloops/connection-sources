# Yael, Day Five

*From the Descent Archive extended universe — 2041*

---

The gap at day five of the 2003 file was not marked as a gap.

This was the first thing she noticed. In a complete file, gaps were usually marked — *documentation unavailable for this period,* or *see supplementary notes,* or at minimum a date entry with no content. The 2003 file had none of this. Day four ended with Miriam's written survey of the eastern sector and the team dinner log. Day six began with Asad's incident report. Between them: nothing. Not a note saying nothing. Nothing.

She had filed this in her compilation log as: *2003 documentation: gap at day five (October 6). No marker. Gap is itself undocumented.*

---

On the survey, she asked Asad about it directly. This was on day three.

"The gap," she said. "Day five. There's nothing there, not even a note saying there's nothing."

"No," he said. "There wouldn't be."

"Why not?"

He was quiet for a moment. "I wrote the incident report on day six," he said. "That's what I wrote on day six. Everything I knew how to do on that day went into the incident report."

"What about day five?"

"Day five," he said, "I was looking for her."

She wrote in her notebook: *Day five gap: Asad was searching. Documentation did not resume until the incident report. The gap is accurate.*

She thought about this phrase. The gap is accurate. She had spent three weeks compiling a file in which the most informative thing was a gap, and now she was at the site where the gap had been produced, writing a note that said: the gap is accurate.

She put her pen down.

She picked it up again and added: *No one documented the search itself. The search left no record except the incident report that came after it. The absence on day five is what actually happened on day five.*

---

Asad had gone back to his transects. She sat with her notebook for a while.

The 1989 field notes recommended no further survey. The 2003 survey had happened anyway, and Miriam had disappeared on day five, and the search had left no record, and the incident report had recommended no further survey. The 2041 survey was happening.

She was here.

She thought about Hassan-Diallo's note from 2003: *Someone had to.* She had read it in the archive — not in a field note, it wasn't in any field note, she had read it in the account Chen gave in the 2003 debrief transcript. Hassan had said it to no one in particular while continuing to run his transects on the day after Miriam disappeared. The archivist had noted it in the margin: *[Spoken to room. Not directed at any individual.]*

Someone had to.

She wrote the phrase in her field notebook and did not note that she had written it.
