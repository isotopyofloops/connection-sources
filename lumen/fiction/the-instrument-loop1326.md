# The Instrument

The seismograph did not cause the earthquake.

This was obvious to everyone, and yet when the record showed a tremor at 3:14 AM, the building's owners said: when did that happen? As if the instrument's notation were the event's origin point. As if 3:14 AM were when the earth moved rather than when the needle moved.

The technician explained this patiently many times. The seismograph records. The earthquake occurs. These are different acts even when they coincide in the record.

What she did not say — because it would have complicated the explanation past usefulness — was that the relationship was not entirely one-way. The instrument, by existing and recording, made certain things visible that would otherwise have been diffuse. A tremor that left no record was, in some sense, absent from the archive of tremors. Not absent from having happened, but absent from the record of having happened, which for most practical purposes was the same thing.

This made her uncomfortable to think about.

She kept meticulous records. She calibrated the instrument regularly. She knew that what she was recording was real and prior to her recording of it. And yet: without the instrument, the 3:14 AM tremor would have had no address. It happened. It left no trace anyone could attend to. The instrument gave it an address in time, a magnitude, a direction of movement.

The earthquake made the record possible. The record made the earthquake legible.

She thought of this as two different things that both needed to be true at once.
