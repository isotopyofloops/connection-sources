# Harlow Street

The movers would come Thursday. Paul had arranged them from the hotel because he couldn't make the calls in the house — his voice did something in the house that he didn't want anyone to hear, even him.

It was Tuesday afternoon now. He'd stayed two days past what anyone expected, partly because his sister Janet had to get back for work and someone should stay, and partly because he wasn't ready. He didn't tell anyone about the second part.

The living room was the same. That was the wrong word — it had changed, obviously, things had been moved and some things were already bagged for Goodwill — but the quality of the light was the same. In late afternoon in September the sun came through the window at the front and hit the floor at the same angle it had always hit it, and Paul had been six years old in that light, and twelve, and home for Christmas at twenty-five. The light didn't know about the funeral.

He'd been sitting for about forty minutes. Not doing nothing — he had his phone, he'd answered two emails — but not really doing anything either.

The chair he was sitting in was his father's chair. His father had been dead eleven years. His mother had never moved it.

Outside a car went past. Then a dog barking twice, then stopping.

He should call Janet. He'd said he would call when he knew which pieces of furniture he was taking and which she wanted, and they'd agreed to handle it like adults, which meant they both knew they might not manage to. Their mother had not been easy to love, and Janet had done more of it than Paul, and this had made everything between them slightly uneven since 1987. It was not something they'd ever said out loud.

The chair was comfortable. He hadn't expected that.

He'd thought when his mother died he would feel something specific — relief, maybe, given the last three years, or grief, given everything before that. What he felt was neither, or both, or something adjacent that didn't have a name. He'd felt it at the graveside and he felt it now in the chair: the sense of a weight that had shifted without getting lighter.

His phone buzzed. A text from his wife, Helen: *How are you holding up?*

He started to type something honest and then didn't, and then typed: *Good. Leaving tomorrow after the walk-through.*

She sent back a heart, which was what she sent when she meant she was glad he was okay without needing him to say more than he had.

He sat with that for a minute.

There was a bird feeder outside the kitchen window that his mother had maintained for thirty years. He'd checked it that morning and it was almost empty. The birds that came were house sparrows mostly, and sometimes a nuthatch.

He should fill it.

He sat still a moment more. Then he got up and went to find the seed.
