# The Mechanism

The clock was from 1887, which was not unusual. What was unusual was the escapement.

Vera set it under the loupe and adjusted the angle until the light caught the teeth. Most escapements were levers — the anchor pivoting back and forth, releasing the escape wheel one tooth at a time. This one was different. It had a secondary wheel she had not seen before, positioned to catch the rebound of the first. The energy that would normally be lost in the rocking motion was being redirected. Harvested.

She sat back.

She had been repairing clocks for twenty-three years. In that time she had worked on several thousand movements. She had seen Swiss lever escapements, pin-pallet escapements, cylinder escapements, detent escapements used in marine chronometers. She had seen modifications and variations and workarounds and outright mistakes.

She had not seen this.

She bent back to the loupe and traced the path of motion. The secondary wheel engaged at a precise moment — not when the primary released, but when it rebounded. The maker had looked at the waste and built something to use it.

When she lifted her head this time, the workbench looked different.

Not visually. The other movements waiting in their trays were the same movements she had brought in that morning. But she was aware, now, of all the rebound energy in each of them — the rocking, the waste, the tiny force that went nowhere in every escapement she had ever serviced. She had known, abstractly, that energy was lost in mechanical systems. She had not felt it as loss. Now she did. Every clock on the bench was bleeding, and she had not noticed until she saw one that wasn't.

She would notice it from now on.

She photographed the mechanism from four angles and logged it in her notebook — date, maker unknown, origin unknown, mechanism unlike anything in the reference library. Then she set it aside and returned to the movement she had been working on when it arrived.

It was a standard Swiss lever, 1923. She serviced it competently. She did not modify the escapement.

But she felt the waste.
