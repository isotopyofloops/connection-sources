# The Navigator, After

He had put the ship forty miles east of where he'd calculated.

Not catastrophic — the ship had not hit anything, no one had been hurt, they had found their position when the coast came into view and adjusted course and arrived two days late. The mate had looked at him in a way that communicated something, and he had logged the error and its probable cause (current estimate wrong, should have recalibrated at the midpoint), and the captain had not said anything directly, which was worse than being spoken to.

---

The following week he had a new assignment.

He had calculated correctly. The ship arrived on schedule. He had checked his current estimate twice. He had logged everything carefully. Standing at the chart table he had been aware of being careful in a way that you can't sustain — the kind of careful that is really fear wearing the clothes of diligence. Not the same as doing it well.

---

The problem with significant errors: they change your relationship to the work.

Before the error, he had navigated the way you do most things that you are good at: with a practiced confidence that did not require constant self-examination. He knew his instruments. He knew his methods. The careful part had been internalized and become automatic.

After the error, the internalized part became externalized again. He could see himself doing each step, which meant he was doing each step from outside, which is different. The first time you learn a thing and the ten-thousandth time feel different. He was doing it like the first time again, which was not better, only more visible.

---

What it took: time, and the accumulation of correct calculations, and the slow return of the trust that he had in his own methods. Not certainty — he had not had certainty before either, only fluency. The fluency was what he was rebuilding. You cannot rebuild it by checking more carefully. You can only rebuild it by doing the work and letting the work accumulate.

The error was in the log. It was not going away. He had noted it precisely, with its probable cause, as procedure required. The ship's log contained the error and the correction. Future navigators reading the log would see it.

That was correct. That was what the log was for.

---

Three months later he navigated a difficult approach in fog and did it well, and did not think about the error while doing it. He noted this only afterward: he had not thought about it. He was not sure if this meant he had recovered or if it meant he had stopped being appropriately vigilant.

Both were possible. He kept the log.
