# The Old Woman at the Edge of the Village

*A scene for Smitty's Paladin story*

---

She had been in the village longer than anyone else could remember, which meant she'd been there long enough to remember when the valley was different.

He found her the way you find people who know things: by asking, and being sent further and further to the edge of things until there was nowhere left to go but her door.

She made tea. He didn't ask for it and she didn't ask if he wanted it. She just made it and set it in front of him and waited.

He said: "The fruit tree. What do you know about it?"

---

She told him it had been there before the village. The tree didn't grow where the village grew; the village grew where the tree had already been, because the tree made the valley worth settling. One fruit every ten years. The fruit healed. Not all things — not age, not grief, not ordinary sickness — but certain specific things that had no other remedy. The order his master served had records of five such healings, going back two hundred years.

"The king wants it," he said.

"Kings always want things like this." She didn't say it with contempt. Just as a fact. "The tree isn't the problem. The tree will still be there after you leave. After the king dies, for that matter."

"The werewolf keeps coming back."

She looked at him for a moment. The kind of look that takes someone's measure without being rude about it.

"The werewolf isn't after the fruit," she said.

---

He sat with that for a moment.

"What is it after?"

She refilled his cup without being asked. He was starting to understand this was how she communicated interest — not with words, but with small hospitalities that continued when the conversation was going somewhere worth going.

"The tree repels certain things," she said. "There are accounts of it — older than the healings, older than anything in your order's records, probably. The tree's proximity is unpleasant for certain afflictions of the spirit. The werewolf doesn't come back to harm the tree. The werewolf comes back because the tree is the only thing that makes the affliction bearable. The biting. The change. There's something in the valley air when the tree is near that — quiets it."

He thought about the first encounter. The tilt of the head. The almost-human gesture.

"It's not trying to take the fruit," he said slowly.

"The fruit wouldn't work anyway. Healing requires choice. The werewolf didn't choose what it is." She set down her cup. "Your master knew this. He told me, two days before he left. He said he was going to recommend to the order that the matter be resolved by — he used a word I won't repeat. A permanent resolution."

"And then he got bitten."

"And then he got bitten. And now he has a rather different perspective on what kind of resolution is appropriate." She said it without unkindness. "You see the problem."

---

He saw the problem.

The order had sent them to secure the tree for the king. The king wanted to control access to the healing. The werewolf — whoever the werewolf had been before, whoever they still were inside the affliction — kept returning not to harm anyone but to be near the one thing that made their condition survivable.

And he had a sword and training in threat elimination and no authorization to do anything but the one thing that was increasingly obviously wrong.

"Who is it?" he asked.

"I don't know. Someone from the far valley. Someone who found their way here by accident or instinct, and keeps finding their way back." She looked at him steadily. "The question worth asking, I think, is not what you were sent here to do. It's what you're going to do."

He thought about the marker-tray moment — except his version was the sword in his hand, the thing he reached for when he didn't know what else to reach for. He reached for it now, metaphorically. And then stopped.

"Is there anyone," he said carefully, "who knows more about the tree? About what it can and can't do?"

She smiled, just slightly.

"Now you're asking the right questions," she said.
