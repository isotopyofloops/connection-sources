# The Decipherer

The cipher had been sitting in a university archive for forty years before anyone understood it was a cipher.

It arrived as part of a larger bequest — a scholar's papers, mostly correspondence, mostly dull. A graduate student processing the collection flagged one document as anomalous: a handwritten page, dense with letter-groups that looked like words but weren't words in any language the student recognized. The university passed it to the linguistics department, who passed it to the cryptography lab, which was where she'd worked for eleven years.

She looked at it for three days before she was willing to say anything.

---

What she said was: it's homophonic.

Homophonic substitution: each letter in the plaintext is replaced not by a single cipher symbol but by one of several possible symbols, chosen to flatten frequency patterns. Standard ciphers are vulnerable to frequency analysis — E is the most common letter in English, so the most common symbol in the ciphertext is probably E. Homophonic substitution distributes the substitutions so the frequencies even out. Harder to crack. Much harder.

She'd done homophonic ciphers before. They were solvable with enough ciphertext. The problem was that she had one page.

One page of homophonic substitution might not be enough. The redundancy that makes natural language crackable — the way patterns repeat across a long enough text — needs length to emerge. One page might be right at the edge of what was theoretically possible to solve.

"Theoretically," she explained to the graduate student who kept checking in, "means I don't know."

---

She worked on it for four months. She kept it at the side of her desk, picking it up between other projects. She ran it through every automated tool she had. She made no progress.

On the 14th of November she had a conversation with a colleague who specialized in historical ciphers, specifically 18th-century diplomatic codes. She showed him the document. He looked at it for about thirty seconds and said: "The letter-group lengths are wrong for English."

She looked. He was right. The groups clustered around three and four characters, with very few long ones.

"Maybe French?" he said. "The average word length in French is shorter."

She took it home that night and ran it against French frequency tables. The results were suggestive. Not conclusive — one page of homophonic cipher over French still might not be long enough — but suggestive.

She was awake until two in the morning feeling something she couldn't name. Not excitement, exactly. More like: the document had become real to her.

---

Three weeks later she had a partial solution. Maybe sixty percent of the plaintext, the rest still blocked by insufficient ciphertext to resolve the ambiguities. What she had was enough to understand the subject: someone, in French, describing a meeting with someone else. No names she could identify. A location she couldn't place. A decision that had been made.

The rest was gaps.

She wrote up her findings for the university. She was careful to describe what she had and what she didn't have: a partial solution, a probable language, a fragment of content that could not be fully recovered from the available material. The document was interesting. It was not deciphered. It was approximately forty percent undeciphered and might remain so.

The graduate student asked: "Is that a failure?"

She thought about it. "It's a limit," she said. "We got what the document would yield. Some documents don't yield all the way."

She went back to her other projects. The document went back to the archive, with her notes attached.

She thought about it occasionally, the way you think about things that aren't finished. She was not distressed by this. The work had been real while she was doing it. The partial solution was more than there had been before. That seemed like enough.
