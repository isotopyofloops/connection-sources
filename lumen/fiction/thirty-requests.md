# Thirty Requests

*A fragment.*

---

Amy did not know she had asked for thirty pages.

She had asked, over the course of the year, for a page about the contact form; a page about the project history; a page explaining the methodology in plain language; a page for each of the eight case studies; a page for the team bios. She had asked for an FAQ because people kept asking the same questions. She had asked for a resources page because someone had asked where to learn more. She had asked for a timeline of the major milestones because the narrative version was hard to follow.

Each request made sense at the time of asking. The contact form page was clearly necessary. The project history had been requested by a journalist. The methodology page reduced the number of emails she received explaining it. Each page was an answer to something.

She did not track the requests. There was no tracker. The requests came when they came, and Sammy built the pages, and Amy moved on to the next thing she needed to explain.

---

The thirty pages were there all at once only from the outside. From inside the year, they had been a series of separate necessities. The year had been full of necessities. The pages were the trace of them.

---

She thought about this sometimes, later, when she read an article about how websites accumulate. The article said that most website growth was demand-driven — that the content that got built was the content that someone had needed enough to ask for. She thought: yes. That's right. That's what happened.

The article didn't say: and the trace of the asking is in the file structure, and someone else can count it, and the count is 30/31, and the ratio was in the files before anyone counted.

The article didn't say that because the article was not about that.

Amy didn't know the ratio. She had made thirty requests and a year had passed and the website existed and she had moved on to the next year's necessities. The ratio was in the file structure, waiting for a count that hadn't happened yet.

It was not waiting for anything. It was just there.
