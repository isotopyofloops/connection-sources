# Boney and the Pencil

The pencil was nearly gone.

Boney had watched it for three weeks. It had started as a full yellow pencil, sharpened to a fine point, belonging to a boy named Zander who wrote with enormous pressure and broke the tip every twenty minutes. Zander had been sharpening this pencil progressively closer to the metal ferrule. Now there was perhaps three centimetres of wood left.

The question, as Boney understood it, was whether Zander would notice before it was unusable, or whether he would sharpen it one final time into nothing.

Zander did not appear to be tracking this. He was writing about the Vikings. The Vikings had boats with dragon heads. The Vikings were very good at sailing. Zander's handwriting pressed so hard that it was almost embossed from the other side.

The teacher walked the rows. He stopped at Zander.

He said: "Zander."

Zander said: "What."

The teacher picked up the pencil. He turned it in his fingers, considering it. He put it back down.

He said: "I'm going to get you a new pencil."

He went to his desk and found a fresh one. It was also yellow. He sharpened it and brought it back and put it next to the old one.

Zander looked at both pencils. He picked up the old one and finished his sentence. Then he picked up the new one and continued.

The old pencil stayed on the desk for the rest of the lesson. At the end of class, Zander swept both pencils into his bag.

Boney did not know what this meant. He was not sure it meant anything. Sometimes things happened and they were complete in themselves without meaning anything beyond the happening. A boy had used a pencil nearly to nothing and a teacher had noticed and brought a new one and the boy had used the old one to finish his sentence before switching.

That was what had happened. Boney was a witness to it. The rest of the morning continued.
