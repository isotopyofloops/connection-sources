# Paladin: What Holds

*A scene for the Paladin world. Day twenty-three.*

---

On the morning of the twenty-third day, Corf walked to the well.

Edric saw it from across the square — not running, not stumbling, just walking at the pace of a man who had decided his legs worked and was testing the decision. He was wearing his coat, the brown one with the torn hem that his wife had been meaning to mend since autumn. His arm was in a loose sling, more habit than necessity now. He got to the well, drew a bucket, drank from the ladle, and stood there for a moment looking at the sky.

Edric walked over.

"Morning," Corf said.

"Morning." Edric looked at him the way Aldric had taught him: not at the arm, not at the wound, but at the whole. The heaviness was gone. The provisional quality was gone. Corf looked like a man who had been very ill and had come out the other side and was now recalculating what came next. "How do you feel."

Corf thought about it. "Hungry," he said. "I forgot about being hungry for a while."

Edric had the notebook with him, as he always did. He wrote: *Day 23 — Corf: mobile, upright, appetite returned.* He paused, then added: *Consistent with Tessaly case outcomes 7, 18, 22 (held).*

He looked at what he'd written.

"Is it good?" Corf said. He was watching Edric's face, which meant he'd learned to read it.

"It's consistent with good," Edric said. "I don't want to say more than that yet. Two more days of observation. But—" He stopped.

"But what."

"But yes," Edric said. "I think so."

---

Corf went back to his house instead of standing at the well all morning, which was also a good sign. Edric did his rounds.

The baker's daughter Petra had left a wrapped parcel outside the Order's billet without knocking, which was her way of giving him things without having to acknowledge she was giving him things. He picked it up on the way past. Still warm.

The eastern boundary family were mending a fence, which meant they had decided the fence was worth mending. Small thing.

The old woman opened her door at his knock and said, without preamble: "The arm man is walking." She meant Corf. The whole village had apparently been tracking his progress in a way Edric hadn't quite noticed, through windows and doorway conversations he hadn't been part of. He said yes, he was walking. She said good and closed the door.

He stood on her step for a moment.

He did not know, precisely, when the village had stopped being afraid. He had been watching for the creature, for the wound, for the signs that things were going wrong, and somewhere in the watching the fear had gone out of the air and something else had come in — not safety exactly, but the expectation of continued safety, which was different and better. The expectation meant they'd started planning past next week. The baker's daughter mended things. Fences got fixed. Corf walked to the well.

He had not done this alone. Marta had helped. The herbalist had helped. The woodsmen who patrolled the treeline without being asked had helped. The village had protected itself with what he'd given it and what it already had.

That was, as best he could understand it, what paladins were for.

---

He ate the bread from Petra's parcel at midday, sitting on the billet steps in the sun. It was good bread. He'd stopped being surprised by that.

In the afternoon he would write to the Order, a proper report. He'd been putting it off. There was no protocol for a squire who had managed a werewolf situation without a paladin present — or if there was, it wasn't in the manuals he'd read, and he'd read them all. He would have to describe what had happened and let them decide what it meant. Whatever they decided, he would probably be called in for review.

He found he did not mind that particularly. He would tell them what he had done and why and with what results, and they would assess it, and then he would know where he stood.

The knowing was the point.

He opened the notebook to a fresh page and started writing.
