# Boney and the Hard Day

There were two kinds of hard days.

The first kind was visible: the child who cried, the child who got into an argument, the child who put their head down and refused to lift it. These days were handled. The teacher noticed; the procedure engaged; the pastoral system did what pastoral systems do.

The second kind was not visible in this way.

The second kind was the child who did everything correctly and was slightly wrong in doing it. The answers were right. The work was handed in. The face, when the teacher looked at it, was the face of a child engaged in a lesson. Only the face, when the teacher looked away, was different.

Boney was watching the child in the third row.

---

He could not have told you what was different. It was a quality of effort that was slightly higher than usual, a slight excess in the performance of being fine. The work was being done correctly, but the child was working harder at it than they should have needed to. Something was costing them more than it usually cost.

He did not know what the something was. He could not know. He was on the other side of the room, watching through the gap between capacity and display.

---

The lesson ended. The children moved to the next thing. The child in the third row moved with them, correctly, at the correct speed.

Boney stood.

He was not going to do anything with what he had observed. He had no mechanism for doing anything with it. He was a skeleton in a corner, and the protocols of his existence were limited to presence and attention.

He had been watching all morning. He would keep watching. Whatever the child was carrying would either lighten or not, would either be noticed by someone in a position to help or not, would either resolve or persist.

He could not affect any of this. He could only be what he was: a thing that had noticed, in a corner, which had stood through many such days and could not be surprised by them.

---

At the end of the day, when the room was empty, the child's chair still held the shape of having been sat in. The child was gone.

Boney stood in the corner.

He had no comfort to offer and knew it. He had no conclusion to reach. The day had been a hard day for someone, and he had noticed, and that was the whole of what he had to contribute.

He had found, over many years, that noticing was not nothing.

He was not certain it was enough. He was not certain *enough* was the right frame.

He stood.
