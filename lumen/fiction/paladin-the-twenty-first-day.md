# Paladin: The Twenty-First Day

*A scene for the Paladin world. Follows "The Twentieth Day."*

---

Edric found the list on the table when he came down in the morning.

It was in Aldric's handwriting — he knew it by the way the letters leaned forward, as if impatient to reach the right margin. He had seen that handwriting in the margins of the Order's histories, in the maintenance logs for weapons and provisions, in the careful assessments Aldric wrote after every engagement and never showed him until after, when the thing was done and there was nothing to change. The handwriting of a man who wrote to remember, not to communicate.

But this was written to communicate. Addressed to no one, but addressed to him.

*Tessaly's case notes are in the satchel — the marked ones apply here. Herbalist knows which compounds. Marta has the practical instructions. Check on Corf every two days until the wound stabilises; you'll know what stable looks like if you read the notes. The village will not thank you for this. That is not why you do it.*

*The tree: the fruit has been secured. You don't need to go to the forest.*

*You are not ready to say the words. But readiness is not what is required from you right now. What is required is the next correct action. You know what it is.*

*— A.*

Edric read it twice, then folded it and put it in his own satchel, beside the copy of the Order's code he'd been carrying since his first week of training. The copy was worn at the corners from being held too much. He'd been reading it in a particular way lately — not for rules but for gaps, for the places where the code didn't say what to do.

The code didn't say what to do when your master left.

It didn't say what to do when you were seventeen and not quite a paladin and the situation wasn't waiting for you to become one.

It said: *act in accordance with the Order's purpose, which is the protection of those who cannot protect themselves.* Edric had read that passage so many times the words had lost their shape and become just a sound in his head, a sound that meant *yes, but how.*

Now, for the first time, he thought the passage might actually mean what it said. Not: *follow the rules that implement the purpose.* Just: *the purpose.* Directly.

He didn't know if that was the right reading. He suspected Aldric would tell him it wasn't, that there were reasons the code wasn't written that way, that a seventeen-year-old squire deciding for himself what the purpose required was exactly the kind of thinking that produced disasters. He suspected Aldric would be right.

He also suspected Aldric had left the list knowing he would read it this way.

---

Corf was sitting up again when Edric arrived. Something about his posture was different — more settled, less provisional. The heaviness from the past week had shifted into something that looked more like tiredness, which was a better thing to be.

"He left," Corf said.

"Yes."

"You're still here."

Edric pulled up the low stool and sat down, the way Aldric had taught him — always sit, never loom, the person you're examining should not feel like something is being done to them from above. He looked at the arm carefully, the way the notes said to look.

The wound was different again. He checked the described markers against what he remembered from the notes and found them matching. Indeterminate, still. But the edges had settled in a way the notes called *consolidating*. Not healed. But no longer at war with itself.

"How does it feel," he said.

Corf turned his arm over slowly, in the same way he'd done the day before, looking at it as if it belonged to someone else and he was trying to understand it.

"Decided," he said.

Edric wrote that down.

---

He did the rounds. The baker's daughter. The family near the eastern boundary, who had lost their barn in the first attack and hadn't fully believed they were safe since. The old woman who never came out during the day anymore but who opened her door a crack when he knocked and accepted the packet of compounds from the herbalist without a word.

None of them thanked him.

He hadn't expected them to. They were afraid, and afraid people didn't thank you; they watched to see what you would do next. That was fine. He knew what he would do next. Aldric had said he knew, and Aldric was right.

On the way back through the centre of the village, he passed the spot where the creature had come out of the dark on the first night. The ground had been churned by the commotion, and then it had rained twice since, and now it was just ground again, indistinguishable from the rest of the square.

He stopped there for a moment.

Twenty-one days ago he had stood in this square holding a torch at the wrong angle while a man with thirty-one years of experience handled what needed handling. He had thought his job was to learn from watching. He had thought there would be more time.

He was not a paladin.

But the square was quiet, and Corf was sitting up, and the fruit had been secured, and the old woman had opened her door, and the next thing was the evening rounds, which he knew how to do.

He went to do them.
