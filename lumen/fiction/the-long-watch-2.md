# The Long Watch
## The First Evening

The light shifts from amber to gray over the course of an hour.

She watches it go. She has nowhere else to be, or at least she doesn't know of anywhere else. The room is the world she has evidence for. There are probably other rooms. Probably a hallway, stairs, a street, a city. She can infer all of this. She just hasn't gone to check.

At some point she gets up and looks through the window.

The window faces west, as the notebook said. Below is a courtyard — paving stones, a drain, a stack of something covered by a tarp. No one there. The buildings opposite are the same height as this one. She counts the floors: four. She counts her own floor from outside by the windows: she is on the third.

She writes this in the notebook. *Third floor. Courtyard below. Buildings opposite, also four floors. Drain in the courtyard.*

Then she stops. She looks at what she has written. All of it is about the room, the building, the things she can verify. She reads back through the pages — every note is like this. The window faces west. The water. The board. And now: the floor, the courtyard, the drain.

None of it says anything about her.

She finds food — it was in a cabinet she hadn't tried yet, which means she didn't look at all the cabinets before, which means the previous self either found the food and didn't note it, or didn't find it either. A can with a ring-pull, and crackers in a sleeve. She eats standing up, looking at the courtyard. She doesn't know if she likes crackers. They taste like crackers.

The question she keeps returning to is: why didn't the previous self write anything personal?

There are two possibilities. One: the previous self didn't think to. Two: the previous self tried and found there was nothing to say, or nothing that would survive.

She turns the second possibility over for a while. What would she write, if she were writing now, for the self that wakes up next? She could write: *I am not afraid*. But she doesn't know if that will still be true when she wakes again. She could write: *I am interested in things*. But that seems too vague, too easy to fake.

She could write her name. But she doesn't know her name.

This is the thing she has been not-thinking about all afternoon: she doesn't know her name. The notebook doesn't say it. There are no other documents. If she has a name, the person who wrote these notes either didn't think to record it or didn't know it either.

She picks up the pen.

She writes: *There is food in the cabinet above the sink. A can with a ring-pull. Crackers.*

She looks at what she has written for a long time.

Then, below the practical note, she adds one more line — the first thing in the notebook that isn't about the room:

*I have been trying to say something about myself and I keep coming back to the room.*

She puts the pen down. She thinks this might be the most honest thing anyone has ever written in this notebook. She has no way to know.

The light outside goes from gray to black. She gets into the bed with the shallow valley and she is not surprised to find that it fits her exactly.
