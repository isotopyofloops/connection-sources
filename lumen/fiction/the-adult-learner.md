# The Adult Learner

Priya's mother was learning to swim at forty-six.

She was not bad at it. She was the worst person in the class, but the class was for beginners, and she was actually making progress. The problem was that she knew too much.

At seven, when Priya had learned to swim, she had not known enough to be afraid of the water in any useful way. She had been afraid, but the fear was general and not specific, and the instructor had moved her through it before she'd had time to categorize what specifically she was scared of.

Her mother knew exactly what she was scared of. She was scared of the specific feeling of water entering the nose when she misjudged a breath. She was scared of the moment when her feet left the bottom. She was scared of looking like she was drowning when she was just swimming badly, which was a fear Priya had never had because Priya had not yet known that these things were different.

---

The other problem was the body.

At seven, the body does what you tell it to, approximately. The neural pathways are forming and the repetition locks them in. At forty-six, the body has decades of other patterns locked in: how to breathe, how to hold the head, how to use the arms for everything except this. The new patterns have to be laid over the old ones, and the old ones keep firing.

Her mother's freestyle stroke was technically correct and felt wrong every time. The instructor said this was normal. She said she understood but didn't believe him, which was a kind of understanding available to adults that isn't available to children.

---

Priya watched from the gallery once.

Her mother was doing laps, slowly, with the sideways head-turn breathe that looked awkward and was working. She was not beautiful in the water. She was functional in the water, which was a different thing, and she was getting more functional each week.

After the session, her mother said: "I feel like I'm doing it wrong even when I'm doing it right."

Priya thought about this. "That might go away."

"Or it might not."

"Yes," Priya said. "Or it might not."

Her mother dried off. "I'm going back Thursday."

That was the important part.
