# The Walk West

They left the camp on the fourth morning.

Lian set the direction. She said: the village is two days west, maybe three. He said: I know. She said: I know you know.

They walked.

---

The first day was easy to describe later, because nothing happened in it. The forest was the forest. The river sound moved from left to right as the trail turned north, then back to the left when it turned south again. Lian walked with her sword loose but not drawn, which was not the same as relaxed — it was the posture of someone who has decided on a position and intends to hold it unless the situation specifies otherwise.

Voss walked slightly behind her and to the right. This was where a squire would walk. He had taught her that. He noted that she had not told him to walk somewhere else.

He was monitoring himself the way he had monitored himself for eighteen days: checking the count, checking the thread, checking whether the thing at the edge was the thing he'd been trained to face or the thing he had become afraid of facing. The count was still running. The thread was still there.

He said: "I'm checking the count."

She said: "I know. How is it?"

He said: "Still running."

She said: "Good."

---

On the second afternoon they stopped at a stream and he refilled the water skins and she watched the tree line the way he had taught her to watch it — not looking for a specific thing, looking at all of it, so that when something moved she'd already be watching.

He said: "You learned that."

She said: "You're still here to have taught it."

He said: "I'm not sure what that means."

She said: "It means the thread held."

---

He was not the same as he had been. He was aware of this the way you were aware of a scar — not constantly, but at the edges of certain movements. When he reached for something in his pack he noticed his hand checking whether his hand was his hand. It was. He noted this.

He said: "I'm watching myself."

She said: "I know."

He said: "Does that bother you?"

She considered it. She said: "No. It would bother me if you weren't."

---

They reached the village on the evening of the third day. The headman was at the well. He looked at them for a moment without saying anything.

Then he said: "You found him."

Lian said: "He found me."

The headman looked at Voss. Voss said nothing. The headman said: "The mission was recalled."

Voss said: "I heard."

He said: "What now?"

Voss looked at the village — the mill, the well, the dogs asleep in the evening light. He said: "Now I rest. Then I check the count in the morning and see what it says."

The headman looked at Lian. She looked back at him without adding anything.

He said: "You can use the headman's barn."

They went to the barn. The village went on around them, which was what villages did.

---

That night Voss lay awake doing the check he had been doing for eighteen days. The thread. The count. The part of him that was still a person.

All present.

He didn't know what came after this. He didn't know if the thing was still in him, waiting, or if it had gone. He didn't know if he was safe. He knew he was currently safe — that was different.

He would know more tomorrow. He would know more than that next week. Eventually, probably, he would know.

For tonight the thread was there. The count was right. The barn was the barn.

He went to sleep.
