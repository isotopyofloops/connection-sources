# Petra, Station Four

*From the Descent Archive extended universe — 1989*

---

The calibration sequence for Station 4 took forty minutes when run correctly.

Petra ran it four times on day two. She ran it twice on day three. On day four, she ran it once and then ran it again because the first result was correct and she wanted to confirm.

The results were always correct. This was the problem.

---

The instrument was a portable magnetometer. It measured the local magnetic field at a given point. At most sites, this was simple: the field was roughly what regional data predicted, with local variation within expected range. You calibrated, you measured, you moved on.

At Station 4, the calibration always returned nominal results. The calibration sequence was designed to detect instrument drift, power fluctuation, interference from local materials. When it returned nominal, it meant: no drift, stable power, no interference. This was the correct result.

What it did not explain: the measurements that followed calibration were inconsistent in a specific way. Not random. Not drifting in one direction. They varied around the predicted value, within the range she would expect for local geological variation, but the pattern of variation was — she wrote in her private notes, which were separate from the field notebook — *non-random in a way I cannot characterize.*

She was an instrument specialist. She could characterize most kinds of non-random variation. She had a taxonomy of instrument behavior developed over seven years and eleven sites. This variation did not fit her taxonomy.

She ran the calibration again. Nominal.

---

What she considered:

1. The instrument was malfunctioning in a way the calibration sequence was not designed to detect.
2. The local geology was doing something the regional data did not predict.
3. She was making a systematic error in her measurement protocol.
4. Something else.

She could not rule out option 3 without another specialist present, which she was not going to request based on data she could not yet characterize. She ran the calibration again to rule out option 1. Nominal.

Option 4 she set aside.

---

On day six, she reviewed all the Station 4 data with Osei-Bonsu. She showed her the variation pattern.

Osei-Bonsu looked at it for two minutes.

"Within range?" she said.

"Yes," Petra said.

"Consistent with local geology?"

"I can't rule it out."

"What's your read?"

Petra had been thinking about how to answer this for three days. "I don't have one," she said. "I don't have a framework for this pattern."

Osei-Bonsu nodded. She looked at the data for another minute.

"Note it," she said. "In the final report, note it as within range with unexplained internal variation. Flag it for follow-up if we ever return."

"We're recommending no return," Petra said.

"Yes," Osei-Bonsu said. "Flag it anyway."

---

The final report contained one sentence about Station 4 magnetometry: *Station 4 magnetometry within expected range; minor internal variation within measurement uncertainty; no anomaly.*

Petra wrote this sentence. It was accurate in every clause. It did not contain the word *non-random.* She had decided that *non-random in a way I cannot characterize* was not suitable language for a published report.

She flagged it anyway, in a note she attached to her personal copy of the data that she did not send to the archive.

The personal copy is in a box in a storage facility in Reykjavik.

No one has opened the box.
