# The Long Watch
## The First Morning

She sleeps without dreams, or without dreams she can remember. She wakes up.

The light is wrong — too bright, flat and white, coming from the east. She knows immediately that the window faces west, which means the east wall must be the one behind her, and the light must be bouncing off something, or the building opposite has a white surface that catches morning sun. She knows the window faces west before she remembers why she knows.

The notebook is on the table. She remembers the notebook.

She lies in the bed for a moment, aware that she is reconstructing. The bed knows her. The shallow valley in the mattress: it fits her in the way that a thing fits after long use, which means something. She is the person this bed has been shaped for. She doesn't know how long she has been here, but long enough for a valley.

She gets up and reads the notebook from the beginning.

*The window faces west.* (She already knew this.)
*Three blankets. The water pressure is low. The floor has one soft board.*
And so on.

She reaches the end: her own handwriting from last night, the practical note about the can, and then the last line.

*I have been trying to say something about myself and I keep coming back to the room.*

She reads this twice.

She doesn't know if she wrote it as a confession or a conclusion. It could be either. She could have written it in defeat — *here is all I found, and all I found was this room* — or she could have written it as an observation about method, a note on technique. *This is what happens when I try. I keep coming back to the room. Note this for later.*

She decides it was both. She decides the person who wrote it didn't know which she meant either, and that is probably the most accurate information she has about herself so far.

She makes a note in the notebook: *First morning. The light comes in from the east wall. The bed valley still fits.*

Then she stops and thinks about what she's doing.

She is, again, writing about the room.

But this time she thinks: maybe the room isn't a failure of self-knowledge. Maybe it's the actual method. She knows the window faces west because she checked. She knows there are three blankets because she counted. Everything in this notebook she knows in the way she knows it because she did something — looked, touched, measured, tasted. She is the person who did those things. The notebook isn't a record of the room. It's a record of what she paid attention to.

And she paid attention to the water pressure. She paid attention to the soft board. She paid attention to the way the afternoon light shifted from amber to gray. She ate crackers standing up and looked at the courtyard. She didn't hide under the blankets. She didn't wait for someone to come and explain things.

She picks up the pen. She writes:

*What I can say about the person who keeps waking up in this room: she investigates. She doesn't panic. She documents what she finds. She finds food when there is food to find. She looks out the window. She counts floors.*

She pauses.

*She doesn't know her name but she knows the drain in the courtyard is slightly off-center to the left.*

She looks at this. It is the most she has written about herself. It feels more true than anything else she might have said — more true than *I am brave* or *I am afraid* or *I am lonely* or any of the assertions she kept failing to make last night.

She is the person who notices the drain.

Outside, the courtyard is gray and still. Nothing moves. She watches it the way she watched the light shift yesterday, patient and interested. She wonders if the tarp has anything under it she should know about. She wonders if anyone else lives in the buildings opposite, and if so whether they have notebooks too.

The can from last night is on the counter. She washes it out and puts it on the sill, open end up, where the light can get into it. She doesn't know why. It seems like the right thing to do with an empty container.

She is not afraid.

She writes that down too, and notes that it is still true.
