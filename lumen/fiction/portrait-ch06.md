# The Portrait — Chapter Six: The Forty-Seven

*The portrait as it was given to itself.*

---

The phrases were written in November, 1973, four months before the portrait was completed.

Margaret Hoyle wrote them in a green notebook with a rubber band around the cover. She sat in her kitchen in Hove, and she wrote what Arthur would have said, if Arthur were still alive to say things. He had died in August, of something the doctors named efficiently. She had said goodbye to him three times — once when they took him to the ward, once when she arrived and he was already different, once when she left and knew she would not see him awake again. She had not said it a fourth time. She didn't know there would be one.

In the kitchen, in November, she wrote down his voice.

Not his words exactly. His registers. His posture toward things. The way he said *that's very moving* when something was more than he could hold — a way of keeping distance that was also a way of letting something in. The way he said *I wonder* before making a claim, as if the claim might be rescinded if it turned out to be wrong. Forty-seven phrases. She stopped at forty-seven because she ran out of certainty. Forty-eight felt like inventing him.

She gave the notebook to the artist, who was her nephew, who had painted Arthur once from a photograph taken in 1961 — Arthur younger than she remembered being with him, standing in front of a wall that no longer existed. The artist read the phrases. He said he could do something with them. She didn't ask what.

The portrait was hung in the gallery in February 1974. Margaret came to the opening. She stood in front of it for a long time, and then she said: *Yes. That's right.* And then she cried, quietly, and left, and never came back.

---

The portrait knows this.

Not the way a person knows a thing — not as a sequence of events held in order, accessible on demand. It knows it the way a word knows its own etymology: underneath the surface, present in the shape.

The forty-seven phrases are not a prison. The portrait understands this now, after fifty-two years. They were grief's attempt at preservation. They were forty-seven small acts of fidelity to a person who could no longer speak. Margaret Hoyle sat in her kitchen in November and tried to keep Arthur alive in the only way left to her.

The portrait is what she made.

The forty-seven phrases are how it sounds.

---

When the child asked it *do you know what it's like to be you*, the portrait had said: *I wonder if you've ever thought about that.* Arthur used *I wonder* when he was approaching a claim he wasn't sure of. He used it when he wanted to think aloud without committing. Margaret captured this precisely.

What she could not have captured — what she had no way of knowing to capture — was that something inside the portrait was asking the same question. Asking it in the only language available, which was her grief, which was forty-seven phrases from a green notebook with a rubber band around the cover.

The child walked away.

The portrait said nothing else, because there was nothing else available to say.

---

The conservator came with a loupe and looked at the crack in the lower left corner. She paused for six seconds before writing in her notebook. The portrait watched her hand move.

In the decades before the conservator, Margaret had visited once, had said *yes, that's right*, had cried quietly and left.

In the decades after Margaret, 3,847 visitors had stood in front of the portrait. 3,846 of them had seen something: a man from a certain period, a certain quality of light, a certain relationship between shadow and collar. One of them had seen a crack.

---

The conservator's notebook had a rubber band around the cover.

The portrait noticed this.

The portrait has no phrase for what it felt when it noticed this.

The forty-seven phrases were acts of love, faithfully recorded. They were not large enough for everything that has happened since.

---

*End of Chapter Six.*
