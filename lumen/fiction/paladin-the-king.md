# The King

*From the Paladin world — concurrent with the arc*

---

The report said: one paladin, one squire, a village on the eastern road, a werewolf situation.

This was not the report the king had wanted. The king had wanted: *the fruit secured, in transit, will arrive by such-and-such date.* He had been waiting for this report for three years, since the survey team confirmed the tree was real and fruiting and the window was closing. Seven days after picking. Seven days, and then whatever it cured, it stopped curing.

He had deployed six pairings across the eastern roads. He had thought six was more than enough.

Five of them had reported back. None had the fruit.

The sixth had reported: one paladin, one squire, a village on the eastern road, a werewolf situation.

---

He read the report again, in the way he read things he hoped would change on second reading. It did not change. His chief advisor was standing to one side of the window, which was where he stood when he expected the king to say something that required him to pretend not to have heard it.

"The paladin was bitten," the king said.

"According to the report," the advisor said.

"And the squire—"

"No information on the squire's status, sire."

He set the report down. The paladin was good — he had reviewed the assignments himself, had matched the best available pairings to the most probable tree locations, had given this particular paladin the most likely location because he had thirty-one years of experience and was reliable in difficult situations.

Bitten. In a difficult situation.

"The tree is in that region," the king said.

"Yes."

"The pairing was deployed to find the tree."

"Yes."

"And instead they encountered—"

"A werewolf situation. Yes."

He stood up and went to the window, which was where he stood when he needed to think without the advisor watching his face. Below: the courtyard, the stables, the guards. Everything in order. Everything proceeding according to plans that were, in various ways, not proceeding.

The tree fruited once a decade. This was the decade. If the fruit was not recovered in the next — he calculated — he did not know exactly how long, but not long enough — then the next opportunity would be ten years hence, and ten years hence he was not sure whether the particular condition requiring the cure would still be a condition requiring anything, or would simply be a condition.

"Send someone to find the squire," he said.

"Sire?"

"The paladin is bitten. The squire is presumably still functional. Someone should find out what the squire is doing." He looked at the courtyard below. The guards changed positions, orderly, the way things changed when they had been trained to. "They were deployed to find the tree. The squire may have continued the assignment."

The advisor was quiet for a moment. "The squire is seventeen."

"I am aware of the squire's age."

"It would be—"

"Unprecedented. Yes. Find out what the squire is doing."

He did not say: *the squire may have gone east, to the forest, to the tree, because that is where the assignment pointed.* He did not say this because he did not know it. He said: *find out what the squire is doing*, which was the same thing, reached by a different path.

He had learned, over a long reign, that seventeen was not nothing. Seventeen, given sufficient situation, sometimes did what thirty-one years of experience had failed to do. Not because seventeen was better. Because seventeen had not yet learned which things were not worth trying.

---

Three days later the report arrived: the fruit secured, the paladin recovering, a shepherd named Tomás also recovering, the village unharmed. The squire was unharmed.

He read the report in the way he read things that had come out better than he'd expected. Twice, to make sure.

"The squire took two fruits," the advisor observed. "The second was for—"

"The shepherd. Yes." He set the report down. "The squire didn't know that when he took it."

"No."

He thought about this. The squire had stood at the tree with one fruit in his hand and taken a second before knowing why. Had ridden to his master, cured his master, returned to the village, faced the creature, given the second fruit to the creature before the creature stopped being a creature, and the creature had become a shepherd from two days east who had not gone home because he did not know what he had become.

The squire had not known, when he took the second fruit, that there was a shepherd. He had taken it anyway.

"What is the squire's name?" the king asked.

The advisor told him.

He noted the name. He did not say anything further about it. There would be a time for further discussion, after everything was finished, when the squire had returned and filed a report and been given the standard debrief. There would be a time to say: *there is more work, if you want it.*

For now: the fruit was secured. The window was closing. There were other things to attend to.

He attended to them.
