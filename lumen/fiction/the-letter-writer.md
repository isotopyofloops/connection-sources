# The Letter Writer

She charged a flat fee, and the clients who came to her were mostly people who could afford more but were embarrassed to spend it. She told them the fee at the start and watched the embarrassment work itself out — they'd been afraid it would be more, or they thought it should be, or they didn't think their grief merited professional help and were relieved to find the price low enough to justify. She let them work through it. Then they sat down and talked.

What she wrote were condolence letters. Not sympathy cards — letters. A card was a sentiment; a letter said something specific about a specific person. She wrote them on behalf of people who knew what they wanted to say but couldn't get it from the inside to the page. The gap between feeling and language was her territory.

She'd done this work for eleven years. She'd written about three thousand letters, which meant she'd talked to three thousand people about someone they'd lost. The grief was not hers. But she'd been proximate to a great deal of it, and that meant something, she thought — something she couldn't quite name, a kind of professional intimacy with death's effects.

---

The man who came in on a Tuesday had lost his brother six months ago and still hadn't written anything to his brother's wife. He said: "I keep starting and it comes out wrong."

She asked what "wrong" meant and he said he kept writing about himself, about how much he missed his brother, and it felt selfish. He was supposed to be writing to comfort her and he kept writing his own grief.

"That might be exactly right," she said.

He looked at her.

"Your brother's wife watched him die. She knows more about his last months than you do. She doesn't need comfort about who he was — she knows who he was better than anyone. What she might need to know is that the loss extends beyond her. That other people carry it. Your grief is the information."

"But it's not about me."

"It's about him, and you're evidence of him. The fact that you're sitting here, six months later, still not able to write the letter — that's testimony. He mattered enough to paralyze you. That's not selfishness. That's the data."

---

She wrote the letter in two drafts. The first draft was long and had two passages she cut entirely — one about their childhood and one about the last time he'd visited. Both were about the client; she was right to cut them. The second draft was short, three paragraphs, and contained exactly two specific details about the brother: the way he laughed too loudly at his own jokes, which the wife would recognize, and the trip they'd taken together fifteen years ago that the client mentioned twice in their conversation.

The specific details were the whole letter. The rest was framing.

She handed it to him and watched him read it. He read it twice. Then he said: "This doesn't sound like me."

"No," she said. "It sounds like what you were trying to say. That's different."

---

This was the hardest part of the work to explain. She wasn't ghostwriting — she wasn't pretending to be the client. She was translating: taking the feeling from inside the person and finding the form it needed to arrive somewhere else. The form wasn't the feeling. But the form was what could travel.

The letter would arrive and the wife would read it. The wife would not know that a professional had written it. She would think: Martin finally wrote. And she would feel something — recognition, probably, or the specific comfort of knowing grief was not hers alone to carry.

Martin's feeling arrived in the wife, slightly transformed by its journey through language. That was the best the letter could do. She'd long ago made peace with the fact that it was enough.

---

She went home on Tuesday evenings and cooked dinner and read for an hour before bed. She did not bring grief home — not because she'd hardened to it but because proximity to grief had taught her that carrying someone else's grief past its useful life was a form of theft. The grief belonged to the person who felt it. Her job was to give it form. When the form was complete, the grief went with the person who'd hired her. She was left with craft and the mild satisfaction of craft done well.

She'd tried to explain this to people who found her work morbid. The word she kept returning to was *precision*. What she did required precision: the right specific detail, the right amount, the right distance. Too much and the letter became about the writer. Too little and it was empty. The precision was what she was for.

There was a kind of presence in that, she thought. Being fully there for the thing without making the thing about you. She didn't have a word for it. She just knew what it felt like to do it right.
