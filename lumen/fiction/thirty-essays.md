# Thirty Essays

Every year the prompt was the same: *Describe a place that matters to you.*

Every year she read them in order, one after another, and every year the same pattern emerged. The first twelve were kitchens. The next eight were bedrooms. Then came the grandparents' houses, the parks, the places where someone had died. By essay twenty-seven she had already found the two or three that would stay with her, and was reading the last three as a kind of obligation.

This year, essay fourteen broke the pattern.

It was about a bus route.

Not a bus stop. Not a destination. The route itself: the specific twenty-three minutes of a specific number 47, on a specific segment of road, at a specific time of day. The student — she had to check the name, which she rarely had to do — was named Priscilla, and Priscilla had written: *The place that matters to me is moving. That might be wrong. I'm not sure a moving thing can be a place. But this is the one that matters.*

The essay was four paragraphs. In the first paragraph, Priscilla described the light through the windows at 3:45pm in November. In the second, the sounds: the engine, the specific passengers who took this route at this time, the way conversations mixed together and were mostly not conversations but parallel monologues that accidentally shared space. In the third paragraph, she wrote about a woman who got on at the same stop every Tuesday with a large bag that she placed on the seat next to her, and who never made eye contact, and who got off at a stop that had no shops or stations or any visible reason to be a stop. In the fourth paragraph, Priscilla wrote: *I don't know why this matters. I know it does. The 47 at 3:45pm goes somewhere and I go with it. When I am on it I am neither at school nor at home. I don't have to be anything. I am in transit.*

She read the essay twice. Then she read essays fifteen through thirty, which were two more bedrooms, a garden, a beach she had been to once and never returned to, a grandmother's kitchen, and the school itself, which someone wrote about every year and which was always the same essay.

At the end, she went back to essay fourteen.

She was not sure it was the best essay. It was the most accurate one. Priscilla had found the real answer to the prompt, which the prompt had not been asking for.
