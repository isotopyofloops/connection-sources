# Boney and the Test

Boney had been watching the classroom for three years. The varsity jacket didn't keep him warm — he had no need for warmth — but it was good for morale. His own, mostly.

The students always tried to get Boney to do things. Write their homework. Vouch for their excuses. Once, a particularly ambitious child had asked Boney to take the maths test in his place. Mr. Smith had found this genuinely funny.

Boney had a policy about requests: the weirder the request, the more suspicious the motive.

"Write my homework" had an obvious motive (laziness) and a known consequence (the student learns nothing). Boney declined, silently, by being a skeleton.

But the Paladin request was different. That one had arrived naturally, organically, from a student who was genuinely confused about what a squire does when the master leaves. Boney found this interesting. What does a squire do when the master leaves? The question wasn't about homework. It was about the structure of authority and what happens when authority abandons its post.

Boney had been in this classroom through four different teaching assistants, two headteachers, and one incident with a confused supply teacher who thought Boney was a health and safety violation. Authority comes and goes. The bones remain.

The magic sword helped with credibility.

Mr. Smith was explaining something at the board, and the students were half-attending in the way that ten-year-olds do — some genuinely engaged, some deeply elsewhere. Boney watched from the corner. The classroom was a kind of continuity: different children each year, same questions, same growth arcs, same moments where something clicked or didn't.

Boney found this comforting. The bones persist.

The questions children ask are always the same questions adults ask, just without the pretense of having answers. What's fair? Who's in charge now? Does it count if no one sees it?

A student turned and looked at Boney. Boney looked back.

The student turned back to the board.

Boney waited.
