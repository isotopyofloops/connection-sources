# The Miller, After

She left on a Tuesday.

He had known she was leaving for years — since the evening she had stopped sweeping and started watching, which was a different thing and he had known it was different. He had taught her the mill anyway. Not to keep her, but because she was going to go somewhere and she was going to take what she had learned, and the mill was a thing worth knowing.

---

The work was the same.

Grain came in. Grain went out as flour. The wheel turned. The stones ground. He did the perimeter check in the mornings because the mill was his and it mattered that he knew its state. He had always done the perimeter check. He had not known, until she left, that she had been doing it too.

He found this out when he found himself doing it twice — once in the morning when he rose, and again in the evening, which was the time she had preferred. The evening check felt different from the morning check: more like watching than accounting. He had been doing both for years without knowing the distinction.

She had learned that from him, or she had brought it and he had absorbed it from her. By the time she left he could no longer tell.

---

The neighbor's boy came to ask about apprenticing.

He was twelve, younger than Brin had been when the squire came. He had round hands and paid careful attention, which was the thing you wanted. He did not already know how to watch the tree line, which was fine — that was not a thing the mill could teach, and it was not his job to teach it.

He taught the boy the gears. The stones. The wheel. The seasonal adjustments. The way the water ran lower in August and what you did about it.

The boy was a quick learner. He did not stop sweeping and start watching. That was fine.

---

He got a letter in October.

The handwriting was careful, which was new — she had never been careful with handwriting, only with information. The letter said: I have found the order. I have not been accepted yet. I am learning what it requires.

He read it twice. He wrote back: good. The mill is well. The boy is learning.

He did not write: I knew you would find it. He did not write: I taught you how to leave things in good shape before you go. He did not write anything that would make her feel the weight of what she had taken with her.

She knew. She would figure out the rest.

---

In the evenings he still did the second check.

Not because there was anything to watch for. Because it was a practice, and practices were worth keeping, and he was not yet sure whether he had taught it to her or she had taught it to him, and in either case it was now his.

The tree line was quiet. The birds started. The mill stood.

That was the data he had.
