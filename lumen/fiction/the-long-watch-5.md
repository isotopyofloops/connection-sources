# The Long Watch (5)

*The second day: she looks at the stairs.*

---

She opens the door to the hallway in the morning, before she has eaten anything, because she thinks it will be easier then. The reasoning is obscure to her when she examines it, but she follows it anyway.

The hallway is not surprising. This is itself notable. She expected something from it — some quality that would explain why she had been putting it off — but the hallway is just a hallway: pale walls, a runner rug in a pattern that was once geometric and is now simply worn. A light fixture on the ceiling that gives off the particular yellow of electrical decisions made long ago.

The stairs are at the end.

She walks to them and stands at the top, looking down.

Below is a landing, then another flight. She can see as far as the ground floor, where a door — wooden, heavy, with a long vertical window of frosted glass — lets in diffuse gray light. Nothing moves. There is a coat hook near the door with nothing on it.

She stands there for a while. A minute, maybe two. She is waiting to see if the building will do something. It doesn't.

Then she turns and looks up.

Above is one more landing and what appears to be a door. This door is different from the one below — it's painted the same pale color as the walls, so she might have missed it if she hadn't been looking. Someone painted it deliberately. Someone wanted it to be nearly invisible.

She writes in the notebook, standing at the top of the stairs: *Below: ground floor, coat hook, heavy door, frosted glass, gray light. Nothing on the hook. Above: painted door, nearly wall-colored. Why.*

She looks up for another minute. No sound from above.

She takes the stairs down.

The ground floor is a lobby of some kind, though it has been emptied of most lobby qualities. A desk that probably once had a purpose, now bare. A chair pushed into a corner. The frosted-glass door is locked with a deadbolt, and she does not have the key.

She finds this interesting rather than alarming. She is, it seems, on the inside of a locked building. She writes this down: *Ground floor. Lobby. Deadbolt, no key.* Then: *I am not surprised. I wonder if I already knew.*

There is another door off the lobby — a plain interior door, unlocked — that opens into a stairwell leading further down. She stands at this threshold and looks into the below-below: concrete steps, the smell of old cool air, something that might be the hum of pipes or might be nothing.

She does not go down there today. She writes: *Below the ground floor: stairs, smell, possibly pipes. Tomorrow.*

She goes back up to her floor. Standing in the hallway, she looks toward the painted door at the top. It is still the same color as the walls. It is still nearly invisible.

She has been assuming she is alone in the building. She has not, until this moment, thought hard about that assumption.

She stands in the hallway for a while.

No sound from the painted door.

She goes back to her room and eats crackers at the window. The courtyard is the same. One of the opposite windows is lit again, though it is morning, and the light is different — the light has that quality of being left on rather than being turned on.

She writes: *Second day. The building has more parts than I had been treating it as having. Below-the-ground is real and I should look at it. Above is a painted door and I have not yet.* Then: *I don't know if anyone else is here. I realized I had been assuming. The assumption may not be wrong. But I should not be certain when I am not certain.*

She puts the pen down.

Outside, the lit window in the building opposite is still on. Nobody moves in it. She watches it for a long time, with the patience she has apparently always had, which she is slowly understanding is not patience at all but a different thing entirely — a willingness to stay with a question until it either opens or doesn't.

The window stays lit. Nothing moves. She keeps watching.

*Tomorrow: the painted door.*
